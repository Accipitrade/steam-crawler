\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFew things piss me off more than entitled gamers saying a game has been abandoned, when in fact it is finished, feature-complete and mostly bug-free. That said, Lilith Odyssey is pretty much a definition of an abandoned game. It's abundantly clear that things were meant to be more complex, more refined... but the devs ran out of life energy, and decided to cut their losses and release the game in its current state.

And I can't blame them. Developing a game of this magnitude while also working a full-time job is an arduous task. Unless we find some kind of way to allow indie devs to work full time without having to worry about sustaining themselves and sales, this is what most projects will end up like. And it's a shame, because there's a pretty solid base here.

It feels like this was a game developed "concept first", which is almost always a red flag. For example, there's a ship radio, with custom songs, talk shows, even ad breaks - all quite well done and giving some nice background info on the world of the game. At the same time, the walk and attack animations look like placeholders, some systems make almost no sense... and there's a lot of stuff which looks like there's supposed to be more to it. But alas.

Everything feels incredibly clunky. It's possible to get stuck in the tutorial, since you need a certain resource to perform an action, and there's a pretty high chance you won't have that resource at that time. Unless you look around, you won't even figure out what the problem is, and chances are, you'll think that the tutorial is simply broken.

And even if you do get past the tutorial... well, there's the excitement of a journey in a new world, but once you realise just how clunky and empty everything is, you either cut your losses or try to find something worth playing for.

I'm not going to lie, the atmosphere of the game charmed me enough that I really wanted to find that something. But then eventually I reached a planet which had large, aggressive monsters. Once I got spotted, I couldn't beam out, I couldn't fight back with my starter weapons... I ran into a city, hoped that some guards or something would help me, but nope - so I died. And that was the last straw for me.

A real shame. Lilith Odyssey is a beautiful mess, but at the end of the day, a mess is still a mess. All we're left with is the bitter sadness at what it could've been, and the knowledge it will never be.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally enjoyed my first play through I highly recommend the game!

I enjoyed the gameplay loop and the goal of reaching Lilith kept me enticed enough to actually play through it and reach it!

One of my favorite parts has to be the radio, the ads are hilarious, I swear I’d wait before going onto a planet so I could hear the whole ad, really wish there was a handheld radio!

Now it wasn’t without issue but my issues weren’t game breaking so I’d recommend the game nonetheless.

The most annoying issue I had was refueling, anytime I’d convert a fuel cell to fuel it would lock up the game for a few seconds, this was so much worse on a ship with a massive fuel tank it froze for about 6 minutes.

This may only be an issue due to me playing at 1080P so take this with a grain of salt, I noticed a lot of the words didn’t look very clear and the game didn’t seem to respect any changes to the resolution so potentially it was running for me under 1080P making text look blurry.

The manual specifies any item that is bought or crafted can be dismantled which is not true no item that I bought or crafted gave an option to dismantle so I ended up with a huge inventory of things I didn’t need, also made it difficult as only common resources can be sold to the market the rest must be sold directly to NPCs which you can’t pick the quantity or what it is they’ll just offer to buy a random item from your inventory.

I made an exorbitant amount of money and had absolutely nothing to spend it on, the most expensive ship I could find was $41,000 and I had well over $160,000 from hunting primarily and selling extra batteries, the tier 3 items I unlocked via research never ended up in the stores so it kind of made money totally useless after I bought the ship as money’s only purpose was to heal injured family and ship repair which at the point was an insignificant cost.

There was no assigned key binding for “Open Manual” and when I finally found a key that wasn’t already set to something it listed the name of the key not the symbol so you had overlapping text over where it should say “Manual” all I could read was “Back:Quote” as I used the back quote key.

All in all this game was very shway and I will be replaying it! I would have loved slightly more content maybe a few more ships and the ability to create a custom family would have been awesome but as it stands I think it’s worth the price of admission!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, I selected "No" to recommend, but I want to be clear that this is because of the binary yes/no option Steam gives me. If I had like a scoring system I'd given this somewhere between 3-4 out of 5. It's got a lot of greatness to it, but what it also has are a lot of little drawbacks which when added up make the game less fun.

By way of metaphor this game was about as enjoyable as a cake that was pulled out of the oven just a little too soon. Its structure is there, and you can eat around the undercooked center and be like "man, good stuff." But that cake isn't winning awards or going to please anyone's mom or birthday kid.

The world is cute, and a little goofy, and that works really well. I like how the game kinda brands itself as a just-enjoy kind of experience. Most of the actual gameplay is pretty easy, even on the "hardest" difficulty, which if you know going in means this is the kind of game you could play for the fun of it and/or for the worldbuilding. Lots of good things to say to the two Devs on that.

At the same time, there is just a lot to nit-pick at. Buggy and insanely slow movements while on planet, poor information relaying and retention, UI challenges - you quickly learn that this "chill" game has an steep learning curve since you need to basically do everything yourself without the benefit of any kind of tooltips (and the tutorial which bugs and crashes out). All the fun items with quirky names? You have to intuit what they do, if they do anything, and most of them just seem to give a small xp% boost over time to things like fishing or farming. So good luck figuring out what does what, you just have to guess or know from past experience. And outside of ship equipment and maybe weapons, "what does what" is kinda pointless since most items aren't going to be all that useful to you. I'd think some of them would help with space madness but nope. You have the passive worry about space madness, which requires you to pitstop at a planet - this is clever - but you have no way to know 1) which planet is populated other than looking for satellites or 2) the conditions on the planet before you land. So you beam down and find out it's full of roving monsters or whatnot, but also other SPUs who just seem to be chilling around. But you better put your suit on because you might die, even if they are in shirts and shorts. And if you "go inside" the mall you'll still need your suit AND can still be attacked while navigating the buy/sell screens.

Also, you don't get to choose where you beam down. Not a big deal given that planets aren't large, BUT the insanely slow movement speed means you're invested in just trying to find a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ mall or town hall.

Once you've figured out (most) of what does what. you're pretty much set. You start off broke, but if you just harvest and sell all the resources on your starter planet and then passively invest you'll probably have enough to buy a mid-top tier ship within 2 game months + some tier 2 and tier 3 upgrades (which again you know are upgrades because of intuition, deduction, or buying and THEN seeing what the hell a thing does, since the game doesn't give you any kind of detail beyond some flavor text prior to buying). This could make the game ridiculously easy, but you still have to manage space madness and in principle avoid some parts of the galaxy. The waypoint system requires a lot more micromanaging than you'd expect from a game that branded itself as a chill experience though. But once you realize the only real limitation is managing space madness - which, because the litany of items don't really help with that (or if they do, you don't know because they don't tell you, like how a lot of this game works) you're hopping planet to planet (which is fine) but without any details about planets (not so fine) and just beaming down to be "somewhere" long enough to reduce space madness, then off you go again.

I haven't gotten to Lilith - the game crashed a few times and I was already feeling divested - and just don't feel the urge to; the game doesn't stick with me enough. That potential is there with some more polishing, some bug fixes and reworks of how the user interacts with and engages in the world. But right now it doesn't feel like a 1.0 even, and from what the Devs themselves have said this project is now just one of a few they're working on so even with their roadmap it's hard to say when it'll get to a better state of play. I wish them well, and will check in on this later to see if it's better in time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOk, so. This game I feel is a great arcade-style game. You get to your end location through the galaxy whilst watching your supplies of rations and fuel. It can be fairly easy to either grind out the last tier of technology, or bypass the researching by buying certain equipment from traders which saves a lot of time (albeit certain research is needed for creating ammo and such). Earning money is fairly easy. Once you have the latest technology (Three tiers) You can pretty much glide through the entire game with such ease the only danger you have is storms which will only partly damage your ship's equipment. Enemies pose no real threat except from at the beginning when you do know what you are doing. The whole aesthetic and feel of the game is great at the beginning with the radio station on your ship and the different biomes the planet's have. After a handful of hours you notice the planets aren't really that much different, there isn't enough unique creatures or objects to discover spread through a thousand planets. You find yourself wanting to skip to the end planet once you have discovered all you think the game can offer you. There are landmarks in the galaxy like giant structures or a black hole but you can't interact with them at all, you just look at them from your ship in awe as the game tells you "Well done for discovering this, now carry on." That is why I state this is more arcade-style. It is a great game, I enjoyed my time whilst it was fresh but the freshness dies too quickly for a game promising so much uniqueness and size.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLilith Odyssey is a strange, strange game. Mad props to the devs for making something that looks and feels so unique.

The issue with it is like with most high-ambition Early Access games we all know too well: it fell just short of becoming a hidden Steam gem. The game is announced as a expansive exploration and sandbox game, with some sort of lore and story going on, and a bunch of moving parts. At the current state - and it hurts me to say it - the game is mostly a Roguelike Get-to-the-finish-line-quick sort of deal, in which you can ignore all of it and explore, build and customize your ship and just fly around.
It's pretty relaxing, and it's absolutely clear that love and work went into here.

However, the game quickly becomes shallow, exploring becomes repetitive, and the items and equipment just feel like a simple, straight progression of better stats. Moving is painfully slow, and things feel somewhat artificially stretched out for little reason.

If the game was in the early days of its early access, this review would be mentioning the potential and the foundation of the game, rather than Not Recommending it for having issues. However, there's signs of development slowing or stopping, and I wouldn't recommend the game in general in its current state.
Exception here is if you are really into casual exploration games, somewhat in the veins of the exploration-feel in No Man's Sky and Star Control Origins OR if you're into Survival Roguelikes. Sandbox games is my main thing, and I wouldn't call this a sandbox game yet.
Other than that, I'd wait for further developments.

Would gladly revisit if development continues.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't quite put my finger on it just yet, but I'm more than keen to say this game is very well worth the money.

It has this super interesting gameplay loop of harvesting, crafting, trading and upgrading your spaceship - but what makes it really interesting for me, is that its not open ended but has a clear goal: you want to get to a distant part of galaxy, far away from your home.

You basically travel from planet to planet (there are stations too), and you hope to land on a planet with good amount of resources. You harvest those (standard stuff: cut trees, mine rocks, kill mobs), and you build small buildings to help you get stuff like food and fuel. Some planets are inhabited, so you can also trade with locals, which is -afaik- your only way of getting better spaceships.
Once you've had enough and prepared for next stretch of the trip - you pack your buildings up, teleport back to your spacecraft, and map a new route.

During the trip, you can read pretty neat manual, listen to pretty awesome radio stations, and if you have resources - research and craft items, and upgrade your ship.

The quirkiness of the game is what makes it really worth it, for me. For example - if you spend too much time in space, you start developing 'space madness', and the way to offset that - is to set your exercise, sleep and masturbation schedule. I kid you not. There's a graph of 'Sexual frustration' that you gotta pay attention to.

I am not quite sure whether this'll be like... 6h game, 10h game, 20h game or like 100h game... but for sure, its really nice and fun and quite unique, and its worth your money if you like this kind of stuff.\t\t\t\t\t
