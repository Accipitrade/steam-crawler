\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn extremely messy game with a great story and what could have been a great combat system.

As a "Remaster", it doesn't seem all that great as it seems like all it did was have better loading times and it can run on better hardware now. I've had a few crashes in-game and even during cutscenes; I've even had crashes during boss missions, which don't auto-save until the end, making me have to redo the boss as a result. Even the graphics look like they've just been taken from the Wii U without much polish.

Its story is themed around the Japanese transformation super hero stuff, and it can be very entertaining with how over-the-top it gets with its camp and goofiness all the way to the end. You can choose between EN and JP audio and they're both exaggerated, however, the subtitle timings of either are different as well so the text or voices can move too fast or too slow.

It's a character-action arcade game overall, except you're controlling one hero who's leading many more, and you combine them to make giant weapons and other utilities to save the day. To make said weapons, you make use of the drawing mechanic in the game, which can be very frustrating to do, especially when it can be interrupted when you least expect it, like when the camera zooms in and something like an environmental hazard or enemy you can't touch is off-screen as you can't make drawings on those.
The combat itself can be great when you get it working, but alongside these frustrations comes a big learning curve as well, especially since the game doesn't teach you a whole lot.

The camera is almost always at an isometric angle. Thanks to that, it makes inputting directional-based inputs a lot easier. However, that angle also comes with its problems, never mind the weird pseudo-dual-monitor aspect of the game.
The depth perception for platforming sections can be confusing. There's supposed to be a big colored ring under you to tell you where you are, but when there's no floor under you or your teammates are covering that ring, you're left to do some guesswork.
You're almost always required to make yourself visible to the camera, and the environment doesn't always become transparent to show your character either; this is especially more of a problem against larger enemies as you'll constantly be trying to make sure they don't block your view so you don't accidentally touch a spike or get blindsided by a smaller attack.
The biggest problem with the camera is that it tends to zoom in when you just want a better view of the battlefield so you can keep an eye on enemies that are farther away. I'm guessing you're supposed to react to audio cues, but there can be so much noise going on that the sound of incoming attacks can get drowned out. No matter how much you zoom out, the game will always zoom in again at some point for whatever reason, even when there's only one enemy left.

When you actually get the combat working, it's very satisfying to do, but there's so much wonkiness to try and keep in mind, namely the camera itself.
A single playthrough is fine for its ridiculous story, but for a Platinum game, it's really frustrating to even tolerate replaying; even some of the collectibles are frustrating to acquire, namely the ones that require you to do all the secret missions in each level on three specific difficulties.
It takes around a dozen hours to finish the first playthrough as well, which is pretty long for what's essentially an arcade game.\t\t\t\t\t
