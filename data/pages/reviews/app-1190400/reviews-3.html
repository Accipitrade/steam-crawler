\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPositives

+ The story, dialogue and general tone are absolutely over the top but completely endearing and with a solid identity.
+ Good voice acting that compliments the cheesy tone.
+ The overall design of the characters, enemies and levels are really good. The details of the models aren’t amazing if you zoom in, but the fun designs make up for it, and it isn’t supposed to be zoomed in outside of the odd cutscene.
+ The core combat mechanics are unique and really solid once you get the hang of it. The combat has good depth and is almost puzzle-like at times where you have to figure out which abilities to use to beat enemies.
+ Great set pieces - they’re absolute chaos and tons of fun.
+ Good puzzles for you to solve that are neither too easy nor too hard.
+ The game has a good way of handling skill - you can die as many times as needed and still complete the missions but with a bad score, and for those who play well and meet certain criteria they can get increasingly better scores.

Neutral

/ The tutorials are mostly decent but slip up now and then and can leave you scratching your head. Additionally, not making Unite Guts (block) and Unite Spring (dodge) mandatory parts of the opening prologue was a mistake.
/ There are quite a few sections with either modified controls or a new set of controls and they can vary quite a lot in terms of polish and fun. Some add to the mayhem, while others really detract from it.

Negatives

- Every now and then you encounter dual-screen sections where there’s a lot going on between the two screens and it’s a bit of a mess on a single screen.
- Every now and then you have these smaller indoor sections and they’re really cumbersome to navigate - they really highlight how the game/camera wasn’t made for small interiors and leave you fighting the camera.
- There are a couple of timed platforming sections which can be frustrating as it can be extremely easy to lose track of where you are and where you’re jumping around.
- In general the game tries to do a bit too much at times and flips between too many different game modes.
- Luka becomes fairly core to the story but he’s pretty annoying and detracts from the fun.
- The game randomly pauses at times - no idea why. Doesn’t happen that often, but it’s annoying when it does.

Conclusion

This game is really great and one-of-a-kind, but with some glaring flaws. It’s a very much larger-than-life presentation which at times tries to do too much. It’s definitely a hectic game, and there are a few sections where it gets borderline overwhelming and can be pretty confusing. If these sections had been reworked or stripped away, it would have been a much smoother game overall.

That being said, it’s still a load of fun - from both the cheesy Power Rangers style team and story to the unique and interesting combat. I’ve read someone say before that the first playthrough of the game is the tutorial and I understand the point now that I’ve played it. There really is a lot to learn throughout the game and you’re unlikely to get good scores on your first playthrough. There’s a lot of fun to be had here though if you can ride the steeper learning curve - I would recommend spending time at the start to really get to grips with the basic mechanics so that the extra ones that get layered on in certain sections aren’t quite so overwhelming.

Basic Controls Guide

I’ve written the below in an attempt to give new players some basic points to look over, but it isn’t a comprehensive list. It might seem a bit daunting, but it doesn’t take too long to get the hang of it if you know what to focus on, and it’s a really rewarding game. You can practice most if not all of the below in the first level (following the prologue) - I would advise getting to grips with this. If you get particularly mauled in the first level, maybe replay it first before moving on. Note: I’ve written the below based on an Xbox controller layout.
  • Immediately after the prologue on the main menu, go into the store and buy Unite Guts and Unite Spring. They’re so fundamental they have their own button mappings: Unite Guts is LT and is for blocking (most things, but nothing sharp), and Unite Spring is RT and is for dodging. They cost essentially nothing and are borderline essential. Unlike a lot of other abilities/actions, you can pop either of these mid-way through another action and immediately activate them.
  • You use the right stick to draw out shapes for various moves. This is the bread-and-butter for how you do anything apart from blocking and dodging.
  • Drawing out shapes with right stick and NOT pressing B (which is for activating abilities) will just use Wonder Liner - use this to gather up civilians into your flock by drawing around them, and to create ladders (up a wall) and bridges (across gaps), as well as to send this straight down into the Wonder Toilets (for goodies).
  • Drawing out shapes and pressing B will activate what you’ve drawn into an ability e.g. a circle shape will turn the Wonder Liner red and pressing B will turn it into Unite Hand.
  • You can always tell if you’ve got the shape correct because the Wonder Liner will change colour relevant to what you’ve drawn e.g. Red = Unite Hand, Blue = Unite Sword.
  • The bigger you draw the shape, the bigger the resultant ability will be e.g. a small circle activated will make a small Unite Hand, while a big circle using 100 people will make a mammoth Unite Hand.
  • The more heroes / civilians you acquire, the bigger your Wonder Liner can become.
  • The more heroes / civilians you use for an ability, the more of your energy it uses up.
  • If you’re struggling with a particular shape / ability, spend time after acquiring it to get to grips with it.
  • If you’ve drawn something out and want to undo it, or have an ability active that you want to deactivate, press X to revert to neutral.
  • If you get an X prompt on-screen to do something but it’s greyed out and you can’t activate it, consider what you need to do first to be able to activate it e.g. activate Unite Hand to be able to use it.
  • Click in the left thumbstick to group your heroes up around your leader. Mostly useful in situations where you’re trying to avoid certain hazards.
  • If you’re struggling against an enemy, consider what is and isn’t working. For example, if you try to use Unite Hand to punch a shield and nothing is happening, that isn’t the right thing to do and you need to try different things to see what works.
  • If you get hit and lose some of your squad, don’t worry about gathering literally everyone back - if you focus too much on this you’ll probably just get hit again.
  • When fights get more hectic with more enemies, try to pay attention to the bigger picture as to what is going on to avoid getting hit - you can’t just mindlessly attack the nearest thing and not pay attention to your surroundings.
  • Use LB and RB to zoom out and in (respectively). Sometimes the game will zoom in by default, but you almost always want to be zoomed out so that you can see what’s going on.

\r\n\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t\t
\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat initially seems like an absolutely absurd, incomprehensible mess of ideas that almost seemed to hate those who decided to pick this up ends up being one of the single greatest gaming experiences I've had. Nothing about this game seemingly makes sense, but once you start really getting into it you realise just what you've been missing out on by considering it all as such. An idiosyncratic experience that's willing to push boundaries and experiment a whole lot with its core mechanics to make it stand out strongly from the pack while also showcasing nothing but an unabashed love for the genre. It's got the standard ideas that you'll find in a lot of the Platinum lineup with the way it insists on being a mishmash of differing styles, has an insane skill ceiling that'll make you feel like a fool on your first playthrough, and also going absolutely all out in its sense of pure maximalism. The big difference that separates a lot of what this game does compared to a game such as Bayonetta is the way that it takes extra measures to do as much as possible to unify these styles, while still providing a nice bit of absurdity to the sharp left turns it can take to further reinforce its incredibly full on, over the top vibe.

One of the most truly bizarre aspects of this game is the way that almost all of its defining features can be seen as pure negatives when you start off before they eventually reveal their significance once you've gotten a bit better. The weapon drawing is definitely the biggest example of this with the way that it initially seems as if it's adding another layer of complexity to something that doesn't require it that also adds an additional thing that the player could get wrong. There are a few benefits to this however that end up making it extremely well worth it as a mechanic. Most importantly is the way this opens the door to a far more seamless method of swapping between the absurd amount of weapons at your disposal, with the player having access to 10 different ones by the end of the game and then a couple extra for repeat playthroughs, giving the player this method of quickly and easily swapping between them all in a fast paced, chaotic game like this is a perfect fit, not to mention that the additional nuance of the different sizes for each weapon is another benefit of this. Furthermore, additional elements to do with actually drawing are quite interesting too, with enemy positioning and room layout potentially obstructing you and forcing you to make certain compromises or devise another strategy to deal with the situation at hand, providing depth without being frustrating.

The other big point with the game that seems bad at first but has its own little positives is the camera, which I will admit feels closer to being equal parts horrible and genius. I'll definitely say that the camera in the game feels too zoomed in or outright misplaced in certain situations and it's absolute agony at times, but there are also other benefits that it has that end up justifying it and make the problem feel more akin to "they just needed to fix up a few spots here and there". My favourite effect of the zoomed out isometric camera is the way that it makes every threat truly feel gargantuan with the way that your army will look like a colony of ants scurrying around against even a lot of the regular fodder enemies that are sent your way. The sheer spectacle of this cannot be understated and also adds a lot to highlighting the game's core theme about teamwork being what will help fight against insurmountable odds. It's also not as bad as it seems because while it might be hard to see your individual character, the morph weapons are positioned in such a way to act almost as beacons when activated, standing tall with these incredibly bright colours that make it impossible to truly get lost in even the most visually busy scenes.

Of all the flawed masterpieces that I've experienced, this might just be the most flawed with how often it feels as if the game's almost taking pleasure in slapping you around with outright infuriating scenarios and a mix of genres that sometimes just flat out don't work, but at the same time, the highlights are otherworldly and a lot of the frustration feels far more constructive compared to a lot of other difficult experiences. In most cases, when things feel as if they're taking forever, it doesn't quite feel like the enemies are too much to overcome, but rather than the player themselves are approaching the situation incorrectly and need to further explore the seemingly limitless possibilities of their immense arsenal. So many of the tough moments are less traditional action encounters and more like minor puzzles, asking you to further optimise your gameplay, usually showing off just enough for you to be able to figure things out if you're paying attention while never being too obvious to further make it feel like your own accomplishment with each time you're able to take down these enemies slightly faster than before. This is likely the reason for the relatively few types of common enemy in the game too, because it instead wants the player to build familiarity with this smaller selection to truly master what they can do, because at the end of the day, Wonderful 101 is a game that encourages players to master the systems and dynamics at play in a way that I don't really see too often. It's not really fully content with players just stumbling through to the end, it wants them to fully get a grip on everything it has to offer, and the incredibly forgiving continue system is a huge indicator of this, clearly not really being concerned about whether the player is able to beat the game, but how well they can do it, which I do think is cool in a game as dense as this.

Above all, this really does feel like one of the purest expressions of adoration for the video game medium as a whole, and it's a big reason why I think it's a masterpiece. There's an outright ridiculous amount of effort put into even the smallest of details that simply would not be so strongly considered in other games, let alone one where you probably won't even be able to see a lot of it too well thanks to the camera perspective. The fact that each of the titular 101 heroes of the game each have their own unique model and little character bio is one such aspect I love, how even though you're likely not going to be seeing them in any detail beyond "these look like tiny ants", they're fully implemented without any signs of concessions having to be made, each of them also being really funny and charming in how they look. The ever intensifying stakes are also really cool to witness in the way that it's also represented through the gameplay ramping up in ways that not only feel more difficult, but more exciting too.

Rather than frontloading the experience in an attempt to hook people with the sacrifice of making the later portions often feel somewhat less climactic and interesting in comparison, the final 2 missions of this game are instead where things get kicked into overdrive with some of the most insane and silly things I've seen in a game. Nothing about the way the game presents itself is subtle at all but I think it works exceptionally well with the way it plays so strongly into replicating the feeling of goofy superhero cartoons, never straying from making clearly dumb jokes or throwing things all at the wall for no reason more than just "I thought it'd be neat", because above all, it just wants to be a fun maximalist experience! I haven't quite played something like Wonderful 101 before and I'm not sure if I will again, but I can always hope to find another game that feels so committed to being such a joyful time that fully embraces and adores games as a whole that I am willing to call it one of the best things ever despite having so many points that frustrated me to the point of becoming an angry goblin creature who wanted to break her controller.

10/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really want to love this game, but it's such a frustrating experience to try and play.

The wonder-line controls simply do not function with a mouse. They are very close to working, but a portion of the time, starting a wonder-line creates a phantom input that shoots your line out in a random direction. This, obviously, completely destroys whatever shape you were attempting to draw, and you may have to re-start the line several times in a row before you get an input that starts on neutral.

This is something you're supposed to be doing quickly and accurately in combat, which is impossible given this bug. I have failed several very easy quicktime events simply because I was unable to draw a straight line in time.

This might be alleviated if you are using a controller, but the problems with this port don't stop there.

Sometimes, the game will randomly pause. It won't give you a menu or any way to un-pause, it will just stop with the "paused" text on screen. Hearsay says it's a poorly-disguised loading system, but regardless of the reason, on this platform it's equivalent to a crash- since it never unpauses, and there is no way to force it out of the pause. Combined with the stingy use of checkpoints, I've had to re-do several entire missions simply because the game softlocked itself for no reason at the last moment.

Additionally, this is a port of a game originally for the Wii U. While most of the time, the two-screen mechanics are irrelevant, there are a few sections of the game where looking at both screens becomes important.
I don't envy the game designers who had to make that work on a single screen, but as is, they really don't work very well with the pop-up window system the game has to emulate that. They are technically playable but it's highly disorienting. It's possible that there is no good way to port those sections, but the fact remains that they were ported badly.

Finally, the control mapping just... doesn't make sense. There is no ability to rebind keys, and there are several sections of the game where the controls are just bonkers: left and right punch bound to right mouse and middle mouse respectively, for example (yes, left punch is bound to right mouse). There must be something tightly coupling inputs under the hood for them to do that, since there is no way those were picked on purpose.

It makes me wonder why they even bothered with keyboard controls at all. They were clearly unable to change the input system to work well with a mouse and keyboard, why not save everyone the trouble and just declare "get a controller or go home"?


The game itself is fantastic, I suffered through the whole thing on a keyboard just to experience the whole thing. I can't recommend that experience to anyone else though.

If you want to play with a controller, the game is good but the port is a technical mess. If you want to play with a keyboard, don't.\t\t\t\t\t

Pros
- Viewtiful Joe 3 (pretty much)
- Signature Platinum Games Charm

Cons

- Selecting any individual hero is ass, thanks Wii U
- The minigames overstay their welcome,especially when your trying to get back to the core gameplay
- The levels are also drag on, I had to put aside time to complete the first level as i ran out of free time due to it being 20-30mins long without saves.

- Blocking and Guarding are for some reason non standard abilites, meaning you can enter the first level without em and greatly increase your chance of suffering a slog(ask me how i know)

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis port is kind of a mess. You need a mod to fix the extremely inconsistent framepacing, there are various camera and visual bugs if you set the resolution above 1080p (probably why it's still limited to 1080p on PS4 Pro & PS5), and from what little I tried the KB+M controls are atrocious and were clearly an afterthought compared to using a controller.

...But man, the game itself is so much fun. It takes a while to click - even compared to other games in this genre it has a very high skill floor before you start having fun, and some (many?) people manage to finish the game without reaching that skill floor thanks to the extremely generous continue system, but if you put in the effort to learn how to play it well then it pays that back in spades. There are no tutorials beyond basic controls - you're expected to learn through experimentation. That's admittedly not for everyone but I found it satisfying. The combat seems simple at first but there's an incredible amount of depth to it.

(Sidenote, this is a DX9 game, so if you have an Nvidia GPU I highly recommend using Nvidia Profile Inspector to force on OGSSAA or SGSSAA which really helps with the 1080p limitation. See the game's pcgamingwiki page for specifics, but don't use the listed "higher quality" AA compatibility flag there because it makes the water rainbow-colored)

The game does have flaws - the minigames and genre changes are a little too frequent (especially the shmup and rail shooter sections), the first half of the game feels a little bloated, you have to buy the basic guard and block abilities for some reason, a ton of combat abilities are locked behind either leveling up or the shop, most of the dual screen sections feel incredibly gimmicky, the camera sometimes zooms in way too far during combat, and there are lots of QTEs (though they're way less obnoxious than the ones in Bayonetta 1). In other words, it's a Hideki Kamiya game; you mostly know what you're in for if you've played one of his earlier games, particularly Viewtiful Joe or Bayonetta.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs with several of the games I have now on Steam, I first played this game on a different system, in this case the Wii U back in the mid 2010s when this game dipped to $30. As such, while my review will show the 16 hours I've put into the game on PC so far, my actual playtime is closer to 100 hours across platforms. I went in completely blind not knowing what I was getting into, buying it with some birthday money because why not, and when it was over I almost regretted not having bought it for $60 months earlier.

What is the Wonderful 101? The description I give to people is always "It's power rangers on crack."

In reality, it's a very niche game with an undeniable appeal and a very interesting blend of gameplay that is easy to get into but has a very high skill ceiling. At first glance many people think this is going to be just like Pikmin, but in reality it's like Devil May Cry, Bayonetta, Okami, Gurren Lagann, and Super Sentai had a very wild night together. This game is what resulted nine months later. Incidentally, Bayonetta is in the game too (as are not-Viewtiful Joe and his girlfriend Sylvia).

The basic premise is having control of several dozen heroes (at the start) and slowly expanding your reservoir as the game goes on in order to grow into a mighty force. There is a "light" attack that throws these heroes forward and can stun enemies after a time allowing you to hit harder with other moves, but this move is individuals going together in a group to attack. The "heavy" attack is where the real meat and potatoes of the game lies: UNITE MORPH! The Unite Morph is an ability granted to our heroes, The Wonderful Ones, which allows them to use their Centinel Suits in order to construct different objects out of thin air. This lets them turn into a rail that tosses a wayward bus away from a collision course with a school full of children, an elevator on a tall building, a massive plug in the glass wall of an underwater facility, and more. These however are not the morphs you'll be utilizing throughout the game, no no, the ones you use are the kind used to hit things, *really* hard. Unite Hand, Sword, Gun, Whip, and others are the tools of the trade, and while the light attack lets the heroes attack in a group, Unite Morphs let them attack as one, and this is a massive difference. Imagine if you will a group of one hundred people forming a giant fist with their bodies. You get what our red-clad leader, Wonder-Red, has jutting out of his left fist on the game page's title illustration but a whole lot bigger.

Unite Morphs are performed by drawing a symbol representing the weapon and then executing the attack which will transform the heroes into that weapon which is then wielded by a single Wonderful One to fight. You can choose the size of the morph, allowing you to keep some of the heroes on the ground to continue pummeling the enemy as a group while using the rest in your morph, adding wild variety as you unlock upgrades, and as you level up your individual Wonderful Ones by assigning them as the leader of that Unite Morph type (lead Unite Hand type unit, Unite Sword type unit, etc) you'll earn new moves for each individual Unite Morph. It doesn't stay as simple as "press button to attack" for very long at all so long as you remember to cycle your members. You'll have enough moves in your arsenal by the end to make Dante blush, so do not take the first hour or two as all there is to this combat system because you'll be depriving yourself of one of the most in-depth combat systems you can find in a game by the end.

All that said, this game can be extremely difficult at times. Enemies are fast and make sudden movements requiring you to have quick reflexes and the ability to take their tells and anticipate what they'll do. This being a PlatinumGames game, you're scored at the end of every combat section, letting you know how you performed overall. In this game specifically, sometimes this system feels very janky. Speaking of janky mechanics, the part of Okami that played into this frankenstein baby is the drawing. Utilizing one of the sticks if you're a civilized person, you'll draw different symbols that will let you access the different Unite Morphs. Sometimes this isn't as accurate as you'd like, and you'll end up with the wrong thing if you're not careful. The line will change color to the one of the morph you'll be getting, so you have that heads up, as well as time slowing down while you're drawing it, so in that aspect the game is very fair.

The world is under invasion from the Geathjerk, returned after a twenty year absence upon their previous attempt to invade and conquer Earth. One Hundred nameless heroes must come together in order to repel the alien invaders and save humanity from complete extinction. They'll face a myriad of different enemies across the entire planet in their crusade all the while overcoming the difficulties of working as a team and getting into boxing matches using giant robots, a shmup stage or two, and even exterminating a virus inside of an alien's body. There is also a giant sexy blue alien woman and a sexy three eyed pink alien woman. Do with this information what you will. I cannot go any further into this without spoiling things, and that would be criminal, so go forward with confidence knowing that you're going for a wild ride.

This game can easily appeal to a broad range of tastes and ages. I'd as easily play this in front of a 4 year old as I would an 80 year old. There are five difficulty levels for those looking to simply experience the story, and higher ones for people salivating for a true challenge. I can highly recommend this to people who love to go for the high score and try to get perfect rankings. You'll definitely get your money's worth whether you get it on sale or not.

I recommend not buying the DLC until you have beaten the game at least once, as well as not utilizing the free codes to unlock even more characters. The natural progression of the battle system works for the base game, and getting all this stuff beforehand would make the game far too easy on a first go-around, cheapening the experience. Now go forth; TEAM, UNITE UP!\t\t\t\t\t
