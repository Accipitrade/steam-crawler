\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can safely say this game and the franchise in general is the masterpiece it is regarded to be. I've played a part of Telltale's take on Sam & Max before and was curious about where it all started. Both installments are great, Telltale's has a more convenient interaction scheme (this game has interactions that are sort of typical but I'd say on the clunkier side for 90s point&clicks, nothing you can't get used to though), but I liked this game because I didn't have to have a dictionary on my lap to appreciate the humor, the jokes seemed much more straightforward to me. The plot of this game takes turns that are unpredictable but at the same time so very cliche, and I mean that in a good way, it's like every turn of the story seems absurd, but not that absurd that you get discouraged from continuing, like there is still some sort of logical connection (and that connection is based on cliches about the USA). Overall, I understand and agree that this is one of the must-plays of the point&click genre.
By the way, I want to add is that I had no trouble running it on my Win7 machine.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an abysmal game. The entire game is a continuous exercise to solve unwritten riddles with random cartoon gimmicks. You will be consulting online guides to even guess what you're supposed to do next. There are far too few clues to guide you and since its all based on a cartoon satire of pop culture and other cartoons, the answers make no sense at all, even by Wile E Coyote standards.

And worse, the controls are horrifyingly bad. There is no GUI escape function in any of the minigames - even the one that one "plays" during the ending credits. Especially the offramp hurdle game on the map. The only way I have found for anyone to exit this thankfully non-critical game is to get Max hit by enough signs that you run out of time before you cross the finish line (and you will be forced to play until about stage 6 because there is no other way OUT).

Getting the characters to move to what a player would think is a logical progression to the next locale is repeatedly and regularly an unpleasant exercise of pixel-hunting. In one scene, you are standing on one side of the pool and you need to get to the gazebo on the other side. Do you click on the gazebo? No. Do you click on the ground next to the gazebo? No. Do you click on the ground on the side of the screen? No. You have to click on the water slide on the right hand side of the screen, but about half-way up to get Sam and Max to get to the other side. Its mind boggling how bad the interface is.

I could go on and on about the atrocious interface. In another scene you need to take action with a time-sensitive plot device, but since Max is standing right behind it, when you attempt to interact with the device, it'll force you to interact with Max instead. Its insane.

Play Zak McKracken and the Alien Mindbenders or Toonstruck instead. They're far superior games in the same vein.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe classic point-and-click game from Lucas Arts: Sam and Max Hit the Road.

While the mechanics of a point-and-click game may seem dated by today's standard, this game is more about clicking and interacting with various people and items and watching the resulting (often humorous) dialogue unfold.

Sam and Max are lovable, memorable, characters that go on a big adventure across America, while getting into all sorts of mischief while they try to solve their case.

This game is a refined version of the genre from many years ago. While it may not be as attractive or fast paced compared to games of today, if you've never played any game of this sort, this is a good one to introduce you to a bit of gaming history.\t\t\t\t\t
