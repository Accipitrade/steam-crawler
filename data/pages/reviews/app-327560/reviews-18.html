\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a super short game but it's one that I think that many people would enjoy. It's a well put together little game that sets out to immerse the player in the blizzardy world of an abandoned polar base, and it does the job quite well. It's a survival game, but without animals and without people running around with guns killing each other. It's just you and the bitter cold of the Arctic. While my playthrough on Hard difficulty only lasted two hours, I can say that it felt a lot longer than that, in a good way.The game has got solid mechanics and it forces the player to make some challenging choices with their limited resources. Overall a neat little experience. However, I wouldn't recommend paying full price for this game, mainly due to the length. If it's on sale, then definitely pick it up. It was fun, immersive, and a nice change of pace.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAntarctica doesn’t need monsters or a horror tone to be tense and dangerous.

In Near Death, the elements are your only adversary, and they are formidable enough. The cold will kill you in a minute or two. It’s conveyed with flair and immersive atmosphere. Try to play this game without shivering at the sound of the wind howling through broken windows while you scavenge for meagre resources or huddle beside an oil heater for warmth. Try to walk a short distance between two stations in the middle of a blizzard, the gale-force winds blowing you off-course. It’s a great game to play in winter, or in summer if you want to forget about the heat.

With streamlined survival and scavenging mechanics, the game slowly raises the challenge, although it never gets terribly complex. You’ll need to set up light beacons and rope guides to get between some of the more distant buildings, and you’ll need to prioritise resources to allow greater mobility through the base. It’s simple and absorbing.

The story is basic but compelling, offering likeable characters who try to cope with challenges in a practical way. The writing builds a plausible world and situation without encumbering it with melodrama or complicated backstory. There is enough inherent drama in the basic scenario, and it was better that the game kept it simple.

It’s not very difficult (on normal) nor lengthy, but it’s probably the right length to not outstay its welcome - although it left me wanting more levels, or a sequel. I enjoyed the 3-6 hours I spent shivering through it the first time, then replaying on higher difficulties.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love Near Death because it offers something different. It's a man vs. nature conflict. Weather is the only enemy, and it's a deadly one. Your plane crashes in the late Antarctic winter near an abandoned base on the continent. You have to get yourself to McMurdo Station where there is help, but it's a long way from where you are.

You run around the abandoned base, which has many different buildings on a large campus, looking for supplies that will help you. Meanwhile you try not to get lost in a nasty blizzard that gets worse with time. You're trying to avoid freezing to death so time outside or in unheated buildings is very limited. Oh, and it's pitch black outside, except for the snow. Your map is helpful, but you need to use your compass because the map, by design, does not give your "live" location. The wind builds and your trek between buildings slows to a crawl, you realize you were off on your map reading just a bit and are now lost (this rock wall isn't supposed to be here!!), you're shivering, the screen is getting blurry -- death is imminent. Good times!

You do low level crafting, making items that help combat the elements, but you rarely feel safe. I died several times on the normal difficulty. There are two levels of difficulty above that, for those who want more than a tourist experience. Ha! Very enjoyable. The only thing I didn't like was that it ended. That said, the game doesn't overstay it's welcome. Highly recommended.\t\t\t\t\t
