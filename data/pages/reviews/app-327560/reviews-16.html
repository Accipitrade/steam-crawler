\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNear Death is an incredible survival game where you play as the pilot that needs to get out of the area thats full of snow after the plane crash. You need to go through objectives and try to get out of the place because its REALLY cold, at the point you literally freeze to death. In this game you will collect items that will help you out on the way. Such as a flashlight and a portable heater. Furthermore, there are ways to get heat other than the portable heater, such as restoring power to the building which can help you warm up without the use of the portable heater. The whole point of this game is simply, get the items needed to get out of there.

Theres also difficulty added in case if the game is too easy for you. I happened to beat Condition One and its by far the most challenging ever. While theres nothing really scary about this game, the suspense can kick in when you are nearly freezing to death (especially when playing highest difficulty). I had an amazing experience playing this, this is truly recommended to those that enjoys a good puzzle and survival.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinished Near Death on the 11.Apr.2018


Who are the game developers?
Orthogonal Games (Orthogonal Games)


What's the game about?
The Shining meets The Fog and (strangely) meets Firewatch.


General feeling after finishing?
I like the cold, but right now I like hot beverages more!


Story?
You, the character, are stranded in Antarctica after your plane crashes near a research station in the midst of a snow storm, and know almost no basic survivals skills (you'll learn along the way). You communicate, with John(?), by using a teletype (whatever that is!). You search for stuff to get to your favorite bar. In the end...


Gameplay?
A simple first person survival, with minimal HUD elements (no life, no flashlight battery status, no kerosene status - you do get a map and a compass along the way, though)... bare bones, for you to enjoy loneliness and the worst weather ever... it's tense, but has a great pace. And you actually get to survive "properly": you have to use a flashlight to see where you're going, you have to use/think of stuff to keep you warm, you have to melt ice to open doors, not run in the storms, plant light rods and use ropes to not get easily lost, among other things...


Graphics?
They're adapted to the game (not AAA, but not expected!) and there's different settings you can use to slightly control them, like use 8-bit, TV mode, pixelated, etc.


Sound & Music?
Both excellent. The music is wonderfully set against the elements and the sound design is enough to make you really feel Antarctica.


Replayability?
You might want to, if you're into completion, or more difficult settings (I played normal), but the game is pretty linear.


Time played?
Around 4 hours.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've grown weary of generic, open-world, crafting-heavy, often procedurally-generated, survival games. Even looking at the Store page was enough to see that Near Death isn't one of these half-broken, abandoned survival clones popular in the last few years. It is complete, focused and exciting.

As someone obsessed with polar expeditions (especially Antarctica), I had high hopes for Near Death and I have not been disappointed. It's not particularly long and, having completed it on normal difficulty I do have a slight yearning for more variation in my future playthroughs at higher difficulties (different item distribution, procedurally-generated layout, etc.), but on the other hand, by being short and hand-crafted, I feel I've experienced a lovingly designed and refined space, survived a believable situation.

The story timeline is short, meaning that crafting is thankfully brief and the inventory isn't intrusive (think Alien Isolation). This is a game about making it from one building to the next without having to apply a crude 'sped-up' hunger meter or rely on zombies as a threat. It evokes a strong sense of place and the various weather conditions add to that as you get start to remember routes and feel relief at finding (or creating) safe, sealed rooms. Being disorientated by low visibility, winds, etc., despite knowing that you only need to walk a short distance is exciting, never becoming frustrating or tiresome. The weather in Near Death is fantastic.

I loved it.\t\t\t\t\t
