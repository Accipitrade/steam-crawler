\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLoved it. I only played it once at "Condition 1" difficulty level and I had a blast. It really gets through the sensation of being alone in an hostile environment. It took a couple of daths to understand how the heather and sealed rooms work. The locations are believable and the whole atmosphere is very compelling. Only minor issue is that sometimes when you load a savegame it mismatches your Goal and you have to walk back to the Teletype in order to activate it, only for you to walk right back where you already were. Also, since the game is kind of story-driven, if you visit a location which is not on your Goal at that moment you might be walking right beside items that you cannot pick up, but that will have to be gathered later on. I guess it kinda makes sense as you don't know you need them until your friend Jack tells you that you do, but in any case it might be mildly frustrating. Other than that, the game is short, straightforward but challenging and really compelling. Exactly what I was looking for.\t\t\t\t\t

Real cool game.

The atmospheric elements are the strong point here. The felling of time running out is always there and you never get used to it. The lack of a HUD was a good decision although I cursed the Devs a few times for it.

It does have it's issues/bugs. The jumping is awful, which would be fine if they didn't make you jump from time to time (that wind is a bish). Ran out of batteries/fuel a couple times, had to reload old saved a few times. Setting up those posts/markers was damn near impossible with the wind blowing you all over and turning you around, the quick deaths from freezing, and the overall 'I don't know where the ♥♥♥♥ I am or how to get to my destination" feeling. The game forces you to 'feel your way out' in order to get to your destination so this doesn't match well with markers. If the markers were already laid down everywhere, that would have been nice.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI LOVE this game!

Being isolated on a remote research base in -100 degree Celsius weather without food or water is an intimidating prospect, and the game wastes no time throwing you into the deep end. You begin to freeze to death outside almost immediately, and finding shelter does nothing to help until you use a portable heater to temporarily warm up enclosed rooms - which runs on kerosene, a limited resource.

This seems stressful right off the bat, and it wouldn't ordinarily be my time of thing. The need to scavenge supplies and craft tools did little to help me warm to the concept. The strange thing is, on normal difficulty, I didn't die in those opening moments, though I really did frequently feel "near death". And as it turns out, the game never did kill me throughout its short playtime. This is because the mechanics are all brilliantly cohesive, progressively adding tools to your inventory as it adds challenge through deteriorating weather and more difficult locations to explore, while soliciting your intelligence to solve problems without ever giving you too much guidance.

Those opening moments, armed with only that trusty portable heater and a flashlight paint the picture of a punishing game, but you quickly restore partial power and can repair fuse boxes to warm up sections of the facility instead. You can board up windows to make certain rooms safer and can later access gated areas through the use of a blowtorch to melt frozen doors. And when the wind starts to impede your walking speed and visibility, you can compensate by using light poles and rope poles to guide you through.

One of my favorite aspects of Near Death is how it handles navigation. You can pull up a map of the facility at any time (though unfortunately the UI scales poorly, so I wouldn't recommend resolutions above 1080p), and a compass rose on it shows you the direction you're facing. And that's all you get! There's no moving dot on the map or any other guidance, so players have to infer their positions based on surrounding landmarks. The designers were smart to orient every single building on the facility to due north, meaning that facing any door or looking down any corridor aligns you with a cardinal direction, while the empty space in between each building roughly corresponds to one of 8 basic orientations (E, NW, etc.). Figuring out where you are and how to get to the next area is a lot of fun, inching towards faint light sources and shapes in the night barely visible through the blowing snow. The initial moments of stress you feel as you reach a new location and must quickly find an entry point and a safe place to heat is weirdly satisfying.

The game delivers its objectives through a sort of teletyping machine that lets you communicate with another person and come up with solutions to your problem. This lets you know which building you must explore next while grounding the situation with some cheeky character work to alleviate tension. It may not have a narrative per se, but the writing is good, as you would expect from the creator of The Novelist (also a very good game, albeit in a very different way!)

Presentation-wise, this looks like a Source engine game (not sure about that though), and while the atmospherics are all top-notch, the modelling is pretty basic and the texture work is very low-detail. Despite this, it has a more coherent sense of place than most games I play, and players will be required to really internalize the base's layout to survive. The music is perfectly suited, transitioning from dark and menacing while fighting the elements outdoors to soothing and beautiful as you find shelter and warmth.

Near Death will take an average player around 2 hours to finish, maybe 3 if they're backtracking to previous installations to hunt for resources. There are two harder difficulty levels than Normal, and admittedly that might be a better way to go for people who are adept at navigational challenges and problem-solving / puzzle games.

I already want to go back for a second playthrough on the hardest difficulty. It's such a well-designed experience that I think anybody who appreciates exploration and puzzle-based games will love it, so long as they can keep their calm in the face of adversity. This is an absolute hidden gem. Superb!\t\t\t\t\t

Stranded in Antarctica you have to fight the wild forces of nature in order to survive, and at night it's not a nice place to be stuck in. Playing Near Death you can almost physically feel the cold blizzard seeping into your bones, and the decommissioned research station you find offers little shelter. OVERALL: 7.5/10
GRAPHICS:10/10
AUDIO:10/10
GAMEPLAY: 7/10
DIFFICULTY: (NOT VERY)
STORY: 6/10
REPLAY VALUE: 4/10
DURATION: SHORT
BUGS/ CRASHES:0 (I have a low end laptop).
COST: Bought on sale for £1.50 which was a BARGAIN! Max I would pay for this is £6.00 (Only because its quite a short game).

I had not heard of this game and happened to see it in the sale and thought...what the heck, why not?? This game is short with limited gameplay- but it kept me on my toes. You are a woman stranded at the station in the Antartica and you basically need to fix things and prep for a journey. You need to walk from station to station in darkness, blizzards and freezing temperatures without dying. Its not difficult and thats all you need to really do- so the gameplay is limited. But the atmosphere really carries this game and I was hooked on what was going to happen start to finish. There are no monsters, deadly wildlife, or starvation meters- just the temperature. Its a brilliant game to play if you want to get into a little story for a few hours- this took me about 5 hrs and for the ridiculously cheap price it is worth it. I probably wont play it again for a while as there is just one storyline. This game can be played on low end computers without any problems- I had 0 bugs and it was incredibly quick to load. Nice game and even nicer price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA non-violent survival game where your only enemy is the extreme weather!

PROS:
+ Non-violent gameplay
+ Minimalistic, (mostly) immersive HUD
+ One semi-open map with no loading time
+ Scarce but solid voice-acting and sound design
+ No forced combat nor hide and seek with enemies
+ Tense atmosphere despite grounded, realistic setting
+ Harsh but fair difficulty that only punishes the careless
+ Simple, intuitive crafting, scavenging and heat management mechanics

CONS:
- Anticlimactic ending
- Slightly dated graphics
- Pointless dialogue choices
- Over too soon, ironically enough!
- Not as open-ended as it seems to be
- One inexplicably unrevisitable location
- Slightly awkward inventory management
- Mandatory immersion-breaking training tips
- A couple of (thankfully rare) gamebreaking bugs\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNear Death is another one of those games that's been in my library for years and I just never gave it a chance past the first five minutes. After finally reaching the ending, I'll say that I was pleasantly surprised for the price. That said, I do have a few bones to pick but part of that comes from playing exclusively on the hardest difficulty.

In Near Death, you are a pilot who crash landed near an abandoned Antarctic research base. It's the dead of the continent's winter, 24 hour darkness, and a blizzard is coming in. Most of the supplies were taken, but there's just enough to McGuyver your way out of this situation.

You start by getting a portable heater that drinks fuel like a Hummer. Although you'll be running a marathon around the base, you only need to keep track of your warmth. This is a survival game where you only have one bar to take care of and I think Near Death is weaker for that because it essentially becomes a point and click adventure game without and puzzles. You can't spend much time outside and when you come inside you need to ensure all the doors are closed and all the windows are secure before you can warm up.

Most of the gameplay is a flimsily disguised series of fetch-quests extended by the need to scrounge for supplies and wait to heat up. Eventually, you can make circuit breakers that allow you to power nearby buildings. A powered building has unlimited heat, meaning you can just hang out for as long as you need.

Generators are where the concept of Near Death falls apart. The first half is full of tense, isolated moments where you're praying there's another kerosene canister at the next objective. Then, once you power up buildings, it becomes a matter of trial and error. You scout, put out some movement-assisting rope lines, go back to warm up, then go back to grab the objective. This is the CORE gameplay loop of Near Death and if you don't like backtracking and scouting I don't think you'll like this game.

Access to generators is limited by crafting materials. You need to scrounge for and collect rare items before you can get that unlimited heat. On the hardest difficulty, resources are so scare that it really ups the tension as you desperately scrounge for the last bit of wiring you need before your kerosene runs out. But once you have it, it's just a matter of fighting the weather.

About half your actions in Near Death consist of carrying an enormous object through a blizzard. This is fun at first, but quickly gets boring. The more objectives you complete, the more serious the blizzard becomes. That means you visibility is cut down and it becomes much more difficult to move. Ocassionally, it is downright impossible to walk into the wind. Again, this can be solved by scouting and leaving a rope trail (making movement easier) and then taking the object.

And that's really all there is to Near Death. You go somewhere, repair it, heat up, scrounge, find item, scout a route back, then carry the item. Do that 5 times and the game is over. There's no enemies, no big twists, and it becomes a cakewalk once you turn the power on. Also, I would say that the sound design is excellent in the howling wind, knowing when a building is unsecure, the rare bits of music, and especially the main character talking to herself.

All that said, I enjoyed my time with Near Death. I think it's a good buy for the value. Where it stumbles in gameplay, it more than makes up for in its feelings of isolation. The soundtrack and setting are so well defined that I found myself feeling paranoid once I took my headset off. That's something few HORROR games achieve, much less a survival game.

So, while it's repetitive and it has its stumbling blocks, I still heartily recommend Near Death.\t\t\t\t\t
