\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed Near Death. The game is really just you vs. the weather, which makes for a surprisingly intense antagonist. I found the constant need to remain warm nerve-racking and atmospheric: you have to be very careful about your movements, especially in the beginning, lest you fall victim to the Antarctic winter. The bitter cold keeps you on your toes!

Gameplay consists largely of collecting resources and learning your environment to survive. I found Near Death most fun when I focused on immersing myself in the situation of the player character. It's easy to get lost in the rough terrain, so craftable light poles and rope lines allow you to map out routes between buildings to stay alive; and it's easy to freeze, even indoors, so you have to find reliable heat sources. I liked the visual design and thought it made for exciting scenes of exploration.

The UI is very minimal and there aren't any survival stats in your face. The game lets you know when you're getting too cold or when you're running out of fuel for something, but I appreciated not being bombarded with numbers. It added to the realism and urgency of the situation.

This is not a puzzle game, so failing to reach an objective is usually the result of being slow or indecisive. This forces you to actually learn your surroundings and make use of your map! The mission objectives are straightforward, and your (limited) external communication provides a bit of humor--amusing, if corny--but unfortunately I found some tasks more irritating than inherently challenging. I didn't find getting lost in the snow frustrating because it added to the atmosphere and because it's recoverable: there are signs and landmarks that can help you. However, I occasionally ended up running in circles and dying from exposure in places I shouldn't have, not because I was playing poorly, but because a quest item wasn't easy to spot or because the path forward was unclear.

I was a little disappointed that the story was so linear (I had expected a few potential escape routes), but it was still satisfying to get it all figured out. My one other nitpick is that you have to open the menu to not only check your map but check your compass, so you can't tell which direction you're facing while actually moving.

Performance was OK, although I had a few recurring framerate dips due to the weather and loading certain areas. I experienced two game-breaking bugs: 1) that you are sometimes frozen in place for no apparent reason, and 2) that closing a door while equipping a particular tool can sometimes lock it in your hand and delete other tools from your inventory. Both of these can be quickly fixed with a reload (autosaves are plentiful, so I never lost significant progress), but this behavior is still unfortunate and made the game a little annoying to play.

With that said, I had more fun than not, and the game isn't long enough for any of these issues to make the experience particularly tedious. I recommend Near Death if you're interested in a short, simple, atmospheric survival game!\t\t\t\t\t
