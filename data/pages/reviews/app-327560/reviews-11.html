\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed this game for what it is, a snowy wilderness traversal/survival game. After playing Firewatch, despite being immersed and intrigued by the story, I felt the actual park traversing to be less exciting than I thought it could be. Near Death on the other hand is light on story with the focus instead being on traversing the dangerous snowy frozen wasteland. Leave light posts for guidance in time of poor visibility, create rope lines to aid movement during blizzards, and repair facilities to stave off an icy death. I don't know if food mechanics would have made for a better experience, but if they ever make a sequel that could be something to consider. Overall I really liked it and would recommend it to anyone wanting a short survival experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile a little short on playtime (~3-5hrs depending on difficulty), there's still enough here to make a great game.

You need to travel around an abandoned research base to collect supplies for your escape. You can build limited items to help you access different buildings and compartments, upgrade your items, and to create rope\\light trails that help you find your path. You have a portable heater, but it only works inside enclosed spaces. Any space open to the elements won't keep you warm, so you will need to close doors and seal broken windows. This will also allow the room to "thaw", uncovering more supplies that were otherwise caked in snow/ice.

I never had any technical issues or bugs, and the game is actually pretty well polished for an indi release.

Overall It's an original concept that plays out really good imo.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery solid survival game, with base-building, exploration, and puzzle elements. Played on ubuntu linux, with an xbox-type gamepad controller or a keyboard & mouse. Graphics ran mostly fine, with some significant lag spikes in areas, with my radeon mesa drivers. Excellent story-driven adventure.

Gameplay is especially good because the level design is so solid, which includes crafting items to improve buildings. The game mechanics are really good, and there are a number of difficulty levels for replayability. The puzzles are usually great, but the "shake the screen and no visibility" snowstorm effect gave me eyestrain (although it can be disabled). Further, the rope-pole minigame was buggy, and it did not always work correctly. Otherwise, an overall positive experience from an indie developer.

One highlight is the use of a voice actor gasping, instead of using a meter, as an indication of the player's hypothermia levels. And the background music is good and fitting, too. I also really liked the story, which is told through a two-way typewriter, as the player frantically cries for help. I really felt the isolation and danger, with these added effects. By the end of the game, I was totally invested in the story, the character, and the location.

I would recommend this game for someone looking to get their toe into survival horror, who is looking for a game focused on mechanics and puzzles instead of monsters and jump-scares, but something easier than other complex titles like "The Long Dark" or "Amnesia". This game takes up 1,000 MBs of diskspace, and the game uses the Unity game engine.\t\t\t\t\t
