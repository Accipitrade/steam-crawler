\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn December 5, 1914 the Endurance set out from South Georgia island carrying Ernest Shackleton and his crew on an expedition to cross the Antarctic continent via the South Pole. Before reaching land, the Endurance became trapped in the ice and sank, leaving her crew stranded. After an incredible ordeal that lasted more than a year and spanned hundreds of miles, over ice and mountains, deserted islands and some of the deadliest seas on Earth, Shackleton and a small party were able to reach help. The entire crew of the Endurance returned home alive.

More recently, I set out to play this game, and froze to death within the first 5 minutes.

As I lay dying from exposure, my last thoughts were of Frostbite: Deadly Climate. Similar sort of game; but whereas there on top of the cold you had to contend with ice monsters, Resident Evil-style, here it's just you vs. the elements.

You find yourself in this predicament after your plane crash lands outside a vacant Antarctic research base. If your survival instincts are better than mine and you manage to make it inside, things proceed pretty sensibly from there. Step by logical step, you discover where you are, learn the layout of the base, take stock of your surroundings and the resources at hand, make contact with the outside world and then start using your ingenuity to get yourself out of this mess.

Sounds kind of familiar, huh?

Yeah, what this actually reminded me of more than anything was The Martian. Obviously the stranded alone in a hostile environment part; but also the overall structure and tone.

Starting with that first mad dash to shelter, a pattern emerges where you alternate between islands of safety that let you pause and catch your breath, and desperate scrambles toward your next goal while a howling blizzard does its best to drain the life out of you.

In Martian-like fashion, most of your objectives come in the form of advice from your home base delivered via a teletype machine. They update you with the latest problem, you have a little Mark Watney-style banter where you try to put a brave face on your grim situation, and then you get back to work while you wait for the next shoe to drop.

Don't worry, though: they're surprisingly fair about the sort of challenges they throw at you. I kept expecting them to cut the power all of a sudden or have the storm destroy one of my safe rooms without warning, but to their credit they never stoop to cheap tricks like that. Well, I guess they do spring a trap on you at one point mid-trek, and there's a part near the end where you're forced to make a highly improbable climb up to a tower which nobody in their right mind would ever attempt under these conditions; but in general they stick to their own rules and avoid contrived drama.

This even-keeled approach helps build a real sense of investment that you don't get when everything feels arbitrary and out of your control. It really keeps your head in the game and allows you to make plans without having to worry too much that they'll throw you some ridiculous curveball that will render them all meaningless.

Which room would be the best for my next waypoint? Do I repair the window here or in the next building? Should I use the propane heater now or try to tough it out a little longer until I can start the generator?

There's one part, to give you an example, where you have to haul a big clunky piece of machinery across the base, and meanwhile the storm has picked up enough that you can barely move or see outside. You need to figure out how to set up a route using guide ropes to get from one end to the other. Problem is, you may not have enough rope to make it all the way. So do you set up segments and try to make it across multiple smaller gaps? Risk leaving one big gap and use light beacons to direct you? Set up a route to an intermediate waypoint, then retrieve the rope and construct the second half? Plenty of options, and it's your call how you want to approach it.

Except for trying to be a hero and going without the rope. Trust me, don't do that.

You're constantly facing decisions like this, evaluating your resources, thinking about the layout of the base and where your next goal is located; but thanks to the steady pacing it never becomes overwhelming. There's not a ton of detail compared to something like The Long Dark in terms of the arts-and-crafts stuff, which was fine with me. Your tools are relatively limited but everything has a clear purpose. It makes for a simple, focused little game that's more about putting you in a survival mentality than breathless action or complex inventory management.

In the end, much like The Martian, you're left with the impression of survival as a succession of small victories, ultimately leading you home.

But first, you do have to make it inside without freezing. Yeah, they probably won't name a school after me once they hear about that one.

Oh well, I guess if survival were easy everyone would do it.
