\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAbout a 2-3 hour experience. Gameplay consists of "carry object from location A to location B", with some very simple crafting mechanics. The primary challenge is orientation; the wind and lighting will make navigating a challenge, necessitating the use of items such as guide poles and light beacons. In the normal difficulty, I had an abundance of items (fuel for the heater, supplies to craft window covers, etc), and never felt like I had to ration resources. The puzzles, which consist of moving objects around the map were not terribly stimulating, and were the primary source of bugs. I occasionally had items clip through the floor and out of sight, and repeatedly ran into the "CANNOT PLACE HERE" message (which required me to spam the action around the room until I could place the item). There are some minor movement mechanics, which mostly consist of crouching if the wind is too high (or following rope lines if you placed them).

The gameplay was frankly too basic to be interesting for long, and the item placement mechanics felt very half baked. At $9 CDN, I cannot recommend this game. The length of the experience, combined with the lack of polish makes the price outrageous. For a cold survival experience, save your dollars and get The Long Dark. If you are able to pick this up for under $5, it might be worth the experience (assuming you need an arctic survival fix).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRecommended for fans of the genre. It's a short survival game / walking simulator with rather straightforward puzzles (mainly fetch items) and a uncomplicated, but effective story line.

Don't expect a high degree of "realism". Crafting is simply picking up two or three items (rope, duct tape etc.) and combine in 2 seconds. You freeze to death in 30 seconds, but when you find a warm place, you regenerate in literally 2 or 3 seconds. So it has a "game-like" feel to it, although the immersion is definitely there (for example the music is quite atmospheric).

There's nothing occult and no monsters or wild animals, so in that sense it's "realistic". So if you enjoy a game that's simply about staying warm and getting the heck out of there ASAP, then this might be a game for you.

I completed normal difficulty in 3.5 hours. I was neither in a hurry nor extremely slow.\t\t\t\t\t

Story:

You play as a pilot that crashes near an abandoned research station in the arctic. It's a cold night and you will freeze to death unless you contact someone that help you guide your way to safety. That's about all the story you really get with Near Death, however the story is not really needed as it does everything it needs to do as it goes on.

You can find stations that advance the story, however these are nothing more than just task updates given to you so you know which direction to head in and they work fine for the most part. So what's there is good, however do not expect anything super in depth.

Graphics:

Near Death looks nice for the most part, however the game is constantly drenched in the winter darkness, so you'll only be seeing your environments through blurry eyes, the flickering of broken lights and the illumination of your flashlight. The environments do look pretty decent for the most part, however pretty soon you will notice how a lot of the rooms look pretty much identical to one another and that becomes a problem pretty soon, however more on that in the gameplay section.

Audio:

The audio design is top notch and crisp. I absolutely adore everything done with it, especially the wind and snow howling constantly when outside and often reminding you even when you end up inside in a cabin and are safely warming up next to a heat lamp.

It helps build a really unsafe atmosphere, it honestly felt really oppressive whenever you wound up going outside, not to mention dangerous. It made me feel like I was constantly under pressure to return to a building so I would not freeze to death and whenever inside it just barely made me feel safer. Voice acting while there's not much of it is also pretty decent.

Gameplay:

For the most part Near Death is an exploration game where you run around the base collecting stuff to fix up a snow truck so you can leave safely. You're given an idea of where to look and then you go there to find those items, however how you get there and how you get the item is for you to solve.

This starts off being simple enough, but ends up quickly becoming pretty complex . You're constantly having to leave the safety of the buildings of the base to go outside to reach another section and this brings in another mechanic which is freezing. When you end up being exposed to the cold for too long you start getting blurry vision, your stamina lowers and you end up on the brink of death.

You can heat yourself up with a portable heat lamp or other sources to regain your so called "health". Which works fine for the most part, but a lot of the times a heat source will be slightly problematic for you to get. Also as I said before most of the base buildings look too similar to one another and you wind up going back and forth trying to find the building you and just simply getting lost.

There's ways to track where you've been like flags you can plant in the ground however these barely help as once your vision gets blurry, the darkness plus everything else makes it impossible to see as you wind up going in circles. I wish there were more ways to help you find your footing, but overall you can definitely manage.

Verdict:

Near Death is a very entertaining game, although a short one at that, barely clocking in at three hours long. It's a very cozy game for a winter night, when you're bundled warmly with a cup of tea, as your character is freezing to death. It's definitely not going to be for everyone, but for those who can handle a game that barely holds your hand, this will definitely be for you.

Final Rating:
7.5/10

Pros:
+ Decent visuals
+ Amazing audio design
+ Fun exploring the base

Cons:
- You can easily get lost when exploring
- Many buildings look pretty much identical

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFantastic, bite-sized survival game where you dart around outposts at an arctic base and find shelter away from the aggressive, freezing cold. There’s a narrative driving it too, which immediately differentiates it from your stock survival game where resources are as bountiful as your play time allows. In Near Death, everything is limited, scattered around the different facilities at the base. You need to get around by battling nature head on: carving a path throughout the base; cover exposed windows with canvases, and utilize poles and markers to guide you. You need to keep warm too: insulating clothes, shutting doors, and making every available inch as habitable as possible before you move on -- knowing you’ll probably be back later.

The whole base is relatively small and quaint. Just big enough to encourage smart play, but not so big as to confuse your priorities.

I love how diegetic the design of Near Death is as well. You won’t find any hard survivability metrics here. No health gauges or temperature bars. You just have to listen to the voice actor, as she struggles through the elements; signaling when she’s cold, when she’s dying, and when her body temperature is normalizing. It’s such an amazing feeling to get to share a sigh of relief with her as you manage to shut the roaring blizzard outside for a moment -- hearing the wind die down in the background as you deploy your portable heater. Even if it’s just a lull. Even when you know you’ll be heading outside again in a few moments.

Highly recommended if you like freezing, oppressive environments.\t\t\t\t\t
