\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros and Cons, here we go!

Pros

1. Quite intense
2. Started on normal and you really do feel like you have to fight to survive.
3. The music at times knew how to take everything from intense to unsettling. Always quiet but disturbing
4. The price tag is really great.
5. Games like this continue to erode my confidence in huge titles and companies and now favor more Indie games(this being indie of course)
6. The story line while simple made sense.

Cons

1. The game felt like it set you up perfectly for a second stage but instead ended. I don't mean it felt like a forced ending because it didn't. I'm just saying it easily could have gone further. DLC could very easily be added to this game and not feel forced in the least bit. DLC e.g. you have to make your way home of course but have your radar direct you to different outposts to gain much needed supplies. A different station, different requirements and so on.

2. Crappy batteries made sense and was spot on. Batteries sitting to long lose charge and therefore it easily stands to reason that you would chew through them quite quickly (as is the case) Kerosene did not make sense. It burns slowly so it made no sense that you burn through your fuel as fast as a drag racer goes through fuel. At the same time it's a semi con to the game because its understood that your fighting to gain body heat. If your fuel went on forever, half the strain of staying alive would be eliminated. That being the case I hold that con with a very light hand.

And that's it on the cons. All I can say is I really wish they would add DLC and new areas so you have to fight your way back home.

Also, for there not being a single monster in the game the intensity, darkness and soundtrack really freaked me out at times and I wondered if something was going to step out of the shadows. Even when I knew that there were no other creatures in the game the feeling was always there. If anything had stepped out I would have ♥♥♥♥ myself. I know horror games that try to instill this level of fear and don't even come close to what they achieved here, maybe by accident.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA rare find in the survival genre, Near Death is a tighly packaged experience that proves that you don't need monsters to make being trapped in the Antarctic horrifying. Playing a pilot whose plane went down over a decommissioned research station during polar night, the infeasability of rescue means you need to scour every inch of the station to put together what you need to make your own way to safety, all while the dark and cold do their level best to make you as miserable as possible.

This game is fairly short - I beat it in around four hours - but any longer would've just dragged on as the game sends you into a desperate survival situation at a fairly breakneck pace. Near Death presents a lonely, isolated setting where the air itself is such an oppressive, omnipresent threat that spending more than a minute outside of a sealed room will kill you, and such havens can feel few and far between as every other window is blown in and even a number of the walls have collapsed from the incessant siege of wind and snow.

Outside of the mere challenge of having to exist in such extreme conditions, the game is fairly straightforward and isn't much more than a series of fetch quests, but when the basic logistics of getting from point A to point B without freezing - or worse, getting lost when visiblity is next to zero - are so mounting such basic tasks are anything but trivial.

All in all I find very little to criticize about this game, and while the premise may not be for everyone, if what I've described here sounds like your cup of tea I suggest giving it a try.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this on sale, and I can say it was worth it.

Near Death has no zombies, vampires, plagues, robots, ninjas, trolls, elves, etc etc. There are no enemies at all, and you have no weapons ether. The whole story feels very realistic--I imagine that wandering around while stranded in Antarctica in 1982 would probably be very much as depicted in Near Death. I kept wondering if there might be some jump scares, if I would find some creature lurking in the cold darkness of some room in the base. Or, perhaps I would uncover pieces of some twisted military plot, some terrible research going on at the Sutro base. But in fact, there's basically no one to interact with in the game, except some people you communicate with via a teletype (like a typewriter and a telephone together), hundreds of miles away, as they provide you guidance and warm wishes to help keep you, and the story, moving forward. Apart from that, and the bit of humor that comes through at times in your communications, there is really only one main thing that drives the game forward--and that's the gameplay itself.

In a much larger and much broader game, perhaps a Mass Effect or Elder Scrolls or even Half Life, the gameplay of Near Death might make up a chapter or two. But in this game, the creators have chosen to focus in on a few very specific gameplay elements.

The challenge is to survive the worst nature can throw at you: the wind, the snow, the darkness, and the freezing temperatures of Antarctica. You do this with a few pieces of equipment, such as a flashlight, and later light rods you can plant in the snow; a heater you carry around to warm you up inside closed rooms; a blowtorch, to melt snow and ice and gain access to new rooms and supplies; and even your character's clothing is important, as you realize you can find ways to upgrade your clothing (and other items) to stay warmer for longer. The game actually reminds me some of Neo Scavenger, another game in which it's important to manage the cold, stay warm with good clothes and scavenge stuff to stay alive--though Neo Scavenger also features food and sleep, and combat with sci-fi enemies.

If you manage to survive the earliest and most disorienting stages of the game, you will begin to get fetch quests, essentially, as you are sent off to the different parts of the base to retrieve pieces of equipment to aid your in your survival and escape. Along the way you must properly manage your equipment and supplies, and avoid getting turned around too much when you're in the heavy wind and snow outside, and be certain you can get to heat sources in time.

Do that, and you should have a good chance of surviving and ultimately escaping Sutro.

If that sounds fun to you--a game where you're pitted against Mother Nature herself, rather than any kinds of enemies--you may find Near Death to be a wonderful little game, as I did.



Now, with all this said, there were some issues I had with Near Death.

Firstly, my laptop moved at a crawl much of the time while my character was outdoors. Having some options to turn down the detail of the snow falling, and other such things, would have been nice.

I fell through a catwalk next to the power station--this was a clipping issue, not a piece of catwalk that broke (though there were a few catwalks that broke in scripted events).

And a few more gameplay-oriented annoyances:

It would have been nice to jump a little higher, and be able to climb up short objects. There were several times where I would have just climbed up the side of a building, rather than being forced to find some way to go around. And the duck-jump feature, by which I was able to gain access to certain areas, felt very clumsy compared to in Valve's FPS's, often requiring me to make several attempts.

The main character gets cold and dies too quickly in my view. Even with all my clothes fully upgraded, I think it would only take one or two minutes to die. Perhaps this was a deliberate decision to keep up a sense of urgency, but I think it could have been handled better. Also consider that, in addition to dying too quickly from cold, I think the character also warms up too quickly once in the presence of heat. You can go from being right on the point of death--wobbling around with your vision and hearing badly blurred from being too cold, literally ready to fall down dead--to being 100% OK and ready to go out into the cold once more, all in less than a minute, so long as you enter the presence of heat. No frostbite, no numb limbs, almost no recovery time. It's fun and scary to deal with, but it's also a kind of simplistic system that I think could do to be revamped.

Every single door in a room must be closed, and all of the broken windows must be sealed--no matter the size of the room or how closed off certain parts of it may seem--before your personal heater is able to warm you up. I was bothered that I couldn't just move into a secluded corner, turn on the heater and get warm.

On a similar note, I kept on waiting for one of the generators to fail or run out of fuel, taking my best heat sources and forcing me to go on a hunt for more gasoline. I was kind of disappointed that never happened.

I would have preferred if the locations, shapes, and sizes of the buildings, and their rooms, and the supplies available within them, were randomized. Near Death kind of suffers from convenientitis. Of course the very first building you find has the personal heater. And of course that is the sole building that possesses the only personal heater, of the ~15 buildings in the game. I think it's pretty clear that the game is meant to be played in a linear fashion, moving from one particular building to another in a particular order. Early on I didn't realize that I was supposed to activate a generator, and instead ended up moving to another building. Only after getting there and spending some time there did I realize I didn't have enough propane to stay warm with the personal heater. I kept searching for propane but didn't find enough to last long. Realizing I was probably going to freeze to death, I checked my inventory and objectives and such, and only then discovered that I had skipped an objective earlier, so I had to restart from the beginning. Personally I think that randomizing building locations and supplies would really have added a whole new level of fun and difficulty to the game.



Overall, I enjoyed Near Death a lot. It doesn't take too long to finish, even on the highest difficulty, which I've now completed as well.

***Minor spoiler alert***
Other than that, the only I have to say is that, on Normal difficulty anyway, the game ends with what is basically a sandbox mode. After completing what was basically the last objective, the temperature suddenly went up from about -100 C to -5 C, and the wind and snow and darkness cleared. It was the perfect time for this to happen, as I was up on top of the radar tower at a high elevation, and thus able to look down on all the other buildings below, able to see their lights and the green lights I'd placed around on the snow. It was quite a beautiful moment, perhaps equally as memorable as all the chaos and peril of the game up to then. Under those conditions, with virtually no fear of death, I happily went back to every building I could, exploring all around inside and out.
***End spoiler***

7.5/10 Near Death is nothing amazing, but it's certainly a lot of fun to play around with for a little while.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNear Death is a very linear survival game. You have set goals with set locations. The only thing between you and survival is the harsh weather of Antartica. Through my own observations, it appears that the weather and temperature are dependant on which "checkpoint" you are at. When you first start, the temperature is well below 0 degrees Celcius. As you progress further in the game, the winds pick up and temperatures reach nearly -100 degress celcius. Im not saying that this is a bad thing, however, when the game is as short as it is (about 2 hours + if you understand what youre doing), RNG with the weather might be a nice touch.

The game itself isn't very dependant on the map and compass (default button is M). You'll notice the compass in the top left hand corner, and the blue dial is the direction you are facing. While early game this is useful, towards the end, you should have rope poles and light poles set up so that you can run from building to building without much problem (rope poles allow you to sprint, even in the heavy winds).

Now, since this game is linear, there isnt much "re-playability". Yes, there are 4 different difficulties, but you are essentially doing the same thing over again, just on a different difficulty. (And a lot worse weather)

It is a wonderful game for what it is, and if you are looking for a nice survival game with a wonderful atmosphere, then Near Death is a good bet for you.\t\t\t\t\t

I did a speed run of this game in about 2 or 3 hours time, but there can be a lot to do if you just want to explore. My favorite part about this game was probably the conversations back and forth between your character and McMurdo that were full of humor! It was much better than Campo Santos Firewatch in my opinion, I had high hopes for that game, but this one takes the cake.\t\t\t\t\t
