\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr This is a phenomenal game with a lazy, low-effort port. If you look past the lack of options, a pause button, and other conveniences often given to other FMV ported games, you'll find that the actual game itself is a very memorable experience.

While it shows on here that I don't have much playtime, I actually played the original cabinet at Galloping Ghost Arcade (GGA) many times. I genuinely think it's a one of a kind experience, and I'm very grateful for all the work people put into resurrecting this game and giving it new life.

Most of my bitterness regarding this port comes from just an overall lack of effort. After playing the cabinet at GGA, it's abundantly clear that the devs put as little effort as possible to simply get this on the store. There are no options to change volume levels, resolution, or include subtitles. Furthermore, there is NO pause button whatsoever in the game. If something comes up and you gotta pause, your run is as good as dead.

GGA had a great opportunity here to take the original material and really spruce it up with some quality of life features, but rather, the port just seems like a cash grab used to exploit the previous arcade exclusive nature of the game. Given the nature of choose your own adventure-esque games like this, it SORELY needed a restart feature or some form of save states. You have to go through the drawn out ending sequence every time, making it take upwards of a minute simply to just restart the game.

All these complaints aside, credit REALLY needs to be given to Brian Colin for creating this one of a kind experience. The whole thing reeks of B-movie vibes, and it's certainly a good thing. The writing and loads of possibilities will keep you coming back for more, and each encounter you make in the mansion REALLY stands out from each other.

If you get past the lazy port efforts made by GGA, I'm sure you'll find this to be quite the gem of a game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNow THIS is one hell of a backstory with this game. I won't bother regurgitating what the steam description says, but regardless I LOVE the idea of taking a once lost game and bringing it back and to the public one way or another. This went from a Galloping Ghost staple to still being a staple, but one that is brought to the house, just like my ass is in the 90s/early 2000s. One thing I will reiterate is that this game is made by the creator of Rampage, Brian Colin, (Who I've been fortunate enough to meet and discuss game design with, he was impressed, but I digress) and that DEFINATELY shows. The tongue in cheek, blunt, and subtle yet visual/cheesy humor shows. And much like how Rampage was/is a parody of Kaiju/monster movies, this game feels like a parody/homage to 80s/indie esque/B-Horror films. All the tropes are there, and they are explained and exploited in a very humorous, in your face, and makes you smile kind of way.

Gameplay wise this feels like a 80s horror take on the Dragon's Lair formula, but it's just a PINCH less hectic and TAD more forgiving, but DON'T let that fool you, every choice matters and affects you, your outcome, and ultimately whether or not you'll solve the case you're given as Detective Ed Specter.

While I wouldn't call this game an award winner. It's charm and personality and all the funny visuals/gags and encounters are what not only make it fun,. it's also what makes it stick out as one hell of a unique ride. While the concept of a choose your own adventure has been done before, one things for sure, they don't make games in this style anymore. Hopefully some more dumb fun laser disc esque games come into the indie scene after this game


Also, bonus point for this being a lost game that has been fittingly "brought back from the grave"
