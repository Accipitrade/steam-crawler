\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game tries to copy the "Getting Over It with Bennett Foddy" experience and fails miserably. I gave the game a good try, I really did, and I found myself leaving after about 20 minutes. It wasn't the game play itself that scared me away, but the quite terrible audio that really threw me for a loop.

The game imitates "Getting Over It" by following the difficult parkour interlaced with music and commentary, except that it simply gets every part wrong.

The music is bog-standard elevator music but has really strange cues that I can only assume are based on height level. For about one minute in my 20 minutes of playtime, I was stuck on a fence jumping over it repeatedly and triggering the same voice lines and music cues repeatedly, which completely destroyed any appreciation I actually had for it.

I have to assume that the voice lines aren't recorded and are in fact AI generated because they sound absolutely terrible. There just isn't any emotion to be found in the voice lines, which is such a shame for a game that seems to be attempting to tell a really deep and personal story on the narrator's behalf. I just couldn't appreciate the narration because I felt like I was listening to my Amazon Alexa tell me about it's trauma while I just couldn't be bothered. Finally, I stopped playing after 20 minutes because at no point did I feel that the game was really difficult. This game holds your hand by auto-catching yourself on the nearest ledge meaning all you have to do is walk sideways and fall backwards on any straightaway and you'll never fall off. In addition, the game treats itself to really tedious obstacles; forcing you to run across train tracks and pipes for 70% of the "Parkour", essentially just functioning as a walking simulator. The existence of slow-motion just adds on to my theory that the purpose of this game was to cash in on the popularity of Bennett Foddy's own parkour game.

I just can't recommend it on any grounds; real disappointment.
