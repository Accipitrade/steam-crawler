\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis a weird one for me to review because I don't particularly enjoy traditional deck builders and this one is as traditional as they come. Yet, between the polished charm and a few mechanical choices... I've found myself coming back to this hour regularly for years now. If I had a crew of friends that owned this, I could see it as a regular game on my virtual table.

The core gameplay is that you start with a deck of 10 cards. Each turn you spend your cards to generate money which you can use to purchase new cards to strengthen your deck or to "Back A Princess". The first player to Back a Princess and then earn 21 points of succession points wins the game.

This leads to the one really unique aspect to Heart of Crowns deck-building. You may EITHER purchase new cards, or cash-in politicians for succession points. This is a strong decision that you need to make each turn that forces you to balance the economy with the politics. "Do I have enough politicians in my hand to justify skipping my purchase phase? Is it worth cashing in these negative value politician cards to clean my deck?" Knowing half of your hand will be useless each turn creates a unique and interesting challenge.

To make each game unique, Heart of Crown uses pre-made Supply Sets. These mean that instead of using the hundreds of cards, you only use 10 per game that guide the particular strategies you need to win. Whether you choose the balanced "Beginner Set", or the PVP focused "Military Set", or just a fully random selection of 10 cards... you're force to create unique strategies on the fly. It's not deep, but its enough that I haven't gotten sick of it.

There's an online multi-player mode that may actually be dead. I've had enough fun with the computer opponents that I've never felt the need to go online. If you have better luck finding players to join you than I have, it'll just make the experience better.

There is a "Campaign" option in the menu, but it's a more of a Random Unlock Shop where you spend points earned through play. I would have loved a bit more of a focused single player experience, playing specific handcrafted supply sets in order, unlocking cards through play, etc... but its much more of a casual pick up and play from time to time experience.

I may not have played this game much in terms of playtime, but I have played it off and on for years and I've found it well worth the purchase.\t\t\t\t\t

100% Heart of Crown PC Review

This game was a great game to play as a deck game when my wife got it in person, and it made me wounder if they had it on PC, which they did! The game was pretty fun, but it constantly has issues with playing online and isnt very stable either. All of my playtime I have working towards this 100% is playing vs the AI, grinding the points needed to draw the cards it wanted me to do to 100% this game. 2 of the achievement needed me to play with other players, which i thank those who helped me get those, but considering we had to find a way to work around the servers to even play together was as hard are trying to getting the achievement.

In addition you need to play at least 3 games vs the AI to get enough points to draw a single card, and you don't wanna draw more then one card since the game punishes you for drawing additional cards, and there is 157 cards to draw, so if you stick to just drawing 1 card a day, taking 157 days to 100% this is a tackle in its self. If the game didnt take so long to 100%, with addition to the server not working well to play with other players, forcing me to only play with AI for half a year, I would give this a up-vote, but if they fix the online play, it can be okay, but for completionist out there, unless you wanna spend half a year playing at least 3 games a day, i suggest you stay away from this beast of a game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHey

Hey you

Yeah, you reading this

Do you like deck building games? Cause this is one of them

Heart of Crown does a lot to distinguish itself from the many games birthed from the concept of big daddy Dominion. The scaling for cards available do not use bloated numbers. Instead, it's difficult to afford to buy cards for your deck at costs of four or even five without buying some supplemental money. Cards are also even more RNG as the market is a single deck with cards only revealed when piles have ran out; revealed cards going into piles of their respective copies until all spaces are occupied. This means that some of your key cards may not be available come round to your turn, or are simply bought out for the moment. This is all to eventually back a princess, which unlike other deck builders where the ending condition is not tied to winning the princess is a core part of the game. Once you got your choice of girl, who usually also bolsters some kind of game play altering effect, just start putting cards into your Domain until you get enough points to begin the end and become the winner

As a PC version, it's alright. There's a lot of flare to its style, and the digital nature really helps when it comes to the dozen or so different tokens that can come into play. AI is pretty competent. Multiplayer works as needed. Game generally likes to give you a warning when you're about to make a terrible misplay. Only critiques I have are more nitpicky. The "campaign" is a horrendous time sink, taking about 100 days of consistent play to be able to complete. Achievements are a little cryptic on their own, and are sometimes things that wouldn't ever realistically happen over the course of most games. Game has been on Steam for a while and only has one of several different expansions (though word is they're wanting to add a few more soon^TM)

I would recommend this game... underneath the stipulation that you either already have friends to play or that you really like the game. There is a ranked mode, but it's still a hard sell if you're going to be playing it by yourself\t\t\t\t\t
