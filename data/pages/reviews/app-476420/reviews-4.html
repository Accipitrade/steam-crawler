\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played this once in tabletop and was hooked. The moment I saw it was on Steam I added it to my Wishlist, and finally during the last sale got around to purchasing it.

The game is a deckbuilder - if you've played other tabletop deckbuilders such as Dominion, Thunderstone, Trains, etc. the mechanics will be familiar. It is not the same as digital-only deckbuilder games, like Slay the Spire or (I assume) Ascension: You do not upgrade cards, and there is no phase of gameplay during which you play with the deck you built but cannot purchase new cards.

There is a supply of cards to choose from, 10 kinds of which go in a set; in any given game, you'll only be using 10 types of the supply cards, of which there are 42 (with the DLC adding 13). The game has several preconstructed 'supply sets' you can play with, and it comes with a separate program where you can build and save your own. There are also some basic cards which are always available in every game. Where it diverges from many other tabletop deckbuilders is that while the basic cards are in their own stacks, always available for purchase until they run out, the supply set cards are shuffled together and dealt into eight slots. You will note this is less than the number of kinds of cards in the set. Once all slots have been filled, it stops dealing. At the end of any player's turn in which a slot is empty, it will deal cards until all empty slots are filled - possibly dealing more cards now available for purchase to existing stacks.

Also, there are Princesses.

At some point during the game, you will be able to back a Princess - so long as no other player has backed the same one already. There are 7 to choose from in the base Steam game, and the DLC adds an 8th. Each one has unique abilities. You will not want to always back the same one, because under different situations, depending on the supply set, your current deck, and what your opponents are doing, different Princesses will be more helpful for the occasion. Once - and only once - you have backed a Princess, you can start earning succession points towards winning the game. When you get to 20, you threaten victory - every other player gets one more turn, and if you are still the only one at or above 20, you win. If no one is at 20 anymore (some supply cards can do tricky things), play proceeds normally, and if one or more of your opponents managed to get to 20 succession points themselves, just the players who are at 20 or higher go into a sudden death overtime: Whoever gets to 30 pts first wins.

Games are quick against the CPUs, less quick in multiplayer because of lag and turn timers. The tutorial does a good job of explaining the rest of the rules. New to the digital release: There is a 'Campaign' menu option; this is not a single-player campaign mode. You get points after every game (including against CPUs); these points can be spent to draw scenario cards from the Campaign deck. These are skits which can explain different cards abilities and how they work in-universe, or give more personality to the various endearing characters.

The perfect audience for this game is someone who already enjoys deckbuilders, likes visual novels, and cute anime (non-fanservice) aesthetics.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very fun and light hearted deck building game. You pick a theme for the game which has the cards you'll be playing with and go. It's very fun multiplayer and I really didn't think I was going to enjoy this at all.
Most deck builders are a drag and slow, but the game goes rather quickly and very enjoyable. I've played the physical table top and this electronic version, both are fun.

The main premise is to gather resources, back a princess to rule, then gain victory points to ensure she wins. There's no direct combat or monster cards to worry about, just a few cards that may add junk cards or force you to discard a card or two.

If you buy this, please try to rope a friend or two into playing with you, it is well worth it.

The only cons I can think of are if you do not enjoy the anime aesthetic or if you simply detest deck building games, then this game would definitely not be for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI first learned about this game in it's tabletop form several months ago. It's a very fun deck building game with cute artwork and a fun theme and premise. The game has a nice flow to it, and gameplay progresses through several phases, each being more crucial than the last as you approach the end goal of completing a Coronation Ceremony. The rules are simple enough to learn, with most people usually being able to get the hang of the game after one game or so. I've taught several people how to play the table top version and they really enjoyed playing it. The PC version is no different in that regard.

Getting a copy of the tabletop version's rulebook would probably help iron out any questions you might have that the tutorial doesn't address.

In addition, the game includes 30+ different types of cards for the "Random Market" within the game, and several presets are available to allow you to pick sets of 10 of these cards to use during your game. You can also generate a random set, and there are over 100 trillion combinations just in the base set of 30 cards, not including the expansions. A sound strategy in one game might be completely useless in another, further adding to the replayability of the game.

If you like the PC version and also enjoy physical card games, I would suggest picking up the physical box set of the game. There are 2 expansions available for it as well. In addition, Japanime Games just recently released a second box set (titled Heart of Crown: Fairy Garden) which add a new set of 30+ Random market cards, along with an additional 2 expansions. Each expansion has it's own theme.

Far East Territory focuses on a fictional Japan like trading state.
Northern Enchantress is similar to Northern Europe/Russian, and involves magical elements
Fairy Garden takes place during the summer solstice and adds fairy magic and other fantasy elements to the game
Six City Alliance represents the trading cities of the Middle East and northern Africa
Path Before Heaven focuses on a fictional Imperial China.

I know this probably sounds like an advert for the game at this point, but I just can't say enough good things about this game. I've had a ton of fun playing it, it's easy to set up, easy to teach, fairly easy to learn, but it has enough items, some randomness and a decent learning curve that makes it hard to master. I keep coming back to it, and each time I learn something new, and it always generates a conversation afterwards about strategy and counter strategy and what could have gone better. I really can't recommend it enough!

Anyway, pick this game up, especially on sale! I'd be happy to play a game (or several) with you!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: The game mechanics are well designed, but the cards are not.

The game is a lot like Dominion, the fantastic OG deckbuilding game, but with cute anime style, some awesome different mechanics like princesses and holding cards, and a link system that is much more intuitive than keeping track of action points.

They went out of their way to make the cards different from Dominion, which is classy, but the result is cards that are often overcomplicated and for the most part too weak to bother with compared to the simple money cards. The terminal actions (actions without links, which are similar to +action in Dominion) are all far too weak to justify their downside, which in turn makes 2-link cards hardly worth bothering with either.

A shame that the game doesn't live up to its considerable potential. Even if you're really into deckbuilding games, this is probably not worth the money. However, a well-designed expansion could easily rescue this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's got everything in the physical game, and plays about five times faster. The automation is insanely nice to have when you draw your deck every turn.

Not only that, but it has surprising depth to it, even over that of Dominion. Domain management can make or break games, and the variety of princesses unlocks many different strategies. While Klam and Berg are most consistently good, the power of suddenly Backing Lulunasaika with the Imperial Capital is not to be underestimated for blowout potential, nor the ability to grab Imperial Estates and Alchemists with Flammaria. Laolily is of course super efficient if you have a strong filter & draw engine.

Ohka is perhaps the hardest to use, but even she can do hilarious things like grabbing the Imperial Crown to your hand.

I honestly believe that there are no bad cards in this game. Every card I thought was bad has eventually turned out to be vital to one of my strategies.

Online multiplayer took some finagling to set up, but was definitely worth it in the end; I got my money's worth out of Heart of Crown.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a confusing mess. I played the tutorial and only got the vaguest idea of what I was supposed to be doing. I played against the AI five different times and lost every single time even after looking up strategy guides. The UI is cluttered and difficult to read sometimes. Most of the time I think I'm actually doing well and then suddenly the AI grabs 18+ points in one turn and I'm left here trying to figure out what the hell happened.

I can't play the campaign at all because it says I don't have enough points, but this is a concept that's never explained in any way shape or form. The only way I could find to make the required points go up was to play versus matches against the AI. Which I can never win.

This had a lot of potential, but I feel like the game is actively trying to keep me from liking it. The core mechanics that I managed to grasp actually seem rather solid and I really wanted to learn how to use all of the cards to my advantage. But there's no way I'm going to force myself to suffer through this painful grind. You shouldn't have to work this hard before you've even gotten a chance to play the game.\t\t\t\t\t
