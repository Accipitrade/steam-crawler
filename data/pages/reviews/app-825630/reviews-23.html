\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNo longer is this the story of the mad scientist of madness, but the story of the sad scientist of sadness.
Steins;Gate was a perfectly self contained story, so what purpose does Steins;Gate 0 serve in the narrative? Well Steins;Gate 0 starts with (spoilers for the original Steins;Gate) an Okabe Rintaro who failed to save Makise Kurisu, killing her accidentally, the true ending of Steins;Gate has Mayuri slap some sense into him and a message from the version of him who originally failed to save Kurisu, but has discovered a way that the Okabe in the present can save Kurisu, and prevent WW3.
Steins;Gate 0 is essentially the story of the Okabe after a bad ending in Steins;Gate and how he will ultimately find the answer to achieving the true ending. Bearing the burden of the pasts, and PTSD, Okabe has a long road ahead of him, whether he knows it or not.
The voice acting is as quality in this VN as it was in the original Steins;Gate, with new characters having done exceptional jobs, it was a joy listening to Leskinin, Maho, and Kagari's performances.
As for the writing, no matter what path you find yourself down the story holds together exceptionally, the full picture of Steins;Gate 0 and answers to all of your questions almost demands seeing every story branch. The one ending that I've determined to be the major bad ending is the only one who's pacing gets awkward right at the end.
The CG, character art, and setting art are all great. Daru's redesign is the only one I'd say is inferior to the original Steins;Gate, otherwise the textured character art is wonderful. This game has notably more CG than Steins;Gate proper, and it goes without saying they're all beautiful.
That concludes my very general assessment, now get on with the mission.
El. Psy. Kongroo.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA visual novel about time travel. Follow Rintaro Okabe’s journey through the depths of despair.

The characters are interesting, likeable and have entertaining interactions with each other. The dialogue is pleasant to read overall, though it can be weird occasionally. The voice acting is very well done, the music is great at setting the mood, and the art is great. The game occasionally uses character sprites from the previous game, which has a different art style. It’s jarring if you notice but doesn’t degrade the experience in the end. As engaging as the plot can be, the fact that it’s based on time travel can lead to questionable developments and plot threads. And those can also result from things other than the time travel aspects.

The major gripe I have with the game is that whenever the perspective shifts to someone other than Okarin, the game uses an omniscient narrator to detail things that Okarin’s thoughts normally would. This can be expected for things like describing the actions characters are taking, but you also end up with exposition for things that would be better conveyed through dialogue, or do get conveyed through the dialogue later anyway. It’s also a missed opportunity, as being able to experience the world from the different characters’ perspectives would’ve been really interesting, but we’re left with this. Disappointing, but not bad enough to ruin the experience outright.

Compared to its predecessor (Steins;Gate), the previous game is better in that it lacks the above mentioned flaw, thanks to the perspective exclusively being Okabe’s. Its plot can be better in some regards, your mileage may vary. This game’s paths branch out in a more interesting way than the first, in a way that can make the different aspects of the plot you discover in different paths feel like uncovering more and more of a big mystery. You can opt to play this without playing the first, but this game begins at the very end of the previous game’s true ending, so expect to have one of the previous game’s biggest revelations spoiled right at the start.

It’s really great overall, and I would highly recommend both this and the previous game to fans of visual novels, media with a sci-fi leaning or time travel.\t\t\t\t\t
