\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you've played the original S;G to the true ending, this is the story of the hell that Okarin and company who sent the motivational video had to go through to get that outcome. You may be thinking that this would be unpleasant to experience after how sad the original was, but I assure you it is still worth it. Okarin's hilarious mad scientist persona is gone, obliterated by the overwhelming trauma of the first game's sights, and he is constantly suffering from PTSD, but this game still manages to have many warm moments as the focus shifts to family, what makes someone human, and the wider implications of what Okarin discovered about Kurisu's inventions and memories between worldlines. This time around we get to see the story from the perspective of other members of the cast too, which gives further insight, and the pacing is also much better with how much less has to be explained.

Overall I was very pleased with this as a sequel (inter-quel?), and it answers a lot of backstory questions, but somehow it lacks a certain amount of the awesomeness that was the original. Perhaps due to the muted tone and the cast's general lack of agency this time around. This is largely a story of discovering how the future will end up as horrible as the lab members know it will, not tinkering with a microwave to play god. I might compare it to how I feel playing Halo: Reach v.s. the original Halo trilogy, since both games are more technically impressive with arguably better gameplay than their predecessors.

Unlike the first game, the story branches quite significantly, and the separate endings themselves are far lengthier, but the individual runs are thereby shorter. It's no longer just a choice of whether or not to send a D-Mail and end the game how things are with the girl you're with, so anticipating the outcome will be more difficult. But at the same time, you always know the long-term outcome regardless of anyone's actions due to worldline convergence and the ending of the first game. Aside from Okarin's obviously mutilated personality, I'd say all the characters get an upgrade in quality this time around. Daru in particular becomes a much more likeable character, but all the new faces are welcome too. I especially enjoyed how Suzuha relates to her parents and tries hard to bring them together, despite knowing it is inevitable. The background music is still great, though the original's opening and ending songs are much more memorable. The art is better however, especially when it comes to the UI.

There is some obvious fan-service partial-nudity like in the first game for a tiny bit, but at least this time there was somewhat of a story reason for it...\t\t\t\t\t
