\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo the question to ask about this game is: "Should this exist?" And, having played all the routes to 100% completion, I do feel that the answer to that is no.

Steins;Gate was a story with a very coherent beginning and end, with well rounded and thought out character arcs. It's probably one of the most compelling time travel story there is and the ending tied everything up perfectly with a nice bow. The anime got a few OVAs and a movie that are pretty much unnecessary fan service, but didn't really detract from the ending's initial experience.

This thing though is an attempt to take a plot thread from the original game and draw an entire sequel out of it and, while it is nice to see more from characters you know and love very dearly, it still cheapens Steins;Gate as a whole.

The three big problems are as follows:

1) The plot becomes convulted and often baffling because it gets too complex for its own good.

The original VN played with the idea of the butterfly effect in meticulous ways, taking small events and examining long term consequences. In this though, a huge storyline is being stuck in the craw of an event between episodes 23 and 24 of the original anime and the result is awkward and weird.

A cult is introduced and never mentioned again. Wars start and abruptly end for reasons I really did not follow. Entire plotlines have these big gaping holes left behind, which is a stark contrast the tight and smart planning of the first story.

2) The female lead, Maho, is crippled by the fact that this is a side story.

Maho is an excellent character that I do like quite a bit. Her character arc is interesting and compelling, but the fact that this is a Steins;Gate storyline means she is always stuck in this weird place where she can't really shine because she's always played off Kirusu. And it just strikes me as a waste of good ideas. I mean, the girl has a computer clone of herself and never is seen interacting with it. And why does the game spend so much time developing a relationship between her and Okabe when it is damn well set in stone that Okabe only has eyes for one woman?

In the end, I'm left feeling that I really would have preferred to see these ideas for a character used in a fresh new setting.

3) The game doesn't have a set theme.

Steins;Gate's strength is it focused on one element of science fiction: Time Machines. Zero makes a big push for being about human-level intelligent AI and does interesting things with the concept, but then is dragged back to Time Machines and leaves those ideas not fully explored. The game never deals with the full implications of whether the memories of a person taking into digital form actually make that person into a person because it has to shift gears to figure out its time travel plot.

I would have much preferred an original story that dealt with these issues rather than a sequel.

Overall:
I love Steins;Gate and can't say I didn't like spending more time with its characters. Maybe you want to do just that too. Your call. But just make no mistake: This thing is an unnecessary sequel that dilutes the storyline. The creators would have been better off just making a new semicolon game.

Your love of Steins;Gate would be better served if you just hold on to the memories of the first VN/anime and leave it at that.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSteins;Gate was one of my favourite visual novels, with a tight narrative that by its end wrapped things up with a satisfying ending, tying up many loose ends. Steins;Gate 0 as a narrative fits right within the third act of the original Steins;Gate, from the perspective of Okabe in the Beta timeline. It's easy to imagine that as a side story of sorts, it's destined to not live up to the original, particularly since the ending is a known quantity going in. But the surprising thing about Steins;Gate 0 is that rather than feeling like a side plot to the original, it feels like an integral part of its story. It recontextualises the events in the original, turning a moment that was a deus ex machina to its own plot that fits right into the original setting and fleshes it out. Events that are talked about in the original such as the escalation of hostilities between SERN, other countries and power groups leading to WW3 are expanded upon.

Whereas in the original the writing was let down by its first act, which adhered too much to cliche with a protagonist that went around solving everyone's problems, this game's writing is let down by pacing issues caused by an overabundance of slice of life moments. It also runs into the same problems with the inclusion of characters that don't contribute to the story, such as Faris and Lukako. Certain important characters are never fleshed out as much as I would've liked, to the point where their motivations are nebulous. It's a problem when their actions are often the driving parts of the story. Finally, some parts of the ending are not explained that well.

Despite its flaws, Steins;Gate 0 is an excellent game and fans of the original should absolutely pick this up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt was sort of an underwhelming experience for me, to be honest. I was expecting this sequel to be more fast-paced, but instead it begins with lots of daily life moments that goes on and on and on. The story is a little too predictable, but maybe it is just me. Perhaps after reading the Zero Escape Series I am just immune to mild excitement.

There are also scenes where they mix (dunno if accidentally or not) portraits of both old (Steins; Gate) and new styles (Steins; Gate zero) and it looks extremely odd. At one point I was even thinking: 'Hmmm maybe they use the old art style as a hint that this character traveled from alpha world line?' but clearly that is not the case as I finish the game. So the mixed up art style is just totally confusing.

There is one character that I still don't know anything about after I finish true ending. What, then, is the purpose of introduce that character anyway? I think Steins;Gate is introducing a lot of extra characters without actually introducing them. It manages to flatten personalities of some existing characters, while introducing more flat characters.

The music does not feel as mysterious and surreal as those in the first Steins;Gate. Personally I think surreal music is one of the components that made the first Steins;Gate so outstanding.\t\t\t\t\t

Just one of the things I enjoyed so much about it - character development is done right. Rather than trying to artificially "develop" and change too many characters at once (and there are more than enough for me to keep track of) or develop in a very specific direction as part of their own story arc, most characters here remain consistent, fairly predictable and simple in personality. Two or three characters go through much more dramatic (and painful) personality changes, naturally our main character Okabe gets hits the hardest. Repeatedly. Possibly just a little less brutally than in the first VN, though.
But in fact my favorite character this time, simply in terms of how his very unique personality is both maintained and developed in a very fun and unexpected way - is Daru. Oversized perverted super hacka wins all the bonus points from me, for a perfect balance between silliness and serious tones. And for delivering a really good solid punch to someone who needed it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSteins;Gate 0, a.k.a. "The Edgier Timeline"

All jokes aside, the "game" is quite good: while I didn't enjoy the story quite as much as the one in Steins;Gate, that is largely due to the fact that the sequel isn't a "closed story" as the first one was. All the old characters make appearances and are characterized better, as once in a while you get to see events from their perspective. New characters are introtuced as well , and they're all well written (though, once again, the most prominent one is "waifubait").

My biggest problem with the game is the ending. However, it isn't like it's bad, far from it. The game features multiple endings, and the branching endings can be quite different (rather than just the choice of love interest, like in the previous game). The problem lies with the true ending; it feels like a sequel hook rather than a satisfying ending that ties all the loose ends together. It doesn't measure up to the original's one, though that might not even be possible in the first place.

Tl;dr

Story is good, characters are good, ending isn't as good as in the original.\t\t\t\t\t
