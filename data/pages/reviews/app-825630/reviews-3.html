\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWow....playing this game was an experience to say the least, but definitely not a bad one.

To all those coming here after watching the anime, do not expect to see the same linear story line that it provided, this is a far different experience. The Visual novel contains 6 different routes/endings all with their own individual chapters, along with a branching story depending on the ending. The anime, however, combines parts from all of these routes into one, linear route. As opposed to being a remake or adaptation, the Visual novel and the anime work with each other to fill in the gaps, with the anime giving a concise and clear conclusion, and the Visual novel giving all of the characters more time for character growth and providing a clearer answer to everything that the anime skips.
That being said...
I think this Visual novel is extremely enjoyable and does a lot of justice to the original steins;gate, which is very impressive considering how great the original was. There are plenty of touching moments and lines throughout the series, even some that have made me rethink the entire way I look at certain characters. Although I think that the best way to experience this VN is after watching the anime, I still believe it will be very enjoyable regardless of whether or not you have. My only problem with this game is that the story is much faster paced, and can seem to be all over the place at times. However, if you can look past this, you will find an amazing sequel (?) that is fun and lives up to the hype of its predecessor.\t\t\t\t\t

Out of all the games in this series, despite using the same engine this one bucked me off the hardest on both Linux and Windows, so prepare to invest some effort getting it to work well

I love the tone of the story, it can really get serious and stay serious, which leads to heavy emotional payoff.
In this regard I think it is even better than S;G and does an amazing job at juggling time travel plotlines, real technology, and romantic topics.

Introduction

Steins;Gate 0 is a visual novel that delves both in time travel and artificial intelligence. It's a sort of parallel story that branches from the original story, and it has a darker tone than the original game. While the story has solid writing and well written characters, it doesn't have the same impact as the original story. In this review I'll explain why.

Worldbuilding

The worldbuilding in this game is essentially the same as the original game, with the addition of AI research, which plays an important part on the plot. I liked the fact that the writers used something that was already established in the original game to another purpose. It helps to maintain the world an its rules consistent. Another thing worth mentioning is the stakes of the story are higher than in the original story. In the original game there were hints as to what the stakes were in the beta worldline, and I liked to see them realized in this game. The rules of time travel remained the same, with no alterations. In short, the worldbuilding is as solid as the original game, but with higher stakes.

The plot

This time, the plot is about the same group of teenagers (with some additions and absences) trying to prevent a disaster that could end in the extinction of mankind. The difference with the original game is that, in this case, the choices made in this game create entire new plotlines instead of endings, which makes this game a true branching VN (more on that in the gameplay section). While the different plotlines are well written, I got the sense that this game doesn't have the same level of clever writing present in the original game. Besides, I detected some minor contrivances in the plot, especially during the chapter "Promised Rinascimento", but they don't break the narrative at all. They are merely instances that could have been better written. Another things I liked about the game were there presence of a bad ending and the method by which the player gets the true ending of the story, because it's consistent with the setting of the story. The pacing issues present in the original game weren't as notorious in this game, but there are still problems, because there are instances when the plot progression slows to a crawl. I don't know if this is an issue in the anime adaptation.

The characters

Maybe the plot took a slight hit from a writing standpoint, but I'm glad that the character writing is as good as the original. There are also new characters in this game. We get to finally know Mayuri's cosplay friends, Fubuki and Kaede, who have some relevance to the plot. There is also Yuki, who is somewhat relevant in the chapter "Vega and Altair". But the most important new characters introduced in this game are Hiyajo Maho and Kagari (I won't mention her last name, because it's a huge spoiler). At first, I thought Kagari was a mere plot device to facilitate certain payoffs, but from the chapter "A Stray Sheep" to the chapter "Recursive Mother Goose", the writers added depth and nuance to her, and became a very good character. Maho was the best written new character, by far. She has relevance to the plot, she is multi-dimensional and has a meaningful character arc. Her rivalry with Makise Kurisu was brilliantly executed and added to her character development perfectly. The only thing I missed in this game was the interesting dynamic between Rintaro and Kurisu, but I guess it was necessary for the setting. Speaking of Rintaro, he undergoes a different character arc, but it's just as good as in the original game, with excellent and earned payoffs.

Gameplay

In this game, Phone Trigger retuns, but with some changes. Instead of e-mail, the characters send the player RINE messages (RINE is a play on the known messaging app LINE). RINE messages, save for one, don't influence the plot at all, but it adds to the sense of immersion at least. What really influences the plot are the interactions with Amadeus (there is a dedicated app on the smartphone). Sometimes it's answering calls or ignore them, sometimes is a direct interaction with the smartphone, but it's always something related with Amadeus that branches the plot. This makes branching easier, because there are limited instances where the player can influence the plot, but that removes an element of challenge that was present in the original game in the quest for the true ending. In the end, it's up to you if this a good or bad thing.

Artistic and technical elements

I didn't see a meaningful improvement in the art direction, but that doesn't mean it's bad. I noticed that the devs reused some assets from the original game, which makes sense, because the setting of the game didn't change, but there are additional background CGs, and I liked that the devs commited to drawing crowds in the backgrounds. The soundtrack is very different from the original, but it fits the setting and the tone of the scene. The voice acting remains as solid as the original, especially from Miyano Mamoru (the voice of Rintaro) and Hanazawa Kana (the voice of Mayuri). But one thing I noticed is that appears that the translation was done for a different team than of the original game, because Japanese honorifics are seldom used and the names are reversed (in Japan, the last name comes first), and I detected some mispellings here and there, but they don't damage the scene in the slightest.

Summarizing:

Pros:

-Interesting premise for a parallel story.
-Outstanding character writing.
-Well executed branching method.

Cons:

-The plot writing took a slight hit in comparison with the original.
-The pacing issues weren¬¥t entirely resolved.

This is yet another excellent entry on the Steins;Gate franchise. Maybe the plot took a slight nosedive, but I'm willing to overlook plot issues if the characters are good, and in that regard, this game truly delivered, because the characters are at the heart of storytelling. Fan or not of the franchise, if you like good storytelling, this is an easy recommendation, but I strongly recommend to play the original.
