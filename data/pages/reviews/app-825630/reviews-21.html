\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this on sale after having seen the original "Steins;Gate" anime adaptation and finding out it originated, oddly enough, from a video game and not an anime. So I gave this one a try.

The first thing I have to say that it's not really a video game, even if occasionally referred to as such. "Steins; Gate 0/Absolute Zero" lacks almost completely what makes a video game a video game: interactivity. Nor does it actually really provide a tutorial of itself. I really thought I had missed something vital when the action always proceeded without me, nor could I pick any dialogue options. The character also has a seemingly interactive phone that most of the time you can't really use. I sat for hours in front of the game reading text and listening to voiceover without any true interaction.

I eventually looked it up, and the interaction with the game is limited to only one thing: Interacting with your phone. And only on instances where the game permits it. These you are made aware of, at least. Guess what? Most of them are meaningless too.

I've seen other reviewers argue this is a "visual novel." So let's take it under that consideration.
- Unlike "interactive fiction" or "choose your own adventure" this means you get very little in terms of interacting with the story at all.
- Unlike a "graphic novel" you get very little in terms of portraying scenes, you mostly get the same backgrounds recycles with a few (nicely drawn) character images in front of it, and everything is conveyed through text and voiceover.

So, what "SG0" is is a novel that you are forced to read through a video game like interface that consists 90% of dialogue and 10% split between descriptions and the characters' thoughts. Part of the reason why the interface exists is to hide from you the fact that you have to make a few binary choices leading to several different endings. So the game part is really like a really tiny short decision tree and you might finish the game with one ending without even realizing that you had a choice.

This might all be acceptable if "SG0" was a good novel. It is not. It is for one horribly paced, dragging on through lots of meaningless dialogue that adds nothing to the story, introducing a few new rather uninteresting characters. The original "Steins;Gate" built its story around a cast of characters that it introduced and then explored, interweaving their fate with the evolving plot while accompanying the unfolding mystery. You got to care about them, and what happened to them. This is entirely absent. The same characters are just there like window dressing, talking incessantly to whoever is currently the chosen point-of-view protagonist, but if you weren't aware this is a sequel (however unlikely), you would find them rather bland and wonder what their significance was.

The most annoying part is probably the continuously same reaction of the main protagonist and some of the others who temporarily take over: Knowing something, saying nothing, sighing dramatically. I can't say how many times I have seen the "..." ellipsis on screen signifying a significant pause and knowing that it marks yet another occasion where a character will outright refuse to move the story forward and say probably "It's nothing" or "I'm fine" next. When something actually happens, it's always shoved aside, not mentioned, and endlessly ruminated over, preventing any plot progress. This is a story of very few things actually happening, leading to hugely delayed action, while you sit there, watching nothing much at all happen until that.

The second most annoying part is the repetition of information we know. We have seen something in a character flashback, we know it already. We then get a full conversation from another character's point of view where this information is revealed later in another long-winded drawn out conversation, probably punctuated by that character not revealing information in turn, marking the need for a future drawn-out conversation where some of these bits of information are tied together. These people talk, talk, talk, but they don't communicate. The game would be 80-90% shorter without this boring stalling.

When you do additional playthroughs to get different endings, it's up to you to skip through whole chapters to reach decision points. The game quicksaves into separate slots while you play it, but finding your decision points there is equally not obvious.

At some point when skipping actively through already known content and I felt like skipping the whole experience altogether and watch the anime adaptation instead. The whole skipping is only necessary because the game hides from you (kind of) what the branching points are.

The original "Steins;Gate" anime adaptation was a master piece. I feel this is a rather poor sequel to a clever story, way too drawn out, doing way too little in terms of something new. It starts off with some exciting new concepts and then... keeps going on in uninteresting ways, mostly with nothing happening, people explicitly not doing things, only to veer into drama towards the end. (And the anime adaptation seems to try to gloss over the failings of the game/novel by adding more stuff that is not better, leaving in turn stuff the game goes on and on about out, it seems. Sigh.)

Watch the anime adaptation of the original "Steins;Gate" if you haven't done so, it's real good. And if you really can't skip this one, have really low expectations. It might help.

What this game needs most, besides a better plot, pacing, and characters, is navigable scene index where you can revisit scenes, tag them for their importance (like interactability), and showing what new scenes unlock. This whole "skipping through dialogue" interface is the laziest and most horrible interface they could have come up with unless you consider sitting through it over and over again.\t\t\t\t\t

Final Thoughts Steins;Gate 0 Review
No matter where you are, you cannot outrun fate

TL;DR
Overall Rating: 8/10 or maybe 9/10 depending
#Discussions_QuoteBlock_Author
Where do I begin to describe this VN...
I- I cannot live anymore... Where... where am I? I CAN'T... I CAN'T SAVE THEM ALL! WHY... WHY WHY WHY WHY WHY?! WHY MUST THIS KEEP HAPPENING!!! WHY!


For a true honest review, see below
(may contain spoilers)


Story-line 9/10:
This story is, as said, a sequel to the original story. The story begins with our hero, Okabe Rintarou, formerly known as Hououin Kyouma, as he progresses a fate worse than death. If you played through the original game with 100% clear, then you understand the true ending. This story is basically the "true" ending of S;G, but ultimately ending in failure and inability to save that one person... As for the rest of it, you can feel the story-line being tense at every corner, from the beginning to the end, with minor scenes indicating happiness, but mostly will be ruined with a sense of tension. No matter where you go, no matter what choice you make, no matter the moment, you will feel absolute dread as you play through every scene. It is, without a doubt, a VN full of PTSD that cannot be erased.


Characters 7/10:
Our characters are as follows:
  • Okabe Rintarou - Mad Sad Scientist. As our main protagonist, he's the one leading the story. Every time he speaks, he always has a form of hesitation, depression, doubt, and fear. The text in description, the thoughts, the actions, he is only acting on impulse, as he remembers his life back from whence he lived.
  • Mayuri Shiina - The Protected. Mayuri plays a role to be fuel to the fire that drives Okabe's fear. Every moment she is put in danger, or when something small happens, she is the one that Okabe fears to lose the most. Her character is always ditzy, but nonetheless, as a caring and innocent being who Tuturu~s into our hearts, she is a central anxiety bringer.
  • Amadeus - Kurisu Makise. Imagine Kurisu, but she's an Ai. Another one of Okabe's fear factors. As he relives day to day remembering about his "eventful" journey, he either uses Amadeus as a tool to cope with it, or altogether runs from using it due to his pure fear and doubt.
  • Maho Hiyajo - Kurisu Replacement. Basically the genius scientist that replaces Kurisu. She is smart, curt, and probably has a similar demeanor of Kurisu. The only thing that is different is her height and maybe outlook towards Okabe. She isn't brighter than Kurisu, no, but she does have a slight attitude problem.
  • Itaru Hashida - Genius Super Hacker. Daru is still Daru. The only thing that changes about him, is that he has a daughter and a "love interest"
  • Suzuha Amane - Itaru's Daughter. Suzuha plays a decent role in this VN, but not as innocent as the previous. She is also someone that Okabe has trouble dealing with due to his previous alpha world-line experience.
  • Ruka Urushibara - Lukako. Lukako may be one of the more innocent characters in this VN, without any idea with what would be going on. Lukako is still Lukako, I don't know what else to say. JUST REMEMBER: HE IS A GUY!
  • Faris NyanNyan - Rumiho Nee-san. Faris still comes off as strongly delusional and anime-like. I feel like she plays a less important role in this.
  • Moeka Kiryu - Rounder. Quiet girl. Has glasses. Doesn't speak much. Gives off weird vibes. News Reporter. Also allots to Okabe's PTSD for the living memories from the previous World-Line. She is much better to deal with in this VN than the previous.
  • Yugo Tennouji - Mr. Braun. He is a bald, very muscular guy who owns the TV repair shop downstairs. He isn't a main focus in this VN, but he does appear in several scenes.
  • Nae Tennouji - Loli. Basically Mr.Braun's kid. Unlike the previous VN, she is a little less shy, and is more cutesy. She is still her daddy's little icon, but at least she isn't hiding behind him anymore.
  • Alexis Leskinen and Judy Reyes - English Speaking People. Terrible English, and very irritating. Probably someone you shouldn't really care for until the end or the middle of the story.
  • Kagari Shiina - Mayuri's Daughter. Someone you probably don't pay attention to until near the end. Honestly, if you're looking at character development, she probably has a pretty deep backstory.
  • Yuki Amane, Katsumi Nakase, and Kaede Kurushima - Mayuri's Cosplaying friends. Although they have different personalities, they are technically side characters. They aren't introduced very much within the first few chapters, and that out of the three characters, the only one that can be forgotten is Kaede. "Amane" is related to Suzuha, and Katsumi is... special... in a way. Kaede is just a quiet girl.


Artwork 8/10:
From the previous VN to this one, the changes in character design are substantially more vibrant. Compared to the previous VN, that feeling of "added layering" and "dotty" texture followed by having a "watercolor" and "hand-drawn by pen" feel are gone. Instead, you have simpler models. The backgrounds remain similar to its previous, and the CG's still follow the same design. However, the characters no longer have those strange looking eyes (which I find better), but also no longer have the same "vibrant" feel to them. It eventually grows on you with the art change. In all honestly, I preferred the previous Daru than the new one, but I guess I can find that all the girls and "girls" in this VN are cuter than before.


Soundtrack 9/10:
Good soundtrack, as most of it fits the theme of the VN. I guess you could say that it is a lot more tense due to the shift in character.


Endings ?/10:
You can end it as soon as chapter 4? arrives? Just don't pick up any of Amadeus's calls to do so. Everything is full of woe, so I guess you should just expect pain.
The endings, unlike the previous game, actually are part of the game's true plot, which allow for you to get information on every questionable thing that happens. This part of the story is much more important than the last game's (technically only important ending for the original S;G is the TRUE ending)


Overall 8/10:
Based on the above statements, I really adored the story for both its depth, and its extremely depressing content. If you watched the anime before playing the game, you'll probably feel bothered that you decently know what will happen next, but I guess that's on you for watching the anime before playing the game. Aside from that, if you're a Steins;Gate fan, then yes, enjoy it as a New Game+ (Rewatch). The soundtrack was good; it was applied well; and the voice acting was good too. Characters were memorable and endings were ???

As Steins Gate wills,

A companion piece to a time travel classic

The original Steins;Gate is not only a great VN game, it is an excellent piece of fiction. Steins;Gate is the best time travel story I have ever experienced. It features a phenomenal cast, wonderful chemistry between its eccentric characters and rigorously sticks with its time travel logic. The story is detailed and complex, yet comprehensible at the same time. The game does a great job explaining how wordlines and time travel work in its mythos. The True End of Steins;Gate is a brilliant conclusion and features our protagonists after many many failures finally mastering the rules of time and space to reach a worldline of peace and happiness.

Steins;Gate 0 on the other hand, has major issues. It is a much more difficult piece of work to comprehend. There are hanging plot threads, multiple red herrings and a conclusion that will leave some fans of Steins;Gate utterly confused and/or disappointed. The game heavily relies on conjecture and circumstantial evidence to fill in missing gaps in the story. Most fans of the original game will need to resort to online forums and resources to fully understand what goes on in Steins;Gate 0.

S;G 0 is neither a prequel nor is it a sequel. It is best described as a middle chapter during the True End route for the original Steins;Gate. S;G 0 is an alternate timeline where Okabe Rintaro gives up on time travel completely after realizing that he was the one responsible for killing his love, Makise Kurisu. This Rintaro strives to live a normal college student life. He joins a tennis club, goes on group dates and socializes with other students. One day he attends a seminar lectured by the spunky youthful scientist, Hiyajo Maho. Maho is an older colleague of Kurisu and has created an AI called Amadeus that is powered by an individual’s memories. It so happens that Maho has taken a snapshot of Kurisu’s memories before she went to Japan and is killed. Maho introduces Rintaro to Amadeus [Kurisu]. He is surprised to see that his interactions with the AI [Kurisu] is uncannily accurate compared with the real Kurisu. Rintaro in a playful jest then asks [Kurisu] if humans are capable of building a time-machine and is stunned by the AI’s reply.

S;G 0 plays very similar to its predecessor with some subtle improvements. Instead of communicating via email, Rintaro and his friends communicate using RINE (based on the real communications app LINE). RINE allows for group conversations and the use of stickers. It’s a minor gripe but some of the sticker designs feel like fan-service and don’t quite fit with the mood of the story. The other improvement is the shift in perspectives. While you will be playing as Rintaro most of the time, the perspective will shift occasionally to other characters like Suzaha and Maho. The perspective shift makes for a nice palate cleanse and allows for further development of these characters.

The biggest issue with S;G 0 is a lack of clear causality between worldline divergences. Rintaro is subjected to the divergences seemingly at random. The original game made it crystal clear why there are divergences because Okabe and friends directly influence them when using their time traveling device. In S;G 0 on the first playthrough, it’s a complete mystery how choosing whether or not to answer a call suddenly changes timelines. As with the original Steins;Gate, I highly recommend consulting a walkthrough after reaching an ending to see the bigger picture and start putting the pieces together.

Another issue with S;G 0 is the lack of a consistent antagonist. The original game only had SERN as the opposing entity. In S;G 0, there are multiple organizations fighting over the time machine technology. The introduction of competing antagonists adds significant complexity and confusion to the story. It often becomes unclear who the opposition is whenever the timeline shifts. This is another case where consulting a walkthrough is necessary to figure out how to reach all the separate endings but also comprehend how each timeline relates to each opposing organization.

S;G 0 heavily relies on the original cast to do most of the heavy narrative lifting. With the exception of Maho, the new characters in the game are lackluster. The roles of these new characters are muddled and pointless. There is one character whose only significant plot role is to help identify a secret code and then is promptly forgotten. There is another character who shares the same ability as Rintaro and can perceive shifts in worldlines but their character arc is completely abandoned halfway through the story. Fortunately the existing lab members: Faris, Luka and Daru play more important roles in S;G 0. It is a delight to see these characters develop and not always resort to their archetypal stereotypes like in the original game.

Maho is the shining highlight of S;G 0. While Maho might initially seem like a Kurisu stand-in, her character is exceptionally interesting due to her inferiority complex and her relationship with Kurisu. She is older than Kurisu, not as pretty as Kurisu, nor as smart as Kurisu, yet loved Kurisu as one of her few friends. Maho, while a brilliant scientist in her own right, considers herself an underdog. She constantly strives to better herself and prove that she can accomplish the impossible. Maho delivers the best lines and monologues in the entire game and dare I say it, exceeds Kurisu’s performance in the original Steins;Gate. While Kurisu is revered and idolized to a god-like degree in S;G 0, Maho by comparison feels extraordinarily and refreshingly human.

Steins;Gate 0 is a difficult game to review because it is more of a companion piece than a standalone game. Fans of the original Steins;Gate will voraciously consume this game regardless of reviews, while players who did not enjoy the original game will not have a sudden change of heart playing S;G 0. As for fans of the Steins;Gate anime, I recommend watching the Steins;Gate 0 anime instead and/or waiting for Steins;Gate 0 Elite to release before jumping in. Due to the nature of being a middle chapter, players will know how S;G 0 will end. The only reason to play S;G 0 is learning the journey of how our heroes got there. S;G 0 is not the unqualified success of the original, it has major issues with its plot and most of the new characters. The answers are all there, but they require more work and effort to acquire. The best thing that I can say about Steins;Gate 0 is it is more Steins;Gate and if you have enjoyed the original game, you will enjoy Steins;Gate 0.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA worthy sequel to Steins;Gate, although it isn't without flaw. Firstly, the change in artstyle is quite jarring and took a lot of getting used to (what is even more jarring, however, is sometimes the game reverts certain characters back to their old artstyle which just doesn't fit the new aesthetic). Secondly, the antagonists in this game seemed promising from the beginning but in the end felt underdeveloped. Thirdly, there are some lengthy scenes that are just pointless in the grand scheme of the narrative (looking at you, Sergeant Clean). Finally, like the antagonists, some plot points feel underdeveloped as well. I can't go into detail here because of spoilers.

However, I did enjoy the World War 3 moments and Maho is a great addition to the cast. Overall, I would recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t
