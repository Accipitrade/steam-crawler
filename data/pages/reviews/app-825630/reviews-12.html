\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA worthy successor to one of the best narratives ever written. It falls behind in a few areas but it improves in others; I'd say that the net change in quality is... wait for it... 0

(actually it's probably just a bit lower, but the game's still pretty great -- unlike that pun)

Pros:

+ Writing worthy of Steins;Gate. In many cases it has the same emotional punch. It still has Okabe interrupting every lecture he attends like a badass. OBJECTION!
+ New characters are not bad. This was one of my biggest concerns going in but they're actually fine. In all but one momentary and glaring incident, they don't try to displace the original cast.
+ Less linear story than the original, and some very creative design choices were made with regards to how things can play out. Fun fact: the narrative paths can actually make the number 0 if you plot them out.
+ Important choices are more obvious. You don't need a wiki to win. You don't need to reload saves 1048596 times to send the right text messages to get all the flags so you aren't horribly depressed 20 hours down the line.
+ The soundtrack's great. El Psy Kongroovy.

Cons:

- Email (or texting in this case) is less impactful and less open than in the first game. This can hamper replayability as you see the same text conversations every time. And each text message exchange is a one-reply thing: you don't have branching phone conversations like in the first game.
- The artstyle is different than the original. It's mostly good, and there's definitely a higher budget that is paying off -- for instance, a much higher ratio of scenes have unique art rather than moving portraits of characters -- but some characters look... off. Daru is sort-of one. And Kurisu is another, in all the scenes where her original art or artstyle isn't used (ie, flashbacks to America, or Amadeus scenes). They switch between two styles throughout the game and it's annoying.
- The true ending of this is, imho, not as good as the true ending of the first game. I mean that's a freaking high bar to clear but still. Also, moderate but not catastrophic spoiler: I think that the ending which unlocks the true ending is actually better than the true ending itself. Even more spoiler-y reasoning: I think this because certain characters don't overstep their bounds, and the memory of the iconic duo of the original is reinforced rather than slightly tarnished. Anything that slightly infringes upon the primacy of Kurisu makes part of me want to shout "OBJECTION" in a lecture hall.
- This is much shorter than the first game. I have 90+ hours in that one, whereas I've reached a fulfilling conclusion and achieved the true ending in 32 hours -- three days of play -- here. That's about the length of one playthrough of the original. And unlike the first one I didn't even use a wiki (extensively) to speed things up by avoiding trial and error. You're still getting value for money, because this IS Steins;Gate... you're just getting less value than before.
- Less Kurisu than the first game. Amadeus is a poor Kurisubstitute.

Thank you for reading my first review! And if you're a true Steins;Gate fan (and have the points to spare) consider awarding some of the good reviews here the "Mad Scientist" points award for irony. I've already drained my own reserve of steam points to do that on the review page of the first game.\t\t\t\t\t

Having similar to almost an exact prognosis as Okabe, it's hard to read this visual novel from a fictional point of view.
I understand that this story is supposed to be taken literally but there are so many red flags for someone who needs proper counseling.

When people think of OCD they tend to think of obsessive compulsion limited to stereotypes. For instance, checking the blinds or flicking a light on and off consistently for a periodic session of time in fear of something terrible happening if they don't. It's far more complex than that: OCD is a very complicated disorder and is just as serious as anxiety and depression, I'd argue worse.
Okabe suffers from reassurance OCD based off of a traumatic experience of reliving the death of Mayuri on a daily basis for an extended length of time, a length that is not humanly possible. Okabe wants to be, needs to be, reassured to the point of being obsessive over the matter - Mayuri must be safe, even on of a different 'world line' where she is not subject to harm. This trauma started with anxiety lead to depression and is now obsessive behavior which he can no longer control. Okabe is seemingly passive yet ever moving not even knowing what is happening around him as if time itself is no longer a function of this Universe. Okabe's OCD is turning into schizophrenic episodes or forms of psychosis where Kirisu will appear, gift him with what he already knows and bring him back. This is blatant self-realization of the fact that Okabe has to deal with the death of someone close to him and/or a death he could do nothing about. Both of these failures to act come with the exact same consequence - guilt.\t\t\t\t\t
