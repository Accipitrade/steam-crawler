Worse than Democracy 3 in every single aspect and here is why:

==How the budgeting is broken==
The most fundamental flaw of this game is the budgeting. For some arcane reason budgeting is not handled by setting a certain percentage of your total budget but by incrementing the relative change. That means if e.g. your poulation grows you have to manually adjust every single subvalue of infrastructure, education and so on. What really breaks this is that all increases cost the same number of action points.

Want to increase your budget for parks by 1% totalling 1 million dollars? Sure, it costs 1 point.
Want to increase your budget for streets by 1% totalling 20 billion dollars? Sure, it costs 1 point.
There are very few exceptions to this and they don't really relate to the country or comitment you are making.

Especially when you have a population boom, due to the system it's impossible to keep up, I had a growth rate of about 6% and the money to keep up. The problem is, you get between 15-20 action points per turn and raising all the things that are impacted by population in that range would cost about 75-100 action points. Due to this I should have been in a death spiral but because the rest of the system is broken as well nothing really happened. I know I could have implemented a one child policy and similar things but at no point did the game give me any reason to do so, my infrastructure tanked from 85% to 25% within 8 years an I still got 100% of the votes.

Democracy 3 has static values for things like population, while that's far from ideal at least they model changes in the composition of your countries political landscape and where applicable they work with percentages of your budget. This means unlike this "simulator" you don't run into situations that snowball out of control because you lack the action points to do anything about it.

==How the tax system is broken==
Taxes don't seem to be simulated at all, in one turn abolished corporate taxes and it had no impact on the economy whatsoever. Then I started to implement property taxes, in the end I had a country that relied to 70% on property tax. Raising and lowering it had pretty much the same effect as raising and lowering other taxes, I couldn't find a meaningful difference.

Same goes for Flat Tax, it had no effect on any of my taxes or my popularity.

Democracy 3 on the other hand models membership of different income brackets and the income of every group. That means that it does matter what you tax since that does have a noticeable impact not only on how much you can generate but also how the political composition of your country and societal situations develop.

==How the popularity system is broken==
The main issue seems to be that there are no groups within your country, every citizen is the same so everything you do has the same impact. Taxing the rich or the middle class comes with pretty much the same impact to your approval rating. That impact being none whatsoever.

The popularity system seem to become increasingly broken the longer you play, while at the beginning a slight decrease in education would have a unreasonable strong impact on your approval, later on the citizens kind of lost interest in everything, I was pumping all my money into the military while infrastructure, social security, health, education and so on were absolutely tanking in the end my country had lost about 75% of the afforementioned stats, mortality increased by 300% due to bad healthcare and I still had an approval rating of 95-100%.

My best guess would be that the GDP is a strong influence on the general popularity and there is no system in place that caps your max popularity when certain conditions are met. So my people literally where dying in the streets because of bad drinking water and no heating without any medical help while gangs roamed the streets but somehow they still thought I was doing a brilliant job because of our countries multi trillion dollar reserves that could have been used to alleviate the situation if it wasn't for the action point system.

Democracy 3 assignes every single voter to multiple interest groups. So someone can be a middle income liberal enviromentalist or a poor homeless neo nazi that really likes cars. This determines their approval towards your policies and how they are influenced by them down to their income.

==How the economy is broken==
As far as I can gather neither taxes, social policies, infrastructure or anything else has a predictable impact on your economy. What I can say for certain is that it's completely unrealistic and doesn't take into account real numbers.

Within 20 years of game time I had a population of about 111 million people (started with 80 million, so apparently it was a country full of rabits) and a GDP of about 50 Trillion dollars that's 3/4 of the current GDP of the whole world. It's estimated that we'll see a world GDP of around 100 Trillion dollars by 2050 so even that would be double the share the US currently has with a third of the population.

From my perspecitve there are two issues that lead to those nonsensical outcomes first of the game doesn't seem to model the world economy whatsoever so you are pretty much in a bubble universe with infinite growth and the game completely ignores inflation/deflation. Having both of those you'd have a controll layer that would limit growth to a realistic level.

==How everything else is broken==
Since I don't want to invest more time in a review than the dev in fundamental systems here are just some other things that don't work as they should:

- When you lose an election while being a Dictatorship and stay in power due to... well, being a dictator, your approval rating automaticaly jumps to 50% as if you had won the election. This is not how people react to election fraud.

- When conquering a country your crime rate skyrockets immediatly, this is neither due to returning soldiers (since I redeployed all of them without any impact to my crime rate) nor is it to terrorism (Intelligence budget is not impacted) or population influx (stays the same). Somehow peace makes people become criminals, declaring war doesn't reduce crime though.

- There are no indicators what department impacts what value. Are hospitals infrastructure or health or both? Nobody knows and investing in them doesn't impact either.

- The range of laws you can pass is very limited and the sliders don't make sense you can make prostitution 100% legal and enforce that legality with very strict punishments.

- All laws (except for dictatorship and one other) cost the same amount of action points to implement. So apparently having the death penalty for every single crime, no matter how small is about as complex and controversal as subsidizing public transport.

- The war system is a joke, there are no conseqeuneces whatsoever you don't benefit from it, you don't get penalized for it, it's completely inconsequential. Apparently it's an instant game over if you nuke someone. Yeah, that's about the the level of attention to detail I'd expect. Where is the fun in diplomacy on the world stage where you can break up NATO and build your own power block and start developing covert biological weapons? Nukes = bad everything else = meh That's more than enough complexity.

- While a game like this doesn't need to have impressive graphics the UI could use tweaks like sub menues or a colour that's not brown. Or how about some headlines in the newspaper that are interesting to read and come with nice pictures instead of pixelated nonsense with a factual statement on it? The only creative thing I have seen is that the headlines reflect that you have media censorship by not showing any information whatsoever instead of subversive subtle headlines while the policy is invoked (think David Tennant's character in Just Cause 3)

Just buy Democracy 3 instead!\t\t\t\t\t
