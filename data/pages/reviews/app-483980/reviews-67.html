Best Dad
or
Mad Father

No, EA. Single player games are NOT dead, and this little gem is proof of that. Although several years old, this pixelated treasure still holds out. I first heard of this game through Pewdiepie during my high school years, when Let’s Players were just rising in popularity. Now 6 years later, I have played and finished the game, and I will now tell you why you should buy it (spoiler free!).

Aya Drevis is a ten year old girl living in her mansion with her father, Alfred Drevis within Germany. You are then presented with the option to skip the backstory, which gives more insight to Aya’s and her family’s story.

Shortly, after you exit your room, you realise that everything has gone south within the Drevis household. Who are the monster roaming the halls? What’s up with that red-eyed mysterious salesman who appears before Aya on an occasional basis? And what does he mean when he claims that those monsters in the house are Alfred’s own doing?
Gameplay is very simplistic. You have an inventory, which catalogues all the items that you have collected on you journey. Mid-game, you are given a mini-chainsaw which allows you to cut open barrels and access keys, as well as collect gems. The only insight you are offered in regards to the purpose of the gems is the possibility of an unlockable feature.

Another aspect that I found enjoyable was the manner in which you acquire these gems. One example of this is when you are given the task to fetch the eyes for this eyeless girl. Doing so requires you to backtrack through the house and retrieve them. Although a very minor feature in the game, there is purpose and a sense of accomplishment within this task, and one that makes games like these so appealing.

Mad Father’s graphics are standard pixel animation. One of small gripes I have with this Steam release is how the enemy models aren’t as scary as they were in the earlier versions. For example, the enemies roaming the halls in the earlier parts of the game aren’t hideously deformed, half-naked horrors resembling humans, but rather scary dolls.
The story is an intriguing tale of murder, macabre, supernatural and a young girl’s wavering devotion to her father as she struggles to uncover what dark secrets lie within the Drevis household…

My final thoughts are that if all of the above are your thing, and if you feel like proving EA is still full of it, please buy this game. The price is totally worth it and it will be a great addition to any Steam library.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game, is pretty good. Definitely not one of my personal favorites, but it is damn worth playing, especially for the price it costs!

Mad Father is a horror puzzle game where you play as Aya (the black haired girl you see in the pictures) trying to find her father after a curse has been laid upon the mansion you live in. However, it is not an easy task, as Aya's father's works come to light, and ultimately try to hinder your progression. That is the synopsis for this game. This game has 3 endings (bad ending 1, bad ending 2, and true ending), a bonus ending, and an extra playable ending that is unlocked after achieving the true ending plus the bonus ending.

As for the horror, it consists of the usual jumpscares that are present in many horror-themed things, as well as a conistant horror theme applied to the game. You never feel entirely safe, as the creepy mapping makes you suspicious of anything that could come your way. As for the puzzles, they are like the kinds you see in an escape room. You basically go on a linear path throughout the mansion, going into various rooms to obtain various clues and items, in order to unlock more rooms until you reach the end. They aren't bad, but they aren't neccesarily "the best".

Is this game worth the full price? Definitely. The game at most can be 6 hours long, maybe even longer if you waste some time examing the detail, or when you get really stuck on a puzzle and can't figure it out. I took a while because I spent probably 20 extra minutes stomping on a corpse because it made a sound effect. But that's just me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnother classic RPGMaker horror game by the creator of Misao, you play as a young girl named Aya who is faced with a sudden curse upon her house on the anniversary of her mother's death, in which her father is kidnapped by horrible monsters and Aya must set out to rescue him. Along the way, she meets the lost souls of some of her father's past victims, and helps them complete their "unfinished business" so that they can finally be put to rest.

As a newcomer, I am not familiar with the "original version", other than the fact that this "version" has several graphical upgrades as well as some changed or added story elements (i.e. Ines' backstory). It was quite a thrilling experience, though I do have some major gripes. From least to worst:

- The Gem in the Entrance Hall: Because the Gem is "guarded" by the two monsters roaming around near the bottom of the stairs, you need to be really smart about how you're going to get it. Trying to trick the monsters into getting "trapped" upstairs and running into a nearby room on the bottom floor after getting the Gem would probably be a good option, otherwise you can wait until later when you return to the area during your trip to/from the Attic, as the monsters are now gone. Because of the latter point, this is the least awful.

- The "Killer Doll Stealth Minigame": The object of this "minigame" is to get past the dolls without getting caught, which requires good timing and patience. The first time is required in order to get a Gem and to free the imprisoned person back out in the Jail for the "No Fear" achievement. The only bright spot about it is that each section has a "checkpoint", so if you pass one part but fail the next, you start at the beginning of the part you failed instead of having to do it all again (but only if you choose "Continue" at the Game Over screen). The second time, however, you have to start over from the beginning of the level, but then again, this time it's all one area.

- The "Quicktime Events": Apparently they decided to make escaping from the grasp of a monster more complicated by making you press a series of arrow key combinations instead of simply mashing the Z key like in the original (though the Z key is still used in certain other events). You have to press enough combinations correctly (about six or so) to free yourself before your HP drops to zero. Lovely.

- The Monster in the Guest Rooms: JESUS. CHRIST. ON. A BIKE. THIS F*CKING THING. You enter the Guest Rooms, right? You probably talk to the save crow in the open cell and leave, but as soon as you do and proceed to the right, the door closes. That door needs to be opened again, because as soon as you try continuing to the right and through the doorway, a monster will appear and lunge at you full of p*ss and vinegar, and it will instantly kill you if you don't take cover into the open cell faster than the speed of light. Once you do that and open the secret passageway to the next cell, you need to move the vase out of the way to get through and... to open the next cell. The monster immediately notices and chases you back into the passageway, and you need to quickly pull the vase back to cut the monster off before it can catch you, and then you need to go and close that cell door before the monster can get out. Bottom line, when this thing is chasing you and you are a nanosecond too slow or miss the opportunity, you're dead. This is, fortunately, made a lot easier in the 2020 remake (thank God).

...Okay, moving on.

The game is yet another choice-based branching storyline with 3 possible endings: 2 bad endings, and the true ending. There is also an extra scene after the credits of the true ending, achieved by collecting all 21 of the gems scattered around the house during your adventure. After all of that, you unlock "If" (which, like the extra scene, is some sort of "bonus epilogue") and "Gallery" (which allows you to view character sprites and read their profiles, listen to music, view all the CGs, etc.) on the title screen.

In conclusion, despite the frustration I endured, I rather enjoyed this game. If you've been wanting to try it out, then go right ahead. Just be warned of the annoying parts that I previously mentioned.

...Oh, and the nightmare fuel, too.
