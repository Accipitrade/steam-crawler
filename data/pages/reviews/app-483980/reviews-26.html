\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThough there are moments where a key fear tactic is abruptness, jump scares are not what define this game. The atmosphere of Mad Father is worthy of its notoriety, and even in its narrower paths retains a constant sense of true exploration. While dialogue can feel clunky, the world is immersive and believable with the driving factor being the love of an 11-year old girl for her parents. Aya's love is innocent, and strong enough (apparently) to withstand quite drastic encounters. I'd probably give up if I was her, but I'm glad for our sake that she doesn't.

On a more casual note, it's an addicting game. The fairly easiness of most puzzles drove me to completing this in only two sittings. However, seeing how many accomplishments I have yet to achieve, and also knowing the possibility of alternate endings, I know I will be playing it again. The pacing of the game quickly lets me feel proud and excited in my continued findings and progress, but withholds enough of the satisfaction to always make me want more, of the good AND the frightening. This is one of my favorite games that I have played this year, and though it is short, it is very worthwhile.

9.5/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've always been a huge fan of the old rpg maker horror games, so my review might be a bit biased. But given that I already know and love the story, I won't harp on it. Instead I'll talk about the differences between the reboot and the old game, which might be important for players who already played the original and are wondering if they should buy the new version or not. There's definitely more story than the original, which fleshes out the characters a bit more. There's also more endings than before, though I haven't finished the game yet and don't know what they are. But the game explicitly tells you that they exist when you first open it. I also like that it tells you what your objectives are in the new "missions" tab, as well as in popups on the screen when you discover a new mission. A big problem I always had before was when I opened up an rpg game like this after a day or so of playing it was not being able to remember what I was doing the last time I played it. It was always confusing and frustrating, which took away from the enjoyment of the game. But now I don't have to worry about it. And if you find those popups annoying, you're easily able to disable them in the menu screen. Another thing they added was keybinding for controllers. I don't use a controller, but I thought I'd mention it for those who do.
Some things that I didn't like so much were how they kind of nerfed the chainsaw??? It's a little hard to explain, especially if you haven't played the original. Basically you can only use it in the basement, which isn't something super obvious unless you're paying very close attention to the texture of certain game assets. I understand why they did it, because it would be kind of op to be able to just destroy the cabinets and other wooden assets to acquire gems, but I just feel like they could have done it differently. In the basement, you can chainsaw very specific barrels and crates and sometimes they'll either have items or gems in them. But most crates have a metal frame on them, which the game tells you is too strong for the chainsaw to cut through. Fair enough, I get that. But I would say probably 80% of the crates in the entire game have that metal frame on them. To me, it feels less like I need to search for things, and more like the game is directly telling me where an item/gem is, because the crates that I can break are so minimal. And for the barrels it seems like they kind of gave up on giving you a reason as to why you can't break specific ones. Basically once you're out of the basement, the chainsaw no longer works. Instead, when you try to use it, it makes the error message sound. So like...yeah, it's kind of weird. It's not like a game breaking, unplayable con, just something that kind of annoyed me while I was playing. I thought maybe I could use the chainsaw to solve certain puzzles outside of the basement, but no. Totally useless.
Another potential con is the difficulty levels. I don't personally mind this, as I normally play on normal difficulty anyway, but I thought maybe some people might be annoyed by it. When you first start the game, you can only play on normal difficulty. In order to choose a higher difficulty, you must first complete the game on normal and get a certain ending to unlock the other difficulties.
Another con that may or may not be an issue for some players is the walk though guides for this game. I played though the majority of this game with no guide, but got stuck on the part where I needed to give someone their eyes back. (vague for the purpose of not spoiling anything). I already had the item in question, but didn't know how to give it to them, as the room they were in was locked. I tried several different ways to give the eyes to them, but couldn't figure out exactly how to do it. I eventually gave up and decided to look up a walk through. I found an extensive guide on the game, and tried to follow the steps given by the guide, but quickly became frustrated and confused by the instructions given. For example, I was supposed to put out a fire in a specific room by collecting a bucket and water. However, there was no bucket in the room they were talking about. It was after a long while before I realized that this guide was for the original game, not the reboot. Thankfully the reboot gave you a similar item which has the same effect as the water bucket, and the fire was in the exact same room as the original game. But it was still a very frustrating experience. Another instance of this happened when I was trying to figure out how to navigate the tunnels, and the turn by turn directions made absolutely no sense, and I realized that this other guide was also outdated. I actually couldn't find an up to date guide on that specific part of the game, and ended up figuring it out for myself. For those who don't need walk throughs, this might not be an issue. But I thought I'd let people know that outdated walk throughs might be an issue for them. This also isn't really the game's fault, because everyone solves puzzles differently in their heads, and what seems easy for one person might not be easy for someone else. The puzzles weren't "unfair" and required a walk through, it's just that I needed one for certain parts of the game because I was either too impatient to figure it out, or I just couldn't solve the puzzle on my own. This is an afterthought of me googling guides and being frustrated by them. but you could probably go into the steam guides for this game and find what you're looking for too. I normally just google guides. but I think you're better off just looking at the steam page for the game, because you're definitely getting information from people who played the newest version of the game.
All in all, I would definitely recommend this game to others, even those who have played the original. The cons I listed aren't really even cons, they're more like nit-picks. I think Mad Father is a great game, and this reboot does add a lot to the original story. I know I missed a lot of the changes they added, but these are what stuck out to me the most. I might update my review once I've finished the game and gotten the multiple endings to see what's different about them as well.\t\t\t\t\t
