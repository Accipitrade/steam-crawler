\r\n\t\t\t\t\tthis game is amazingly complex and brutal. normal mode alone was too much for me at first. getting a few hours into the game and coming up against an enemy who would crush me and end my campaign got extremely frustrating. but i kicked it down to easy and played it like a tutorial for a bit, learning a couple classes and the story and the massive amount of side content such as radiant side quests, or your house you can build/upgrade and dig for scrap/items. you can run your own tavern and build your relationship with different factions within the world. the ASCII graphics are also impressively detailed and the amount of depth they put into their combat system is impressive. this is easily the best text based game i've played. i have 17 hours in it and i don't have any idea where i'm at in the story with any of my characters, but i don't care. i'm having a blast levelling up and questing and exploring the 4-5 areas i've unlocked with them so far and finding a large variety of enemies/bosses/events/items. i'm hoping there's no level cap or the level cap is pretty high, because i love how many different things you can put points in when you level up. the class based skills, the general skills and weapon skills. you can become a specialized badass in just about anything. wanna be a mage who wields greatswords and/or bows instead of the classic staves/wands/daggers? you can. with the overwhelming amount of things there is to do and discover in this game, i don't have enough space in this review to list it all and i'm constantly finding more all the time.

the bottom line is, if you like text based games or wanna try a text based RPG, this is the best one i've found so far and i wholeheartedly recommend you try it out. wait for it to go on sale if you're worried about the $8 price tag, but i assure you there is easily a hundred hours worth of content to this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love this game. I really do. It really almost is a great game, however it deters me from actually playing the game.
No matter how hard I tried, even when I did amazing (Especially On Classic Mode) I still ended up getting two shotted by some prick of a monster that was not to be expected anywhere in miles.
It always ended up being (except for like 2 runs) skeleton, skeleton, skeleton, some random dude, skeleton, dragon, death
I didn't want to play soft-core due to the fact, you wouldn't get anything out of it and also I want to stay true to the game rather than keep dying over and over if there ever is one of those stupid, unexpected monsters.
I tried the lunatic game mode and got past the tutorial monster and then proceeded to (basically) get one-shotted from a skeleton. I ended up never playing lunatic ever again.
I like the inventory management slightly, however if you accidentally throw out your legendary weapon, you're absolutely f**ked.
The library pin should definitely harder to find rather than,
1. ask library dude for 3 letters
2. library dude gives you another hint about a phonepad
3. guess the first number you missed
I think it should be more obscure and a little harder to find.

Since, I could never get passed that much of the game, I never really understood a lot of what was going on.
Like how everyone was talking about potatoes, there was a resistance?, how I ended up aquiring the deed to the tavern - several times, etc.

I love the crafting system and I've never seen anything like it really. I wish I got to use it more than 5 times but whatever now.
I despise the madlibbed story you get in the beggining of the game. It doesn't make since and is one of the most imbecillic things I've ever seen.

EX: You're a barbarian of goern descent.
You like potatoes and were an orphan as a child.
You loved to be a murderer so you put a banana on your head.
There is a woman right in front of you and she hands you a potato.
You give her an avacado
This is your story

---------------------------------
So basically to sum this all up

PROS:
- Unique crafting system
- Really nice D&D kind of feel
- Cool weapon system
- Amazing music
- Getting to own a tavern
- (Really cool gameplay mechanics)
- Potential to be an even greater game
- Sympathizing with an NPC for disliking potatoes

CONS:
- Nothing happens after you die, the game just restarts itself
- No replayibility after 3 hours
- Nonsense throughout the game
- Getting one/two-shotted by some random bs encounter
- Really weird letter presses for a command like press [g] for camp or [r] to pray
- Non-secretive guildhall in library
- There is a lot to miss if you are not looking in the right place
- You can wait several days and the town is somehow still being attacked
- Potatoes
- A LOT, LOT more worse stuff than this

I recommend it to the people who are masochistic and/or will laugh at something that only 4 year olds would laugh at
however, I don't think there really is anyone that would play the game due to them not being 5 year olds or that you only play soft-core mode

I may have missed some more key points to liking/disliking the game (both in-game and in this review but I don't want to waste more of my time.)

It basically is basically 3/10

overall sh*t game\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSanctuaryRPG is a peculiar mix of brilliant and lazy design decisions. In concept, it's a turn-based, menu-driven distillation of all the "number go up" bits that make games in the mold of Diablo so addictive. In some places, the game delivers on that concept in a way just short of genius; in others, it goes "eh, whatever, this will do" and calls it a day.

Brilliant: The battle system is complex and engaging despite its extreme abstraction. Attack and defense abilities, status effects, monster affixes, range (sort of), weapon properties, character class features, and more besides--the better you learn these things, the more successful you'll be. Various elements require that you stay mindful of what's going on rather than run on autopilot, lest you miss opportunities or suffer dangerous consequences.

Lazy: The UI is atrocious. You have to mash Enter after every menu choice, as if you were working with a terminal prompt instead of pushing virtual buttons. The mouse does nothing, but the game doesn't bother to hide the cursor in fullscreen mode.

Brilliant: There are tons of ways to get experience, gold, and items. If you're feeling behind the curve against the current crop of storyline foes, you don't necessarily have to farm lower-level monsters--you can take on some level-matched foes in the arena, read books in the Library, tend patrons in your tavern, or craft, all of which will move you along at least a bit.

Lazy: Some of these activities, though, are deadly boring, and you'll roll your eyes when some new system presents itself and it turns out to be yet another thing that awards experience, gold, and random items. Crafting? Experience and random items (you get only minimal input into what you craft). Tavern? Gold, experience, and "gifts" from patrons that are, yes, random items. Fetch quests? Experience and random items. There's all of one well-hidden quest that gives you anything unique or interesting.

Brilliant: The aesthetic. Well, maybe not brilliant exactly, but at least gutsy and unusual. The ASCII art is well done and the music catchy.

Lazy: The art you see has nothing to do with what you're doing, most of the time. You can be trudging through a hellish underworld, and still see pictures of green forests and blue oceans. The writing is terrible; there's a nonsensical main plot about robots and fallen angels that tries to get you to take it seriously even as the rest of the game cracks jokes about potatoes. (If you can even call them jokes. The author seems to think that simply saying the word "potato" is the height of hilarity.) Don't bother talking to NPCs--their dialogue is bland and doesn't change from start to finish.

Brilliant: Itemization is very well designed. You can't stash old equipment, and each item has at most a core attack/defense stat, two attribute boosts, and an affix. That means that you're almost always trading something off when you upgrade, prompting reconsideration of what's in your other slots. Just changing out a weapon of one category for another can drastically change your play style.

Lazy: Permadeath. I don't take offense to the idea in principle--Nethack, that age-old Roguelike, is one of my favorite games of all time. But it doesn't fit at all here. There's not enough variety from one game to the next, games take too long to proceed to the latter stages, and there isn't enough of a skill learning curve to justify it. I recommend playing a couple short games to get the idea, then once you settle on a build you like, backing up your save files as you go.

In the end, I do give this game the thumbs-up because it's such an unusual specimen, and I found it enjoyable enough to play to conclusion on the standard difficulty. But know what you're getting into: it is, in many ways, half-assed.\t\t\t\t\t
