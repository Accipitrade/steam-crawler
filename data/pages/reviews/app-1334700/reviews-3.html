Ive come across the in my bitter search for more surral indie rpgs. Omori filled me with an intense desire which Deltarune chapter managed to only fuel further. After playing "Off" and "Middens" I had this game on my list of interest. Bought it and finished in 2 days.

Before commenting anything further: This game meets the player with a cutesy artstyle heavily reminding one of Omori. The similarities are not absolute in such superficial aspects like the protagonists being very tongue and cheeks with Omori ("Ori"). In flashbacks the player uncovers the past of Ori and it unveils that he has a sister named "Mari" - just like Sunnys! That is nothing I give a bad rating for but it strikes as a bit of unorginiality and odd taste in ones mouth.

That is not the last fragment of this game being unorignial unfortunately. As the two protagonists "Ori" and "Rem" complete puzzles in their wish to cross across a mysterious veil blocking the hall. they are forced to solve dozens of puzzles in typical RPG Maker manner. As it is with these types of games, the puzzles reach a simplicity of an addition equation or the complexity of figuring out how to reach Dark Souls 1 Artorias DLC all by oneself, albeit by asking the rosary for help and putting ones mind into focus for some minutes makes this chore a piece of cake.

In what aspect the puzzles exceed in superbly are the metaphor and symbolism. They represent the doctrine and teachings children are indoctrinated with in sunday school or other institutions. Over the course of the whole game the player encounters symbolism which encrypt themselves into a criticism of institutional christianity and its abstract tradition and dogmaticism. In the midst of the game even in a very nietzschian sense as the world view and teachings of the conductor is a nihilistic one which only can be saved by an absurd belief calling out for a purification, guidance and traditions as all "meaning" and purpose" is degraded into an abstractum.
I recommend to play the game on your own if you wish to dive deeper into the philosophical context of the game.

Closing this repeated written review, the message and essence of the game loses itself in vague dialoges and events. In the "silence ending" the author springles a question of perspectivism all of a sudden and tumbles into a real of obscure imagery - which I am a fan of but this game is trying to achieve everything in a super-tight playtime and ends without making a clear point. Not that is not to be recognizable by reflecting upon the events occuring in the game but it simply fails to be conrete.
Furthermore, the dialoge is striking to be pretty odd as the protagonists joke naively despite their absurd situation in which theyre lost in. albeit it can be reasoned by explaining it with certain points in the story but it leaves a muddy feeling in the mouth whilst playing.

In the same sense the story and its lore also does fall into the traphole of becoming absurd in what it is trying to dipict. On the other hand there is a certain red line in the contrary which can be uncovered by assembling key points together and reflecting about them. Obscure, nonetheless.

As for my final point before closing the review which I had to write again: The OST entails the goal of adding a sense of profanity to the surrealism one encounters in the game yet the white static and shrill sounds only provoke a sense of annoyance after 10 minutes. Omori and Undertale manage to convey the meaning of the scenes in their sound without losing its intergreity, this game should take an example of that.

All in all, this game is a ?????/10.
Jk, 6/10, despite me sounding very negative I extracted lots of enjoyment from the artstyle and trying to peace the message together, I recommend this if you got time to beat to death!\t\t\t\t\t
