\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is, overall, a pretty good game. So why can't I reccomend it? Difficulty spikes. The game, as currently balanced, has points where no matter your gear, you have to proceed through multiple enemy encounters without a single mistake. And that would be okay if the category of mistake didn't include "RNG decided the attack that normally hitstuns an enemy isn't going to this time, but the hitstun animation plays". The animation locks and generous input buffering break down to put you in situations where you can't avoid taking a hit, and enemies hitstun is much more reliable, and they can 2 shot you. It leads to a game with severely frustrating areas that block progress and make it very hard to git gud - the indicators for a safe hit and an unsafe one are identical.

Otherwise, this is a massive step up from the developers previous titles, I don't regret having bought it, and I hope they can tweak damage a bit, but I'm looking forward to what they do next. Soulsborne die hards should enjoy quite a bit, even if it's not quite up there with From's series.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't write many reviews, as I tend to go overboard. However, I want to write this up for people on the fence for this game. Hopefully I'll help someone out as to whether or not this game is for them.

So let's get the basics out of the way first. People are going to compare The Surge to Dark Souls and the like. This is a fair comparison, and I understand why they do it. However, the game plays a little more like Bloodborne than Dark Souls. It encourages you to be aggressive. You build up energy which enables you do spend that on things like consumables, executions and etcetera. It has a dash dodge rathen a roll dodge. You gather tech scrap (souls) to upgrade you equipment and your characters power core. You can also lose said scrap if you die on your way to recover it.

If you're still intrigued then let's move on. But let's do a little history session first.

Back when the original DOOM came out there was no FPS. And whether you want to believe it or not, it was not classified as one. Instead it helped kickstart the sub-genre of action games to become what we now know as FPS. So why am I saying all this? Because of the rise of the Dark Souls series, we now have another DOOM like situation. There is no official term for this sub-genre but the popular verbiage is Souls-like. With games like Lords of the Fallen, Salt and Sanctuary and now The Surge, a new sub-genre of Action RPG is emerging. And just like not all FPS games are the same, this applies here too. These games share the basic concepts of Dark Souls, but usually have their own twist on it.

What I Liked

I didn't realize steam reviews have a character limit, so let me bullet-list my positives of the game:

  • The combat has greatly improved since Lords of the Fallen.
  • The levels are intricate with lots of hidden and not-so-hidden shortcuts and pathways.
  • It's modern sci-fi. The world is believable and I'm a sucker for that kind of stuff.
  • The emphasis on the normal mobs instead of tons of bosses is not a negative for me.
  • Getting schematics by lopping off limbs and other body parts is a great feature.
  • Having a combo system (although not very intricate) feels cool and adds a layer of depth to the combat.
  • The game is well optimized for PC (ymmv - my pc specs at the bottom) and has a ton of menu options.
  • The game looks and plays great. No more LotF clunkiness and sluggishness.
  • Lots of build options via implants within the game.
  • NG+ and on are a lot of fun due to "new" enemies and such.

Things That May or May Not Bother You

Now for the "juicy" parts. Hopefully I can help someone out there decide if this is a Buy at Full Price, Wait for a Sale, or Skip. This might be a wall of text so strap yourself in.

Let's start off light. There is an aspect of the blocking system that the game teaches you early on that I have a problem with. It involves holding the block button and then pushing the right stick up (to jump up) or down (to duck down). I only have a problem with this because it is weakly implemented and honestly doesn't even need to be in the game. If you want to duck first and then jump the second attack (or vice versa) you have to basically complete the first animation and then press block again, and then you the second action. If I could just hold the block button the whole time and switch between the two it would be much better. However, there are more enemies that attack too quickly to even think about doing this in any practical way. This should either be removed or reworked to make it a more viable option.

Second, the level design can put people off during their first playthrough. I understand this, and respect this opinion when people state it. My NG+ playthrough was much more enjoyable than my first since I knew the lay of the land. But this shouldn't be the case. I fear people will be too frustrated with the game afterwards to even think about doing NG+ and on. You can and will get lost at times. The levels can be a big web of tunnels, areas and shortcuts. And it's sad because I feel the game is even better once you get to the NG+'s.

The "lack of variety" in the areas can put some people off. I got over this quickly by putting myself into the mindset that I'm walking around a huge facility that people built and worked in. It's only natural that environments like this can look the same because of this. But I get why people don't like it.

I can understand the frustration with the weapon proficiency system. I can see why people feel shoe-horned into a certain weapon type. This can lead to people getting burnt out by using the same type of weapon for the whole game instead of exploring different types. BUT, I feel the system is necessary for NG+4 and on so you can still get incrimental doses of attack power after upgrading equipment to the max Mk (Being Mk. XX). And after I got a lot more comfortable with the combat it wasn't so bad to get different weapon types up to snuff, even in NG+. But most people will not want to do this, which is understandable.

The story isn't the best around. Don't expect Dark Souls series intricate webs of stories here. But if you don't care then it's a great game to kill stuff in nevertheless.

There are atleast two parts of the game that have a big spike of difficulty. It can be frustrating to some people. But it needs to be brought up because of the nature of the situation. But, just like the level design, I felt everything was fine and fair once I got into NG+. They could have done with a more gradual increase of difficulty much like Dark Souls however. I think this is caused by the emphasis on the normal enemies rather than the bosses. As each one almost feels like a mini-boss most of the time. Not to be confused with "bullet-sponge" enemies however. As these are not.

Upgrading equipment can get VERY expensive. Had I known this going into NG+ I would have upgraded everything to (almost) max level before moving on. Once in NG+ you can not upgrade from say, Mk. II to Mk. V. It forces you to upgrade directly to Mk. VI. And it accounts for the costs it would take (scrap wise) to do so. So going into NG+ and wanting to upgrade a set of armor I stopped using earlier to Mk. VI was super expensive.

Final thoughts

Overall, I enjoy playing The Surge. Sure it has it's problems. But it's the best Souls-like to come out in my opinion. I can only get so far into Lords of the Fallen before getting bored. I'm not big into the 2-D attempts like Salt and Sactuary. But maybe I'm just burnt out on 2-D games as I grew up with the NES/SNES/Genesis.

Look, all in all, if you're a fan of Dark Souls, don't go in with that mindset. This is definitely it's own game within this new sub-genre of games. It brings with it new ideas to keep it fresh enough. And it borrows ideas from the best to keep it familiar. The game plays more like Bloodborne with it's encouragement to be aggressive due to the energy system. But it's also mixed with Lords of the Fallen, just more polished and way less sluggish. It feels weighty, but in a good way this time. And at the end of the day, man does it feel damn good to rip off the arm of an enemy that's been giving you a hard time.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's almost good; it has a lot of potential, but doesn't deliver. Maybe their next attempt at a soulsbourne-like will be good. Or maybe my standards are too high from other soulsborne games.

The intro was a neat idea, but didn't accomplish anything; being able to walk (and hop a little) was not significant. This guy was on an empty bus, walked into an empty (except for some security guards, presumably preventing him from running) building; of course it was a terrible idea.

The main character is a non-silent protagonist, but he's extremely boring. The whole point of a non-silent protagonist is to have an interesting character! And if the main character is so boring and non-essential to the game, why not have character creation?

Similarly, character customization is almost non-existent. "Leveling up" just opens up the number of "equipment" slots that you get (1 every ~10 levels). A slot might be used to give you 3 heals ... or +30 max health.

Enemies and fights are very boring; they are nearly identical with simple patterns (and some poorly telegraphed moves). Perhaps to compensate (and artifically increase difficulty), dodging/avoiding attacks is rather unfair. I often got hit because dodges seems to have no i-frames; to compensate, I just baited out their (almost-identical) attacks, hit the enemy twice, then dodge away, repeat. The same strategy for every enemy. Two enemies at once means run or die.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game tries its hardest to be an enjoyable experience but falls too short in too many areas for me to recommend it to anyone. The visuals are fine, the setting is interesting, and it comes close to a strongly satisfying core gameplay but the biggest problem is that it comes close but never goes further.

The proficency system asks you to choose one type of weapon and stick with it for the rest of the game unless you want to have a good ol' time grinding enemies and this wouldn't be a problem if the depth of the weaponry was more than it is, but unfortunately it feels shallow. Most enemies have a specific pattern that falls into "slow guy with big thing" "fast guy with two stabby things", "medium slowish guy with a big stabby thing" or "robot whose AI can be abused by circling to the right" so most of the in-game encounters settle into using one combo repeatedly except for the rare encounters which warrant more thought.

On top of this the game has a problem with hitboxes (I have not checked any other reviews this is personal experience only) and it throws enemies at you that use poorly explained/telegraphed moves, which in itself isn't the worst thing in the world, but just adds to the mess of fights that had potential for stand out moments.
The biggest thing I feel also needs to be pointed out is that the developers of this game love to talk about the similarities between their games and the "soulsborne" series and honestly it is an unfair comparison they are forcing on themselves, certain game mechanics aside the pacing and battle to battle experience for this game and Lords of the Fallen are much more "duel" focused as any individual enemy can kill you in 2-3 hits with almost any of their attacks and the progression of your strength never really feels significant.
Also a few quick hit dissapointments before ending this:

-very few bosses, I won't say a specific number for anyone looking to play but honestly for how lackluster the bosses are I expected there to at least be more of them

-lack of main character personality, if you are going to go with a narrative where the player character has a given name and presumably a story to why they ended up in a nightmare scenario, why not use that

-I already touched on this but I just have to mention it again, why are there so few options for weapon types and literally no variety in combos within those weapon types

-The backtracking element, this is pretty minor since most of the backtracking is non-essential and the primary story backtracking is fairly like/non-obnoxious, but it honestly just felt unnecessary

I do wish I could recommend this game because despite all the negativity in the review it does come close to being a very enjoyable if flawed experience and I was excited to see how a sci fi take on the slower paced action rpg genre that has become popular in the past few years would play, but it falls too short in too many different places.

Auto-Save feature from developers is in the game and uses usual hot-keys (F5 and F8). However, this simple feature breaks the entire game if used in the wrong time as it did for me. This means after investing hours of hours, you encounter this bug and can start from 0. Though known this is not even mentioned by the developers one week after release. This is the most sloppy bug I have ever encountered in a game. I (personally) will not be able to recommend this game for this fact alone.

(if you read this past the first patch, you might discard the above comment but for me it ruined the initially good experience of the game)

However, here is the full review:

- Graphics -
The quality is top notch. However, the variety is almost non-existent, despite some refreshing exceptions when for example in Area 3 a lift lets you revisit the surface again after hours of tunnel grinding. There is also not enough variety or customization in armor or weapon design to keep you going much longer than the first play-through.

- Music -
Other than in your home base, there is not much music in this game. You have the occasional song that plays in boss fights or tight situations but you will notice that for keeping the mood, the game designers decided to keep music at a minimum. For me personally this works very well with the overall theme of the game.

- Sound -
Like graphics, the sound quality is top notch but due to the lack of variety, you will have heard it all very soon.

- Gameplay -
As many probably will notice, this is the strong point of the surge and will carry you through the game. Again you could wish for a bit more variety, especially in encounter designs or ways to engage enemies but for what is in the game, it is a good start for further projects from Deck 13. There are many mechanics in this game that you will discover only later in the game and with enough time invested. Some might need to be fleshed out more but overall there is more to it than you can initially grasp.\t\t\t\t\t
