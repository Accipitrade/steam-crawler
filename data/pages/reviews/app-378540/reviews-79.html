\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWoah, did I actually think I had already reviewed this game!

Easy answer, get it!

People like to give a lot of [insert excrement analogy here] over their previous title; Lords of the Fallen, that it was a poorly thought out Dark Souls clone.
But the Surge isn't that game. It's a vast improvment on their previous game, with a more unique setting.
With their improvment between these two games, I am excited about The Surge 2, which is around the corner.

The combat has a good mixture of both a sense of weight and responsivness.
Your attacks will feel like they have a impact, with very smooth and easy to understand controls.
Learn the patterns and cake walk over enemies that previously had you dying over and over.

It has less focus on bosses like dark souls, but all of the "minions" provide a significant threat if you don't know their pattern - or you just screw up and end up with no stamina and are royally stuck in the mud for a very damaging swing.
This is subjective weather it was better or worse as many would very much enjoy more big and stompy enemies.
Personaly, I like the focus on making "trash mobs" actually be a threat rather than throwaway garbage. But, I would absolutely not mind more bosses like the ones we got in this game.

There is an overwhelming amount of possitives I could say about this game.
I'd meassure it in roughly 90% of a perfect game.

What are the downsides?
* Well, like most Souls games, it can feel a bit grindy at times. You might need to go and mindlessly kill the average mobs to get enough currency to upgrade your power to wield the gear that you would like.
* And, I will not spoil anything, but the ending feelt a little... meh. Granted, the buildup was really good.

Conclusion:
I'd be hard pressed to say that it is a perfect games. Combat is objectivley good, the style is subjective, story is subjective.
But in the overal sense, I 100% got a game that I was glad I bought.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNot a good game to get a fill on souls-borne style gameplay. Unless you like the sci-fi setting a lot, there isn't much in the game that would appeal to traditional fans of the genre.

Pros
  • Visual design is pretty solid, at least from aesthetics. Some of the armor sets look quite neat. I'll get into the issues with how the game sets itself up later, though.
  • The gimmick by which you gain new pieces of equipment is cool. I'd say in another game, or the sequel, it'd be worth exploring the idea further and in a way that gels better with general playability.
  • Small convenience features. Now, this is certainly something that would be more of a middling or perhaps con for a hard-core "git gud" style soulsborne player, but having the ability to bank your salvage does mean it's easier to preserve progress when it comes to collecting resources. All things considered, the game is pretty good at limiting the amount of farming that's required and so it doesn't usually feel like it's deliberately wasting your time. Contrast to even DS3, where some pieces of armor or weapons are only attainable through random drops from specific enemies.

Cons
  • If you were expecting to customize your character to any great degree you'd be disappointed. Props for taking a unique approach of making the protagonist parapalegic, but other than that you'll only be playing a pre-set white middle-aged male with a single voice. If customization isn't your thing anyway, all the more power to you, but then I'd imagine Sekiro would be a way better option for a game regardless. The most choice you get with this game boils down to what jumpsuit you wear, and character builds - and even on this front it doesn't satisfy, because all this really ranges between is 'lightweight low-endurance-cost melee' and 'high-endurance-cost counterattack melee'. if the appeal of this game style was playing as any gender, cosplay, or monstrosity you can think of, then it's best to look elsewhere.
  • So while the visual design is solid, the visual setup for UI and level design is generally awful. Tutorial messages are 'immersively' placed as floating text in the world (making them harder to read and easier to miss with how many moving elements are on screen), and it generally is difficult to tell which direction the game is intending you to go to make progress, versus which directions just have side-paths or NPCs. This was the number one reason I stopped playing, because after I reached the apparent hub-area after the first boss I had almost no idea where I was meant to go, and at one point had interrupted some NPC dialog because I had wandered into some strange loaded area with a cowboy voice in it (I think that's DLC). It's not a good environment for teaching players how to tackle challenges via practice.
  • The control scheme! This is another big one: A huge draw of the Soulsborne genre is that, while the games are tough, the control scheme has almost universally been extremely simple: important actions such as attacking, blocking, dodging, changing targets, so forth, are all reachable through single-button or single-input commands: easy to play at its core. Not so in The Surge: It requires a combination of a shoulder-button hold and stick movement to change targets (otherwise you just change what part of the enemy's body you're hitting) which can make group fights hairier than necessary. There's also mechanics of ducking and jumping while blocking, which is really its own can of worms:
  • Too much reliance on arbitrary scripting: more complex enemies can only be fought in one way, and this is a massive downfall especially once you start getting deeper into the game. Ducking and Jumping start becoming necessary to counterattack specific enemies (in the hub for example, one enemy is a maintenance bot that's shielded from the front, and swings a huge arm at you either high or low.) While it's readable (assuming the player even realizes ducking and jumping are options), it's still an action you need to deliberate in order to win. This flaw also shows through in spades during the first boss, where the way to make it vulnerable is obvious (make it hit itself with missiles) but in practice performing the motions causes a lot of mess - even when successfully performing this action I still was taking damage from the blast radius, and had no idea how to avoid this. And of course, to build up to the point where the boss would fire missiles again was an entire fight cycle I'd need to reperform all over again to activate, so in the end beating it felt more like I was just lucky rather than nailing the mechanics or timing of the enemy.
    If you look up any boss guide for Dark Souls there will be at least 3 different strategies on how to defeat them. They're challenging because they just are, from attack timing to hit areas. It's not because the enemies pipeline you into using very specific mechanics to defeat them, some of which aren't reliably reproducible.
  • While it's viable to make any weapon usable through upgrading, it's questionably not even necessary or useful, because each weapon type has the exact same mechanics and range at its core. Really the only reason for the existence of variances is to activate different set bonuses, because there's otherwise no variance in range or timing between all the different dual-blades, hammers, or claw things. It ties into the 'scripted' feel of the game, because oftentimes it's quite possible to have a shorter model still land a hit that looks like it really shouldn't, or the opposite.
    Speaking of set bonuses, they're kind of hard to read. Equipping a full set displays a buff icon, but it isn't immediately clear as to how it helps. I had to read a wiki to learn exactly what it meant when a symbol appeared after equipping a full Goliath set.

Overall, the experience is frustrating playing this game. It offers the player few options for overcoming challenges, game mechanics that overly-complicate themselves that make its learning curve unsatisfying to climb, and creates situations that lack effective visual feedback, often confusing the player with unexpected results in fights.
The game would have been better either by leaning more into its genre basis by simplifying its core controls and designing a wider berth of playstyles, or by leaning harder into its own unique take and foregoing features such as using death as a mechanic or railroading the player only to horizontal movement. As is, however, the final result of The Surge is simply not fun to play.\t\t\t\t\t
