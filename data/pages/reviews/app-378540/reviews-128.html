\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're a Darksouls hardcore kinda player this is perfect for you. However, the bosses are insanely hard. Having beaten Darksouls 1 and 2 I can personally say the bosses in this are sadistic in comparison. While in DS bosses are complex enemies that need to be understood to be defeated, bosses in The Surge offer no such niceness. They're extremely tough, taking tremendous damage and they have no explanation as to how to damage them in any special way. From the two I encountered they take virutally no damage and virtually insta-kill on contact. While the cannonfodder combat is easier and actually more enjoyable than Dark Souls, the bosses are crueler and counter act any improvement over DS with serverely unenjoyable boss battles. If I had more than 2 hours to try the game before steam would cut off my return option I would try to delve deeper into this, but sadly that's all I'm allotted before I get stuck with a game I might not enjoy. To be clear, the exploration and grind of The Surge is terrific. However the boss battles ruin the entire experience for me, there is no explanation as to how to defeat a boss, not even hints in the gameplay. They do tremendous damage to you and seem to take none, this isn't fun it's just brutal. I presume however the typical person looking into The Surge is a Dark Souls player, most DS players are way more into slamming their head into a wall repeatedly than I am, if you are that kind of player then this is for you, hopefully you'll figure out the secret to the bosses because the rest of the game is awesome.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was a very enjoyable game that I finished in about 2 sittings, it doesn't feel as polished and the areas aren't as nice as dark souls but I think the melee combat is alot more fun. It feels really good flying in out of combat instead of that awkward rolling on the floor, the weapons are actually well balanced with heavy ones having high stunlock and damage, while the fast ones have fast attacks that you can weave in easier. There are some really cool weapons aswell like fire shooting duel blades and an axe that shoots out energy blasts (my personal favourite).

The game works waaaaay better on a mouse and keyboard than dark souls does, I actually prefer the mouse and keyboard to the controller for this game which means alot since this is a 3rd person action game.

The story is more in your face than dark souls and it has a really well done moment with the presenter hacket later on, everything in the office area is well done actually. I don't quite get why they didn't add character creation (I myself don't really care) because alot of people really love it and the story definitely didn't need you as a set character at all.

The maps can get rather confusing especially in some areas where everywhere can look the same, but it has alot of cool shortcuts build in all over the map which feel cool to unlock.

The cutting off of limbs is a really cool aspect, much much better than the random rng dropping in dark souls. Any piece of equipment you want you just cut off and take, feels great (always annoys me in games where you can't loot equipment that is just sitting there). There are enough different enemies to keep you interested along the way but they do reskin a couple of them unfortunately.

Just try out the demo and if you like it alot like I did then give the game a shot because it is alot of fun.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: I picked up and played this game when it was at full price, it was worth it then. Now that the price is reduced it's totally worth it. Its one of the few games that can boast that they're in the same genre as Dark Souls. The combat is similarly slow and deliberate. That said, that's just the skeleton.

The game makes both successes and failures beyond that point. I'd say 90% of the gameplay decisions are interesting, unique, and fun to play. There are definitely parts of the game that creak a bit, but the core game is great. Do note that mechanically I wish they made more varied weapons with a higher weapons scaling level. The equips can only be upgraded a few times, but theoretically your stats can go up forever. So logic dictates you need a high scaling weapon... which are very limited (one or two in each of a variety of weapon types), functionally deeply restricting weaponry as the game goes on.

The primary issue that I had with the game was involvement. The plot slowly unravels in an intriguing way, but the information that it reveals is never revelatory. Of course the megacorp is making decisions that ignore the value of human life... duh. That's really the only extended revelation in all the audiologs. They do have interesting tones and personal stories, but they ultimately arent worth anything beyond the quality of the voice actor's delivery. A few of those voice actors do a real good job and a few do... less well.

I also do not recall the music at all. That may be a failing of my memory, or it was mostly just ambiance.

Summary of my experience:
I enjoyed the introduction and began learning the system, after a few stumbles got rolling along with smooth progress.
Slammed my head against being killed repeatedly by a certain hostile environment early on... I mistakenly thought I was supposed to push on like I did in DS's swamps... wasted time and effort on the dead end with minor equip.
Gained full command of the combat system and built my character up over the next few hours.
Tore apart my first few robot dogs... I'd come to get very good at that.
Made steady progress through new environs and enemies.
Slammed into a brick wall of a boss in the form of one of the hammer bros.
Tried repeatedly
Gave up and ran off to level up more
Came back and tried till giving up
Returned and immediately succeeded with patience and crystalized tactics.
Was rewarded with somewhat vague plot reveal information
Continued to push through
Something about 'nanomachines' (insert MGS meme)
Ended up stopping after just getting lost as to where to go to progress.
Didn't feel like coming back any time soon
Uninstall
... months later
Writes review and makes mental note to reinstall and finish it off.

Essentially the combat becomes repetitive, the plot banal, and while the environs are intricately connected in a manner similar to dark souls it lacks any 'weanies' (things like a castle in the distance you're making progress toward). Your movement and progress has no indication as a result. Expect areas to be connected by deeply similar jefferies tubes, loading screen subways, and hallways that end up being increasingly disorienting even for someone who has been gaming for years and has a pretty strong mental map. There are more interesting environments that are definitely memorable, but their connections are often baffling and serpentine with quick turns and multiple elevators leading to separate branches. All in a 'jeffereies tube' tileset that despirately needs more variation or large signage.

Hopefully this has been informative as to some of the mechanical aspects of this game and how they work together. If you can put up with those flaws its definitely fun and worth the reduced price. :)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDark Souls, but in the FUTURE!

That's a simple way to describe the game.

The game isn't without its faults. It's brutally hard at the beginning. It takes a lot of practice to get your weapons timing down. The game insists you stick with one weapon type through the game, because your damage is based on how much you use it. Switching weapon types late in the game is a recipie for disaster unless you plan on spending too much time grinding. There's only 5 bosses compared to Dark Souls 5 billion, and each has a "special" way to defeat them that gets you a fancy weapon, but the game makes no hint that defeating the bosses a certain way would give you anything special. The maps are crazy confusing at times, with shortcuts within shortcuts, making it very easy to get lost, and the maps are mostly quite similar.

So why a positive review? I had an absolute blast playing it. In the beginning I was getting mopped by basic enemies, and by the end I was mopping them up. The game punishes you hard for messing up and overcomitting like Dark Souls would, but if you're cautious you can pull through. Every fight at first feels like a mini-boss battle. The system of getting armor and weapons is a little weird at first, but as soon as you see a new enemy with a weapon or armor you haven't seen, your first instinct is chop off all their limbs so you can play with their toys.

Overall, I personally had a ton of fun with this, even with its brutal difficulty. It could defenitly use more polish, more bosses, more maps, more everything.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs long as you do not go into this game expecting the depth and breadth of the Dark Souls series, it is a wonderful experience that is in my top 10 games of 2017.

The Surge has a setting that feels fresh and unique in it's genre. The visual and audio effects are, in my opinion, great. While the setting and lore feel wonderful, the story is a bit lacking. It's definitely not a bad tale, I just wish that the world and characters were expanded upon and explored more deeply. Combat isn't as nuanced and complex as other games in the series, but it is a far cry from 'bad'. I've never felt cheated, or that the game's combat treated me unfairly. Moves are snappy and feel great. On top of all of that, I'm enjoying the break from the normal fantasy-genre games.

Overall, I'd conclude that not every game has to top it's predecessors. Some games aren't the best of the best, but they're good. And in this day-and-age, I am so happy to play the 'good ones'.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed the hell out of this game. Once I started playing I pretty much spent every free moment I had playing it until I beat my first playthrough - and now I'm gonna go ahead and start my second playthrough.

Part 1: Souls-Like!

Lot of games coming out with this label and I can only hope they're all this good. I've played the entire souls series and I see the things I like from there in this game. Lemme go through what you'll find here that you also found in Dark Soul.

- Deliberate, measured combat with impact. This game actually rivals any souls game in this regard as even the lighter weapons feel like your beating someone over the head with a lead pipe. There's no gracefulness here just brutal beat downs. The way your character attacks feels a bit clunky, but that makes sense since you're not a warrior using weapons of war , you're a factory worker using tools. It should be a bit awkard, but that awkwardness actually makes it more engaging as you have to work with the specific rythm of each weapon's attacks. As far as pacing goes, they managed to get about the speed of DS3, with even more weight than DS1.


- Shortcut Porn. This game is crazy with shortcuts and sometimes you're amazed to find yourself back at your hub at the beginning of the level when you thought you must have been miles away. Boss shortcuts in particular are fantastic and often lead you from your hub directly to the boss with only a handful of enemies to run past. Progress in this game is measured in shortcuts.


- Limited but powerful healing. Just like estus, your self heals will heal a lot very quickly, but you have a limited number of them and things can get dicey when you're not really sure where you are, you don't know how soon to the next shortcut, and you just ran out of heals. Being careful and proceeding slowly are important when exploring new areas. You have some choices here though with the implant system: load up on heals, or get other stuff instead.


Part 2: Beyond Souls-Like.

So this game brings some new things to the game that really adds to the gamplay, or does some things better than a souls game.

- Promoting aggression. The energy system, which is simply a bar that fill up as you hit enemies more, rewards you for fighting aggressively rather than passively. You can use that energy to activate various attacks or self buffs that can have a big impact on the fight.

- Upgrade system. Implants as they're known in the game provide you with a variety of options when it comes to passive buffs and active effects. For one, Implants are where you heals ( estus ) comes from, so it's up to you just how many heals you venture out with. Maybe instead you'd like to have a truckload of hit points? Or maybe you'd like to be able to burn energy to do a bunch more damage? Maybe you'd prefer healing-over-time instead of estus style burst heals? Or maybe you'd like to use your implants to improve your core power so you can equip heavier rig armor? There's a good amount of options here to play around with and I found myself switching out implants to deal with various situations.

- Limb Targeting. In combat you can target specific areas of the opponent ( head, legs, arms, body ) There are two considerations her. On the one hand, targeting areas that are unarmored will do more damage. On the other hand, beating on armored areas makes it more likely that you can rip them off, and ripping off limbs is how you get new recipes to craft different kinds of armor, as well as the crafting materials to build and upgrade armor. The system stayed relevant throughout my entire playthrough and it adds a few seconds to the start of every battle as you check out which areas are armored and decide if you want to go for the mats or the faster kill.


Part 3: Things I didn't like.

Won't lie this is gonna be a short list and the entries are gonna be pretty shallow.

- It crashed a few times. Nobody likes that!

- The targeting betrayed me at one point by locking on when I didn't want to which cause my camera to do things I didn't want it to do.. but that was easily solved by turning off auto-targeting on attack. Told you the dislike list was gonna suck.

- Some things are unexplained, like why does everyone burst into flames when they die? The answer is: so the game would run better by not having to render defeated enemies. An in-game lore explanation would have been nice.

- One enemy type has an attack that looks kind of silly.\t\t\t\t\t
