\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPersistence is key in this game, because you'll die a lot. It is a souls-like, after all, and my introduction to this sort of challenging games. Also, I always wanted a souls-like set in the future. Now we got one. If you can overlook the obvious fact that most enemies don't use ranged weapons (are there no pistols and/or rifles left anymore?) then the setting is quite solid, too. You get to traverse intricately designed levels that get you the feeling that you really travel through some sort of megacorporation headquarters. Production facility, waste disposal and security wing included, all the way up to the executive forum where the board members make their decisions. On the way you get to meet and chop up a lot of enemies with a set of diverse moves that you need to anticipate and dodge quickly or block. These enemies also fit into the corporate theme. Crazed workers, welding or maintenance bots, security personel and hazard disposal teams. No monsters or weird creatures. Instead, everything is in accordance with the setting. The human enemies can give you their armour, priovided you can cut off the body part that you need. Same goes for weapons. However, after harvesting said body part, you only get a schematic. To equip the body part, you need to construct it first. And for that, you require an ops station, sort of a safe haven for you. Most levels have one. Here you can return to heal, to construct gear as well as to upgrade your core level and equip new rigs. I'll explain the latter one first, because this is another idea that I really enjoyed. You see, there are no skills in this game. Instead, there are implants that you can equip your character with. Some give you more health, more energy (required for finishing/chopping moves) or more stamina (for sprinting, attacking and blocking), others are healing items or give your weapon elemental damage. These implants can be found all around the playable world, which animates you to explore each level to the fullest. How many and which ones you can equip, is determined by two factors. First, the number of available slots. You start with eight, and can go up to 16. That's dependant on the rig you use. The rig is kinda like the exoskeleton, where all body parts and all implants are mounted onto. And the second thing that it's dependant on is the so called core power. That's the equivalent of a level. Also it is the capacity of the rig. The implants you equip cannot consume more core power than is available to you. With few exceptions. So if you have a core power of 10, for example, you can only equip implants with a total capacity of 10. At core power 100...you get the idea. To increase your core power you need to collect scrap that you get from defeated enemies. However, if an enemy defeats you, you respawn at the nearest ops station and your scrap is gone...provided, you reach the place where you died in a specified amount of time. If you do, your scrap can be reclaimed. The sum of these rather innovative gameplay elements was what made this game so exciting for me, coupled with a difficulty curve that is steep but challenging and never feels unfair until you fight the black cerberus and his 5! robot companions in succession, each of them equalling the very first boss. So instead of one boss, you fight a total of six in this encounter. Oh, and while you fight the robots, black cerberus heals, so if you take too long, you'll encounter a fully invigorated enemy each time you dispose of a robot . You die, you learn, you try again, you die a bit later, you learn, you try again and at one point you manage to defeat the enemy and move on. This souls-like principle applies to The Surge as well. Buy it, if you like this sort of games, and don't be deterred by the setting. The game mechanics and the level design make up for it totally!

The few points of critique I'd have would be that you don't get to experience much of the story, except through audiologs that can be found through exploration. I'd have loved for the game to tell you more about what's going on around you and in more ways. Audiologs and sparse bits of conversation aren't enough for my taste. Also, the few sidequests feel a bit unnecessary. You don't really get an indication what to do for what person and what the reward would be. I, for my part, didn't bother to complete a single one. Beat the game anyway so the reward couldn't have been that important. And the last bit of critique is the ending, that left quite a sour taste in my mouth.

To sum it up, this is a rare case where the gameplay, game mechanics as well as the challenge more than make up for the shallow story, in as much as I didn't really need one to be motivated to beat this game. The challenge was a driving force by itself in this case.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs I played The Surge, and my initial curiosity was sated, I had to constantly try to convince myself to boot up the game. After a while, I simply failed, which is a shame since the game really does have some good moments. However, at the end of the day, it unfortunately feels a lot like a combat, lore, and environment-wise subpar souls clone.

Everything starts with an interesting starting sequence. The main character, Warren, is disabled and cant walk at the start of the game. My hopes were up as the disability was revealed to the player in a creative camera shot as the wheelchair was cleverly hidden in the starting metro ride, with Warren‚Äôs upper torso only visible. When I was given controls and Warren moved his wheelchair, I was of course surprised. It made me feel like this would go to a unique place. Oh, maybe I would run away from whatever was about to happen in the wheelchair! Or perhaps I would learn how this happened to him to flesh out the character.

Instead, I moved through a cordoned off corridor to an ‚Äúenlisting‚Äù procedure, with barely any human interaction. I guess learning that the company takes on disabled people and kits them out with exoskeletons was very interesting, and there was I believe even a large TV talking about that and other values of the company. However, I barely had any time to appreciate any of that as Warren was thrown into some anaesthesia malfunctioned automated torture-surgery, blacked out, and then woke up in some hellscape of a company ruins with no fanfare, explanation etc. The problem is that this all happened in like 5 minutes, not that it wasnt interesting.

And I mean that is fine. Dark Souls essentially does the same thing, if anything this game showed me more of what happened before ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô• went oh so very wrong. But by not having a more direct story lead you through the game, you need the environments, the combat, and the mystery of it all motivate you instead. Unfortunately The Surge doesn‚Äôt really excel at this similar to how Dark Souls does.

First of all, the environments were somewhat monotonous, made up of the same dull colours and tight corridors all the time without needing to be (it also runs horribly on my high-ish end PC despite being an old game. 60 FPS only :/). It all honestly feels like trying to navigate a labyrinth, you get lost so often that I feel like the game had a minimap at first or something. And in all these labyrinths, there are some places that I am obviously (and sometimes frustratingly not so obviously like the one in the starting level) meant to backtrack to once I get a higher core power, game progression drone upgrade, etc. Like it‚Äôs all so confusing to navigate from the get go, with very little reward for exploration outside of actually being able to progress. There is a difference between a nice, initially jarring but still fun to puzzle out level, versus a giant hedge maze. This game feels like the latter at times. By contrast, DS has varied environments that not only hint at a rich lore, but also has things like landmarks, meaningful shortcuts (instead of OH LOOK WE DID THE DS THING!!), COLOUR, and CONTRAST between environments. When I am having trouble seeing and differentiating things in The Surge, I am just playing the game. When the same happens in DS, I am in some notorious level that is meant to kick my ass and frighten me since it contrasts so much with the rest of the game.

The environments did have some basic nice things like showing me something I can loot early in an environment so that I can figure out how to get to it. Later on, however, it became apparent to me that some was only reachable once I unlocked something later on in the game and backtracked, at which point I lost some motivation to solve things while actually playing through the game. Actually, I might have just not looked hard enough, but this I think is largely justifiable because:

There is very little combat variety. The core system is solid (except for when you cant see well and someone swings at you, chugging 50% of your HP), with an interesting limb cutting mechanic. There is a parry-ish mechanic (not much stagger), dodging, stamina management, light & heavy attack combos etc. However, there are only a couple of types of weapons and when you get a new variant of a weapon, it just does more damage. Also, the lock-on systems that make limb cutting possible encourage 1v1 encounters. Enemies do a whole lot of damage so that also encourages 1v1. However, enemy variety is low, and the fact that there is less 1vX drives the encounter variety even lower (since they cant design enemies specifically to swarm you etc.). The lock-on, although a nice gimmick, outside of targeting armored vs unarmored or the head for extra damage does not really add too much to the combat itself, it adds to the gear progression mostly as you must decide which robotic part to loot.

The weapon proficiency is kinda weird. It encourages using the same weapon non stop, but same proficiency doesnt really have real multiple weapons under it, there are just more weapons that have same moves etc. but more damage. In DS your build might favor for example DEX weapons, but that there are a ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô• ton of DEX weapons with different movesets and such in that game. For example both katanas and curved swords scale with DEX in DS. In here, the weapon classification itself has a proficiency and so you are just encouraged to play with the same thing all the time.

And well, the mystery of the game did not really captivate for long either. Warren as a character is just a flat stoic guy, the characters around sometimes pop up but are either somewhat generic, or dont really play a big role in the story. The bosses as far as I made it through the game did not really have any bearing on the story either. Like yeah okay everyone lost their minds. And the bosses are just bigger machines that also lost their minds? The environments, being the labyrinths that they are, did not really make me stop and wonder at any significant landmarks. Also like where the ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô• do these employees eat and ‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•‚ô•? Now that‚Äôs a mystery.

CREO World and the arena DLC though were top notch. It had great audio logs that built up the lore of the game wonderfully all the while telling its own stories. The software engineer dude and his crew was not a crazy good NPC story but it was still interesting, and the NPC actually interacted with you and formulated a plan with you for a change!

The enemies in CREO World lunge, they have ranged attacks, they gang up on you sometimes, and their design is overall interesting! There is a trick to beating all of them too. I wish more of the game was more like CREO World, I would have loved it.

As for the Arena, I just liked the debuff modifiers, especially the one that turned the game into an old school silent movie. The comments made by the Supervisor as he supervised and narrated your adventures in there were also a fun twist!

Starting off, learning the enemy movements and how to fight is really engaging and super Souls like.
But the emphasis on grinding in the game in order to advance is just way too much and is the reason I have to give this game a "thumbs down".

Also because of the Souls like combat and how unreliable enemy combat moves are, you'll need to heal quite a bit and "return to base" for healing, which will make all enemies re-spawn and thereby making advancing the storyline extremely frustrating. I ended up using a god mode hack to actually enjoy the story, but otherwise I would have honestly just uninstalled to avoid the frustration.

If you like endless grinding and repetition, by all means wait for the game to go on an 80% sale before you buy it.
