One of the best Souls-like games on the market.
Six years after its release and with the noticeably more crowded genre, 'The Surge' remains a surprisingly intriguing, satisfying, and original game worth checking out.

       In fact- during my recent comeback to the game to complete two story expansions (both really nice and worth picking the 'Augmented' edition of the game right away) I was slightly shocked at how well it holds up and how fresh it still appears and feels during gameplay, how good, responsive and satisfying movement and combat are, how its identity remains very strong without any other competitors that would try to challenge The Surge in this grounded sci-fi setting. And after a few years even the visual layer of the game, both on an artistic and technical level, remains really remarkable. Of course, time waits for no one and the game is getting older and assets are obviously not up to par with what's achievable now, but by no means it feels dated or stands out.
Models, textures, particle effects, animations... all look seriously good and there is a hard-to-describe subconscious feeling of 'this is a solid engine, matched nicely with this game, used by people that know what they are doing'. By that point, Deck13 had 16 years of experience in game development, and it's showing. That baggage of course includes a debut of the first proper souls-like title 'Lord of the Fallen' released in 2014, three years before 'The Surge'.
It's firmly above many modern games trying to find their way in this astonishing genre. From design and graphics of the elements of UI and HUD and matching audio (from combat sound effects to voice acting) through variety and amount of available weapons, armor, or enemy varieties, to the general worldbuilding, plot, and tone of the game- almost every building block of this game falls into the 'good' and 'great' categories without anything dragging down the experience. One has to focus on elements like lipsynching or assets when a player character faces the wall and as a consequence brings the camera very close to the model, to notice signs of the game's age.

   Just the mentioned design of armor or weapons is one of those things that still pop up and amuse, despite choosing a theme that, to put it bluntly, is not exactly uncommon. Designs are original and clever, even if the world might occasionally appear too utilitarian and generic on the surface when taking into consideration some environments and tilesets of various warehouse/factory/utility storeroom/futuristic workspace nature. Once you start playing the game it rarely stops being startling. From powered work loaders from Aliens to rejected designs from S.H.O.G.O or Metal Gear, the spectrum is wide enough to accommodate a lot of various tastes. After adding wildly different packages from both DLC expansions- nothing to complain about in this department.

   Overall it's done well and rephrasing this about every part is ultimately boring and not especially useful, so I'll just skip to things that stand out in a mixed way.
There's not a lot of it, and what's there is at best of second-grade importance or highly subjective. Like maps in the possession of the player. Granted, it's an issue most relevant when starting a new playthrough or going back cold and re-learning all the ropes, but nonetheless, I'd like to see some utility tool providing a bit of convenience, at least for a NG+. The game is well designed and there's a lot of signage in the world, including some maps as part of the environment, but so many games have spoiled players that despite the best intentions and valid approach of the designers, it would be nice to see at least some kind of POI tracker similar to one introduced in Dead Space games.
Even after beating the game I found myself occasionally wandering, taking wrong turns, forgetting shortcuts, or mistaking parts of the levels because of the lack of visual distinction. It becomes a minor issue after acclimatization and starting to pay attention to the environmental directions, but still it never fully goes away.
There's the aspect of a more relaxed fast travel system, but honestly never felt a need for it. Environments are tightly connected, often cramped, featuring many shortcuts, few bullet sponge enemies blocking pathways, and there's a tram system player can use to get around. Even finding DLC content is not an issue once you know from which sectors they can be accessed.
Second topic- ranged combat. I just love it and it absolutely has its place within this genre ('Remnant: From the Ashes'), even in titles very focused on melee (well, no need to search for examples with the Souls series in hand). The Surge technically does feature ranged attacks, but very limited, heavily regulated by timers, and disconnected from a player, as they're pretty much constrained to the abilities of the player drone, and some of the enemy NPC.
Nothing wrong with a focus on melee, but the sci-fi setting is perfectly suited for it, and seeing enemies with blasters is low-key infuriating because of how appealing and fun it looks. Well, maybe in Surge 3.
Besides that- I guess I'd complain about a bit too restrictive saving mechanics with too sparse autosave opportunities. Don't quit in the middle of nowhere, always go to saferooms (Operations rooms), and make sure the game is saving. The system is not very flexible.
The combat could be more gruesome? Well, it's just nitpicking at this point.

   The Surge is a really good action game and souls-like, that should make a fan of the genre happy. I would say within it it's already a bit of a classic and mandatory thing to get acquainted with. Satisfying combat and hero progression, an intriguing world, and a sense of adventure into a familiar yet strange world - all crucial components are well realized.
Deck13 did a great job focusing on its strengths and not spreading its resources too thin, ~25hrs runtime for the base game is just right and doesn't overstay its welcome.
Unless you're allergic to sci-fi or slightly grim dystopian visions of the future, I highly encourage giving this one a go, there's a lot of fun to be found here.
Helluva IP debut!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is a sci-fi souls-like that is heavily inspired by the first Dark Souls game, and while not perfect, will give fans of the genre an immersive, yet unique experience from the standard souls-like formula. I have been a fan of souls-like games since Dark Souls 1 came out, so this game definitely grabbed my attention, and executes the sci-fi spin excellently, with enough of it's own ideas to differentiate it from other games of the genre.

Let's start with the good.

- The world design both aesthetically, and mechanically, is amazing. Without spoiling anything, The Surge takes place in a part industrial/google campus from hell, where catastrophic events go down, and ♥♥♥♥ hits the fan. The entire game has an oppressive and lonely atmosphere, with a hint of creepy/horror, which really lends itself to the genre, and gives some of the feelings of desolation you get from the Soulsborne games. This game also has some of the best inter-connectivity I've ever seen since the original Dark Souls. You will be winding through paths and corridors, feeling like you're miles from the Medbay (see Bonfire), and open a locked door, only to find yourself right back at safety. No souls-like has ever nailed this vibe, even FromSoft's own games, but The Surge certainly comes close. If you miss this feeling of exploration, you will get constant dopamine hits throughout the game.

- The combat is unique enough to separate it from other souls-likes, and ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ is it a lot of fun. The gimmick here is that you can target individual parts of enemies, humanoid or otherwise, and by dismembering these armored limbs, you can get new gear and armor. This creates an awesome "make-your-own challenge," because you can instead target un-armored limbs, which are significantly easier to cut off and therefore kill enemies, but you will get no armor or crafting components. Combo this with the fact that there are awesome distinct finisher animations for left arms, right arms, left legs, right legs, torso and heads, that are unique to each weapon type , and I never got bored with the combat.

- Speaking of weapons and armor, I love the design of all them. Being that the game takes place in a giant industrial plant, most weapons are practical sci-fi tools like fusion cutters, chainsaws, exo-suit forklifts etc, which makes a lot of sense in a sci-fi setting. I was concerned the game wouldn't work with futuristic guns and lasers, but the game takes this great approach instead that totally works.

Now for some things that are hit or miss, and some players may find hard to get used to.

- Character progression is quite a bit different from your classic soulsborne RPG. Firstly, there is not character customization, and you play as Warren during his first day on the job at the company CREO. Gameplay-wise though, you do not level individual stats, like Strength or Dex or Endurance. Instead, all you can spend your scrap (see souls) on, is your exo-rig's reactor core, and upgrades for weapons/armor. Your reactor core works essentially like carry weight, and how much you have dictates what armor and augments you can carry. What are augments? Essentially where you get your stats and builds from. These are modular items you place in your exo-rig to get bonuses ranging from "Gain stamina when you hit an enemy," or "Increase energy gain after severing a limb," all the way to how many health injectables (see estus flask) you can carry. Overall I enjoy this system, as it is much different than leveling stats, but it does mean that you cannot increase your stamina for example at all until you find your first "stamina boost" augment.

- There is also a timer on your dropped scrap (again, see souls), which can certainly feel a bit frustrating at first. Overall, this was never really a problem, as it gives you ample amount of time to retrieve your dropped scrap, and killing enemies increases the timer. I do feel that this feature is sorta pointless, but like I said, I don't think I ever lost a meaningful amount of scrap due to it.

Finally, some things I felt were quite weak about the game.

- Although the combat in the grand scheme is excellent, I found it quite frustrating at certain points. Whereas in FromSoft games, 99.99% of my deaths I felt were due to my failings, whether not knowing attack patterns, or dodging poorly, and therefore I hardly ever feel frustrated in those games. The Surge knocks that down to about 80% non-frustrating deaths. There is a combo system in combat, which can sometimes lock you into the final hits of your combos, and the enemy staggering/poise system is incredibly difficult to figure out. Even after completing the entire game, I still can't really tell you how it works, just that sometimes enemies get staggered, and sometimes they don't. And when they don't, and you take damage because you were locked into an attack, it feels bad. Combine this with the fact that enemies are veryyyy bulky due to the armored limb system, and your character dies in about 3-4 hits depending on the enemy, it can feel like you die really quickly.

- The bosses. This game is very much structured opposite of most FromSoft games, where in those you have to wade through tons of trash enemies before an epic awe inspiring boss fight. In The Surge, it focuses more on interesting and explorable levels with mini-challenges being each enemy encounter, and bosses serving as more segue's into the next level. There are only about 6 bosses in the entire game, and vary from just okay, to downright dog-water. As far as aesthetic designs, they're all super cool looking, but mechanically, they are almost all gimmick bosses and feel not that fun to fight.

Overall, I highly recommend The Surge to anyone who likes the souls-like genre, and is looking for something new to play that freshens things up. It's not perfect, and if you go in expecting "Dark Souls 1 but with robots," you may be disappointed. It shares a lot of themes and vibe, but the gameplay is certainly it's own thing.\t\t\t\t\t
