\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is a game that, when I first found it, came across as something that had been plucked right out of my malformed psyche. As a complete sucker for edgy cyberpunk settings, hulking exosuits, fast-paced industrial action and cynical dual-sided sociopolitical messages, I couldn't resist picking this game up at full price. In the end, I was thoroughly satisfied, as The Surge, though not a flawless game, sated my desire for cyberpunk-meets-Dark Souls perfectly.

First off, a few disclaimers. The Surge is not a technical masterpiece. There are a number of bugs and flaws that have interrupted my game on numerous occasions, sometimes causing me to die or otherwise lose significant progress. The game's difficulty and pacing also takes some getting used to. Combat is much quicker and more fierce than in any Souls game, and many foes, even low-level ones, can kill you in just a few well-placed hits. Likewise, you need to hit fast and hit hard in order to succeed in this game. Defensive playstyles usually won't cut it, especially in the later areas of the game. On my first playthrough, I learned that the hard way. Of course, this learning curve is to be expected from a Soulslike, and is something that compelled me to keep playing the game even when it became extremely frustrating, something all three of the Dark Souls games did back when I first played them.

As a storyteller and worldbuilder, I was deeply enthralled by the game's atmosphere and narrative. The level designs of The Surge are quite creative, even despite the prevailing industrial aesthetic of the entire CREO facility. The story isn't incredibly complex, but it has a lot of perplexing questions you don't always get the answers to, and numerous side stories that you learn about through audio recordings, as well. Much like Bioshock Infinite's relatively neutral portrayal of sociopolitical issues, The Surge observes the follies of corporatism and unchecked technological development from a number of angles. I never felt as though the game had some sort of explicit, biased agenda, as it shows you both sides of the coin and ultimately lets the player decide how they want to deal with the events that unfold in the facility. You do have a bit of leverage in how the game ends, believe it or not (though, if The Surge 2 is any indication, only one of the game's two endings is actually "canon").

Overall, if you enjoy the things I listed at the start of this review and want to see those same concepts incorporated into a Soulslike action RPG, I couldn't recommend The Surge more. It does have its rough spots, but, in my opinion, these pale in comparison to the overall appeal of the game itself.\t\t\t\t\t

This game is an underrated classic in my opinion. I firmly believe its a must-play for anyone who enjoys souls-like games but also wanting that cyberpunk-fix. One aspect this game nails is innovation. So many 'souls-like' games copy without innovation the surge however, provides more than enough new ideas to keep even a dark-souls player engaged.

Every combat scenario is different, yes you can mindlessly farm scrap/souls from enemies but you can also target specific limbs to acquire the associated armor/weapons. This subtle change to classic combat flow is a genius way to spice things up even into new game plus. Player combat is a mixed bag. Weapons feel impactful in hits and sound crunchy in the most satisfying ways. The vertical dodge/parry system felt superfluous as regular horizontal dodging was easier and makes more sense to any prior dark souls player. This eventually became more useful but never justified the learning curve, However I can respect an appreciate new ideas. Weapon combos seem generic at surface level but I found after several hours of experimenting I was finding new combos and unique animations for certain weapons which became even more useful as my stamina increased.

Level layouts are fantastic each area revolves around a separate hub (ops) and new shortcuts which were originally hidden intelligently out of sight, reveal themselves making me say out loud "aha!" every.. single.. time. Though winding paths and tight corridors do feel labyrinthine at first, you will get to know routes very well. That's because this game kicks your ass if you underestimate it, and you'll be hooked. Level design is also impressive, somehow making mundane environments seem exciting from neon lighting to punchy colour palettes. Exploration was always a joy as I found myself excited for the next new area.

Boss fights are as important for this genre as the combat itself and I thoroughly enjoyed them, save for one specific encounter. My main gripe is that there aren't more of them. Dlc adds some more but I would have liked some more additions in the base game. I can even pick certain story moments where they could be added flawlessly.

Bottom line this game is fantastic and if you're even considering getting it you should (on sale). I got this on sale and it feels like a steal. I will 100% be playing and reviewing it's sequel creatively named: 'The surge 2'

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a massive fan of Dark Souls, I generally stay away from souls-like games (because nothing will come close), so I put off buying this for a long time. I finally decided to give it a go and can't believe how much I enjoyed it! The combat is really incredible, there's a good selection of weapons, a mysterious, dark story and a big, maze-like interconnected world.

It has a lot in common with the Souls titles, but it also does plenty differently. I think if you approach this thinking it will or wanting it to be anything like Dark Souls you will be disappointed, but if you take it as its own game it is tons of fun. If you like a challenging game with cool bosses and plenty to explore, I would highly recommend this!

8.5/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR; Second game is better, play that.

Cool idea, mediocre execution. Attacks/hits feel empty and with no impact sometimes. Dodge mechanic works against you most of the time. Don't even try to do a last second dodge like in Soulsborne games because enemies have this weird tracking issue sometimes. They turn at the last second and hit you anyway.

I'm a Bloodborne junkie and finished every Souls game and I instinctively tried to play this game in the same fashion and it didn't work and I became too tired trying to figure out what the game wants me to do and adapt to it. I ended up playing using the "get close, hit twice, back off, wait for enemy hit attempt to end, get close again, do the finisher etc, rinse and repeat" tactic and it works to some extent but it's freaking boring.

2nd game plays better and the mechanics actually works so overall everything is better. if you are like me you are better off playing that. Really cool concept and world but sadly I lacked the patience for getting into the broken/boring/weird mechanics. Still I'm hoping to return to the game in the future.\t\t\t\t\t
