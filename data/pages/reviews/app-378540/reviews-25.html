In the beginning of the game you can have a fun scruff while trying to figure out the clunky parry mechanic and block some attacks but as you reach the endgame the flaws really take over. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've always stayed away from souls-like games because usually I don't like challenges. I often fail in things that are difficult and just contribute to an overall bad mood in my life.
However, ever since I played Deus Ex: Human Revolution, I've had a soft spot in my heart for near-futuristic cyber augmentation stuff like robot arms, exo skeletons, and stuff like that, which is why I decided to buy this game after long consideration and a short time on my Steam Wishlist.
And honestly, I really don't regret buying it!
It's fun!
It has this sweet balance of being challenging, but not being unfair. The feeling you get when dying is not "This game is unfair.", but rather "Oh man, I should've done this and that.".

The combat feels great, upgrades don't make you overpowered, and there is lots of stuff to explore.
Story progression is kinda slow if you play cautiously like I do. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is a good, but not great, Soulslike game. It has some issues, but was overall enjoyable, and I would recommend it to anyone looking for more of that Dark Souls style of game design, even if The Surge doesn't play quite as well as its inspiration.

The biggest issue with the game, by far, is that as other reviews say, the combat feels a little off. It's not awful by any means, just subtly... floaty and disconnected feeling. Animations don't read as well as they should, it can be hard to tell what part of an enemy attack animation is the actually damaging part, which is important for a game that expects tight timing on blocking and dodging. And player animations can be a bit more long and elaborate than they should be, making it hard to tell when exactly when the animation has reached the point where you can input more actions. Which, again, is an issue that expects you to act fast or die in combat that doesn't leave a lot of room for error. I'm not saying that combat should have been overall faster by any means, just that the animations should have been snappier and more readable.

Beyond the animation aspect, the other problem with combat is that enemies are just all around a lot harder to stagger than usual for these type of games, and even then, the stagger isn't as consistent as it should be. It's not at all unusual for enemies to just hit you mid-combo, even if the same combo seemed to have staggered the same enemy before. The game has a variety of weapon types, but I found that due to difficult and unreliable staggering, the heavy weapons were the only way to go- pulling off the longer combos of the lighter weapons just could not be reliably done safely.

Speaking of weaponry, it was also a bit disappointing that while there was adequate difference between weapon types, (even if they weren't all that well balanced,) individual weapons of the same category have disappointingly little variety. The Surge follows the Dark Souls weapon design principle of not making later weapons blatantly better than earlier weapons, which isn't a bad idea, but it misses the important second step: Making them noticeably different instead. Any given one-handed weapon will have the same attack animations as any other, the only difference is that a few weapons (almost exclusively the rare boss weapons) get an extra effect- for example, extra fire jets on one specific otherwise unchanged attack. Aside from that, the primary difference between most weapons of the same type is just minor stat variations between different attack attributes that never make enough difference to be worth switching weapons over. Thus, new weapons aren't really better or different, which makes finding them unexciting and leaves combat feeling the same beginning to end. Just make sure to get the rare boss weapons and you'll have all the weapons you will really need or care about.

Beyond combat, the game could have really used an in-game map. Despite none of the maps being very large, a few end up surprisingly easy to get lost in due to a combination of vertical design and areas that either all look fairly similar, or are literally identical copy-pasted sections the same access tunnels. Which are often pitch dark on top of that. And considering that your character has actual sci-fi brain implants, and is an employee in a vast business complex that would have had every reason to make sure its employees don't waste time getting lost, this game would have had the most justified in-game automap EVER. If anything, it's hard to explain how you don't have one on-screen at all times.

So, that's the main gripes. beyond that, the game is good, and even does a few things better than Dark Souls. I definitely prefer its system for using special abilities- they cost energy charged via combat, so as long as you have enemies to fight, you can charge up your heals. Much better than having spells and estus with a set per-ability limit, or that draw on a single pool of points, that can only be refilled at rest points. With that said though, The Surge is definitely too stingy with your ability to actually hold onto those points in the early game, they decay much too fast until you get the right upgrades. And the best of those upgrades are in the "The Good, the Bad, and the Augmented" DLC, unfortunately. Still, once you get enough omni-boosts, the ability system works pretty well, allowing you to actually reliably use skills without worrying about burning through them.

Additionally, The Surge, like Dark Souls, lets you start a new playthrough once you finish the game, but improves over Dark Souls by also unlocking new upgrade levels on subsequent loops. So whereas Dark Souls would pretty much top out your damage and defense by the end of the first run with its hard and soft caps, making subsequent runs harder and harder as the enemies had much more room to grow than you did, The Surge lets you keep pace by upgrading your equipment more and more with each run.

And speaking of equipment, while The Surge's equipment may lack the practical variety of Dark Souls with its very samey weapons, I definitely liked its method of obtaining new armor/exoskeleton pieces, (as well as the weapons used by human enemies.) No inexplicable random drops like most games, you get new gear and/or parts to craft it by hacking said gear off of your feral brain-fried coworkers. Chop their arm off, and you definitely get their arm armor, simple as that. Between needing to pay attention for parts you want by looking at each enemy, making sure to target the limb with part you want to soften it up for a successful severing finish, and then ending combat with enough energy for the finisher, its part-severing loot system was definitely a good thing for both combat and the loot grind. I just wish more games used systems where enemy drops were about reliable, logical combat performance rather than random RNG rolls that make no in-world sense as to why enemy equipment usually just magically disappears upon death and sometimes doesn't.

So that's pretty much the key summary of where The Surge took steps forward and backward gameplay-wise, but what of its technical execution? Also weirdly uneven, but mostly good. For some reason, I could only get the game to run smoothly by alt-tabbing out than back in again once per session. I've never played any other game that needed that, I have no idea why it did, but one in-and-out and it ran buttery smooth from then on. Never crashed or hiccuped otherwise, and it has all the display, graphics and control options I could expect. So one weird issue that nobody else had aside, The Surge definitely succeeds on a purely technical level. The art direction itself may be very bland realism with no particularly interesting designs, and the environments all feel a bit flat and sterile, but at least those visuals are effectively delivered.

With all that said, if you're looking for a sci-fi take on the Dark Souls formula with a few changes for the better and several rough spots, then give The Surge a try. The combat is decent, the exoskeleton upgrade system is an interesting variation on character and equipment progression, the art direction is bland, and the plot is minimal but presents an interesting choice at the end. It's not perfect, but it's gotten cheap enough at this point that it's definitely worth the cost of entry if the Soulslike genre is your cup of tea.
