\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you think you'll like this game but not sure whether to buy? Have you tried the demo? If both of those questions are yes then you were once me.

Comparatively I think the Surge is somewhat lacking in actual gameplay content. It is a bit repetitive although visually very cool and well optimized. The Surge has a great story and that's what sets it apart from the other 3rd person rpg "soulslike" games out there. It's obvious the Surge isn't some half assed attempt to cash in, this game is well made and made by people who clearly wanted it to be great. The story is interesting and there are a lot of high quality visuals in the game, to the point where it carries this game.

I don't know if they patched the game but I had no problem with the difficulty increases between zones and thought it was very well paced actually, I wanted to mention this as it seems like a lot of negative reviewers seem to think the difficulty spikes are insane. I honestly didn't find this game that difficult at all, even NG+ I'm coasting through pretty easily.

The demo is a small taste of the game but it accurately represents what you'll get more of. The later levels can become extremely complex and backtracking is quite frequent if you're trying to see everything. The thing about the demo is that what you experience in it is largely unchanged in the main game. Enemy types are few and bosses even more sparse. If you want more of what the demo has to offer then you'll enjoy this game, but the thing is there isn't much more gameplay wise. It's more of the same in a new location, even choosing a weapon type will probably come down to what animations you like to watch more rather than playstyle because your playstyle depends more on what you are fighting than what you are wielding.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGraphics, sounds and atmosphere: 9/10
Plot: 6/10
Gameplay: 8/10
Level design: 9/10
Enemy placement: 4/10
Pacing: 3/10
Overall: 6/10 Not bad, but not especially good either. Grab it during a sale, if you feel starved for a souls-like.

Having played Lords of the Fallen, I ended up liking this one more up to the point where I reached the final levels. Keep in mind, that the game itself is very well designed; the environment fits a threatening, cyberhorrific atmosphere with a complex layer of facilities and open areas used in conjunction with each other. There is no map, and you really have to explore stalwartly in order to find those juicy shortcuts and hidden items. This game does not hold hands with you, but it's not quite at a level where you should be drawing a map on paper to keep yourself on the chart.

Let's talk about combat. This is the game's forte, it's fluid, and there's a lot of weapon disparity and lengthy combo potential. You are targeting the target's limbs instead of the target in whole, which will result in some areas being weaker against attacks than others. As you target those limbs, indicated with a blue font, as opposed to a yellow one, you are able to keep the targets stunned, and build up energy used to dissect the part. This part is used in crafting, which is done quite neatly.

There is not a lot of enemy disparity, the game is filled with "Hollows" aka. humanoids, and some different drones and constructs. Nor are there a lot of bosses, and even out of them, only 4 feel like a real boss encounter, of which 3 were enjoyable to me.

The plot is quite good, even though you figure one of the twists out by yourself soon enough. The end was worthy, and in the end I do not really regret slogging through the last couple of levels. I'd say this game is worth to get when a sale strikes, and if you do not expect to fight bosses on the same pace as Dark Souls series, or even Lords of the Fallen. The good thing is that this time, there are next to no gimmick bosses.\t\t\t\t\t

So here we have The Surge. The second 'soulslike' effort from from developer Deck 13.

Right off the bat I can confidently say that this is a MUCH better effort than Lords Of The Fallen. There were elements of that game that were enjoyable but it was heavily marred by some infuriating combat mechanics with enemies rotating on the spot to land attacks on you even when they'd commited to their attack animations. Fortunately this has been addressed in The Surge.

That issue was by far my biggest complaint with their last game because it took it from a game of skill to a depressing exercise in tedium. As a result The Surge feels much more balanced and the combat is actually very enjoyable. The mobility is much more akin to Bloodborne and the limb chopping mechanic is a nice addition. What's really cool is some of the clever little techniques you can learn for combat to gain the upper hand on your enemies. Some enemies will have weak points like their back and they're behaviour will change depending on your attack patterns.

For example there are some big maintaince mecs that tend to try and repair themselves if you attack their legs leaving them open to attack. It adds that little extra layer of tactical thought to the combat that elevates it to being not just good but great.

World building wise it's pretty subjective. For me I really like the industrialsed sci-fi look. Gives me glimpses of Alien and Red Dwarf which is an odd mix but a good one! There's a surprising amount of atmosphere in the game too. Some of the dark environments can be quite unnerving especially when an 'infected' comes barralling out of the darkness screaming like a nutter and lashing at you with arm blades! Verges on survival horror at that point which is pretty cool.

I'm running the game at 4k with a resolution scale of 70% with settings between high and medium with motion blur turned off and get a fairly steady 60fps so I wouldn't say the game is as well optimized as it could be but it's not awful either. I'd say if you're playing at 1080 you should be okay even with meager hardware if you're conservative with the settings.

All in all this is leaps and bounds better than the Dev's previous effort and I'd legitimately like to see a franchise made out of this. Fingers crossed.

Recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm writing this review being very close to the end of the game. So far I found it very enjoyable. It's definitely souls-like, although I'm finding it much easier than any game on the Souls series. Dark Souls 1 is probably where the authors took most of their inspiration, seeing as the level design in this game revolves around shortcuts and the combat has a similar feel (with the added fun of dismemberments).

There were some reviews that almost made me not buy this game, so I'm going to address some of the negative points on those:

1) Lack of enemy diversity.
This one is true, but it didn't bother me much. Combat is fun enough and the animations are so detailed it's underestandable why they couldn't create more enemies.

2) Lots of backtracking.
There is some backtracking built into the plot, but it felt natural to me; it's not about having to fetch some mcguffin you left three areas behind and had no way of knowing was neded later. There is also some optional backtracking required if you're a completionist (there are collectibles locked in areas you'll only be able to access after acquiring end game equipment), but it's entirely optional.

3) Lack of a map or proper direction to progress the plot.
I guess people who complained about this aren't fans of Dark Souls. Not only it didn't bother me, but I found the progression in this game almost linear. There are frequent branching paths, but they are mostly shortcuts back to the Med bay and short paths to hidden loot. I can imagine having trouble getting my bearings if I stopped playing for a while, but it shouldn't be much of an issue for someone with an average memory.

4) Regular enemies kill you in one hit.
This one had me worried the most. Some guys wrote that they ended up only using running attacks and then backing of, because enemies were so punishing; it sounded terribly boring. Fortunately I found that this can happen, but it depends mostly on the armor and implants you use: if you use light armor and don't equip a few implants that raise your health, you will get one shotted by most enemies; if you play with heavy armor and equip health impants you'll be able to survive a couple of hits from even the strongest enemies.

Finally, I just want to vent about a particular mechanic, that I think detracts from the overall game experience: weapon proficiency. Weapon proficiency boots your damage with a weapon type, is gained through using that weapon type and doesn't seem to have a cap, so it punishes using different weapon types. It's a damn shame this mechanic exists, because I tried all the different weapon types for an achievement and they were all pretty balanced and fun to use.

P.S.: I played this game with mouse and keyboard and it was fine. Unlike Dark Souls, playing with a controller is not a necessity here.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor a game that's so heavily constructed around its plot, it's odd that the plot should be the weakest part of the game. Nevertheless, The Surge is a huge step forward for Deck 13 from their previous Soulslike, Lords of the Fallen.

A lot of Lords of the Fallen's design elements are present in The Surge, from weapon movesets to UI decisions to the general cumbersome feel of combat, but the rough edges have been sanded away at to the point where they've mostly disappeared. Instead of going for a same-y fantasy setting like LotF, The Surge goes for a same-y sci-fi setting, but fights feel more or less identical. Mooks are damage sponges. Bosses are huge rather than interesting. Weapons upskill as you use them, forcing you to funnel into a particularly build based on what was available early on, or else grind away at your subpar weapon stats to get the most out of a late-game drop.

Unlike other Soulslikes, in The Surge, you cannot increase your stats. You have one stat, Core Power, and every other bit of your statblock scales off of that. You buy up core power with scrap (the souls equivalent), and you equip items to tweak your stats. Weapons and armor are abundant, and the game's complete lack of vendors is made up for by letting you rip the limbs off of your enemies to wear on your cyborg body.

Like the railroaded path through the plot, limb-ripping is central to the game and fundamentally kind of boring. You build up combo meter, lower the enemy's lifebar, and then hold X to end the fight. The first time you do it, it feels kind of visceral, but then it promptly turns into a chore. To get every piece of armor in a set (legs, chest, hands, helmet), you have to rip each one individually off of a different enemy (since ripping is a kill, and all corpses in this setting instantly self-immolate to prevent you from scavenging further). Worse, to upgrade a piece of gear, you need to do a number of additional rips of the same limb off of equivalent-strength enemies to build up the crafting components. Combat ends up having a flow because of it, and that's nice in a Nioh/Bloodborne sort of way, but overall The Surge still feels fundamentally bland. I like a lot of complexity in my Soulslikes (Dark Souls, Salt and Sanctuary, etc), and this does not deliver in that respect.

So, after all that, you might wonder why there's a yes vote.

It's because The Surge is not actually a bad game, and it's a fundamentally solid step forward for the studio. It might even be a solid step forward for the genre, as I would love to see more scifi Soulslikes on the market.

The Surge has decent game length (decent, but not tremendous. My playtime represents a single runthrough, with me trying to be as completionist as possible and grinding occasionally for scrap), its plot has good pacing (apart from the slow start and abrupt-feeling endgame stretch), and there's a clever twist in the story that the game never completely spells out for you, in true Souls fashion (scroll down to the bottom, past the spoiler tags, for the twist.)

It's not a bad pickup on sale, especially if you love the Soulslike genre without reservations. If you're on the fence, $30 is probably a reasonable ammount to risk on it.

On the other hand, if you have very specific things you require from a Soulslike for it to be fun, it's probably best to give this one a miss.
