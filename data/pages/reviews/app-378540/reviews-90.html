\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's science fiction Dark Souls.
That is pretty much all you need to know, if you like Dark Souls then you'll probably like The Surge.

The good stuff.
- Nicely detailed graphics and a really cool art style which makes me think of 90's anime but not overly fantastical or wacky.
- Fluid, impactful and brutal combat with combos and finishing moves where you chop of limbs and heads.
- Because you're basically a mech the combat feels weighty and it's awesome watching your guy swing a huge slab of metal around and bashing enemies across the room.
- High difficulty which you would expect from a Souls-like but mostly fair and manageable as long as you take your time and are quick to react.
- Chopping off different limbs to get new armor sets is a really cool idea and gives you something extra to do while farming materials and XP.
- You're character looks like he decided to glue random car parts on to his body for armor.
- Lots of different weapons which have unique move sets including fast stabby knife type weapons and big flaming hammers.
- Great dark and tense atmosphere.
- Feels more linear than a Souls game. Some might think this is a negative but I enjoy knowing where to go and what to do without having to use Google.

The not so good stuff.
- Sometimes once you're locked in to an attack animation you can't cancel out of it so you might end up walking off of a ledge to your death and it isn't your fault.
- There's way less bosses than you'd find in a Dark Souls game. Dark Souls 2 has over 30 whereas The Surge has less than 10 but they are pretty cool and will kick your head in.
- Less class diversity than in a Dark Souls game. You pretty much have to pick a couple of weapons you like and stick with them for the whole game.
- Movement is a bit floaty and took me a while to get used to but once I did it wasn't a problem.

Well optimized too, I don't have a space age PC and I'm getting 50-60 fps all the time with all settings to high.
It's a good time if you like balls hard action RPG's.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, I want to start off by saying that I really wanted to like this game. It presented itself well and I love the theme. The following critique is not the result of bias against the game.

This game's main flaw is in its combat. It's slow and cumbersome, and I could be a better fighter than the protagonist right off the bat. There is unnecessary delay in every attack, slowing them down so much that an obese person could operate faster, and you're supposed to be the one with augmentations.

There is no dodge mechanic, just a terrible block that doesn't even work. There is no dodge-canceling either, meaning every attack is a commitment. This could be tolerable if you were only ever fighting one-on-one, but the game quickly pits you against multiple enemies, making combat a ridiculously luck-based ordeal of whether your slow attack will get interrupted by a much faster attack from some enemy you might not see.

When I can fight better and faster than someone who's supposed to have robotic augmentations, you've officially stepped into the realm of the absurd. I'm not even trained to fight, I just know that being slow is how you get yourself kicked to the curb. It's common sense.

I can't even touch on the rest of the game's assets because I just put the game down. Nothing but a video review could make me pick it up again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNow, with most games like this, Nioh, Lords of The Fallen etc, Souls-likes. It's hard to escape the comparison because the Souls franchise was seemingly the first to employ this gameplay formula.

But the actual Souls game were forced in to the same formula in the same way multiple times, with only Bloodborne being a title that stood out. But from Demon's Souls to Dark Souls 3, the formula was improved upon but still failed to improve on much of what made the formula fun.

Games like Nioh, Lords of The Fallen and now The Surge genuinely improve upon the formula to the point they stand alone.. And this is a good thing. It revitalizes this kind of game and makes it feel fun and fresh again.


The Surge is the best game in this type of genre I've seen thus far. It has wonderful graphics, lighting, visual effects as well as smooth combat (A noticeable improvement from Deck13's work on Lords of The Fallen is especially clear here, where in comparison combos now blend much more seamlessly, and it really improves the games feel treameandously)

But, I will note, a lot of people complain the combat is clunky, and the same was said about Lords of The Fallen.

But this actually isn't true. It's just you have to get used to it and learn how to combat effectively. The game utilized various moves. Jumping attacks, sprint/slide attacks, combo moves from following up individual attacks with repeated button presses. And I can profess, I went through the game with the Single Rig Weapon Type, which is on the slower end of the weapon type spectrum in The Surge. And once learned to handle the moveset, and utilize it effectively with my dodging and enemy attack patterns, it felt much better and easier to grasp.

Just because it takes more than 10ms for the attack to hit after button press, doesn't make it clunky.

Another complaint is the maze-like level design. Which once reaching the second aree starts to become blatantly obvious. And I will admit I had some trouble. But perspective changes things, literally. When you realize there are actually maps on the walls dotted around each area to help you locate specific places and learn the layout. And sooner or later you will learn to remember the maps by heart.

If someone has little patience, I can understand how both the weapon attack speed and the difficult to follow the ever-expanding level routes as you progress through the game, can be daunting or difficult to comprehend and learn. But for me I found no issue once I applied myself and took note of my surroundings better. As after all, this game genre is literally designed to teach the player patience, of which if you're not already proficient in, this will break you down until you're better at every game ever. No joke, lol.

With 18 hours playtime on the first playtime I am quite happy with the tangible content to hours played ratio based on how much I paid. But I can definitely see a second playthrough of similar length happening if I decide to go for the rest of the achievements, of course to me this would double it's base value, but I digress.

I can guarantee I'll be buying the DLC's at some point as well.

The atmosphere is key in a game like this, and The Surge fulfills that adequately.

The only dissapointing thing I can say about it is that I wish I had played it sooner. Best game in the genre. But maybe that might change with The Surge 2, of which Ican guarantee will be a Day 1 purchase for me.

Props to Deck13!\t\t\t\t\t

Good variety of weapons, I found myself using most weapons at least a bit just to enjoy their unique looks and feels even within the same class. The hitboxes of both the weapons and characters are much better than in Soulsborne. The setting certainly isn't nearly as inspired, but The Surge pulls off the 'lone survivor being instructed through a crisis via comms' pretty darned well, it's derivitive but in a fun and in-touch way.

The story and mystery were compelling enough to keep me wanting to progress, and my character's power grew in proportion to my skill and understanding of the game.

If you enjoyed the Soulsborne games, this one should feel like a fresh but comfortable experience.\t\t\t\t\t

Mechanics
    \t
  • You have three combat resources: health, stamina, and energy.
    \t
  • Energy is generally gained by hitting enemies and can be expended on finishing moves, ranged attacks, and injectables.
    \t
  • You have "tech scrap," combination experience points and currency.
    \t
  • There are three categories of armor: light, medium, and heavy.
    \t
  • There are five categories of weapon: one-handed, staff, heavy, single-rigged, and twin-rigged. They have different movesets to be useful in different situations.
    \t
  • You have five weapon proficiencies -- how good you are with the weapons listed above. You improve your proficiency by fighting with the respective weapon type.
    \t
  • You can run and dodge. You can also hop over and duck under attacks.
    \t
  • You can block, but you parry by blocking at the last second. This staggers the enemy and allows a riposte.
  • You can get criticals by performing a follow-up attack after hopping or ducking correctly.
    \t
  • You can "backstab" by attacking from behind. The enemy falls to their knees. Attacks against kneeling enemies are critical hits.
    \t
  • You lock onto enemies.
    \t
  • You can target individual body parts of the enemies: head, torso, right arm, left arm, right leg, left leg.
    \t
  • If you damage an enemy to low health, and if you have enough energy, you can perform an execution or finishing move.
    \t
  • If you hit a specific body part enough times, you can cut it off when you perform an execution.
    \t
  • If you cut off a specific body part, you get the weapon or armor from that part.
    \t
  • If you already own the weapon or already have the blueprint of armor that you cut from an enemy, then you get upgrade materials.
    \t
  • There are four types of upgrade component for armor (head, torso, arm, and leg) and one type for weapons.
    \t
  • There are five quality tiers of component, with better tiers being salvaged from better armor.
    \t
  • You upgrade your weapons and armor using upgrade materials salvaged from enemies and your Tech Scrap.
    \t
  • There are four damage types and four damage resistances for armor and weapons, respectively. The types are elemental, crush, slash, and thrust.
    \t
  • There are three tiers of stability or poise, corresponding to the armor types (low, medium, high).
    \t
  • Your "rig," an industrial tool platform, serves as your level progression -- "Core Power Level."
    \t
  • Your rig has slots in which you can equip implants. These serve as your build, the equivalent of stats, skills, and feats in a standard RPG.
    \t
  • Your Core Power represents a limited resource of points to spend on implants.
    \t
  • Implants require a certain amount of Core Power. Better implants require more power.
    \t
  • You have a limited number of slots to use for implants.
    \t
  • Implants can be added or removed essentially at will.
    \t
  • Some items in the world require a certain Core Power Level to access, such as loot, upgrade items, or door locks.
    \t
  • Two keys obtained during the game are required to access some areas -- a security identity and an upgraded power core.
    \t
  • There is no fast travel system.
    \t
  • There are shortcuts to be unlocked in an area to make traversal easier.
    \t
  • There is one safe point per area, called "Operations," or "Ops."
    \t
  • Elevators, doors, and lifts provide most transport options.
    \t
  • Sometimes, circuits must be bypassed for an elevator, door, or lift to function. This is dependent on core power.
    \t
  • Sometimes, doors must be opened from the far side.
    \t
  • Sometimes, elevators and lifts must be unlocked at whichever end you encounter last.
    \t
  • Drones are a utility function that can be activated using energy.
    \t
  • Drones can attack enemies, crowd-control enemies, and shield the player.
    \t
  • Drones can eventually unlock out-of-reach circuits as needed by elevators, doors, and lifts.
    \t
  • There are five bosses in the game.
    \t
  • Bosses can be killed for their unique weapon.
    \t
  • Bosses can be killed in a slightly different way for their better unique weapon.
    \t
  • There are NPCs with unique questlines. Like...four of them.
    \t
  • Loot includes audiologs, weapons, armor, implants, upgrade components, and tech scrap consumables.
    \t
  • There are only a few enemy types: humans, drones, welding robots, cleaning robots, murder robots, nanite humans, and nanite robots.
