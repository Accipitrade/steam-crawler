\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell i have 250+ hours already, so here goes..

The Good;
(1) Immersive, very well thought out story line. Simply Incredible. Well worth 3+ play-throughs to fully grasp it. (I hope to one day see a series adaptation or books written about this story line). Look for EVERY detail!
(2) The Game simply looks beautiful, The Maps are well made, Massive and take a long long time to memorize. which adds to the immersion of the story and confusion and all. And i don't know about anyone else, but with a 10yr old RadeonHD6970 it can pretty well run with almost all graphic settings maxed (slightly lower resolution)
(3) If you can actually keep a good scrap multiplier, and balanced build, the game is pretty fair in terms of difficulty. What i mean is you could play a 'hardcore' play-through with personal rules like "i never die", and "No enemy HP display". Or you could stack all passive-healing, energy-mods and impact-up to tank everything. It's a personal choice of play-style.

The Bad;
(1)Yeah it can feel like a bit of a grind, if you can't get the multiplier up and don't know where to find certain enemies.
But that's the fun of it all. (ALSO YOU CAN CHANGE THE MEDBAY MUSIC ON STEAM. Thank you DEV's <3 )
(2)there are some bugs related to edges, and some more relating to the dismemberment animations and how they can pull you off of walls, through fenced. I'm grasping at straws here. It's the game engine and solid object rendering. i dont hink it can be fixed. BUT AGAIN, THIS ISN'T EVEN A REAL BIG ISSUE.
(3) I got nothing here. This game has been nothing but good to me :D I only "hate this game" when i myself run into a room unprepared. It's My fault. T_T

The Truth; or TLDR;
20/20 - Story
19/20 - Experience Customization depth
18/20 - Graphics (My outdated radeon, this being a 2017 game)
18/20 - Mechanics/Gameplay
20/20 - Replay Value

For a total of 95%. Definately a MUST OWN.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery disappointing. The game had an interesting premise, interesting design, interesting mechanics involving severing the limbs of your enemies to gain new equipment/blueprints. However, the game screws you over for playing it wrong and even when you do everything right, the game just decides that you should lose.

At the very start of the game you are given the choice between a light frame and a heavy frame. If you choose the heavy frame like I did, the game gives you next to no parts for you starting out. Maybe you get more later, I will never know. And I will explain why in a moment.

The game expects/demands that you choose the light frame because those are just about the only parts that the enemies carry. Want to fill up your equipment slots with armor? Better use the light chassis. Want to have lights to see in the dark? Should have picked the light chassis. Want to actually have any measure of mobility and stamina? You better have picked the light chassis.

And now onto the reason I quit and don't care to see the rest of the game. It cheats. Straight up, it just cheats. The game does not want you to win. At one point after fighting a tough new enemy, I was heading back to the starting hub (think bonfires from Dark Souls) to recover and stash away my tech parts (think souls from Dark Souls). But the game/devs decided; nope. You don't get to go back and recover. You have to fight a boss for the first time despite the fact that you are low on health and out of health kits. No preparation for you.

And if that wasn't bad enough, after trying about 7 times, I actually managed to kill it... Except that the game just up and decided that not only did I not kill it, but also that I didn't get to live. That's right! I managed to drop the SOB down to zero health, but the game not only decided the boss would still live with a sliver of health, but also that it was now immune to its own knockdown mechanic that you have to use in order to actually inflict damage. Despite the fact that the knockdown bar was full, and no matter how many times I hit it, the boss just decided; "Nope. I'm not going to let you earn this victory. I'm just going to pull a Sans and decide that it is never going to be your turn to deal damage and just kill you."

Because the game just doesn't want you to win, because that makes it fun, right?

This is the problem with some of these "Souls-like" games: They think that by making it more artificially difficult, that automatically makes it more engaging. It doesn't. The SoulsBorne games work because they follow their own rules. They are tough and unforgiving, but consistent. If and when you die in those games, there is always something that you can do about it next time.

But that is not the case with this game: If you die, it's not necessarily because you did something wrong, sometimes the game just decides that you are going to lose, and there's nothing you can do about it. And that's why this game was so disappointing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think this game is the best non-FROMSOFTWARE "souls-like" I've played, as a souls veteran. There have been others with better combat I think, but this game's progression system is unique and fresh. It also nails what Souls games do best - worldbuilding, atmosphere, and incremental/indirect storytelling. Overview by category, spoiler-free:

Bosses/Enemies: Bosses were a bit on the easy side, I think only two of them took more than 2 tries (last two bosses). I did really like how you can get special boss weapons by beating them in certain ways. Regular enemies, however, were probably the strongest I've seen in games like this. You can't let your guard down against any enemy. One-two shots are extremely common in light ("Operator" class) armor. But, I was extremely maneuverable. Be warned, getting cocky or tilted will be your death. This game is HARD. 7/10

The World: Navigation in this game is probably my biggest complaint. It's often unclear where to go to progress, as characters will mention places and expect you to know where they are. The DLC NPC does improve this by giving you maps when he tells you where to go, but no others do. Deck13 clearly really tried to capture that Dark Souls 1 system of interconnected areas, but it doesn't work as well. Even Dark Souls 1 falls back on a teleportation system late in the game. I recommend always paying attention to the walls, as there are maps and labeled arrows. But an accessible overarching world map would have helped. 6/10

Story-wise, it is very engaging. Without giving too much away, it is set in a near-future dystopia, and your character (named Warren) is a crippled man looking to work for the Mega-Corporation CREO, as CREO tries to find a way to fix a dying Earth. CREO provides mechanical exo-suits that allow crippled people to walk with robotic aid attached to their bodies. The story is told through small NPC conversations, item descriptions, audio logs, and the world itself. I found myself (especially at the end) exploring thoroughly to get any extra story tidbits. 9/10

Combat: Great variety of weapons and armor, interesting classes and a proficiency system that allows for players to switch classes on the fly, if they want to. Combat has weight to it, and dismemberment (a key part of the game) is satisfying. Energy and drone system is interesting but far from perfect. The drone feels underpowered and I found myself using defensive options, even with drone upgrades. Energy is almost like a "momentum" system that builds charge as you land hits, which can be expended to dismember, use your drone, or use all manner of injectable buffs. 7/10

ETC (sound/voice/graphics): Soundtrack-wise, nothing stuck out to me as great or bad. It was fitting. The sounds of the exo-suit and weapons are fantastic. Metal clashing on metal vs flesh is distinct. The somewhat limited voicework is top-notch, zero complaints. This game is quite dark and the emotional moments hit, mostly thanks to the voices. Very cool sound-mixing towards the end of the game. 8/10

Overall: 7.5/10 (not an average of the section scores). Excited to pick up The Surge 2, Deck13 have cracked the soulslike formula and created a great framework.\t\t\t\t\t

The combat is tough and responsive and if you learn how to use the dismemberment system to your advantage you can easily farm and craft powerful sets of weapons and gear to take on the industrial wasteland.

-interesting story & universe
-story is more in the foreground than in Darksouls. Some may find it easier to stay motivated to play on
-interesting directional hit system, sadly the directional block system is only introduced in the sequel
-combat feels smooth, satisfying, and on point (for the most part). also, cool finishers
-mostly bug free
-gain upgrades by hunting enemies & their body parts
-meet some side NPCs and help them on their quests
-interesting assortment of weapons & armor sets, with cool designs
-a couple of cool bosses

-side NPCs quests rarely feel rewarding

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd like to preface this review by saying that this was overall an enjoyable game. Though it has a lot of faults, if you look past that, it's definitely worth a playthrough. The game has a lot going for it if you like Souls-likes.

The good:
-Leveling System
-Loot System (The part where you can cut off any body part/weapon and make it your own.)
-Gameplay and Movement

The meh:
-Weapon Variety
-Armor Variety
-Enemy Design and Variety

The bad:
-Level Design (♥♥♥♥ is confusing as hell. It's not well executed, you're basically guaranteed to get lost at one of the maps not knowing what to do next.)
-Boss Design (Only one or two of them are good. Others are frustrating to fight and "artificially" difficult, where it doesn't really come down to skill. It makes the fight frustrating just for the sake of it, without added value and learning.)
-Too few boss fights (Surprised when the game abruptly ended.)
-Story (It feels tacked on tbh. Nothing special at the end, although the introduction to the character and lore was really good, it fell off really quick. So much lost potential.)


Bought the game when it was bundled with The Surge 2 when they we're both dirt cheap. I believe it was $9.99 for 2 games, so the value I am basing this review from this is based on that price point. So dear reader, I recommend you buying this at a sale and have a great time.\t\t\t\t\t

The Premise
This review contains no spoilers.
The Surge is a sci-fi souls-like set in a near-future where Earth is suffering from climate change, pollution and industrialization. Mega-corporation CREO comes with a solution, Project Resolve to fix the planet with cutting-edge tech. However, a mysterious event causes a massive crash of most of CREO's systems, making most people into insane murder machines... that's what Warren, the protagonist, wakes up into.

In-Depth Analysis
Pros
1.
The Surge's combat system is a breath of fresh air for the Souls genre, and even today stands out for its uniqueness, nuances and satisfactory mechanics. The limb-cutting mechanic, united with many weapons having unique powers and the implant system that allows a high customization degree about the combat approach, ensure an experience not easily replicated in other titles.

2.
Each area except the first one, shares an intricate, multi-layered design that makes exploration interesting and at times, labyrinthine. Masterful shortcut placements, a metric ton of secrets and consistent design quality make each level unique in its own way, but still coherent with the global sci-fi style.

3.
From heavy tanks with ample healing reserves, to agile fighters that bet everything on DPS, or tricksters that use active abilities and drones to wear enemies down, there is a play style for everyone in The Surge. The implant system is brilliant and allows players to choose what they want to prioritize in their approach, does so to a fantastic degree.

4.
Replaying the game with a different weapon and/or armor class will feel like a significantly different experience, in addition NG+ mode adds some new enemies, new areas and allows further upgrades to existing weapons with previously inaccessible resources. I replayed this game six times over several years and platforms, it was always fun.

5.
The implant, armor and weapon variety is really great. Around the next corner there can be a new one that can revolutionize the current build, or open a path for a completely new one. Many weapons have diverse movesets and unique powers as well, further adding to the variety, while all armors have a unique set bonus.

Cons
1.
Some weapon classes are just better than others, no matter what. For instance, Twin-Rigged and One-Handed are arguably the best, while Single-Rigged is by far the worst. Some weapon categories just become not viable against certain bosses or anyway later-game enemies. It's not impossible to finish the game with them, it's just quite depressing.

2.
Dark Souls did this wrong, and Surge sadly followed: quests are obscure tasks without even a remote hint on what to do most of the time, as NPCs will spit out very vague indications of what they want. It becomes frustrating to accomplish such tasks, and the rewards are often underwhelming or plain trash.

Gameplay Breakdown
Exploration
Is performed on foot, with Warren being able to jump and leap over great distances thanks to the exoskeleton. Levels in The Surge are intricate, some of them are however definitely more complex than others. Each stage has a myriad of secret areas, hidden loot caches and even NPCs that give quests: exploring every corner, breaking all containers and taking extra time to be thorough pays off with rare gear and extra XP. As in other souls-likes there will be shortcuts to go back to the Ops Station (bonfire) more easily, however there is no fast-travel of any kind and no map.

Character Management
Can be performed in a limited way while on the field, with Warren able to swap armor pieces, weapons and some implants that have the "Hot Swap" propriety, without returning to an Ops Station. The bulk of management, upgrading and crafting is however done at such safe areas, that always have a Medical Bay to replenish healing items, change Implants, use Scrap (souls) to level-up Warren. The Assembly Station allows crafting armor pieces among the known blueprints, and also upgrade weapons and armor using the resources obtained from slain enemies or found in the world. Most blueprints for gear and weapons are gained by severing enemy limbs, defeating bosses and less commonly, exploring the world.

Implants need a special mention, as they are modules that can be inserted in the Exo-Rig to give Warren passive and active abilities. Each implant costs a certain amount of Core Power (character level) to be equipped, with the total level acting as a cap to balance things out. Armor pieces also cost some Core Power to equip, usually heavier armors will cost more.

Combat
Plays out in third-person, mostly in melee. Warren can wield one of five weapon categories, ranging from the slow but powerful Heavy Duty to the nimble Twin Rigged. Using a weapon class will upgrade its Proficiency level as more enemies are slain, granting bonuses - sticking to one weapon class for the whole game is recommended. Most enemies' individual limbs can be targeted to inflict different effects, stability loss and amounts of damage. The goal is performing an Execution, that will kill the enemy and sever that limb, awarding Warren with the armor worn on it and eventually their weapon, if that limb held one. A unique approach that makes any new enemy encounter exciting.

As in most souls-games, combat uses a stamina system and is highly punishing, as even common enemies can deliver great amounts of damage. Dodging at the right times, learning attack patterns and being quick-witted are the cardinal points. The more enemies are killed in a row without using an Ops Station, the higher a passive Multiplier will get, awarding bonus Scrap and even some healing recharges at random. Energy is another parameter accumulated with successful blows, and is used to fuel active implants and drone weapons, but depletes over time. Counter attacks are present. however they're a marginal mechanic simply not worth using over a dodge. All bosses have a secret way to be killed, that will award an upgraded version of their Boss Weapon, that has unique powers and is usually formidable.

It takes around 10-12 hours to finish the base campaign, and another three to five if one also plays the "Walk in the Park DLC. Replay value is high and the quality excellent, so I can recommend the game for full price.
