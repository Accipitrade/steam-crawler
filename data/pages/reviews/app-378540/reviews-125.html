\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVisually, the Surge is stunning. The environments are colorful, beautifully lit and populated with details that help to immerse you in the narrative. The enemies are highly detailed and the animations are as smooth as butter. The game flawlessly blends dark viscera and interesting glitch effects like chromatic aberration in a gorgeous, futuristic world to create interesting and compelling levels. The level design is great, with lots of interconnectivity and exploration available.

The gamplay was highly rewarding, and has a good level of complexity. I found the combat to be extremely fluid, with a high skill cap that rewards timing and patience. There are many weapon types in the game, all of which have unique movesets and cater to specific play styles. As you use a weapon type, you gain proficiency in it, meaning that switching weapon types later in the game is actually disadvantageous. The proficiency system seems to reward the player for sticking with one weapon type throughout the game, but since all weapons of a specific type have a very similar moveset, this might start to get stale. That being said, the movesets and combos available to each weapon type are so complex and involved that I found myself learning up until the very end of the game, despite only using one handed weapons.

The narrative of the game is very interesting, but personally, I feel that the delivery is lacking. Even after playing it through twice, I still only kinda understand the story. This is probably at least partially intentional, as the game is experienced through the perspective of a lost and confused character. However, I also feel like I did not go through the cutscenes and witness the dialogue in the order I was intended to. The game doesn't rail road you, and allows you to explore and experience it in the way you want to, but I think the narrative cohesion suffers as a result.

All in all, I very much enjoyed The Surge and can whole-heartedly recommend it. It's a souls-like, and as a result is being compared to Dark Souls. I will say that the games are similar in terms of genre, but don't expect a sci-fi Dark Souls clone when going in. There are a plethora of differences between the two games and you should try to leave your expectations at the door and experience the game as it's own entity.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf there was a Mixed option to give this, I would, but I'm leaning more towards negative at 15 hours in.

Obviously, it's Souls-esque, but whereas Souls is hard and expects you to learn over time, The Surge is hard and expects you to learn immediately, and nearly demands perfection. Many enemies, especially bosses, can 2-hit kill you. Even areas you return to. Some attacks will sometimes even guarantee you get hit twice (looking at you, heavy-duty users...). So you'll often die quickly to enemies until you even get a sense of how to deal with them, making the learning process even longer due to the backtracking.

And some nitpicky details: having lyrics, especially very simple lyrics and a single verse, to the song you hear in your safe zones (which you will visit frequently) is maddening. I have "~I was booorn...in a pri-soooon~" constantly stuck in my head. Or having the announcer say only a handful of lines when exiting/entering an Ops area. You quickly tire of hearing the same thing over and over again.

The game does have some neat stuff though. The core level system and being able to mix and match implants and gear based on your power source (your level) is kinda cool. The combat can be surprisingly fast sometimes, though as mentioned above, You have to constantly heal or you just die instantly.

I'll revisit this when (or if) I finish.

POST FINISH EDIT:

I did finish the game, though I really didn't see much to change my initial opinion. New Game Plus dials up my comments even further: lots of one shot enemies. I remain of a mixed opinion.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCyborg Dark Souls, and done well. If you think you might like this, you probably will.

The combat is fluid and has a lot of depth; you've probably heard about how you can target limbs to extract parts to loot, but this system also slightly changes your moveset, and if you can actually connect your weapon with the part you're targetting, you can get extra benefits like stagger and knockdown. Apparently if you're good, you can change target limb mid-combo to chain these effects together although I'm not at that level yet. This gives each weapon a fair bit of depth, although there aren't a huge number of weapons (five basic classes, with a few uniques of each with their own variant movesets). The levelling system is also as good as it sounds, allowing a huge amount of flexibility; no need to make additional saves for different builds, just swap in the parts you need at the medbay.

The enemies are characterful, well designed and challenging; it takes a while to figure out how to beat them but practice yields dividends. The only gripe I have is that there could be a few more different types, but given the choice of more varied enemies and diluting the quality of what's already there, I think the game as it is is a good balance.

The story is surprisingly good; nothing new here, but an old tale about hubris executed well. It's not subtle (some of the main messages of the whole story are literally written in two foot high red letters on a wall in the first area) but the heart of it is well conceived, with good pacing, a light smattering of symbolism and some great voice acting. It's not Planescape: Torment. But where say the Mass Effect series promises a lot but delivers very little depth, The Surge promises very little and then delivers on it vastly beyond what you would expect.

Level design is highly interconnected and feels "real", mostly in a good way; each area is built like a spiral around the central medbay ("bonfire"), with progression unlocking shortcuts back that let you start further on in the spiral. I never had a problem with Dark Souls 2's realistic-as-a-dream geography, but if you did, The Surge is the complete opposite. Pacing is pretty good, with making it back to medbay usually achievable, but rarely easy. You often backtrack through explored areas, but usually something has changed about the level, introducing tougher enemies and new rooms. Exploring is a mild challenge that adds tension; fighting through level-appropriate areas is very satisfying, but when combing an old area for some specific item it's just annoying. Some kind of 3D map you could unlock after finishing an area would really help here.

The game is pretty buggy. As well as graphical glitches like juddery movement in elevators, I've had a fair number of CTDs, including a couple of buttons that trigger CTDs every time. None of them stopped me playing the game, and the autosave makes sure you haven't lost much progress, but they can be pretty annoying. No fixes yet for my issues, but I'm hopeful; as a developer, Deck 13 seems to be listening to fans pretty closely. All of the technical issues I had have been fixed since, nothing to see here, move along.

All in all, I'm left wanting more but not at all disappointed by what I got. I'm looking forward to NG+ in The Surge and very hopeful that a sequel is made. Deck 13 have shown that (bugs aside) they can execute a Soulslike game very competently (if not capturing *all* of the magic or scope of those games), and innovate in ways that are clearly improvements. Let's hope they are given the chance to continue.\t\t\t\t\t
