\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is basically a tech-based Dark Souls.

You can expect the combat to be almost precisely the same in terms of style. Instead of bonfires, there are operation bays to run back to and repair, and the map is metroid-style so you'll find shortcuts back to home base while exploring. One significant difference is that you can target body parts and sever them with a killing strike. This is actually necessary to acquire additional pieces of armor and weapons.

Advancement is very linear. You can upgrade your core or upgrade weapons and armor. The area you're in will restrict the range of parts available for which you can invest in weapon and armor upgrades.

Instead of alloting points towards individual stats, you instead simply switch out "implants" that grant abilities (like potions) and stat boots that scale with your core level.

Stability doesn't appear to work the same way as Dark Souls, and I have yet to figure it out. Hitting unarmored body parts tend to trigger a stun more often than hitting armored body parts. An implant exists to view enemy health and stability.

I think the lack of ranged weapons is what hurts the game the most. It is set in a sci-fi setting, but all the weapons are melee-based. There are drones, but they have a delay and aren't very powerful. Some enemies have ranged attacks, but they are primarily robots. I guess the setting involves primarily construction workers and engineers, so they use these cyborg frames and improvised weapons to fight. Later on, you find soldiers, but they do not use ranged weapons either. Being a single-player game, I can't fathom why they wouldn't be more practical with the technology.

But the primary reason I can't recommend this game is due to the complete lack of multiplayer. The more I'm reminded of that as I play this game, the more I just simply feel like installing and firing up Dark Souls. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game was marred by technical issues at its release, and got hit with a wave of bad reviews, which definitely hurt its initial impressions. Getting it on a steep discount, however, I realize the game is definitely a hidden gem for those who appreciate the Souls formula. I will compare this game a lot to Dark Souls, because unlike most games that are compared to it for its difficulty, this game is clearly a "clone" that tried to take the concept of a soulsborne a bit further.

Note: Most all of the bugs have been fixed to this date, however I think it is worth mentioning in terms of customization through menu options, this is easily one of the best PC releases of all time. The menu allows you to customize a ridiculous amount of settings, including how target switching works, various approaches to limb targetting, controlling when music plays. It's also had it's content nearly doubled by free DLC in terms of new weapons and armor sets, so as far as good devs go, I don't think anyone can fault these guys.

Positive:
This game takes combat to the next level, with weapons having much expanded movesets compared to Dark Souls/Bloodborne, a limb targetting/severing system, as well as having an additional defensive option (high/low stationary dodges that combo into special counter moves.) The gameplay is certainly the high point for this game. There are also some very endearing characters, the level design is actually interconnected often in genius ways (outdoing all but Dark Souls 1.)

The upgrade system is very fun and quite unique. You gather gear and upgrades by attacking the limb of an enemy wearing the armor you want, then severing that limb after dealing enough damage. It's a genius way to let you get the gear you like the look of without having to just RNG farm - simply damage the limb with the armor you want, defeat the enemy with an execution, and it's yours. Upgrades work in a similar fashion. If you want to kill an enemy as fast as possible, you can instead simply attack unarmored parts of their body to deal extra damage at the cost of not gaining any upgrade materials or armor pieces. Once you get a little bit more adept with the controls, you can take this even further by targetting an unarmored piece to stun an enemy, then finishing your combo targeting the limb you want to sever while they are stuck in hitstun. Very fun system once you really start getting comfortable with it.

Mixed:

This point would normally be a massive negative for me, but it actually suits the game perfectly. Music-wise, this game essentially only has functional music.

The largest complaint about this game is the "forced" country-music at the bonfire-equivilent. While I dislike country music and hated this song, the devs actually gave you quite a bit of functionality to determine when (and if) that music actually plays, AND you can simply insert your own music extremely easily (they made a folder in the game files for custom playlists, just drag and drop.) I put the Furi soundtrack in and never looked back.

The moment-to-moment soundtrack is either simply ambience in most locations, rising string cluster chords to build tension in dangerous or dark areas, or driving, droning bass+drum synth music with no melody.

Boss-wise, I couldn't even remember if this game had music or custom soundtracks. Upon looking it up, it does, but none of it is memorable like Dark Souls, Revengeance, or Furi where I still listen to the boss music to this date, and hearing a single second instantly gives me a mental image of the boss fight.

Overall, however, the music fits the game perfectly and does a fine job of creating the ambience they were seeking. It's certainly nothing special, but hopefully in The Surge 2 they can create some more memorable music to raise their boss fights even higher.

The other mixed point I would like to bring up is the inclusion of special "hardcore kill" boss weapons. Bosses have a special way in which they can be defeated (no hints on how to do this) that drops a very unique upgraded version of their normal weapon drop. Unfortunately you only get one shot at this; if you beat the boss without knowing the secret, they are gone until NG+ and so is that weapon. There is no way to know the secret way to defeat the boss, and it's often extremely counter-intuitive.

This wouldn't be a big deal since it encourages a NG+ playthrough, but the weapons are so much more interesting than the base game weapons it seems like a huge wasted potential. All of them have some unique or special property and moveset that makes the "2.0" versions so much more enjoyable to use than the base ones.

Negative:
The story is rather generic. The level design for the base game can feel bland as well, since it is predominantly inside a single large complex. Because of this you end up seeing a lot of greyish-brown corridors. The game is also shorter than what you'd expect from Souls - about 15-25 hours to complete the base game, depending on how much time you take exploring (lots of secrets) and reading the lore.

The last complaint I have about this game is the amount of bosses is quite low. It's not what you'd expect from a Souls clone for sure. There are lots of minibosses and many of the regular "trash" enemies are on a tier that might be a boss in a Souls game. Something to keep in mind if the only draw to you for games is quality boss fights, as many of them rely on a couple of gimmicks.

Overall:
I feel this game is a solid 8/10. For the price it's currently at it's insane value. I think this game will also be an incredible stepping stone for The Surge 2, as they have certainly outdone the gameplay mechanics of Soulsborne games. With more work on atmosphere, world design, and bosses, I think they could make a game to surpass the Dark Souls series.\t\t\t\t\t

And the enemies are damage sponges, so it takes many hits to bring them down, which trivializes the significance of each hit, which indirectly makes it just a mashing spree.


A significant amount of the enemies in the game are the same human zombie just with different weapons. So not only are you fighting the same shape most of the time but you fight the same shape wielding the same stick dozens of times until the developers decide to give it a new stick in a different area. The process of the developers introducing human enemies with new generic weapons while taking away some of the old ones and sometimes bringing old ones back randomly, is very transparent. So you spend most of the game fighting the same general enemy, often each area only has one or two different enemy types at maximum and they are recycled constantly between areas. So the already repetitive combat is made even more boring.

The enemies that are not human are often obnoxious. Like the little flying turrets that rip off all your health while potentially hovering over hazardous ground that will kill you, drop offs that will kill you or other enemies that will kill you. Or the box drones that are almost exclusively found in narrow spaces where you have no room to dodge yet their attacks pierce your guard so it's just an actual damage race to kill them. Or those ones that have an instant upper cut swing that stuns you while they leap backwards and thus cannot be hit before they recover for their next instant upper cut so they technically cannot be hit without luck or if they use their cross map wide mid air tracking unblockable leap attack and you manage to dodge it then you can land maybe two hits in before they recover again.


The general challenge of the game shifts from being either inconsequential or broken. Most of the time, the game is just the same monotonous generic fights which overall pose no real challenge to a decent player. Then suddenly the game will throw one of its blatantly overpowered enemies or enemy formations at the player and suddenly progression is halted until you die several times trying to kill that ridiculous enemy or cluster. Like that one enemy who wields a weapon as long as he is tall, deals half your health in one swing, chains five swings per combo and has less then a second cooldown between his combos. Is that enemy even designed to be fought or is it just there to reach the developers game over quota for the player. Or that high level robot in the low level area, that has a flying turret placed in its arena so it's not just a high level robot in a low level area it's a high level robot in a low level area who also has an add and also has a second high level enemy hidden in its arena which will jump you in the middle of the fight. Because the developers seriously just do not understand when enough is enough. But it's not like their intended consumer will notice.

Similar examples can be seen randomly through the game as roughly one out of every four enemy encounters is an ambush. These range vastly in how clever and fair they are. Sometimes an enemy comes out of the fog or the darkness and it's an exciting moment. Other times the enemy rams into you as you turn a corner and instantly takes off half your health. Even more hilarious, I was expecting an ambush and it still killed me because it attacks instantly. You have zero time to stop it from happening unless you know where it is already, you just have to get hit. Is it obvious yet that the developers are desperate to kill the player so they can call the game hard.


The game borrows many mechanics from the Souls tech demos including the currency lost on death stain of a feature. But this time it has a time limit so if you die in a place where there has not been a shortcut in a long time, you basically have to lose all your currency because you physically cannot reach it in time to recover. In general though the game uses lots of classic Dark Souls ways of artificially spiking difficulty like stun locks, swarms, ambushes, instant kills, sudden falls and so on. Fighting swarms of enemies in this game is uniquely impossible because the combat is so weighted and critical yet you almost always are fighting inside a closet; that trying to take on two damage sponges at once is just a joke of an experience. No room to space yourself from them, no room to space them from eachother, they each take a dozen hits to kill and they each are always swinging at you. If you get hit once you are staggered. It seems the developers do not understand the limitations of their own combat system.

What little the game does add to the system is relatively lukewarm. The finishers and crafting are neat but the limb based combat targeting is pointless and just makes an extremely basic feature more complicated then it should be. That feature being, locking on. There's not much else to say of what gameplay elements are new, there aren't really all that many at all. Save for the tedious lock on feature and the use of "come back later" areas, which is just contrived replayability.

The bosses are quite gorgeous in terms of their visuals and animations but in practice they display an extreme lack of comprehension for actual boss design. Every boss is two gimmicks. One gimmick is how they hurt you, another gimmick is how you hurt them. Until you learn the gimmicks, avoiding damage and dealing damage are barely possible. Their attacks are just so overbearing and completely unforgiving that you need to know exactly how the attack functions to dodge it, so you need to die several times. And the way you actually hurt the boss is so obscure and unintuitive that it takes trial and error just to figure out how to actually hurt them. They aren't fights, they're essentially trial and error puzzles. Even when you learn all the gimmicks, is still does not feel like a real fight because now that you learned the gimmicks the boss isn't really a challenge anymore. There's no middle ground, you go from dying because you can't make any progress to killing it without effort because now you know how.


So overall, a comparatively more competent gaming experience then the Souls tech demos. But still bad due to generally repetitive pattern based combat and the random smattering of obnoxious artificial difficulty. You're better off playing a Rogue-like as they usually have reactive based combat that is actually dynamic and thus doesn't crutch on the low entertainment standards of the player to pass as stimulating.\t\t\t\t\t
