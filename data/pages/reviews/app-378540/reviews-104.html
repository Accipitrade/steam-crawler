\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet's get it out of the way first and foremost: The Surge is a sci-fi Dark Souls; with Dark Souls defining its own genre, and Deck13 hopping onto that train with their first game (Lords of the Fallen), no doubt does The Surge follow suit.

However, Deck13 has done sooo much in terms of improving their sequel game—all while also improving the still unofficially named genre: Souls-like. The Surge decides to take the classic Souls-like combat and combine it with an intentionally clunky feel—oddly similar to the movement in the Dead Rising series. Moreover, a targeting system is included in the combat, which allows an ever-so-slightly more strategic approach and satisfying the collectathon gamers since whatever body part you target you eventually chop off and receive appropriate items—which leads into the crafting system. I could elaborate more on all the innovations/disruptions—but there's YouTube reviews for that; therefore, I am quite proud to see a developing studio taking the gloriousness that is Dark Souls and innovate on that formula rather than go for the cash grab; however, that's not me saying all the innovations/disruptions are for the better.

If you're a fan of Souls-like combat and storytelling, I recommend you buy The Surge—ideally during a sale or when you got an itch you can't scratch.
It's not the best game, yet alone the best non-Souls Souls-like game; but it's a solid game that will scratch your itch. Plus, if Deck13 continues to improve their games like they are, their next iteration should only be that much better. Hope you enjoy it as much as I have!

P.S. I just finished the campaign and all of what I said in my original review still holds true if not more. In honesty, the final boss battle and ending was anti-climatic—although I did heavily grind and was many levels above the recommended level. However, almost halfway through the year--and The Surge is my Dark Horse of 2017.\t\t\t\t\t

A Mess of a Game

WARNING - LONG REVIEW

Playing the Surge is an exercise in frustration. I see a game that has so much potential squandered by bad game design and decision making from the developers. Some argue that you can't compare Surge to Dark Souls. I disagree. The game markets itself as being similar to Dark Souls, the second game from the studio that gave us Lords of the Fallen, and it uses similar game design. Comparing the Surge to Dark Souls is like comparing Diablo to Titan Quest. Its a valid comparison, in my opinion, and shouldn't be scoffed at.

OVERVIEW: The best way to describe The Surge is simplified sci-fi Dark Souls. Less time needed for completion (20-30 hours) and less complicated RPG mechanics.

Positives:

+\tThe Setting: I adore sci-fi. Aesthetically speaking, Surge delivers on the promise of sci-fi souls-like. You play in a huge industrial complex wearing a giant exo-suit wielding various weapons that look like someone attached a welding torch to metal rebar. The designs look awesome and the exo-suits look great.

+\tCombat: For the most part, combat is fun. Surge emphasizes speed and raw aggression, encouraging the player to dodge attacks and chain together combos to take down enemies. Combat has a good rhythm of dodging and attacking and is quite addicting. Deck 13 also does a great job communicating the weight and momentum behind the attacks. When you move, dodge, or swing a weapon, you can feel it. Its hard to explain but its there.

+\tRPG progression is fairly well-done. You collect tech scrap (EXP) and level up. You can collect implants as you explore the world, which you can equip to give you various active and passive buffs. You can also mix and match armor/weaponry to fit your preferences. It’s fairly easy to find a loadout that you like and fits your playstyle.

Average:

~ Technically speaking, the game has good graphics and voice acting. Nothing noteworthy but worth mentioning nonetheless. The graphics and art direction don't really shine through due to the commitment to the realistic setting and 99% of the game taking place indoors. I think if they had taken more risks with the art direction or went for a more stylized art style, then this game would be a lot more visually striking.

The Negatives:

-\tThe Setting: So I said I love the sci-fi setting, so what gives? Well the problem is the industrial setting that you see is the entirety of the game. During your entire 20-30 hour playthrough you’re stuck in the same factory, going through the same environments, fighting the same couple types of enemies. It’s beyond lazy and it gets old very quickly. In other games, you’ll explore different locations and areas and fight new types of enemies. No such luck here. There is little variation here at all. You'll be walking through the same non-descript tunnel with industrial pipes for a few dozen hours. Rooms look so same-y and generic you'll easily find yourself lost as you try to navigate the map. And this is without mentioning that you *will* be revisiting the same maps over and over. You’ll be fighting the same exo-suit zombies throughout the game interspersed with machines here there, all of which attack and behave the same. They could’ve spiced it up by adding a cryo-cooling location, a boiler area, maybe some greenhouse and aquatics locations, but nope. There’s very little imagination or creativity here with the sci-fi setting and it shows.

-\tThe Difficulty: Let’s be clear, difficulty is not a bad thing. In fact, I love Souls-like games partly *because* of the difficulty. But there’s a difference between ‘hard’ and ‘frustrating’. The Surge is overwhelmingly the latter. Most enemies in the game can kill you in one or two hits, leaving no room for error. Health bars are way too big. You will die constantly and often to even the most mundane of enemies, even if you’re a good player. Enemy placement is also awful, placing them in cramped corridors and small rooms with very little room to maneuver or dodge. Rarely have I felt that my death was a learning experience or my own fault. As a result, killing a simple trash mob takes too long, and is too risky, too monotonous, and too frustrating. Even grinding is useless; since enemies will scale with your level. So what’s the solution? Running through the level. Chances are, you’ll find yourself running through the level ignoring enemies entirely, simply because you can’t be bothered to kill them. Its just not worth it. Why should I waste time killing an enemy when avoiding them is less risky and avoids the hassle of dealing with them entirely? Why should I bother to kill an enemy and level up if they're going to just scale with me? This is a classic case of bad game design that encourages negative player behavior.

-\tPoor Decisions: Some of the decisions that Deck 13 made with this game utterly baffle me. For example, the timer to regain your lost tech scrap. As with any souls-style game. You kill an enemy and gather EXP in the form of tech scraps. If you die, you drop them and you have one chance to go and reclaim them. If you die again, they’re gone forever. It’s a wonderful concept. So what does Deck 13 do? Well, in what I can only describe as one-upmanship, they added a timer to regain your lost EXP. When it runs out, the tech scrap is gone forever. This is a remarkably dumb idea simply because it doesn’t make sense. Is the game not hard enough without having to add that one extra hassle for the player? In some cases, you’ll have no chance to even regain your EXP because you can’t physically get to your EXP fast enough before the timer runs out. One could argue, you can kill enemies to increase the timer. But as I mentioned before, killing enemies is too risky and too time-consuming. So once again, you’re running through the level, ignoring the game just so you can reclaim your 22,000 tech scrap before it’s gone. But you can’t, because you don’t have enough time. So you just lost all your hard work. This is difficulty for the sake of difficulty, not because it makes sense or is fun.

-\tCharacters/Story: This is just plain forgettable. A interesting concept and strong introduction falls aparts faster than a sand-castle in a tsunami. The story is bland and poorly told with Evil Corp (TM) being evil and blaring propadanda in your ears the entire time. Interesting characters are non-existent. Warren, your player character, is a generic middle-aged white dude who does the protaganist thing because the game said so. Let me make this clear, if you’re going to take away character creation to make us play as a named character then give them a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ personality. Make us actually care about them, give them motivations, likes, dislikes, anything. Nioh did this and Surge did this and its honestly very annoying.

As I said before. The Surge has potential and I do think that Deck 13 are capable of putting together a great game. But this isn't it. One can hope they make a sequel and take feedback into account in the future. I hope in a couple years we see a Surge 2 that delivers on the promise of this concept. For now, play something else.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSince their source of inspiration is obvious, it seems like Deck 13 (the developer) is deliberately trying to differentiate their games from Dark Souls in whatever way they can. While I can appreciate innovation, what they created instead didn't worked out in their favor. Whereas Dark Souls (and Bloodborne) sped up its combat in its last 2 games, The Surge and Lords of the Fallen slow it down, and as a result both feel extremely clunky to play. Whereas different weapons in Dark Souls have drastically different attack speeds, in the Surge there are slow weapons and even slower weapons.It doesn't help that no weapon get any interesting moves, as they all only have 3 attacks: horizontal, vertical and dash attack (which do exactly what they sound like), so all weapons feel more or less the same. The least I can say is that the animations are decent and sell the weight of the mech suit pretty well, although the dodge animation is extremely fast and feel like the character is sliding on a banana peel.

One feature that I can appreciate is the dismemberment mechanic, where you cut limbs off of enemies to deal increased damage if you target unarmored parts or gain loot if you target armored parts. This came at a cost however, since the weapon movesets are clearly built around this mechanic with horizontal and vertical attacks good for dismembering different body parts (similar to the plasma cutter in Dead Space); but to be honest if the cost of having this system is that all weapons feel the same, I would rather they didn't include it and gave the weapons more interesting moves instead.

Deck 13 also seems to have misunderstood what makes Dark Souls a good game. It's not simply its difficulty; rather, it's because it is difficult yet fair. It is easy to make a game difficult, you need only look at unfair mario; it takes similarly little skill to increase difficulty by simply increasing the numbers on the enemy, which is what Deck 13 did here. There are plenty of enemies that can kill you in one hit (or get close to it), and the design philosophy seems to be "make the enemy slow but hit hard". This isn't necessarily a bad idea, but a lot of enemies in this game are robots, so it's often hard to expect which parts of their body can be weaponized, and the telegraphs on their attacks are often counterintuitive, which means there would be a lot of unexpected one hit deaths.

Overall, the Surge is a clunky game that delivers a lot of frustration without the finesse of Dark Souls. While there can be fun to be had, personally I like my games to be good before they are difficult.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor those looking for a "Souls" experience, do not start this game with that mind-set. I am a huge soulsborne series fan, and although this game has many similar mechanics to a souls game, it is really a game on its own. with that said, I truly enjoyed this game.

It has been a long time since I've played a game back to back to back. Yes, 3 times in a row NG, NG+, new charactor in NG. Many of the reviews talk about lack of levels, but i found the level design extremely creative. although you are in a factory/facility the whole time, I did enjoy the many ways the tied the various areas together via shortcuts and access doors. it all really flowed well to me.

The combat is straight forward with various weapon styles to play with. These enemies hit hard.

I did watch a youtube walk through via BlueJellowLizard, and he did a great job explaining the many mechanics of the game that were not so obvious, I feel once i learned these mechanics the game became that much more enjoyable.

Overall - Great experience with fun combat. The full play through is around 25 hours - 35-40 hours if you are completionist and like to level grind as well.

Really looking forward to Surge 2 in 2019\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is really good. In a lot of ways, I liked it more than I liked Dark Souls. It builds off of what makes Dark Souls so good. You've got really sharp combat, high-end difficulty and a good RPG in a horror setting. I think Sci-Fi makes a better setting than Fantasy for the horror - it's closer to something we can actually imagine.

The Surge is a story of technology running amok. What puts it above Dark Souls for me is that we have a character with a story. The opening storyline is awesome and sets the tone for the rest of the game. The ending seals the deal on Warren's journey.

After playing (and reviewing) Lords of the Fallen, I was really hoping The Surge would have been an improvement. It absolutely was. The Surge is more than a "Souls-like" clone ; it has a personality and charm all its own and was a fun ride the whole way through.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou know that feeling when you play a Souls game, where the first area kinda sucks but as the game progresses, enemies become more varied, combat becomes more refined and the environments become more interesting?

This game does the opposite.

Early-mid game is a blast as has you navigating the factory, searching for weapons and learning how to fight its various inhabitants.

Late game: Not so much

Late game sees the challenge ramp up, but not in a good way. Firstly the final areas are as maze-like and confusing as possible, meaning that you'll probably get lost and lose your stuff several times.

Secondly, the game's idea of a difficulty increase is to overfill enclosed spaces with 1 hit KO uber enemies.

This is all topped off by a final boss that can deplete 7/8ths of your health in a single hit without ever telegraphing his attacks.

If you can get this game for cheap in a sale, then it might be worth a play, but be warned that it'll make you feel frustrated with the end-game.\t\t\t\t\t
