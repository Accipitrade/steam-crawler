\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt the risk of overselling it, I have to say that The Surge has turned out to be one of my favorite games of recent years. I love Souls and Souls-like games and The Surge scratches that itch for me. The armor and weapon selection is more sparse compared to a Dark Souls game, but many of the elements I look for in a Souls-like game are really well done.

I felt like the combat has been sped up and refined some since Lords of the Fallen (which I believe Deck 13 also worked on), but you should expect it to still be deliberate and weighty. I suspect that this may be one thing that some people do not like about this sort of game; each action you take in combat is like an animation that has to play out before the next action is taken and uses up a portion of your stamina that regenerates over time, and this can take some getting used to if you're not accustomed to the mechanic. Fortunately, there are both a fast weapon and a light armor choice for those who might want to move as quickly as possible. I absolutely loved the severing mechanic which gives purpose to combat in a genre of game that can seem grindy or unfair (killing the same enemies over and over until you make progress). Do you target the vulnerable spot on a tough enemy to inflict more damage or try attacking a well-armored part to try to harvest a useful blueprint or valuable upgrade material? This is the sort of risk/reward mechanic I associate with this kind of game and it's very clever.

I really liked the level design, with plenty of shortcuts to unlock (usually providing a shorter route to a boss fight), and I felt the various areas had unique feels to them while still maintaining a consistent look to the science-fictiony setting. The difficulty in enemies ramps up nicely, with the occasional tough one or two thrown in, usually in an optional area, guarding a more valuable treasure. My biggest criticism is that some bosses, themselves, unfortunately felt like throwbacks to other action games with a set pattern to exploit rather than providing flowing combat.

I really enjoyed the DLC "A Walk in the Park" which added a welcome dose of humor to the otherwise serious tone of the base game. Frankly, I think The Surge did a better job parodying a theme park than a certain AAA game coughNukaWorldcough. I can tell you my bowels turned watery whenever I heard the mascots' singing getting closer and closer. I do not own the latest DLC, "The Good, the Bad, and the Augmented."

Overall, I really enjoyed this game and appreciate the attention to detail and creativity that went into it. I've only played through once, but it's still installed on my computer and I'm sure I'll play it again (there is incentive to play new game plus, as well, as apparently you can continue to upgrade your equipment as you face tougher enemies).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think one of the major reasons The Surge takes so much flak is that people are comparing it to Dark Souls. Having played The Surge and having played Dark Souls, I can confidently say that both are equally good in their own right. The problem lies in Souls players trying to play Surge like a Souls game. Since it's not a Souls game, you can't treat it like a Souls game.
My favorite part of The Surge is the limb targeting. Being able to relieve your opponents of their weapons, limbs, and head in order to obtain their armor is intuitive and fun to play with. The challenge lies in reliably hitting your opponents in the right way, as opposed to simply pouring damage into them. I just feel like the bosses didn't make full use of the limb targeting, but Deck13 is fixing that with The Surge 2, so they learned from their shortcomings.
Overall, fun combat, interesting lore, big robots, lots of fun. I definitely recommend this one for anyone up to the challenge.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you like "Souls-like" games you'll like The Surge. The mechanics aren't as refined as those and you'll probably only end up using/ needing one weapon you get early in the game and one set of armor. The walking mechanics are a bit wonky, so you can fall off some walkways in some areas and die - dodging feels more difficult than it should be. Blocking is pointless.

It's mostly smaller rooms and corridors with a few open areas, so dodging to the sides is hard sometimes. Also, the lighting is very dark in a lot of areas even with your rig lights on, and it makes seeing enemies hard to determine their attack pattern difficult.

The other issue is that the difficulty and pacing is a bit uneven and "not fair" until you memorize enemy placement - think getting jumped by a few bad guys at once since some are hiding in a dark corner. The level design can be a bit confusing, but it's the typical "go the long way" first to unlock short cuts to the medbay. The last shortcut makes going from the medbay to the boss easy.

However, the world, art design, graphics, and sound are excellent, so these issues are easily overlooked. The story is pretty decent, but suffers from being a bit to dense - like multiple CREO projects seeming to be the antagonists which only one ends up being. Also, there are lots of side characters, but little character development. Basically just help them out for the achievements.

Again, if you like "Souls-like" games, this is an easy buy especially if you like the industrial/ technological aesthetic. I definitely want to check out the sequel.\t\t\t\t\t
