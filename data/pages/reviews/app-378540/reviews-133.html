\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI recently finished my first playthrough of The Surge, and I’m working my way through NG+. It’s an excellent game, equal in quality to any other Souls-like experience I've had (as good as Nioh, even…in my opinion). It has several unique twists and additions to the traditional Souls-like mechanics.

The most interesting new mechanic to me is the surplus energy meter that’s included in the health/stamina HUD. The energy meter sort of operates like a combo meter, in that it builds up with successful strikes and then depletes over time. However, it causes you to constantly make mid-combat decisions between harvesting parts and “potion-free healing,” depending upon which implants you equip. In other words, when the surplus energy bar is charged enough, you have a choice between using that energy to execute a finishing move (and thereby have a chance at harvesting the enemy armor part you were targeting during combat, not to mention a guaranteed end to the skirmish) or using that energy to heal, and thereby save your limited supply of healing injectables for later. Theoretically, a good enough player could have infinite healing power just by managing surplus energy well.The surplus energy-based healing injectable mechanic feels a lot like the Ki Pulse in Nioh; it's a pretty addictive combat mechanic once you've got the rhythm down.

Another mechanic I've noticed involving surplus energy is the potential loss of souls upon death. In The Surge, you have a limited amount of time to return to the scene of your demise in order to reclaim your souls. The clock starts ticking when you exit the save/upgrade zone. However, more time gets added to the clock if you successfully execute a finishing move, so this may force you to spend healing injectables instead of surplus energy in order to give yourself more time to get back to your at-risk souls.

Movement and combat is fluid and feels natural (i.e. this game is nothing like Deck 13’s prior Souls-like attempt, and they clearly learned a lot after LOTF). The graphics are great, and the environmental ambiance is extremely well-done (albeit somewhat repetitive). I personally like the song that plays incessantly in the save/upgrade zones, but it would be nice if this could be changed up in the options or a mod (Edit: you can! There's a "songs" folder in the game files where you can upload a .WAV file of any song you like. Now the Medbay plays Zeppelin's No Quarter, and this small change had a pretty radical effect on the feeling in the game).

The one major complaint I would have about this game is the way implants are managed and used, which is really clunky. There’s no reason they couldn’t have made it easier to sort and arrange the various injectables that are available in the game, all of which have very similar icons and colors so its very hard to go through and select between them. When you do have multiple injectables equipped, they are all on the same button, so it’s difficult to cycle through and select them. Particularly so, given that you don't get a moment's peace during any single one of the combat scenarios, so this forces you to be really quick and precise when deploying consumables. They should have just used a traditional consumables selection and equipping interface.

A more minor complaint is the absence of backstory and lore. The events that led to the state of the game's world really deserve some exposition, and there were plenty of missed opportunities to provide that to us. NPCs have little dialogue or interactive value...they seem as clueless as we are about what caused all this chaos and mutation. Granted, you don't play a Souls-like for explicitly narrated storylines. But, here, Deck 13 was able to create a unique and curious sci-fi world, and even built in all the story mechanisms that would have been needed to tell the backstory (i.e. left-behind emails and journal entries from victims of the incident, and NPC dialogue options). But they didn't deliver on the story to bring it home. They could have taken some queues from Horizon Zero Dawn in this area. If they continue with this IP, I think some lore and backstory would go a long way to give the game a timeless nature (maybe even a prequel DLC for starters?).

The bosses seem pretty fair, but really most enemies in the game are hard. The enemy leveling gets more progressive on NG+ (as described a little more below), but the first playthrough isn't very forgiving. You need to focus on upgrading equipment and weapon proficiency to make it fun. That’s probably intentional, given that they have a parts harvesting mechanic that is very integral to the combat system, so farming and upgrading seems to be intended as a core player objective here.

The NG+ game is very well-thought out, and clearly reflects the developer’s intent to present a new experience for players who have beaten the game. In a very interesting twist, the game seems to have added significant defense and damage to some of the enemies, but left the others alone, so now levels feel a lot more like Bloodborne’s set of tiered enemy capabilities (and less like a game that is nothing but bosses and mini-bosses, which is how I felt throughout first several hours of playing Surge).

Overall, I’d say this is one of my favorite gaming experiences, and I’m pretty surprised that this game isn’t as popular, widely discussed, and highly rated as Nioh in the Souls fan community. I’ll close by saying that I highly, highly recommend “TotalBiscuit’s thoughts on The Surge” for a brilliant and scholarly nearly-hour-long analysis of this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDark Souls without the soul.

On paper, The Surge should appeal to fans of the souls series. Unfortunately, that's only where the similarities exist: on paper. In execution, The Surge lacks much of the complexity and depth of the souls games.

The combat becomes stale, in part due to the lack of enemy variety. The way they look may change as the game progresses, but it all boils down to the same rinse-and-repeat tactics as you come up against yet another randomly-armoured humanoid. The weapon profiency system means you'll probably be using the same weapon for the majority of the game, as your skill with other weapon types will be so low as to essentially negate any real desire to use them. This even stretches to different weapons of the same class, as your proficiency with a particular one-handed weapon doesn't seem to benefit you when wielding another. The dismemberment system however is a satisfying mechanic, and a cool way of collecting upgrades, weapons, armour, and loot.

The boss fights are good, if a little lacklustre, and the small number of them works considering how few unique locations there actually are in the game. Bosses for the sake of them is not always wise, and given the flow of the story and the locations you visit, the number and placement works.

The sci fi grimdark aesthetic creates a sameyness in level design, which can make exploration tedious as you traipse through yet another service duct. There's a claustrophobia which, while perhaps appropriate, made me feel the levels were intentionally maze-like in an attempt to disguise just how small they actually are. The game does have a certain amount of complexity in level layout, made manifest primarily by the traditional unlocking of shortcuts back to your safehouse, but unless you consider this mechanic to be the hallmark of interesting level design you'll soon be yearning for areas with actual personality and uniqueness. Doors which you encounter but aren't yet able to open should provide a tantalising motivation to backtrack and explore previous areas, if only you could remember in which dimly lit, pipe-filled corridor you saw it...

The NPC side missions are glorified fetch quests and have little bearing on the story or your character. And, unless you want to feel like an underpowered weakling with a new weapon halfway through the game, the rewards often just take up space in your inventory.

The Surge feels like a copy of a copy, a half-heard description, a humourous Google translate error. It's the result of a too-literal interpretation of the soulsborne ethos - "make fights hard, make levels twisty, make deaths punishing" - which misses what those games are by aiming to replicate what they do.

People are asking why I've recommended this game. I recommend The Surge because, in spite of the above, it's not an inherently bad game - just be aware of the points I've made and adjust your expectations accordingly. If, like me, you're a sworn follower of the Dark Souls games, you may be left wanting, but there's surely no harm in picking up The Surge when it inevitably hits that 50% price reduction.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great addition to the category of games like Dark Souls, this one brings us a more cohesive and understandable plot and sci fi setting without ruining the feel of these sorts of games with the ranged weapons one would expect in a sci fi setting. The Surge's combat may seem a little slow and cumbersome to some, but having to think about when is the right time to attack and which part of your enemy you should be attacking really makes the combat feel more deliberate and thoughtful rather than rewarding button spamming. The play space's ambiance is filled with great visual and auditory design that really sets the feeling of the games locations in an excellent way, be it signage, post catastrophe graffiti, or the regular intervals of internal company propaganda videos. Level design is excellent any well thought out spaces that actually feel like places where people might actually have worked and not the usual nonsensical semi-linear spaces found in many games. Unlike Dark Souls' many bonfires in a region approach, The Surge instead gives us only one Ops Center per location, and yet still keeps the feel of do you risk pushing on with much of your limited resources spent in the hopes of finding the latest shortcut back to Ops, or turn back the way you came and try another foray out after restocking with the hopes of doing better this time. This style of level design focused on exploration from the single Ops Center per location really brings a feeling of some of the best Dark Souls I level design, but doubles down on it in a wonderful manner.

Deck13's first foray into Soulslike games (Lords of the Fallen) was a passable and fun early days entry into this emerging "genre". The Surge shows they have learned a lot from what makes Soulslike games good, but also bringing to it some of their own good elements.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIncredible game, and strangely underrated. I almost didn't buy it because of the reviews, but I'm glad I listened to my instincts (sci-fi Dark Souls? yes please!). I just finished it after 31 hours of play (I play slow and methodically). Combat is insanely fun and rich - I spent hours grinding for tech scrap to level up and had a blast the whole time.

The game is good in ways it doesn't even need to be - audiologs, NPCs, all with really good voice acting - and the story is appropriately interesting and grim. It also has fantastic level design. There's only one checkpoint per area and as you progress you open up new shortcuts to get back to it.

I really hope this sells enough to warrant a sequel, because this is one of the best games I've played in years. Can't wait to dive into NG+.\t\t\t\t\t
