SUMMARY
"Welcome to CREO. We will improve not only the world, but you too. Now you will be sedated". You will play as Warren in a science-fiction action-RPG with a Dark Souls-like approach. The Surge is an underrated game that many players abandon early in the gameplay because it relies heavily on movement, timing and gear upgrading. And sometimes you may feel like an augmented janitor fighting junk-robots in an industrial scrap-yard. Still, after understanding the gameplay constraints, the game can offer 20+ hours of pretty decent entertainment. Best buy if on sale.

INFO
- This review is a personal opinion. Try the game for yourself.
- Game was played on max graphics setting and ultrawide resolution.
- Don't abandon the game after the first 1-2 hours of gameplay. Until you upgrade everything, combat will feel a bit clunky (especially if you started with Rhino). It requires a lot of timely dodge and movement and a bit of effort to upgrade so you don't feel underpowered when encountering multiple or powerful enemies. But once you got past that, the gameplay feels smooth.
- Bank quite often the Lost Tech Scraps you acquire because they have a timer before vanishing and Scrap is required to upgrade weapons, armor, your exo-skeleton and the power core.
- When selecting your exo-skeleton at the beginning of the game, please note that Rhino relies more on blocking but has a stronger armor and attack power (on the cost of more Stamina and core energy) while Lynx relies more on dodging and sprinting but has a weaker armor and attack power (therefore consuming less Stamina and core energy) but greater attack speed. If you start with Lynx, you will be able to complete your set in the starting area and you can also get the Rhino in the first level. More exo-skeletons available in the game.
- Boss fights are not hard and you can expect an average of 4 minutes per boss.

PROS
- Full ultrawide support.
- Nice dark post-apocalyptic atmosphere with a plot set in a dystopian future where robots and drones generated mass unemployement and mankind relies heavily on augmentations while the world's resources are next to zero.
- Good graphics, nice cutscenes.
- Interesting loot mechanic by selecting what body-part you intent to loot from the enemy.
- Some areas and building interiors create a good sensation of scaling.
- Play as male / female Heavy Operator or Field Technician across 7 core mission zones, with a total of 10 unique bosses (5 in the base game, 3 in the DLC "The good, the bad and the augmented" and 2 in the DLC "A walk in the Park").
- Two different endings (one of which you will understand and continue in The Surge 2 game).
- Good optimization, high fps count.
- 10 side-quests and enough collectibles for completionists.

CONS
- Expect to learn the story by interacting with holograms, audio logs and clickable dialogues with several NPCs. The game has only two cutscenes, the opening and the ending.
- Bosses' design is not the smartest in gaming, they are not epic, you would expect something bigger, smarter and much more impressive.
- No multiplayer, no co-op. One may have expected at least a brawler arena for it, vs players or AI.
- Some incoming attacks will certainly come through no matter what you do, they are kind of "inserted" in your time-gated block and dodge system (working as intended).
- Although large enough, the world does not deliver a great variety of level design and scenery.
- Enemy mob encounters and combat may feel repetitive quite early in the game.
- Camera must be properly adjusted from options to get a satisfactory view.
- Sometimes can be hard to find proper map directions towards objectives.
- Three payed DLCs (two of them required if you wanted more content).

LONG REVIEW: WILL LEAVE TL;DR @ END OF ACTUAL REVIEW

This review will also make comparisons and references to this game's sequel The Surge 2, which I am currently playing at the time of this review. While there won't be any spoilers for either game, you can use this review to see if you want to get this game and/or The Surge 2

-------------------------------------------------

The Surge is honestly one of the better deviations from the Dark Souls formula. There are a bunch of systems in play here that, while much more improved in The Surge 2, make it a more interesting game to play than most other soulslikes that aren't made by From Software.

-COMBAT

This is where the Surge really shines compared to other Soulslikes. The gimmick of this game is that you can defeat enemies by slicing parts of their bodies off in order to gain materials and schematics for armor pieces and weapons (you get weapons right away from the enemy but you have to upgrade them). This means you have to target specific parts to get what you need to cut, usually meaning you have to target armored pieces your attacks will bounce off with little impact and damage, giving a bit of a risk and reward to combat. There's a bunch of nerdier deeper mechanics to combat i could go over like the impact/stability system that can make using heavy armor worth it in some cases or how dodging has fewer i-frames making every fight not just some stupid "just dodge through the attack herp derp" scenario, but this review is gonna be long enough. Needless to say despite the camera being similar, there's more that goes into the combat than just being a dark souls clone.

With all this being said, it's not perfect. There's still some issues with combat that can come up. Certain enemies and bosses have attacks that are deliberately timed awkwardly so you can't dodges or block properly at correct intervals . As some familiar with animation who is used to characters having a certain flow to their movements, this kind of abrupt pacing for attacks is annoying to put it lightly. There's also an option to dodge by hopping up or ducking while holding the block button which is used extremely sparingly to the point of being pointless if not for a certain boss using it. This was a mechanic I'm glad to say isn't used in The Surge 2, being replaced with directional blocking (something I still need to get used to) and the level design for the game, in it's effort to seem like a tangible place, results in some unfavorable locations to fight enemies in, such as narrow walkways over lethal drops, or similarly small spaces with enemies that have strong homing attacks or wide swinging blows where you have very little room to avoid. It's definitely a beginners trap when it comes to encounters, so it will take an extremely long time for fundamentals of combat to really cement themselves, especially when you start encountering the end game enemies that will really test your patiences with having some bizarre attack patterns that are frustrating.

-Character Progression

The description for the Steam Page mentioning "making yourself stronger through a fresh take on leveling-up" is not an inaccurate statement. As you defeat enemies search the environment for loot, you gain Scrap, this game's currency. You use it to not only increase your rig level (which allows you to apply more energy consuming armor pieces and implants [we'll get to this]), but craft and upgrade the weapons and armor you acquire over the course of your playthrough.

Weapons and armor aren't the only way you upgrade your character and build. Instead of upgrading a fixed stat when you level up, you use a system called Implants, where you can use extra energy from your rig to install these implants to give you some passive buffs that can synergize with one another to make some interesting builds.

-STORY/SETTING

The other thing that made this souls-like stand out was that it was one of the first that went for a sci-fi setting instead of fantasy. Another notable thing about the game is that you don't customize your character (unlike the sequel). Instead you play the role of Warren, a cripple who was recently hired by Creo to put himself in a Exo Rig, an electronic rig that gives you super strength and turns you into a walking power tool. When unfortunately after the "installation" (pretty uncomfortable moment) a power surge results in Warren passing out and waking up god knows how long after in a scrap yard on the outskirts on the complex with almost all the employees and robotics going ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ insane, so Warren has to survive grabbing as many of the equipment off his would-be cohorts turned mechanical nightmares to figure out what happened and to answer the question: What is Project Resolve?

All in all a pretty straight to the point story. Newbie who got caught up in events out of his control, try to survive, work your way up the food chain to kick ass and beat the big bad. Nothing new for a souls-like style story. What makes it different in this game is that Warren is able to talk to these characters with a legitimate personality and drive, even showing some agency and slight concern for those around him (only really talks in dialogue options though). Unfortunately you don't continue playing as him in The Surge 2, opting for the silent customizable protagonist with no vocal lines in dialogue trees (Warren is still in The Surge 2 as an important character, just not playable). While still a bit underwhelming you still feel there is a character to Warren (you'll even get a fully fleshed out backstory for Warren with the A Walk in the Park DLC).

The real character to this game is the environment. It truly feels like you're exploring a soaring metropolis-sized factory complex owned by an industrial corporation. Lots of cement, support beams, painted lines and signs everywhere in the actual industrial zones, with fancy ornate and cozy office aesthetics when you reach the public/corporate office levels, all connected between the walls be sprawling maintenance tunnels that, while feeling a bit copy pasted, help to establish that this place is meant to be functional as much as have form. I truly felt like I was exploring a futuristic job site that I would work at. As opposed to a ruined future city in the sequel (if it isn't apparent at this point, The Surge 2 fails in the story and setting departments) which makes everything feel disjointed. Where the first game succeeds over it's successor however is in making it's setting, story and gameplay combine together into a complete package (with the DLC of course, I genuinely believe if you just get the base game without at least the A Walk in the Park DLC you're missing out)

CONCLUSION

While suffering from "First Game Syndrome" as most games do, I believe it's fair to say that The Surge has more merits to it than just being "a sci-fi Dark Souls clone". Hell I wouldn't have made a 5 paragraph essay of a review trying to sell you on this game if I didn't. That being said if you want something with better gameplay that is already here, go straight to the sequel. It's improvements to actual combat and gameplay nearly overshadow the general downgrade to story. If you want an enjoyable narrative that sells you on it's setting in everything from it's environment to the enemies you fight in it, this is the game you want to start with. (And at the very least get the A Walk in the Park DLC to go with it.) This game is absolutely a steal if you get it on sale and well worth the price, especially if you're looking for a sci-fi souls like that isn't Hellpoint. lol

---------------------------------------------------------

TL;DR Good Sci-Fi souls like that does things differently; Get the sequel for better gameplay, get this game and the A Walk in the Park DLC for a better story. Absolute steal if on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
