\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe first hours were awesome! It is a souls-like game, with several pros:
1) You get more and more experiences with your weapons, which means, they automatically do more damage, without the need to grind.
2) You aim at the enemies, when going for quick kills targeting unarmored parts, when grinding items targeting armored parts.
3) You gain more farm when on a killing spree. This means, if you want to farm and you are skilled, this is automatically working extremely fast.

But there are drawbacks:
1) The levels are too complicated - it is extremely difficult to remember your paths, e.g. to get to your corpse, as all paths look very similar.
2) There are some doors, which you cannot open, because you have overseen that your drone could open them - there is no hint, and I oversaw the drone sign at every possibility. What is the purpose of this? It is not fun to look around and spot for these weird signs...
3) Especially in the end the paths are so unclear and with one wrong step you fall down and die. The last few ours really ruined the game for me.

So for me this way 30 hours of fun followed by some not so funny hours.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll start off with a few tidbits some people seem to think are relevant:
Out of the entire so-called "Soulborne" series, I've only played Dark Souls III and all its DLC, which I liked a lot.
I'm on NG+ in this game.
I am primarily a PvE player in all games I play.

This game is not like Dark Souls. It does not try to be, and it does not want to be. To even say that it borrows elements from Dark Souls because it's hard is like saying that Dark Souls borrows elements from World of Warcraft because they're both RPGs. However, because many people think of this as "another Souls clone", I will include comparisons to Dark Souls at the end of each pro.

Pros:

The Surge does a good job of storytelling, if you're paying attention. You can get a pretty clear picture of what's going on through audio logs, announcements, NPCs, watching some in-game propaganda videos, or, if you're feeling up to it, reading flavor text. However, the story is not given to you all at the same time, or even in a linear manner. You're going to have bits and pieces out of sequence that you need to put together, like a puzzle. This is different from most other games, but, again, if you're paying attention, you'll come to like it.
This is different from Dark Souls, in that storytelling comes primarily from cutscenes and tons of flavor text.

Level design is mostly realistic. You're stuck in a factory, so there are catwalks, deadly drops, heavy machinery, crates, elevators, and your weapons are just tools being used as weapons. Detailed maps can be found in some areas, but mostly you'll find arrows pointing you in one direction or another. If you're an immersive gamer, like me, you'll love it.
This is no different from Dark Souls, with the exception that non-boss enemies in The Surge can be primarily found in tight spaces, leaving less room for error.

The looting system is deterministic, which is great, and realistic. If you want an armor schematic, you need to cut off pieces of it from enemies who wear it.
If you want components to craft and upgrade your current armor, you need to cut off the same pieces from enemies. If you want leg upgrades, you have to cut off the legs of enemies wearing leg armor. If you want torso upgrades, same thing. Unlike trying to get schematics, you do not need to cut off parts from enemies wearing the same armor as you.
This is different from Dark Souls, in that drops are almost completely random, with few exceptions.

Combat is also realistic, to some degree. Staggering enemies is difficult with lighter weapons with high attack speeds, but at the same time, heavier weapons capable of staggering enemies are harder to hit due to their low attack speed. Furthermore, to get a successful cut, you'll need to weaken enemy body parts. You also can't just back off from an attack past a certain point, due to momentum. Blocking also takes a heavy toll on your stamina, and just holding the block will slowly deplete your stamina. You are, after all, using heavy machinery as weapons. You need to be careful about when to attack and which weapon to use against an enemy.
This is different from Dark Souls, where you can cancel just about any attack by rolling, eliminating the risk of going "all in", and shields simply slow stamina regeneration..

The implant and armor systems are realistic. Your implants need power, but your RIG also needs power to help you move around, so heavy armor will leave you unable to use many implants, while implants will force you to use light armor. That is, unless you farm scrap and level up until you can have both. However, armor still has drawbacks. Heavy armor typically increases stamina and energy consumption and may even decrease damage output.
This is different from Dark Souls, where you need to meet attribute requirements to effectively use weapons and armor, yet suffer no penalties as long as your load capacity is high enough, unless otherwise specified by your weapons and armor.

Cons:

Storytelling has some flaws, primarily in the form of NPCs. Every NPC you talk to says they don't know what's going on, which gets tedious after a while. Although it is realistic, given the situation, it's also frustrating for players due to the inclusion of a "What's going on here?" dialogue option with every single NPC you meet.

Level design can be confusing. Although the developers had the right idea by limiting the amount of maps and such, there's a lack of signs that is unrealistic in a large manufacturing complex where people are expected to move around on foot.

Combat can be frustrating at first in NG+. In NG, you start with what amounts to Mk 0 armor and weapons, and enemies (damage, HP, armor) are scaled to that. Throughout NG, you can upgrade your weapons and armor all the way to Mk V by finding upgrade items called "nano cores". However, there's a limited number of these, so you'll most likely just upgrade your armor and weapons to Mk IV. In NG+, however, the "baseline" from which enemies in the first area are scaled is now Mk VI, meaning that your Mk IV armor and weapons are effectively worse by comparison than your "Mk 0" armor and weapons from NG. This can lead to some quick, frustrating deaths in the early levels, until you upgrade everything to Mk VI.

Upgrading can also be frustrating in NG+. Because you can't farm Mk I - Mk IV components any more, you jump straight to Mk VI. However, this also incurs a massive increase in scrap costs, since you're effectively upgrading multiple times all at once. If you plan on using multiple weapons in NG+, but didn't previously upgrade them, you may spend a lot of time farming.



All in all, this is a good game, well worth the money. Just don't think it's Dark Souls, because it's not.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe amount of hate and dissapointment I feel for this game has overwhelmed my usually quiet and accepting nature. This is a combination of venting and tilted criticizing. Definitely spoilers in here. For those that still like the game despite all the faults, I am truely jealous.

I really want to like this game but it is so boring and frustrating. It feels like the souls series with a scifi motif slapped on. The potential of the genre is constantly at odds with the game mechanics. I don't feel like im wearing a powerful exosuit, the stamina bar is constant reminder of that. Even more frustrating when I fight the hyperactive enemies, like the first security types you can fight. Them dudes are relentless, plus they even flip right over you. What the hell. The damage you deal feels pathetic when compared to when the enemy can destroy you in 2 or 3 hits. You go for a quick weapon, your combo is almost guaranteed to be interrupted after 2 hits cause it can't stagger enemies for â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥. Go for a heavy weapon and the wind up for most moves is tremendous in its wait time which would have been forgivable if it actually did more damage than what they do. The drone you get is absolutely useless, the excuse being you got a defective one, but you can't fix it. You must beat the enemy to a pulp to charge a bar so the drone can do something noticable, but by that point your better off using the cutting function. When you try to use if for kiting enemies it seem to miss an awful lot as well. The lack of customization is astounding. You supposedly have machine that can print stuff on a nanoscale level, yet you are restricted to a finite number of blueprints, and you still need certain parts to construct things. Many places in the game have enviromental area hazards that hurt you for standing in them. I had originally spent a lot of time farming and building the Scarab suit cause it had good stats and looked pretty cool. But all the acid puddles kept chipping at my health which could already be obliterated in a couple moves from anything. Instead being able to customize my suit, I am forced to build one I didn't give a damn about. So I waste a ton more time building and eviromental suit, which has crummy stats compared to Scarab, but no more acid burns. It even protects me from some poison gas later on. Couple more areas in the game give me a chip I can install on the rig that says I can breath poison gas. I'm too mad to see if it works in the earlier areas, thanks for wasting my time. Visually the game looks awesome, lots of detail, lots of tech. Too bad half of it lost to the damn darkness. Half the game is spent in the maze that is acess tunnels many of which are pitch black. But wait the rig has enough power to short circuit massive electical conduits, surely it could power a few lights. Not a chance, ilumination distance is garbage and then somebody had to give each suit its own color. Looks good on the LEDs all over the suit, but not when I'm trying to see down a hall way. At least I know how Rudolph feels, thanks square hat suit.

But hey, I got 16 hrs in this damn thing. Why do I keep playing, maybe the story's good? It's blander than ovecooked oatmeal. First you got Warren, starts with him watching an ad about some awesome company. Alright so guy's just looking for a job, but then boom. You get control and turns out dudes in a wheelchair, Warren got at least 3 times more interesting than the stereotypical white hero dude I was expecting. You wheel his ass in and make choice between and quick rig or powerful one and just like that he's on a hightech surgical apparatus. No interview, no meeting the boss here ya go. Surgery starts, and holy crap suddenly its a horror film. No numbing, no anesthesia, they just start bolting the thing to him. So he passes out when they plug the last bit into his head and he wakes up to see a drone pickin at him. He kicks it away and stands, woah! But other than a brief smile that's it. He mentions it once to someone over a holo communication and that's it. Nothing about Warren it ever mentioned again. Where he came frome, what he did before, family he might of had, nothing. He turns right into stoic white hero dude that moves forward just cause. Everyone else you run into is pathetic or crazy and needs your help. So begins the mountain of whys that are either ignored or have unsatisfying answers that are mostly assumed. Why did the system dispose of Warren, advanced technology couldn't tell he was still alive? People don't check new employees? Why does Warren even have acess to the OPS stations then. One of the first guys you meet is stuck cause his own rig is locked when it ran out of power. It that can happen why are all the crazies still running about? I assume Warren's rig stays charged because you visit OPS all the time, but OPS locks up when a crazy is nearby. Why are the robots attacking you? A lot of the audio logs hint that people when nuts because of chemical injected into the workers to increase productivity, how does that affect robots? Why are the robots and crazies not fighting each other? Why aren't the crazies fighting each other for that matter. All the sane-ish people you meet only have basic rigs on, why aren't they suited up? You meet a women plain rigged searching about through some trash. First interaction is verbal hostility boasting that she took down some crazies. Alright interesting character here maybe? Wrong, just more whys. Looking for a weapon, really? In the trash on a stair case? She has to have been working here for awhile, but some how forgot where all the tools were? Can't use OPS the build one? Just down that staircase is some security guards. They even talk to you telling Warren to leave. Why doesn't Warren talk back, ask questions, maybe even let them know he's sane. Nothing, just fight them. If enemies are nuts why do they run back to designated spots to keep shooting at you when you try to kite them. It's a crappy game mechanic that contradicts whats happening in the world. If they're insane why are they set on guarding that one spot? Why can't Warren use the same weapon to shoot back? Many enemies are missing parts of a suit set. Why don't they suffer in evironments that punish you? A clean up crew of sorts shows up later on to hide evidence. Why are they missing armor parts? Why do they still act and sound like crazies? Why can't I get their weapons? This isn't a magical world. Why do enemies come back when you visit OPS. Seems like a lazy piggyback on the coattails of a popular game mechanic that doesn't make sense here. Instead of leeching off those game mechanics of a fantastical world they had the potential to use ideas granted by scifi. Warren is in a facility where they build and develop all kinds of science. Yet everyone he meets and Warren himself utilize none of it. Instead it's just hack and slash to victory, the whole thing feels lacking.

Ugh, I'm all burnt out now. I could go on but that's more than enough whining. Probably some fault lies with me for expecting something different. Not worth $65, only playing it out of spite for my money and already wasted time.\t\t\t\t\t
