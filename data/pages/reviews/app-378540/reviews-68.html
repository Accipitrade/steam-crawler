The major difference (and major improvement, imo), is that you can loot specific armor/material by targeting enemies and slicing off the corresponding limbs. You see an enemy with a cool looking helmet? Slice it off and wear it yourself. But be warned, collecting armor sets in this game can be quite addictive and time-consuming.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTl;dr It's fun, but punishing.
Let's call the genre "Stamina Pattern Combat" or "SPC". So, like most all SPCs, it's very obviously inspired by the SoulsBorne series by From Software. That alone gets some idea for how the game plays. So, I'll just do a pro's and con's list to help making a purchase decision:

PROS
+As long as you read the floating UI, the game has a tutorial zone that explains basic mechanics.
+Armor and weapons are split into 1 of 3 or 6 classes respectively, thus setting a good expectation of how you're play style(s) will work.
+Armor and weapons have enough detailed stats that you can min-max builds.
+You can further customize your build using implants of various effects. You'll have to look at a wiki to see them all.
+Implants and their buffs/quantities stack with more copies.
+Loot farming is much simpler than other SPC games I've played; You want it? Cut it from their cold, dead, metal hands, arms, legs, or heads.
+The longer you go without using a safe area, the more you're rewarded for your skill/brazenness; The game increases a scrap(money) multiplier that goes up the more you have on you and the more you kill.
+You can bank/store your scrap in the safe area, resetting your multiplier, but saving that scrap for later.
+Jumping is easier than in Dark Souls
+Having a full set of a given armor name (ex Wearing the head, arms, legs and chest of the Lynx armor) will give unique buffs

CONS
-Basically all enemies can wreck you; You can walk around a corner, be surprised by an industrial hammer to the face, and then be loading back into the safe area before you can complain about balance issues
-It's incredibly easy to lose your way in many of the environments. Very interconnected, but not enough distinguishing landmarks to really remember.
-It's also incredibly easy to forget or never know what your goal is and why it's a goal
-Many enemies have poor telegraphing of attacks
-Blocking is alright, but you can't move while doing it, and there's only a very poorly explained/implemented counter-attack mechanic
-You only have a limited time (a bit over 2min) to reclaim your stuff after you die (but kills along the way add to the timer)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: interesting addition to the soul-like genre, with a sci-fi theme that asks interesting questions but is weak in delivery. Gameplay has some depth but is clunky. 6.3/10

Story (7/10): The surge takes place on the verge of an apocalyptic event and you are smack dap in the middle of it. However, you’re not the big master chief type hero who is locked and loaded to save the day. You’re just the average joe on the first day of the job when everything goes to hell. The story is strongest in its “concept” as well as its attempt to explore difficult relevant questions such as global warming, ethics in science, government corruption, artificial intelligence, concepts of the self, and more. It will really make you think. It is weakest in its delivery. This is one of those games that would benefit from some more cutscenes to tie some of its plot points together, draw the player into the world, create a more cinematic experience, and really make it something special. For the players that like to go through games with a fine combed brush and are okay with a lot of ambiguity, this may not be such a big problem.

Gameplay (6/10): The Surge is similar to Dark-Souls in its approach to gameplay. Understanding your opponents through observation, trial and error, and putting together a game plan is paramount to success. You will get one-shot if you are not a student of the game. Boss fights are satisfying, albeit there are only a few. The mechanics offer a satisfying gritty feel, akin to games of space marine or gears of war; the brutality is delivered well. Unfortunately, it is clunky. While it is enjoyable to string together satisfying combos, the game does not allow you to leave the animation once initiated. This discourages players from attempting to string together satisfying combinations and react quickly to scenarios. The block mechanic is also unreliable and often fatal if used improperly. This quickly devolves the game into a repetitive process of hitting an enemy once, then backing away.

Replay ability (6/10): The games form of replay ability relies heavily on the completionist gamers. To the game’s credit, there is a depth of equipment, weapons, and exo-suits to explore and master. That is genuinely fun. Unfortunately, resources are often scarce and unless you want to grind for parts for hours, you will only have enough to fully upgrade one weapon and suit by the final stages of the game.

Deck 13 has produced an interesting addition to the soul-like genre. For the fellow sci-fi nerds out there, The Surge offers an interesting and thought-provoking story, brutal and challenging gameplay, and some replay ability. I would recommend picking up the game when it goes on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
