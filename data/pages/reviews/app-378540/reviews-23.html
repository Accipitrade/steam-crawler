Very unsatisfying

The combat feels weird. You click attack once and your not-so-crippled character swings once. You hit it again and he does a second swing...followed by another very slow attack. It basically made fighting impossible because you have to wait for the animation to end. Maybe the faster weapons were better, but equipment is very rare in this game and I love heavy weapons anyway. heavy hammers, GS and UGS in Dark Souls are waaaay better.

Level design is confusing. I went through the whole first location looking for the boss, thinking that I must have missed a door or something, but no... He spawned in the middle of the map, but only after I've interacted with a random pipe . These pipes were so annoying. You need high level to unlock some of them (because nobody likes speed-running a souls-like game) and the game doesn't really tell you what they do.

Then we have equipment ...Yeah, I wouldn't say that there is a variety of weapons and armours to choose from. There are just 2 "classes" of equipment, which basically mean light and heavy. You could mix them up to make something interesting, but equipping a full set gives you a decent bonus so why would you experiment?
At least it's easy to find it. You just look for a exosuit-zombie and hack off one of its limbs to get his armour or weapon. The problem is that there isn't a lot of variation so you get like 1-2 armor set per map and like a weapon every now and then. But hey, at least the bosses always drop a weapon.

Also wtf is going on with stats & leveling up ? Leveling up only gives you the ability to equip better stat enhancers/more estus flasks, but you still need to find them first and since everything is so rare in this game you can't just grind your way up. You still need to get through the difficult part and look around to actually "level up".

I would also like to mention the difficulty . I don't know if it's because I played that crap for less than 10 hours or because I'm a noob, but I think it's just because of poor design. Like, DS1 had some annoying enemies and places that felt unfair instead of challenging, but throughout all DS games I found the difficulty to be mostly OK (even easy after learning Miyazaki's tricks). Here it's just frustration and shouting "What?! Why did you do that???" all the time. Your character does unexpected attacks that make you unable to move or lounges forward and falls to his death. I could just memorise the animations, but I'd rather write that pathetic review. The enemies' attacks are annoying, too. They don't really communicate to you that they're about to attack, but that could just be the ♥♥♥♥♥♥ camera in the game. Again, I could just memorise their combos, but I'd rather focus on memorising my textbooks and then play games in my free-time. At least they can't really stunlock you, but you can't stunlock them...?

Also the opening surgery-without-anesthesia scene felt very generic and unnecessary (why are they drilling the bolts in his body with clothes on?? How is he ever going to change... or pee??)


3/10, I tried giving it a chance, but it never delivered.\t\t\t\t\t

“This law is immutable. The user always pays.”
~T.R. Napper, Neon Leviathan

So, Souls-like games... It's like every second game nowadays is Souls-like. After Hidetaka Miyazaki and FromSoftware released the original spiritual successor to their King's Field series, a game called Demon's Souls in 2009, the world finally noticed such niche products and... let's just say those aren't that niche anymore. Everybody's making such games. From big AAA companies like Respawn Entertainment (Jedi: Fallen Order) to tiny little indie teams like RuneHeads (Fall of Light). It's not like it's a bad thing per se (it's all about demand and supply after all), but... let's just say that the surprise element just isn't there in cases like The Surge. Because yes, what we've got ourselves here is yet another Souls-like game. This time from Focus Home Interactive and Deck 13, creators of Ankh and Jack Keane.

And you know what? For Focus Home Interactive / Deck 13 duo, this game is not really that bad. Focus is known for publishing all sorts of low budget second-grade games without caring much about quality, while Deck 13 is a pretty small team that come with only 65 employees. Most of their games look pretty cheap and Moorhuhn: Tiger and Chicken is a nice example of that. And yet, The Surge looks pretty competitive. I can't say it's the best-looking game on market or something like that, of course. When you compare it with those huge AAA titles from 2017, there's nothing good to say here, but still, it looks competitive and I don't think we can ask more from Focus Home Interactive / Deck 13 duo.

I also loved the fact that our main character is handicapped. I mean, seriously, people who live with similar problems, deserved their own representation in video games and I must admit, Deck 13 totally played it smart here. The Surge tells us about yet another dystopian future. One of those from popular cyberpunk novels. You know the drill. Resource depletion, big corporations, evil scientists, dark conspiracy, augmentations... Even though, like Dark Souls series, The Sourge won't tell you the story directly (you learn about things mostly by collecting various audio logs), it's pretty much everything you may want from such stuff. A bit too generic, sure, but still. And with our main guy being a handicapped person who just got himself his own customizable exoskeleton? It totally works.

Unfortunately, when it's about actual gameplay... Well, I can't say that this game is a complete garbage or something like that. Especially since The Surge is not really Deck 13's first attempt to create something like that. The first one was a game called Lords of the Fallen and... well, let's just say that the biggest problem with The Surge is the fact that Deck 13 didn't learn from their mistakes. Like... at all. Lords of the Fallen was a pretty decent, yet mediocre game that, while being a pretty solid Souls-like experience, was very limited. Character creation wasn't a thing, we were unable to manually edit our stats, there was no co-op or any other online features and so on. We just chose ourselves magic and weapon types and that was it. Now, here come The Surge and... it's pretty much the same exact thing.

No, seriously. When it's about The Surge, it's hard not to remember another studio that worked under Focus Home Interactive. I'm talking about Spiders. You know, the guys who created an action RPG called Mars: War Logs and then produced copy / pasted clones of it for many years. Bound by Flame, The Technomancer, GreedFall... It was the same exact thing over and over again. Same gameplay mechanics, same menus, same everything. Sure, it's not nearly as bad in The Surge. Spiders were shameless shovelware makers. While Deck 13 actually tried here. And yet, since the very first screen, where you pick up one of the save slots, you'll be like “Now wait just a minute... it's a bloody Lords of the Fallen!” Because seriously, some things (including menus) are almost completely identical with what we had in Lords of the Fallen.

Exoskeleton helps, though. The Surge is not just Lords of the Fallen in cyperpunk setting. It actually has quite a lot of its own unique mechanics. For example, battles now became all about weak points. You dance around your enemy like Muhammad Ali, you select the weak point with the right stick and then you punch. Hard. Because yes, no firearms here. Only different types of melee weapons. Sure, battle mechanics here is still pretty clunky and come with somewhat cheap aftertaste, but... let's just admit it, when it's about Souls-like games, it's, like, a part of the thing. I mean, even in FromSoftware's titles battle mechanics can be this way, so... yeah. Nothing groundbreaking here and yet it works. Unfortunately, many other things don't.

For example, leveling in this game revolves around so-called core power. Long story short, you grind for scrap, then you bring it to MedBay (which serves as a save hub) and you use it to upgrade your core. Why? Because you'll need core power to equip better gear and implants, which will provide you with various benefits. Some implants will improve your health, the others will help you locate items on levels and so on. And all that will require core power. So far so good, but you'll also need core power to unlock certain doors. And that, my friends, is a huge mistake. See, that was part of the magic in Souls-like games. If you don't have much skill, you can just grind a lot and overpower your enemies there. At least when it's about low difficulties. But if you're hardcore, then you can go directly to the final boss without leveling up at all. Just because you're that radical. In other words, the game gave you a lot of freedom and allowed you to choose your own play style. The Surge, on the other hand? It's totally different. You need to level up because otherwise, the game just won't let you open certain doors.

I can see why they did that, though. See, The Surge comes with pretty much the same amount of maps with Lords of the Fallen (Lords of the Fallen had five, while The Surge offers six, as long as we won't count DLC). Naturally, they wanted to make it feel longer than it actually is. Because people rarely pay the full price for games that take less than ten hours to beat. Well, they've got something about 15 (or maybe even 20) here, but still, it was a big mistake. It also made co-op impossible and, like Lords of the Fallen, this game doesn't have any online features. So, if you're one of those evil guys who just love invading other people's games, there's nothing for you here.

That's just a thing about this game. It's very limited. Heck, there's no even proper map here. Not even the basic one. It's not like levels here are that complicated, but... you know. They can be tricky here and there, especially since there's no even compass here and therefore you'll need to listen carefully and remember where to go, but it just shows how limited this game is. No map, not enough optional content, no proper character creation, no co-op, no invading, only two classes to choose from and only six levels that'll take minutes to cross if you'll decide to ignore enemies. I can't say that this game is bad and if you're out of Souls-like games, you may consider picking this one up, especially when it's on sale, but all in all, it's just not good enough for me to give it a thumb up. Dixi.\t\t\t\t\t
