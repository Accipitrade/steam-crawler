\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of my favorite single player games.

It isn't perfect, but whatever flaws it has do not detract much from the gameplay. I really enjoy the feeling of progression in any role playing game, and The Surge does not disappoint. The blueprints for new gear are very easy to acquire, allowing you to try out many builds without regrets. Most armor sets look very cool (other than lynx and scarab), which is very important for people like me who care a little bit too much about style.

Every weapon or armor piece can be upgraded, allowing you to carry your favorite gear through multiple stages of the game. However, for me, this was often a downside rather than an upside. It is entirely possible to play through the entire game with the starter armor and a weapon from the first area. There is not much incentive to switch weapons other than cool visuals or higher proficiency scaling. This is less of a problem with armor, as the unique set bonuses give each set a unique flavor, but for weapons, there isn't much differentiation. Weapons of the same type have very similar combos, so other than visuals, weapons don't have a lot of personality.

For me, it is the sci fi setting and the sense of progression which really makes this game shine. Although you spend most of your time in Central Production B, my least favorite area in the game, every other area looks great. Most of your time is spent focused on combat, but in the few respites, you can appreciate the well realized world of the CREO complex.

As someone who loves the feeling of growing stronger in the game, The Surge is very satisfying. Often, a few difficult enemies are placed in each area, and those enemies become the normal foes in later areas. This is very effective and really helps accentuate the growth of your skills and equipment when the enemies you once feared are now commonplace. By the end of the game, I was amazed at how the enemies that once seemed unbeatable felt normal once I had gotten used to them.

Overall, I really enjoyed it! Great game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is my favorite Souls-like so far, yet I still believe FromSoftware games are better in general and particularly in design quality. I have yet to try The Surge 2 and Nioh 2.

So the good:
- Awesome atmosphere and world building.
- Awesome labyrinth level design, the best I have found in that regard.
- The implant system it is great to fit your character to your play style.
- Some sets are awesome.

The bad:
- Even with all the DLCs there is not much content. It is enough for a couple of playthroughs, but kind of lacks.
- The combat can be kind of repetitive and trivial and cutting limbs gets old after some hours.
- The testing chamber can be quite boring and repetitive.
- A couple of areas have too much poison or enemies in groups.
- The game is pretty much unbalanced, so there are many play style options that are worthless.
- Very few bosses and enemy diversity.

The ugly:
- Most of the bosses are kind of ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥.
- Many animation attacks are kind of broken: too fast, too wide, too strong.
- The lockon mechanism is terrible when you have multiple enemies and you want to focus on one specifically (which is very often in late game). This comes from using the same binding as the limb selection, which is fine, but the initial enemy selection is not viable to do in the short time you get when ambushed.\t\t\t\t\t

I am still kind of halfway with this game to be honest - 50/50. Nonetheless, if you've played any Dark Souls or simply enjoy facing a good challenge, then this game is definitely worth picking up.

Let me break down a few in-game choices that the developers went with.

The gameplay

The RPG elements of the game have been pretty fun - you can target a specific part of the enemies' body and cut it out in order to learn the schematic for it (so you can build that part with enough materials) or in some cases get it after chopping that part of (you can get either piece of armour or weapon).

However, when it comes to assembling or upgrading a certain part, I was confused for a long time by what parts I needed and most importantly where to get them! The names of those parts were just confusing - I needed to get Mk. Alloy I and then there was verison II and other weapons and gear required version III and then there was version IV...ugh. And there are multiple weird / unmemorable names of those parts. And yeah, the game doesn't explain where to get them and what enemies drop what parts. So I was just relying on sheer luck to get one of the parts that will help me upgrade or build weapons and armour.

The cool part about weapons though is that they get upgraded (in damage) the more you fight the enemies! So your deaths aren't in vain here and you actually can scale up the damage of a weapon of your choice. And you don’t need to repair any items!

Also, just like in Dark Souls, you acquire tech scrap (souls) to upgrade your level and unlock more slots into which you can insert various chips (like rings in Dark Souls) that will help boost your stamina, health, energy or damage of your weapon.

Boss fights had been fun but not really challenging. Most of them I beat on 3rd or 5th try. It's the NPCs that were most challenging for me but I like where the devs went with this game to freshen things up (for those who've played many Dark Souls games).

So all in all, The Surge is less punishable than, for example, Dark Souls Remastered and you'll tear less of your hair out when playing it!

The art / concept

This is where I got a bit disappointed to be honest as I had imagined the game to be like that movie The Virus from 1999 where aliens invaded the ship and started converting humans into robots. Seriously, look up a few snapshots from that movie and you’ll know what I mean. I really wish The Surge would have had this sort of a concept where enemies would be humans sliced into a metal skeleton and be those walking atrocities covered in blood and guts, screaming for help and attacking you against their will (because they cannot control what their actions as the alien AI has control over them). That would have been so much more awesome!

Instead, we get to fight against regular humans in robot armour and machines (as bosses). Meh. This franchise had so much potential! Though I can understand why they had not gone with The Virus movie concept as artistically and budget wise it would have been much harder to pull off I reckon.

The story & soundtrack

I like how they kept the story really vague and complemented the moody and unsettling atmosphere with a decent, gloomy music score. However, every NPC that I’ve spoken to had literally told me they had no idea what was going on. Hmm...at least little something would have been nice! But yeah, I saw absolutely no point in talking to anyone in the game. And that’s sad. Even Dark Souls Remastered had so much more going on in terms of the story. So yeah, don’t buy this game if you’re hoping to revel with a new, unique story.

My verdict

If you’re a fan of Dark Souls games and see this puppy on sale - go grab and try it out. I reckon it will be worth your time!\t\t\t\t\t

This is a good game but there are a lot of things preventing it from being a GREAT game and I'm really just about to vent so the rest of this isn't that important.

The level design is god awful. It looks fantastic and I would say it's mostly believable but it's a gosh darn maze and just like everyone else who's played, I've gotten to points in the game where I am just legitimately lost and have no idea which direction I should be going. Every location looks pretty much the same and it's full of narrow enclosed corridors so you can never really get a handle on where you are compared to the rest of the area. The last area in particular is just a bunch of those same corridors used throughout the entire game which occasionally lead back to the main open area. And what makes this exponentially worse is the use of elevators EVERYWHERE. Keeping your sense of direction while playing this game is almost impossible and to understand exactly where you are in most portions of the game would require a fully rendered 3D map.

Luckily sometimes they display a map on the wall showing you where you are... that consists of maybe like 4 squares with labels... They're worthless for the most part.

I've read a few posts about how confusing the level design is and what some people have responded with is the use of signs on the walls making it more manageable. There are signs every now and then and they are INCREDIBLY helpful most of the time. But they're too few and far in between. There are two big issues with these signs however. Firstly, if you have two of something like a train station, the signs are too basic to tell you which train station it's directing you to. There's one level that has three trains and I just gave up on finding one of them when I needed to backtrack. The second is a very specific gripe that I have personally. When you get to the R&D area there's a sign pointing back to the checkpoint area (OPS) near the top of the entire level. When you follow it, it leads you back to the exhibit room which is this massive room with the checkpoint at the very bottom. You are at the top with no means of getting to the bottom aside from jumping off. Were regular employees expected to follow this sign and then jump off to get back to OPS? That doesn't make sense, this sign does not make sense. This sign does not direct you back to OPS and should not be here.

Backtracking is a nightmare and since there's content you have to go back for after you've reached certain levels, the fact that there is no kind of fast travel for these enormous areas seems like a huge design oversight. They're basically punishing you for wanting to see all the game has to offer and/or progressing through the game.

The level design is definitely the worst part of this game.

Next is the weapons. There are enough different types to keep things interesting I think but I don't understand the point of most of them. I can't find a noticeable difference in damage when using the different physical damage types and all weapons do all the types of damage for the most part, just different amounts of each. And most don't have any kind of effect or buff or anything. I would expect the electric fists to do something different from the basic ones but they really only add a catch-all damage type of elemental. I also couldn't find any difference between a dual wielded slashing weapon I found at the beginning of the game, and one that I found at the end of the game besides appearance and some flavor text. Next issue I think is from part of the free DLC which adds more elemental weapons to the game. Around the time you get access to these, almost all the enemies in the next few areas are wearing gear that is specifically resistant to them. I was better off just using a big hunk of metal than this cool buzz saw pizza cutter cold weapon I found. Although it said it did more damage, it was elemental so it was basically worthless until I neared the end of the game and got a boss weapon that outscaled everything.

Now the Armor. You're given some fancy armor early on that there's really no way you could equip. Big example is the space suit armor that might've been added with that free DLC I mentioned before. It costs a ridiculous amount of points to equip that I could not have had till mid-way through the game but it's in the very beginning. And it's not even much better than standard Rhino gear and it's way more expensive! I'm actually not sure that it is better at all. Also there are bonuses for wearing an entire set. This is really cool but it makes you feel like you're missing out if you mix and match what you actually like.

Then there is the story. It's alright but a lot of it doesn't make a whole heck of a lot of sense. There are a lot of points that just aren't explained or are left unfinished. And the ending is very reminiscient of dark souls which is both unfortunate and impressive. Warren is pretty one dimensional and has no response for most things that warrant it. I only really ended up caring about two characters throughout the game, both of which died. Overall the story was interesting at least.

The UI is fine except for one big flaw that apparently I am not the only one to be tripped up by. It turns out you don't have to keep running back to older levels to get the 1st level parts to make equipment. You can skip upgrade levels by just selecting the appropriate level and crafting it. I knew you could view the different level of upgrade but I didn't realize that you could craft it and that it wouldn't still require older materials. Hopefully someone reads this and skips that huge headache.

This game made me confused, frustrated and disappointed at times but I enjoyed it a lot and am definitely going to get the sequel. It felt epic, everything looked amazing and the overall tone was just on point. I felt good after finishing it. I enjoyed it despite all the gripes I had with it. Hopefully the next one will fix these!\t\t\t\t\t

Overview
The Surge is third-person action-RPG developed by Deck13 and released in 2017. Get in the shoes of Warren, a crippled man looking for a way to restore his body by signing with a corporation called CREO. As disaster surged from the depths of the CREO factory complex, Warren must try to survive amidst endless onslaught from maddened people and robots. Overall, the game managed to establish itself among its competitors by creating an interesting sci-fi world and innovative dismemberment mechanic.

The Good
  • Dismemberment is a unique and brilliant mechanic.
  • Good level design with shortcuts, well-hidden secrets and worthwhile rewards.
  • The bosses are fun, challenging but fair even if they require some trials and errors.
  • Fresh setting and world crafted with great art direction.

The Bad
  • Lack of enemy variety. Most enemies are humanoid with the same weapons that are available to the players.
  • Balance issue. Either it have sudden difficulty spikes in some areas, or the game becomes very easy with the right upgrades. Some weapons are useless.

Gameplay
Structurally, The surge follows a typical souls-like formula. The core loop of the game is exploring the level while fighting enemies, reach a safe area. The Surge differentiates itself by introducing a locational damage and dismemberment system that is well-implemented and satisfying when executed.

Combat

Instead of light or heavy attacks like the game’s source inspiration Dark Souls, this game separates its basic attacks into vertical and horizontal attacks. Players can chain both types of attacks and execute combos alongside sliding and jumping attacks. This mechanic is important due to the location-specific damage system.

In combat, Warren can target specific locations of most enemies’ bodies. One usage of the mechanic is targeting unarmored parts of their bodies to do more damage. The second usage is by damaging an armored part, Warren can perform an execution attack that severs their limbs. By severing limbs, enemies will drop the part of their armor that can be used to obtain schematics or parts to upgrade your own armor.

The Surge has really good and satisfying combat. The controls are responsive, and with 5 types of weapons, and various armors players can build Warren however they are comfortable with. The only problem with combat is there just aren’t enough enemy varieties. Most of the enemies are humanoid with the same weapon types that players can use, and their movesets are quite limited. There are some unique humanoid type enemies that carry unique equipments that usually have unique moves and fun to fight.

Progression

Warren can get stronger by upgrading his suit’s power core. It can be upgraded by collecting scrap parts from enemies. Upgrading it will increase its capacity and allows you to equip better armor and unlock more implant slots. Implants are basically “skills” that can be equipped and build your character as you like. They have various effects from scaling up HP, healing, increasing your stamina and many others. They are the core of building the character in the game.

The second progression system is equipment. There are several types of armor sets and various weapons Warren can get. A complete armor set will give him special effects that will further determine your build. They can also be upgraded with parts enemies drop. There are 5 weapon types in the game. By using a weapon from a certain category, Warren will gain proficiency that will increase your damage with said weapon types.

The progression system in the game is quite streamlined as players don’t have to worry about specific attributes or skill level. It is still quite flexible and Warren can be moulded to your liking. In my experience, this system allows experimentation with several different types of builds as there are no permanent stats tied to it. I pretty much tried all weapon types at the first half of the game before deciding to pick a favorite. There are some balance problem like some weapons can be utterly useless or one type of build is more effective than the other.

Exploration and Loot

The levels of The Surge are good, although they never really reach the same heights as the original Dark Souls level. Each level is a separate area with a transition loading screen between them that makes the CREO factory complex doesn’t feel as grand and interconnected as Lordran.

Enemies and loot are hand placed in the world. They are generally well-placed and well-thought, that makes every encounter unique and loot are worth digging nooks and crannies for. Generally I have no problem with the level design. It could be better, but I am satisfied with it.

Technical Issue and Performance
Specs: GTX 1080, AMD Ryzen 3700x, 16 GB RAM, 1440 60 Hz

The game runs full 60 FPS almost all the time. It dipped below 60 is in the end of the game where a specific enemy type appears. This enemy's effects caused the performance to drop. Overall the game is polished at the time I played. It is bug free, aside from minor problems like characters clipping into objects. One bug that annoyed me is when a loot dropped outside of reach.

Story
In the far future where Earth is on the brink of destruction, one man built a company with the promise of repairing a broken planet. When everything went wrong, the player as Warren is caught in the midst of it and must survive against the horde of rogue machineries and find out what happened.

I like The Surge’s story. It uses a hands-off method of story telling where the plot isn’t directly told through dialogue and cutscene but through environment design and audiologs. The setting is unique for this genre. While the theme and concept has been done before, the central mystery drove me forward and made me want to know what happened.

Graphics and Visual
The Surge managed to build a unique world, and CREO feels like it has a personality of its own. The environment design, weapons, armor and enemies model mostly look good. Some of the locations might look the same as players spend the majority of their playtime indoors in industrial type areas but it feels appropriate to the setting where the game takes place.

The animations are well done. Each weapon animation feels unique, they have different kill animations and combos. There isn't that much variety, but what is available is pretty good, don’t feel too janky. Overall the graphics won’t blow any minds, but it is not ugly and still carries the atmosphere and tone quite well.

Audio and Music
The soundtrack isn’t really anything to write home about. There isn’t any bombastic boss battle music or epic orchestration, the music mostly are just ambient sounds. I might be in the minority here, but I like the song Prisoner by Stumfol that plays in the medical area. Otherwise the music in the game is mostly ignorable.

While the music is lacking, the soundscape of the game is very well done. A lot of effects can be heard from the hydraulics inside Warren and the enemies’ exosuits to electricity buzzing in the distance. Everything feels heavy, metallic and it fit for the game’s industrial environment.

Conclusions
The Surge is a solid entry to the souls-like genre. It managed to differentiate itself through its innovative mechanics and unique setting. While there is always room for improvement mainly in variety, and balance The Surge is still worth your time and money if you are a fan of souls-like genre.\t\t\t\t\t
