\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: A great action game in the Souls-style, also for those who like more straightforward action games.

The Surge is considered a part of the Souls-genre, and while there are similarities (combat, which is melee-focused, is slower and more methodical and even a single enemy can kill you fast if you're not careful, enemies respawn upon returning to "home base", and dying causes you to drop all the "money" you have gathered, but it can be recovered by returning to the place where you fell) there are notable differences as well.

Why I personally prefer The Surge to many other Souls-games is how it strips away the RPG part of "Action-RPG" almost completely. To start with, you don't get to customize your character at all. I am perfectly fine with this as I'm not interested in spending time in tweaking their face, eye color or body dimensions. Secondly, the character's stats are handled in a more straightforward manner. There's HP, Stamina and Energy (a constantly depleting resource, which you get more from hitting enemies and it can be spent on various heals, buffs and attacks). But the stats' base level at the end of the game is the exact same as at the start of it.

How do you develop your character then? With implants and equipment. Implants are equippable items that you can find while going through the game's world, or occasionally as drops from enemies. Some increase your maximum health or stamina or give buffs, others give you limited-use injectables to use (which are then refilled upon returning to an Ops Center, the game's version of bonfires) or injectables that can be used as often as you like, assuming you have enough energy for their activation.

You get equipment the sensible way by taking it from the corpses of your dead enemies. See an enemy with a cool-looking chainsword? Hack that arm off and the sword is yours. No worrying about drop rates, assuming you do enough damage to the limb holding the weapon and then activate a special finishing attack to dismember the enemy, the weapon will drop (assuming you don't already have it, you can't get multiple copies of the same weapon). Armor works slightly differently, dismembering or beheading an enemy doesn't give you you that piece of armor right away, but it gives you the schematics for that particular piece. You can then craft the armor piece at an Ops Center using scrap, which is gained from killing enemies or found in caches here and there.

Weapons, armor and implants have no pre-requisites to use, so if you can get or craft them, you can use them right away. The implants you can equip are limited by the amount of Core Power you have available, and that is increased by spending scrap to level it up. There's also a limit to how many implants you can equip at once, but that be increased as well as you progress through the game and find a better rig with more implant slots.

This departure from RPG-like stats to using items and weapons to make the numbers go higher is, at least to me, more appealing than killing an enemy two dozen times with a sword and then using the points acquired from that to put into my Intelligence stat that makes my fireballs do more damage.

That said, there aren't hundreds of weapons or armor pieces to keep around in your inventory either. There's a bit over 60 weapons to be acquired, split into five categories, and using a weapon increases your proficiency at that weapon type. There are 16 different armor sets to craft and while you can mix-n-match pieces from different sets, wearing a full set grants a bonus exclusive to that armor set. For example, improved stamina generation or immunity to toxins.

I'm sure others will see the relatively small amount of weapons and armor as a negative thing, but I see it as streamlining the experience. I don't want to spend a lot of time looking at every new equipment's stats and comparing it to what I have now. Heck, I quit playing Nioh because the amount of swag you gathered became quite ridiculous. Go out to the field for 10 minutes in that game and you come back with five helmets, three pants, four swords, two spears, an axe and a bunch of various items that you then need to go through and figure out what to keep.

And finally, the combat itself, the bread 'n butter of any Souls-game. To help with the pin-point violence required for chopping off enemy arms the targeting system allows you to target individual limbs. It works great and I never felt that I was fighting the combat system rather than the enemies. One button attacks horizontally and another vertically. Making different combinations with these two buttons results in different attacks, so knowing what to do when you want to pull of a sweeping attack that'll hit multiple enemies is important. Dodge button dodges and the block button blocks, and blocking an enemy attack just before it connects allows you to counter with an attack of your own.

The games takes largely place in industrial-style factories and hallways, and while there are some areas with a different look and feel to them, there's probably not as much variety to it as in some other similar games. That said, I found the world fun to explore and at the end of it I was very comfortably navigating my way through it, feeling even nostalgic for some places. The story and lore are told mostly through audio logs found all over the place, but there are also a handful of NPCs to converse with. It's fairly straightforward, but things are never spelled out and you do need to put some pieces together yourself if you want to understand the whole thing.

It took me about 40 hours to finish the game, and it allows you to restart in New Game+ with all your acquired weapons, armor and implants. It did feel quite fun to go through the first area again and just tear through the enemies (though they are a bit tougher, and there are some new enemies thrown in as well) that gave me trouble the first time.

There are some things that I do think the game could improve upon:

The UI for handling implants is a bit clucky. There are tons of them, and many duplicates as well, and finding the one you want to equip can be a chore despite being able to sort by name, value or other attributes. What I'd like is to be able to sort by what they affect (health, stamina, energy, weapon proficiency, etc). I'd also like to be able to save implant sets, so if I want to try out a different approach, I don't have to invidually re-equip every one of them if I decide to go back to the previous setup.

Better handling when having multiple injectables to choose from. You can switch between equipped injectables at the push of a button, but when you have equipped multiple it can be tricky to choose the one you want in the heat of combat, which is why I stuck with equipping max. two injectables at a time.

More variety to builds. As it is, builds more or less boil down to "Do I want to focus on having a lot of HP, Stamina or Energy or a bit of all?" I do like that changing builds is as easy as switching gear and implants, but I wish the differences were more extensive. Maybe there could be a Drone-build as well? Yes, there are drones in the game you can use to attack enemies with, but I mostly forgot all about them. I only used them for pulling enemies.

Overall, I think The Surge is a great action game that takes some of its mechanics from the Souls games and some others from more traditional action games, making for a cocktail that should be for the taste of fans of both genres, but hardcore Souls-fans might be disappointed at the inherent differences. There's a sequel coming out in 2019 and I hope it only improves upon what started here.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge pits you in the belly of corrupt corporatism and machinery gone awry

Congrats! You were once crippled but now with the power of technology you can move again and more importantly work for the big megastructure that gave you the ability to walk again, until that is you wake up in the sand surrounded by co-workers with their brains fried and drones going out of control.

What's cool?

The combat in the surge is quite straightforward but at the same time has really nice layers to it, targeting weakest points on the enemy bodies will deal more damage to both health and stamina, but going for their more armoured areas allows you to cut them off with execution moves and subsequently craft your own and melt them down for upgrade materials depending which body part /you/ need upgrading.
It's more akin to Bloodborne style of combat, sure there is blocking but the game is a lot more fun and rewarding if you try and play aggressively, getting into the enemies face and using combos to stagger and get the upper hand.
At it's core it does have similar gameplay mechanics to the Souls franchise but it isn't really comparable, other than stamina management, The Surge stands on its own two feet as a competent sci-fi robot smashing romp.

The audio is quite important, detailing prompts and enemies to you as well as the music and atmosphere of each of the areas you visit. The boss music isn't exactly memorable for being unique but they do help to build the drama and tension of the fight.
Also the country music of Ops is really nice to hear and has a contrast to the world of The Surge itself.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5nBvddxrltk

It feels good to progress through an area, chopping off limbs and using your little drone companion to be able to unlock a shortcut back to your Ops base and cut down your path back if you die or if you need to get around quicker.

What's kinda smelly?

Only a few minor complaints in this otherwise really fun game, sometimes its not quite obvious where to go after completing an objective which are only given out if you talk to Sally or any other NPC.
NG+ needs a bit of tweaking, it's really fun to be able to keep upgrading my weapons and gear past the materials of NG with brand new materials and scaling but some of the new enemy placements break the flow and implants still stay the same which means you as a character don't have the same feeling of scaling with the NG+ enemies as they can quickly take you out with no way to upgrade further.

The story is kinda paper thin, it's there and you can pick up audio logs to grasp together slowly what happens and the situation via what you hear from NPCs and over the intercom and video system. However when you get to the end there's this sudden jolt with enemies suddenly having their machinery evolving to be these almost alien-like phasing nanites and overtaking their bodies and with this the Rogue Process isn't explained very well either other than the voices that it's absorbed so The Surge 2 would need some explaining to do and to keep to a more well versed way of pushing the narrative to the player.

Oh and there's not enough bosses, they're really fun to fight with a lot of tension but there's very few so please put more in The Surge 2 thank you.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------
DLC Talk

A Walk in the Park DLC is pretty fun, it's goofy and silly and offers a little backstory to Warren before he enrolled in CREO and it's like fanfiction for The Surge.

Fire and Ice Weapons Pack is free and I literally used most of the fire weapons to carry me until I got the Hardcore weapon from the Big Sister fight which carried me until the end of time.

CREO Special Employee Kit isn't needed really, unless you want a slightly different skin and some implants but its not really needed.\t\t\t\t\t

I really liked the game, to some extent even more so than DS (I´m looking at you DS 2).

And if you try it out (and you should) and find yourself writing a review down the line. Be a better person than I am and try to leave DS out of the review. That's the hardest test The Surge will throw at you!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI had to review the successor to Lords of the Fallen, if nothing else, to see how far Deck 13 has come. I did some research into exactly what the dev team tried to create. A lot of people who played Lords of the Fallen, and I imagine, many people who play this game, will make the comparison to the Dark Souls franchise, which I think is a misnomer. I'll be doing that insofar as the developers do in reference to their own game. I use my gameplay (100% achievement completion of the base game) and these three articles as a basis for my review:

https://www.rpgsite.net/interview/4886-deck-13-interactive-interview-we-talk-the-surge-and-action-rpgs

http://prankster101.com/articles/interview-with-adam-hetenyi-of-the-surge/

https://www.gamasutra.com/view/news/236275/Postmortem_Deck13_Interactives_Lords_of_the_Fallen.php

The success of any hardcore ARPG hinges on combat. I read through the articles and there have been several mentions of the phrase “tactical combat”. I don't know what the developers meant by this exactly. They seem to make reference to attacking body parts, and, further, the distinction between attacking an enemy's unarmored body part, causing more damage, or attacking an armored body part, causing less damage, but increasing the probability that that body part will be severed. I don't know if this qualifies as “tactical combat”, but it definitely is innovative. Part of the reason this portion of the game is hyped is because, well, let's face it, it's the selling point. Additionally, there really isn't any reason not attack an armored body part anyway as body parts only need to be severed once before the player obtains the schematic and then can construct the part. I believe what the developers were referencing, but don't actually say directly, are the WEAPONS and their move sets. I'll discuss that shortly. I'd also like to say this game is so much more polished than Lords of the Fallen. I experienced one glitch in the 90 hours I played this game. The blood, sweat, and tears that went into developing this game is apparent as you play through it.

The combat in is this game falls short for many reasons. Similar to Lords of the Fallen, enemies don't have recovery frames. An active frame goes from its start-up to the end of the animation, which mean you'll take damage at every point along the attack trajectory, whether you're at the beginning of the attack animation or at the end. This also means that enemies can go into another attack right from the end of an attack. I once had an enemy slide into me, damaging me, canceling the follow up attack, go into another slide, then kill me with the follow up. Bottomless stamina reserves. This is what happens when recovery frames aren't added to attacks. I find Western studios suffer from this lapse in judgment quite often. They wish to mimic Dark Souls except the parts that made Dark Souls combat shine. Additionally, and perhaps the most frustrating, enemy attacks track THROUGHOUT their attack animation. Even projectiles. An enemy can alter their trajectory in mid-air or mid-slide to track you as you evade.

The Surge introduces high/low attacks and vertical/horizontal attacks. If you want to attack legs, you have to do a low vertical attack (rising attack). If you want to hit arms, you have to do a high horizontal attack (a lunging attack). If this isn't enough, you can also duck and jump. Why? Why not. I never once utilized these mechanics, but knowing which attack hits which body part is crucial if you want to severe specific limbs. I can appreciate the tremendous effort that went into making the combat system unique.

I won't get into weapon and armor types in too much detail, but I thought I'd mention some things I wish I had known before I started the game: Armor only has two values: stability and defense. Stability is to The Surge as poise is to Dark Souls: stagger threshold. There are no numerical ratings for stability, just low, medium, high, which correspond to the armor types: light (operator), medium (sentinel), heavy (goliath). Will your attacks stagger enemies? The short is a resounding NO! Enemies in this game do ungodly amounts of damage, so you'll die quite a bit trying to figure out the combat system and you will get thoroughly demolished in this game if you try to run up to enemies and mash the attack button because most, if not all, weapons have ridiculously long start-up animations. This game is meant to be played by either performing a lunging attack (pressing A + RB) or a sliding attack (holding A + RB). You need to get in, do damage, then get the hell out. Repeat. Because of this, some weapon types are more useful than others. I absolutely love staves, but they're not good to block with. Single-rigged weapons are slow and leave you wide open, but you can block > riposte all day. Also, you won't really get to see what these weapons can do because enemies won't allow you to string together a 6-hit combo. To the credit of the developers, all of the weapons are very versatile; you can string together combos that do 90% damage and all them are freaking cool to watch. Definitely experiment!

You can stagger enemies, but it doesn't matter, they'll recover while you're in the middle of a combo, then hit you out of it. They seem to have incredibly high stability, no matter what they're wearing. There also doesn't seem to be any point to upgrading armor. The final boss was dealing damage equal to 100% of my life with a fully upgraded armor set. The three legged enemies killed me in one shot too. There just doesn't seem to be any rhyme or reason to the numbers behind the scenes. Another example: you'll get an implant that will show you the health and stability bar of enemies. When you deplete the enemies stability bar, it instantaneously refills, making stability an absolutely useless modifier in the game.


Leveling up doesn't actually increase the players stats, it increases your rigs power capacity. Power capacity is consumed by armor and implants. Implants can raise stats (health, stamina, energy), provide passive bonuses (restoring health every time you sever a body part), and provide injectables (restoring health, increase power, etc.). I personally find this to be quite innovative and makes for quite a versatile character. I went with a high-energy damage powerhouse. My energy always sat at 50% of its max, allowing me to hit harder and faster. This versatility can be overwhelming at first, mainly because there are so many useless implants. Remember: enemies are relentless! Better to focus on increasing stamina and health.

As for story. There isn't any. There are only goals the player has to achieve in order to progress to the next section. The narrative is told through logs scattered throughout the game, which are quite easy to find (I found them all without really trying). The story comes to a head during the final act of the game, and by the time the player gets there, its almost over. The developers went through the trouble of creating a universe without really telling us exactly what's going on in it, which is disappointing. I'd love to know why rigs were created, or some of the backstory of the endgame enemies, or even exactly what happened to the workers. In this, I think Deck 13 attempted to replicate Dark Souls too much: light on story exposition, heavy on lore. The problem is there isn't any lore either; the player doesn't interact with enough people who know anything, and the people that do, don't want to tell you. I'd recommend purchasing this game during a steam sale. There is a lot of content to keep you busy, and you'll definitely get frustrated dealing with the bosses (have fun with the final boss), but I think there are too many shortcomings to justify a full-price purchase.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust got the 100% on this and decided to write a little something.
This game was definitely a pleasnt surprise, I really wasn't expecting much but here we are.
Somewhere between Bloodborne, Dead Space & Rise of the Robots sits The Surge.
The Game is heavily movement and reaction based and executes its ideas generally well.
There is a combo system in the game that does have some variety but the problem lies with the enemies that can poise through a lot of the stuff you can throw at them to attack you through your string.
Ultimately you'll have the most success with dash-in pokes and get out of range, ready to rinse and repeat. Despite this the combat still feels great throughout but an expanded stability system for the sequel would be appreciated.
The environments are good enough but really the level design design shines, every time you round a corner after an hour of exploring to find Ops again it's kinda mind-blowing.
Enemy placement is generally designed to surprise and aggrevate and provide some skill checks in later levels, there are many instant death falls and walkways complete with aggressive enemies dying to poke you off.
If you can, get the DLC as well, CREO World is great and a nice splash of colour to offset some of the more drab environments. Actually, that's somewhat reductive, the environments aren't fully bland, mainly the poblem is there's too many maintenance tunnels, the actual rooms and locations are nice, varied and yes, colourful.
The Implant system is a nice take on speccing builds and adds a lot of diversity, I'd like to see this greatly expanded in the sequel.
Similarly, I'd like to see a lot more enemy variety, especially bosses in the next Title, the current crop of bosses aren't especially difficult and, criminally, are either similar to normal mobs except beefed up, or similar to other bosses in the same game.
Anyway, my main problem with the Soulsborne series have always been that I'm not super into the fantasy setting and even though The Surge is weaker than these games overall I much preferred the tone and aesthetic.
I definitely recommend a playthrough, grab it cheap and enjoy.\t\t\t\t\t
