\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMechanically solid, sci-fi soulslike that does enough things different to be interesting, but doesn't stray too far from the formula to scare off fans of the genre. The combat is paced different enough that it doesn't feel like you're just playing Dark Souls in space; with interesting mechanics such as targeting an enemy's armoured parts for better loot, or their unarmoured parts for an easier fight. That easier fight might be tempting because combat can be quite punishing, but never too much so - a careful approach to exploration will allow you to predict or see ambushes before you rush headlong into them, and not once did I feel like an obstacle was unfairly tough.
Weapon variety is good with enough weapons to offer interesting choices, without there being so many that it becomes overwhelming. And while you are rewarded for specialising into one weapon type, switching never feels like something you're punished for doing..
The level design, while thematically no more than decent, is rock solid: the game consists of a few separate levels connected by a train system, but each of these levels themselves are very dense and packed to the brim with shortcuts and alternative routes (reminiscent of Dark Souls 1) - the CREO foundation really feel like a facility you can walk around in, rather than a level with a set start and end.

If you're a fan of soulslikes then this will scratch your itch; the game is mechanically sound and rewarding to master, which has always been core to the genre. Just make sure you don't try to play it exactly like Dark Souls and are willing to learn The Surge's own playstyle - it's a souls*like*, not Dark Souls in space.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith the sheer difficulty of combat, supplies, direction and even equipment farming, I really have a hard time trying to find the positive aspect of what there is to offer in this game.

Some of the bigger issues that I find in this game is the lack of knowledge provided to the player, such as certain parts of the enemy's body must be targeted for the player to specifically get the armor part they want. Another major problem I faced is the lack of hint where the next mission location is, which got me lost for many hours and even resorting to youtube videos for solutions.

Furthermore, starting a newgame+ is even worse as there will be a madman that could down 3/4 of your HP or even kill even if you have the best possible armor from your first playthrough. I simply cannot recommend this game even on a sale.

Pros:
-Multiple variety of armor design/types
-Weapons with different cool kill animations (Greatsword, Sword, Staff, Greathammer, Dual arms, Spears, etc....)
-Surface environment clearly with much effort put into the design.
-Enemies respawn quickly after returning to a safezone for continuous gear farming.

Cons:
-Tardy lock-on feature that puts the player at a disadvantage. Especially when you face multiple enemies.
-Lack of health restocking outside of safezone and all enemies respawning after exiting safezones.
-Lack of damage and defense value for players. (Especially the butterfly and the Spear.)
-Buggy controls to fully destroy the crystalline mass. (Late-game mobs)
-No general arrows or pointers which can potentially get you lost for hours on end.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI tried to enjoy this game. I put time into learning the skirmishing combat style, into gathering materials to upgrade my equipment, learning the combos for the different weapons. But there was a point where I had to wonder when the actual fun would start.
It's not that there aren't some interesting ideas - targeting individual body parts was nice. Deal enough damage to that part and you're given the opportunity to launch a finisher which severs that part for salvage. This means that if you find an enemy with armour or weaponry you've never seen that you can farm them for the blueprints for the entire set, returning to Ops to make them respawn. Since this unique armour is generally stronger than what you're wearing these fights provide a challenge.
Unfortunately this process is let down by the combat mechanics. It feels sluggish, even in the lightest of armour and using a single-handed weapon. By comparison enemies with heavy armour and enormous hammers can strike instantly - with no real telegraphing there was no way to avoid the half-healthpool damage they'd deal before there was an opportunity to respond. This plants the idea that you should try the heavy weapons if they strike so punishingly, but it's not to be. In the hands of the character the same hammer takes an aeon to land any blow. Woe betide you should you accidentally trigger a combo - you can interrupt regular strikes with a dodge but once a combo starts your controls are locked until it completes or the enemy knocks you on your back, whichever happens first. This sudden loss of control feels like it punishes you for trying to get in just one extra hit that you know there's time for and has another potential consequence: In the second area you can rescue friendly NPCs who will wait in Ops (your restock room.) I found an interesting set of flaming fist weapons and, after upgrading them at Ops I decided to see what their attack speed was like. The first two strikes lunged at one of these friendly npcs and I watched with growing anger as the follow-up strikes, triggered without any further input from me, put said npc in the ground. With no way to reload and undo the damage, I closed the game. I'm done.\t\t\t\t\t
