\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDon't get me wrong, The Surge isn't a horrible game. It does, however, manage to perfectly f*ck over particular types of Souls players, which means that certain other types of players will absolutely love it. For me, there has to be a certain amount of give and take in a Souls game, a balance of difficulty and reward, and support for my particular sub-style of play. It's not here.

Nobody plays a Souls game for the story, and this one probably won't reach out and grab you by the throat either. I want to give them credit for environmental storytelling, but the fact is that because almost all of those environments look the same, it's kind of a moot point.

I don't even think fans of the game call the level design "good", it's just something you sort of cope with. The function of the setting in a Souls game is not only to be interesting to look at, and give you some lore, but also to provide a type of lateral progression through the game. Level design offsets the difficulty by allowing you to go somewhere else, get new gear, and find grinding spots. Sure, there's no map in these types of games, and this is no exception, but every level in The Surge is an industrial labyrinth that all looks pretty much the same, applies its difficulty in a completely linear fashion, and every single enemy you pass trying to figure it out is waiting with a can of lube. As far as I know, every level has ONE "bonfire", and it's right at the beginning where you don't really need it. The game ironically BEGS you to run past enemies, skipping the one thing it does really well in terms of skill-checks.

There is no substantial mixture of enemies in terms of variety OR difficulty. This is really, REALLY important for a Souls game. Factoring in the pacing of equipment and the general stinginess of scrap - the game's leveling resource - I couldn't shake the feeling of doing a whole lot of grinding for a whole lot of nothing. At times enemies have questionable hitboxes, somewhat randomized staggering to their poise (and yours), and unquestionably bullsh*t tracking on some of their attacks.

The game has a brilliant mechanic where you make a decision between cutting off enemies' armored limbs for scrap, materials, and an agonizingly slow trickle of new gear, or hitting unarmored parts to get some generic scrap and kill them faster. This unfortunately bottlenecks really quickly, because once you have whatever new equipment that's going to drop, the game can't keep up with the astronomical leveling costs of your core. Functionally speaking, this means fighting enemies quickly becomes a waste of time, regardless of whether you enjoy it or not. It doesn't particularly help that switching body targets, or rolling the stick around to get the one you want to begin with means you can occasionally expect to need to stay at a distance at first. This in a game where almost all the enemies can two or three-shot you, like to ambush or sucker punch you from offscreen at least 30% of the time, and that's making a large assumption that they aren't bringing a friend or two along.

Equipping something, whether it's armor, a weapon, an implant for injectables or stat boosts, requires energy from your core. Leveling weapons requires a generic metal, and leveling armor requires specific parts. Stat-grinding doesn't technically exist in this game, it's been dumbed down to "energy core level", which although it does technically scale your energy pool available for gear and implants, it has absolutely piss-poor HP scaling in the face of the type of damage the enemies do.

What this means is that you have almost no control or influence over the challenge the game gives you. You just suck it up. When I hit a wall in a Souls game I like to grind, or go somewhere else. Usually both. I like to overcome Souls games through skill, or hard work. If I can't do it through one, I'll do it through the other. There is no choice here. You either "git gud" or you can f*ck right off. There is no number of suicide runs you can make for items and gear that will make a difference, and scrap grinding feels like it makes almost no difference at all. Because materials to upgrade armor specifically are trickled out, you will ALWAYS feel paper thin when you screw up, and perhaps more importantly, the energy and speed costs of heavier armor and certain weapon types are poorly balanced in large sections of the game. In other words, you may find yourself not even using the new armors immediately, because you can't upgrade them enough right then and there for it to be superior to what you're already using.

All of that means the game is exceptionally well-balanced towards brutal difficulty, frequent doses of bullsh*t, and nothing else. There are no creative solutions, no real incentives, and no lateral movement. And that means The Surge is not for me. Maybe I'll have better luck with the supposedly better-designed sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAwful game, do not play!

I'm a massive dark souls fan and really liked this game to begin with but boy oh boy does it get really really bad. The first level is the best, not too complicated layout where the terrain wraps around via shortcuts etc. much like dark souls. The environment is futuristic, the game is hard but fun.

Then you get to level 2 where everything looks slightly different. The game gets harder so you are going to start dying and having to trek through the same tedious terrain to get back to your scrap. The map in the second area gets extremely complicated and labyrinthine. It's populated by difficult enemies throughout and no matter how much time you spend playing you'll never truly master the level design; everything looks the same and your personal compass will always be wrong with the constant doubling and turning of vents. This causes the game to take far longer and be far more difficult than it has a right to be. The game becomes less and less fun because of this.

The third area is navigable and outside so it's a welcome breath of fresh air but it's over quickly and you'll soon find the next area to be hellishly tedious. By this point the vent and duct theme of the game becomes apparent: almost the whole game is vents and passages. When you get to the labs area and the enemies start to hit like trucks, the game loses it's last vestiges of fun and becomes a painful and tedious torture.

Everything that made Dark souls good is missing from this game: the variety of enemies, the multitude of bosses, the level design. Instead they chose to take the repetitiveness and difficulty from Dark souls and turn it up to 11. Add to that, drab level design and labyrinthine vent systems and you'll get the picture.

I stopped playing when a couple of people commented on my post and told me the final area was by far the worst. I could not fathom a worse area than the ones I'd already tried.

You should save yourself time and not play this game. Unless you enjoy mental torture.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t While it does have it's flaws (like sudden difficulty spikes) and have a rather forgettable story, what's left is the best souls-like that manages to do its own thing with amazing execution. The limb cutting mechanic is an amazing idea, and personally, I wish more games had a similar feature like this to cut down of grinding time, helps get the flow of the game time move more freely. Along with the combat having a nice weighty feel to it with enemies getting knocked around when their stability is drained, with heavy weapons making combat feel like a slug fest between to heavy weight boxers, while the lighter weapons do a nice job in feeling quick and nimble.The weapons can even get better with time, thanks to the experience bar slowly but surely adding damage to your favorite weapon type(s). Finally the DLCS bring quite a lot of fresh content to sink your teeth into, either after or during your playthrough. The Good, The Bad, And The Augmented brings a nice episodic story, have the main character go through "stages" with rewards at the end of them, rather then having a connected world with secrets hidden. Creo World has a much more open world feeling to it, considering your setting is in a theme park it makes a lot of sense too, having nearly all the shortcuts go right back to the main saving point, with plenty of secrets to be found in every nook and cranny.
Overall if you want a game that respects you if put the time and effort building your character, with nice combat, small but connected world, amazing character build options, then this game is just for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDark Souls but Sci-Fi pretty much sums up this game, so if you like both it is definitely worth checking out.

I found the regular combat a lot more interesting and fun than Dark Souls though, you can target specific body parts to either harvest specific resources for crafting or to hit poorly defended parts for more damage. It works well and depending on what you are trying to do encourages different approaches to defeating enemies.
The world is great and well thought out, lots of hidden stuff to find, fairly linear so it isn't easy to get lost or go in the wrong direction, and as you progress you will open up shortcuts that will make it quicker to get where you want to go.
However the boss battles feel like the standard Dark Souls affair where it is simply quick time events without being told what button to push when, but if you do push that button at that time you will probably end up dead. Most bosses are incredibly "difficult" to defeat without knowing what to push when, and the only way the game lets you learn is through trial and error, so you have to die repeatedly which IMO is just frustrating. If you watch a guide on what to push when then most mosses become pretty easy though, just like Dark Souls.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of those games that I can lose myself in and just run around and slaughter everything in sight. It's very smooth, and not only is it a gear based game, it has a lot of RPG elements. So, if you pick up a weapon you like, the more you use it the more you get better using it. It's not for that specific weapon but the weapon category. So, if you want to be a hulking tank that wields super heavy weapons but hate how slow they swing? Just go smash some low level enemies until it's quick enough you can use it in actual combat.

I will say though, the biggest draw from this to Dark Souls is this game has a story line. I'm not done so I can't say if it's any good or not, but it gives you a concrete goal and direction to head towards. For whatever reasons that's enough to keep me coming back.\t\t\t\t\t
