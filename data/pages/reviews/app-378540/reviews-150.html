Pros:

- Weapon and armor designs are cool and badass

- The mobs are very difficult and rewarding
- Ducking and jumping is rewarding when pulled off

- Implants and drones are nice additions to your character
- Love the medbay songs :p

Cons:
- Boss fights are easy and too few
- NPCs are boring and forgettable
- Voice acting is dull

Suggestions for next game (Maybe..):
- Give more interesting sidequests
- The story should be more interesting and epic
- Need more cutscences for immersions
- The game world should be bigger and can explore more interesting stuffs, and can do more than fighting mobs(Sidequests like do daily jobs according to the character's employment since he is a worker afterall)

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI... Can't in good conscious recommend this as a 'good' game. Now if you want a fairly difficult game, that gets stale but ramps up in difficulty all the way through, have fun.

It tries to borrow from Darksouls, it gets the short cuts and everything right, but a big part of it is how well you can memorize the map of crampt sci-fi complexes in your head. Lot's of hidden items all over the place, but most of them are similar to the 'soul' items from darksouls.

There's no where near the ammount of weapon variety, and your 'drone' abilities are as close as you get to magic and are pretty subpar, to the point where I only really used it on bosses because I had nothing else to spend the building energy on.

You get mods, based on capacity kind of like in Warframe, and the cap is based on your core level (soul level). Honestly I was just going through the whole game with HP up, and Regen for exploring, swapping it out to instant heals when fighting bosses. All their effects seem pretty eh save for the one I did fall in love with which was cutting dodge stamina in half, essentially letting you dodge like light load in darksouls. Sadly, I got this near the end of the game.

Dodging feels sketchy, it works, but it takes up tons of stamina and it doesn't feel as good as a roll. The block is mostly worthless in the long run the moment you realise enemies can poise right through your counter attack and hit you back by the time your animation finishes. You're better off dodging, then doing a single hit, rinse repeat.

Speaking of, heavy armor seems largely useless, because enemies have the same ammount of health as you, when you're at their level, and do the same if not more damage than you. I realised this way later into the game, when I shed my heavy armor in favor of a nimbler set, and only then was I able to proceed. Granted, after this point, most enemies encounters were either a win, or a death by 1-2 shots.

The story itself wasn't bad, the characters were pretty cool, and I felt bad for ONE character in specific, if you do their quest line. I didn't like the ending, I know there are two, but still sucked. And I rather enjoyed the darksoul endings.

Final part, the game is pretty grindy. The game had open slots for the 3rd exo suit with the most ammount of mod slots, capping at 110. I beat the game at lv 70, for the record, and this of course might explain why I kept getting one shot for the most part. But the game just isn't fun enough to grind on.

There seems to be a new game plus, but I'm just not really interested.

One bonus, I did like the fact that the main character was innitially stuck in a wheel chair. Don't see it too often.

I give it a 6 out of 10, I suppose.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is a more recent Dark Souls clone, and while that may seem like a bad thing, it most certainly is not. One the outset, you play as Warren, a paraplygic man who joins the mega-corporation CREA to heal him of his injury. Things don't go as planned and after a rather twisted operation where they graft an exoskeleton onto you, you're dumped onto the ouskirts of the city to seemingly fend for yourself.

The rest of the game is typical of these types of sci-fi stories; figure out whats going on, meet random survivors along the way, and kill everything and anything that tries to kill you. While that is not a bad thing, anyone expecting some kind of sci-fi epic will be sorely mistaken.

Gameplay is very Dark Souls-y. You have your two attacks, vertical and horizontal, a dodge, a block, and medpacks for healing. Combat is satisfying however, as Warren is inside this exoskeleton, his strength is vastly increased. Every attack has weight behind it, and slamming an enemy into the air as you defeat him really never gets old.

However, there is one tweak to this formula, the ability to target enemy limbs. Most enemies, not all, have various body parts that can be targeted. Majoraly the enemies in the game are humanoid, so you can target legs, arms, heads, torsos so on and so on. This comes into play not only to deal damage, enemy limbs can be armored or not, but also to get new gear. Like what that bad guy is wearing, well attack him enough and you can tear his arm off to get it. Like his weapon too? Well why not rip it from his corpse?

This limb targeting is very satisfying. Seeing new enemies, looking at thier gear, and eventually getting it after defeating them really is enjoyable. This also ties into the crafting system of the game. Inside of the operations center, the games equivalant of the bonfires, you'll find your crafting station. You need materials, and you get them from defeating and tearing limbs off of bad guys who are wearing the stuff you are. Its pretty clevar, actually, and gives incentive to continue to attempt to gather more gear from enemies, increasing your own prowess as you go.

This same cannot be sad for the environments. While initially they seem pretty cool, as the sci-fi setting allows, near the end you'll grow weary of the same old industial catwalks, metal doors, hallways, corridors, and random vent shafts. The game does lackluster job of explaining where you are in the world. This may be my inability to best guess where I am, or simply the environments just look too similar. But in Dark Souls fashion, the game does impliment shortcuts. This makes every area much easier to traverse, especially if you are defeated (and you will be) by the bosses.

Dark Souls always had a penchant for giving you massive beasts of bosses, and The Surge is no different. Most foes tower over Warren and easily kill him in 2-3 hits. But, just like its inspiration, there are patterns to be learned here, and once you give it a few goes, any boss can be bested. The boss design range from a typical Mech to interesting Waste Disposal bots.

While The Surge may never meet the inspirations lofty ambitions, it is fun. The combat is good and gory and give meaning to its limb targetting system. The bosses are great, and hard. Oh, the game is hard. Really hard. So you may want to get ready for that. It is unforgiving at times. But ya know what. I wouldn't want it any other way\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe brutal and satisfying (if sometimes frustrating) combat takes a little getting used to, but the overall experience is rich and fun and rewarding--I am on my third playthrough already.

I've only played Dark Souls extremely briefly, but everyone says the similarities are there; suffice it to say that it's third person melee combat focused with a cyberpunk, dystopian future twist. Love it.

The story IS there despite what some people are saying--It's just not fed to you in cutscenes; you need to hunt down audio logs and start piecing together what happened.
I love that this is clearly a game made to not hold your hand in either the combat, gameplay or the storytelling. I get to delve into the game and experience as little or as much of what I care to look for.

My only criticism is that for the refreshing lack of hand-holding, some controls or abilities aren't so clear, switching targets for example was something I didn't know how to do until second playthrough and it game up on a loading screen (I have an SSD and the loading screens are short, so I missed it before).

I cannot wait for DLC and expansions or sequels, this is probably my most enjoyed game of the year so far!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI just want to clear something up. There's a few reviews out there saying the combat is unfair, enemies are cheap, blocking is bad, etc. None of that is true. The combat is extremely fair, probably moreso than Dark Souls as there's no backstab fishing or cheap parry spamming. Your stamina management matters, knowing when to block, dodge, jump, or duck attacks matters, and properly executing a plan in combat pays off. R1 spammers will not find any joy here, if you go in with the mentality that this is just a Dark Souls knockoff with scifi pants on, you're going to get destroyed.

This is closer to Bloodborne than it is to Dark Souls, it's faster paced and has a serious emphasis on learning movesets, even for standard enemies. If you're not prepared to get a bit of grind on for better gear and to really learn your weapon and how the enemies have to be approached, you'll end up refunding the game thinking it's not good.

It's fine, it's just different.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnother game where I wished there was more than just an arbitrary "thumbs up" or "thumbs down" option when placing a review. Skip to the final paragraph for the tl;dr of this all.

First off, I think this game is very fairly priced for what it provides and there's a huge amount I'm happy with in regards to it. At the same measure this game has a lot of flaws that can quickly become frustrating and would turn many off of the game, so let's get to it.

The game is set in the future where Earth's atmosphere is degrading and Project Resolve is started to reseed the atmosphere and save humanity. You play as Warren, a new employee at the CREO facility at the start of the game where you're outfited with the all important EXO-rig. Shortly after, things get royally messed up and it's up to you to save everything from getting worse.

I've heard it called a Souls-Like game and that's not entirely untrue, there's a lot there for Souls fans to enjoy, as well as some familiar mechanics with some changes.

Visually I will say it's above average. I've seen some texture glitches and enemies popping-in at a distance but nothing fundamentally bad. The locations look good and some of the enemies are pretty decent, the main mob you'll be fighting are essentially brain-fried humans wearing mechanical exoskeletons. Zombies wearing murderbots is a farily disturbing concept and they did a good job bringing that to life.

You pick from a selection of weapons that are either dual-wielded, one-handed, staves, one-handed heavy or two handed. Which range from fast, medium and slow in terms of attatck speed. You also pick between light, medium or heavy armor which all give certain benefits from wearing a full set, as well as a loadout of implants which affect everything from health, stamina to enemy health readouts.

Combat takes some getting used to, as you can target specific bodyparts of an enemy to cut them off and reap crafting components to upgrade your weapons and armor. Similarly, you can attack unarmored bodyparts to kill an enemy faster, but get less or no loot in return. This can make for excellent strategy against tougher enemies as you cycle between attatcking armored and unarmored parts to kill them faster, and get the parts you need, but more on that later...

Combat is quick paced, but can feel stiff early on. Like most Souls games, once you attack you're commited to that action and can't do anything else until the attack animation is complete. This unfortunately means you can open yourself up to a counterattack from the enemy if you're trying to chain together a combo.
In terms of defense, you have a very tight and responsive dodge that reminds me of Bloodborne in its efficiency in avoiding attacks.

There's also a blocking and a manuever you can perform for defense by ducking or jumping to avoid an attack to follow up with a counter that can briefly stun some enemies, but this is where things fall apart.
Enemies deal an incredible amount of damage regardless of how heavy or well upgraded your armor is, most able to kill you in two hits, even some of the earlier enemiens. This makes the high-risk, high-reward system of dodge-countering completely unreliable since if you mess it up once, you'll almost certainly need to heal.
In fact, the damage some enemies deal to you makes it difficult to fight two enemies at once, and nearly impossible fighting three. The camera doesn't have as wide a view as I would like, making it hard to see what the enemy beside you is doing while you focus on the one in front, and while this can make for some harrowing combat, it also makes for some frustrating deaths going from full health to zero as you're hit from the side once, staggared, then hit once from the front.

This frustration is apparent in later levels as well, enemies become increasingly difficult to stagger, while you can be knocked backwards or even off your feet by almost every strike, meaning you could soon have to choose between trying to attack an armored area of the body that you need. You could cycle between armored and unarmored parts, but unless you deal an unspecified amount of damage to that bodypart, it might not be cut off for your finishing move. This lack of late game staggering also makes combo attacks fairly useless against fully armored enemies, and I resorted to a strategy of dashing forwards, striking, and dashing back repeatedly.
This sort of combat structure soon meant my Health was soon a tertiary consideration behind Stamina (which is the determination of how much you can dodge or attack), and energy (Which allows you to perform finishing moves, summon your drone, or if you have the right implants, heal). The main aim ultimately became to avoid getting hit, with the block button becoming completely underused.

Weapons are fairly unbalanced as well, staves are the best weapons, having good speed, stagger most enemies decently, and have a powerful and fast lunge attack. While heavy weapons might hit hard, but they're so dangerously slow that if you hit an enemy with them and they don't stagger, you are almost certainly going to be hit with a counterattack.
I ultimately only ever used the dual-wield weapons or the staves.

With that out of the way, I did enjoy the level design. Each level only has one checkpoint/hub where you can store your tech scraps (the games equivilent of EXP, which is dropped on death and needs to be retrieved on a time limit), level up, and upgrade your gear.
This leads to levels that all have several shortcuts that can lead back to the central area, and during particularly harrowing parts make you start trying to calculate just how far you've gone and if you'll be able to double back soon. I would say the levels have a sort of spiderweb pattern to them, several paths starting out from a central spot that all lead back to it once you follow them long enough.
However this does lead to a few 'Metroidvania' style backtracking as you'll see locked doors or containers you can't access until you've sufficiently leveled or aquired specific gear.

It has flaws. A lot of them. But it also has potential for improvement and has a combat system that only needs some balancing issues to be tweaked to really make it shine. I have seen comments from the developers in some forums saying they will address the issues of the amount of damage enemies deal, and if this is the case I assume other aspects of game rebalancing will also occur.
The game isn't quite a diamond-in-the-rough yet, but maybe some work from the devs will bring out the gleam that's hidden under there.

I recommend waiting for this game to recieve further patches, but if you're a Souls fan definitely grab it if it's on sale. Until then, and unless you really need a Souls-like fix, I wouldn't recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've just finished The Surge, so these thoughts are fresh off the top off my head. For clarity's sake, I've been playing this game off and on since release (6 days), and part of the reason it took so long to beat is that I quit from frustration a few times. This is a difficult game, and while it tries to be 'Dark Souls' hard, I think that a lot of the difficulty is what I'd call artificial. To make things worse, the difficulty is very spiky and nonsensical in many ways. The start is very hard, the second and third areas get a little easier, the next is a nightmare, the next is a little easier, then a nightmare, then a cakewalk... It's not very consistent, and that hurts it a lot as well.

Due to how difficult many of the 'trash mob' encounters are, I can only assume the developers intended for sort of the antithesis of Dark Souls. What I mean is that I think they wanted the monsters to be more difficult than the bosses, rather than Dark Souls' trash that you learn to clear efficiently followed by a very difficult boss. In most cases this stays a theme throughout the game, sans the third boss which is fairly challenging and the fourth which is (in my opinion) outright cheap. There are five bosses, and the fifth is a cakewalk - especially after the crazy that is the fourth.

By now you're probably wondering if I clicked the wrong option on 'Do you recommend this game?'! I do recommend it, despite these and more flaws, because I do actually look back on my time with it fondly. While encounters can feel (and be) a little ridiculous, there is always a way to puzzle out a victory. While the difficulty can be really spiky, I still slogged through the nightmarish parts and then finally enjoyed feeling like a badass during the ending cakewalk. I think that The Surge's biggest weakness, more than any of the aforementioned problems, is that it tries too hard to be Dark Souls.

It goes for the vague plot that you have to puzzle out yourself, but reveals too much in too many words and just winds up a weird jumble of barely understandable plot hooks in the end. Similarly, it goes for the Souls-esque 'quest' system where you meet NPCs along the way and learn their stories while also helping them out. I think it fails there as well, mostly in offering little to no actual reward for them (I paid someone 25,000 Scrap just to have them kill an NPC I liked with no explanation and give me a crappy weapon) and just feeling... Out of place. Like they were just put in because, "Dark Souls does it, so why don't we?" I feel that they could have focused the effort they put into these Souls-esque tidbits into something innovative instead, and maybe something that the writers were a little better at.

Let me go into what I liked, before this becomes too negative, though! I really like the aesthetic. I've heard complaints that it's all just steel corridors, but I found the level design varied enough that I was never particularly bored with how things looked. Speaking of design, I also really love the armor and weapons! Some of them kind of strain to make sense within the setting (why is this guy beating me up with what is described as a medical tool?), but everything looks cool and that's the thing I care about most. The animations are great, I believe motion captured, and everything feels weighty and awesome. I love it!

I really like the combat! Go figure, given I spent most of the game beating up dudes. The limb-targeting system is really cool, and I especially think that having to saw off enemy parts to make your own armor was a really cool design decision. Not only do the executions feel great to pull off, the parts they provide you with are valuable and tuned well enough so that grinding or farming never feels like much of a chore. I wound up using heavy armor, the Cerberus set to be specific, but I've also heard that lighter armor is just as fun. There are a lot of weapons in the game, though separated into only five categories - and those five categories all have very similar movesets... I didn't change my chosen weapon type ever, and stuck with one-handed all the way through the game. In a sort of unfortunate decision, you level weapon proficiencies as you use a weapon type more, and damage scales off of that. That means whatever you choose to use first and most at the start will probably always be the best type for you, unless you want to grind another up.

The implant system is another thing I liked; in favor of a traditional leveling system where you gain stats, you instead gain Core Power which lets you equip more powerful Implants that you find in the world. Certain thresholds of Core Power will let you equip more Implants, and you can also increase your Implant slots by finding new RIGs (though there are only 2). There are some no-brainer Implants like extra HP and your Healing Injections, but it was customizable enough that I swapped a lot of things in and out throughout the whole game regardless of those pseudo-necessary ones.

As for the story, I can't really say whether I liked or disliked it. It was there? I was vaguely engaged throughout most of the game, though the payoff near the end was lackluster to say the least. It wasn't enough of a let down to make me stop playing, or anything, but it sort of just made me go, "Oh. Alright." These types of games aren't known for having an impressive story to drive you forward, though! Even the Dark Souls games typically only have 'kill all the bosses and do the thing because destiny and stuff' as their motivation. You can look deeper into the lore and find a way more intricate story, mind! In the case of The Surge, though, I don't really think it's there.

In closing, The Surge is a fairly frustrating game that has its flaws, but I can still look back and go, "Yeah. I liked that." It has a great aesthetic and a wonderful combat system, but falls a little short in making some encounters mind-numbingly difficult (or just annoying) sometimes. The difficulty is spiky, though you will certainly feel like you've earned the easy jog of a one-man death machine at the end, and while the final boss was rather easy... I still liked it. And I'd recommend it! Maybe on sale, if you wanna play it safe.\t\t\t\t\t
