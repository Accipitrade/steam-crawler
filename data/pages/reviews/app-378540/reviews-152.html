\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is Deck13's recovery from Lords of the Fallen. This time around, combat is fast and tight a la Bloodborne. This game is wholly deserving of the "Souls-like" label and is worthy to stand beside Bloodborne itself, the greatest Souls game of them all.

Disclosure: I bought the game for full price ($50) on launch day. I have completed the campaign once and am at the beginning of NG+.

Opinion on cost: $50 is a steep price for any game. For Souls enthusiasts, the asking price is fair in my opinion. I will likely replay this multiple times on NG and NG+. For newcomers, this game's difficulty coupled with a steep pricetag is likely to cause some frustration. If you have never played a Dark souls game before, or if you are just "meh" about the genre, wait for this game to go on sale, or look to cheaper entries in the Souls series (with the exception of Dark Souls 2) to get you started.

The Pros:
+ Fluid combat, tight controls. The most important ingredients in a Souls game!
+Graphics options are robust, only missing a few key features. Depth of Field is oddly absent.
+ NG+ adds much harder enemy placements and increases the upgrade cap on gear so you actually scale up into NG+ stats in a satisfying way rather than beasting on it with NG gear.
+ Bosses are excellent. Most Dark Souls games flood the game with mediocre bosses and a few gems here and there. The Surge has a dramatically lower boss count, but each is far more memorable, challenging, and mechanically interesting. Many of the bosses in The Surge demand that players have full knowledge of the main character's moveset, which is something that Souls games typically gloss over in boss design.
+ Difficulty is crushingly hard (yes, this is a pro in Souls-land). The Surge is arguably harder than the entirety of FromSoft's work, but it isn't unfair. Bloodstains are timed, so you need to rush to pick them up, but killing enemies refills the timer (retrieving souls is a more stressful matter now, but skill and precision are rewarded). Attacking works differently overall. Weapon types have different amounts of Impact and armor has certain Stability ratings, which affect hitstun strength and recovery time. Enemies usually do damage to you even when you're stringing attacks on them because their ability to attack through your hitstun gives them enough time to hit you. This seems weird at first for Souls veterans, but it ultimately makes combat a lot more intertesting because you rarely can get an easy rushdown kill on an enemy. Encounters are fewer and far between, but there are no "weak" enemies and they are all capable of killing you quickly.


The Cons:
- Depth of Field and Monitor Target settings are oddly absent, meaning console config users need to make Big Picture alter their main monitor target by overriding Windows controls to get the game over to the TV instead of having it pop up on their primary monitor.
-No MAP. In other Souls games this isn't necessary because there are so many bonfires and you can easily fast-travel. Levels are also more iconic and open in Souls games. The Surge has a lot of same-y looking areas, and lots of small corridors looping around each area. A 3D map like Metroid Prime features would have made keeping track of exploration immensely less confusing, and it would even make sense in the game's context as a sci-fi Souls.
-Sidequests don't matter. In Bloodborne, there was a host of sidequests that you could either complete or miss, and they would award you with useful gear or upgrades. They were fun to complete and added to the world. The Surge attempts a similar format in which you discover quest-givers and you can miss them by progressing the game. However, none of them give any real reward beyond a Steam Achievement. This is a huge bummer and totally unrewarding, expecially when you consider that Bloodborne and Dark Souls 3 Have entire areas that are optional to complete, including bosses. The core experience of The Surge is very refined, but the extra stuff feels very rushed.
-Balance is weighted for low-defense, high-aggression armor sets. Technically this could be considered a pro, because the best way to play the game is high-aggression, but it also means there is really only one good playstyle in the game, whereas Dark Souls 3 and Bloodborne have a host of great builds and different viable weapons. I listed this as a con because it makes a large amount of gear you can get in the game completely worthless. Literally don't ever build a piece of Goliath or Sentinel class armor. You will be a little more tanky but so much less capable of killing enemies and dodging attacks.

Ultimately I hope we see a "Surge 2" or something of that sort. The core gameplay here is excellent, but we need a game with more sidequests (good ones!), some optional areas with cool bosses and rewards, and more freedom in customization. A few games-worth of revisions to the core model of The Surge would genuinely rival the Souls francise, and could even surpass it. It is good to see games like this delivering such a satisfying combat and difficulty experience in the absence of new Dark souls or Bloodborne entries.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ excellent. Bosses are thrilling, but there are only five of them. Combat is tense and exciting throughout. Grinding, leveling and building are ingenious. And the story hit me with two themes surprisingly effectively.

The Bosses

There are only 5 bosses in this game. However, this game makes the absolute most of those 5 encounters. The first boss is your warm-up. It's your introduction to the game. It punishes you if you're bad, though. So, as a beginner, it feels good when you defeat it.

The next four bosses are absolute monsters. Every one of them is O&S or Nameless-King difficult, and each for its own unique reason. Their movesets are very complex, so it's hard to learn their patterns quickly. They also have varied abilities to trap you, meaning they have a move or two that, if they start up while you're in the wrong position, you're toast. And they hit like trucks. Very, very fun boss fights in this game.

Combat

First things first: this game is ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ hard. By now, in this sort of game, we're used to "trash mobs." These are groups of enemies that merely stand between you and a boss. They're usually dumb and easy unless you lose your patience and do something stupid. However, even a normal enemy standing by himself is a challenge in The Surge. This means there's no such thing as a trash mob. There are mobs from which you try desperately to pull one enemy at a time. Just looking at certain mob makeups will make you ♥♥♥♥ your pants with fear and rage.

Anyway.

You're a man wearing an exosuit with ability-altering armor welded to it. When you fight, you feel like a man wearing an exosuit with ability-altering armor welded to it. Attacks are really heavy and slow if you're using heavy weapons. They're really bursty and light if you're using the quick weapons.

Defense is suitably complicated. Your dodge is god-tier, no matter your build. You leap whole meters to either side, front or back, and i-frames protect you on your way. If you time your dodge correctly, time will slow down for just a bit. If you attack during the slowdown, you get bonus damage. That's as tasty as it sounds.

You can block, as well. Initiating the block drains a chunk of stamina, then holding the block continuously drains the rest. So, you can't abuse it. If you block at just the right time, then you've parried. Follow it up with attacks for the good damage.

This game has an addition to defense that will give Souls veterans challenge, as well. While blocking, you can press up or down on the right analog stick to either jump or duck for a second. Jump over low attacks, duck under high attacks. Attacking afterward stuns the enemy and amplifies the damage of your next attack. If you're really good, you can use these techniques to burn through enemies really quickly.

Later, you get a drone for ranged attacks. These ranged attacks consume energy, which you build during a fight.

Energy also lets you use injectables (like a special healing item) and execute enemies.

Speaking of executing enemies, let's talk about grinding/leveling/building.

Grinding/Leveling/Building

To me, this is a slog in most games. Build a strength character by committing point after point to the strength attribute. Regret your strength build? Look for a respec item, find the respec NPC, and respec.

The Surge makes me love it. Its method is ingenious.

The four components of grinding/leveling/building are gear, proficiency, implants and power core level.

Gear is obvious--it's what you wear. Each piece of a set has its own perks (defense, stability, stam reduction/increase, damage reduction/increase, etc.). When you're wearing every piece in a set, you get a full-set bonus. One set's bonus, for example, is the Chloranthy ring--stamina regens much, much faster. Another gives you health regen. Yet another grants you lots of bonus damage when you're at full energy. This is the most basic way to determine your character's build.

You get gear by executing enemies in the right way. While fighting, you can target specific body parts with your strikes--head, arm, leg, torso. If you focus your attacks on one before you execute, the execution will tear the body part away from the body. When that happens, the gear held or worn by that body part falls to the ground, and you can pick it up. You obtain weapons straight away. You get armor blueprints, which you use to craft the piece of armor back at the med bay. You also get upgrade materials from the part you chop, which can be used to craft and upgrade armor of the type collected or weapons.

Proficiency takes the place of weapon scaling attributes--strength and dex, as many of us would know them. Use a weapon type, and each kill earns you proficiency. The proficiency level of a type determines how much bonus damage you get from "high-scaling" weapons of that type. What I love about this is that you don't need to redesign your whole character just to switch from "strength" to "dex." Just use the new weapon.

Implants are how you optimize your base build--how you conform the build to your playstyle. Some can be hot-swapped in the field. Others can only be swapped out or in at the med bay. They add health, add stamina, add energy, determine how you regain health, buff your damage in certain contexts, make certain actions drain less stamina, allow you to breathe toxic gas, and on and on. Mixing and matching implants to get the most our of your build is how you build your character in The Surge. No planning necessary. I rebuilt myself entirely for the purpose of one boss fight. Then, I changed my armor and implants back to what they were, afterward, and continued on my way. It's genius. No more anxiety at the start of a game as we decide where to put our points. Those of us who built for faith in DS2 know all too well what that anxiety feels like.

This brings us to build constraints, which brings us to the power core, which brings us to tech scrap. Tech Scrap is your currency. It's what you drop when you die. It's Souls.

With tech scrap, you upgrade your power core at each zone's med bay (your bonfire). You're building only one stat in the Surge point-by-point, and this is that stat. Power Core level determines which doors you can open, how many implant slots you can use, and which implants and gear you can equip. Each implant and piece of gear carries with it a power consumption value. Total value of all equipped gear and implants must be fewer than your power core level.

The Story

You're a guy named Warren at his first day on the job. He's starting with Creo, a technology conglomerate. At your work site, they're developing a program called "Resolve." What is it? There's your story, right there.

Shortly after you're given your exosuit, you pass out and wake up in the dumpster. Your power core is busted, so the drones were about to "disassemble" you to recycle your exosuit. Other manual laborers in exosuits are nearby, but they're homicidal monsters. What happened? There's your story, right there.

The story, by its conclusion, hit me pretty hard with two themes. The first is the feeling of powerlessness as a blue-collar laborer at a massive tech company. I found myself resenting the absolute hell out of management and feeling very anxious about the lack of information they were giving me about what was going on.

The second is environmentalism. This story, however, conveys that theme through a lens of desparation that I find very frightening. The Earth is on the verge of inability to sustain human life. How do humans react to that? Scary stuff.

Conclusion

Buy this game and play it. Unless you're afraid of really ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ hard games. Combat finds a way to be challenging even to a Dark Souls veteran. Grinding/building/leveling is all the reward with none of the hours of filler and prayer to RNGesus. And the story conveys some themes that are worth considering.

So do it. Do it now.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t**Short Review**

Enemies have the same attack patterns throughout the entire game with very little variance. Simply attack up to two times, then back off before you get one shot in a single combo.

Only two real playstyles in the game: Equip heavy gear or light gear, both of which doesn't protect you from dying in a single stunlock from most enemies.

Some enemies have horrible placement. (enemies placed just as you enter a corridor that charge you with no room to back up and without the ability to dodge past them for example)

Everything bad about this game drowns out the good things I did enjoy. While the world is interconnected with tons of shortcuts leading back to your main areas, everything just feels like a boring maze in which you're constantly trying to remember where you came from.

While I enjoyed this far more than Lords of the Fallen, it just simply seems like these developers need a bit more practice, or a different genre. Luckily, the things that feel wrong to me, are easy to fix. I wish them the best of luck!

This game is only 5 bosses long for a $49.99 game. While length doesn't always ruin a game, it simply isn't packed with enough good things to justify it for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is a game I have very mixed feelings on. Gameplay wise it's great, but the balancing of the game feels very odd. The beginning and end are fine, but the middle is easily the hardest part of the game, at least with my with build: operator class rig (light armor) and one handed weapons. Many of the enemies at this stage have ranged weapons capable of high damage in addition to melee attacks capable of hitting 90+% of my health. This continued from around the 3rd to 4th boss, and then got better. To minimize frustration at this point in the game I would stack at 2-3 health and heal mods and definitly upgrade your armor which is something I didn't really do early in the game.

The level design is good for the most part and filled with shortcuts back to the operations, which acts similarly to the bonfires in Dark Souls. Now and then however it can be extremely frustrating with enemy placement or not being able to find where to go, because the areas can look a bit similar.

The leveling system is very comparable to Warframes. As you level using the scrap enemies drop you gain power capacity which is used to slot in mods and armor. This system works well for the most part, but can also make the leveling feel a bit dull.

The combat feels very souls like with a few small changes. First of all after you target an enemy you can further target individual limbs, which may or may not be armored. As you attack you gain energy which can be used for all manor of things, such as using injectable items, using drone abilities, and most importantly cutting off limbs, which is how you aquire many of the armor and weapons in the game. instead of strong and light attacks you have horizontal and vertical attacks which can be mixed together for combo attacks, as well as doing bonus damage versus certain limbs.

My first playthrough took just over 20 hours, which while on the shorter side I think it's fine as the game does have replay potential with different weapons and ng+. Personally I would recommend this game full price if you think you can deal with the high difficulty towards the middle of the game, and if not definitly on sale if this seems like something you would like.

Also, be warned that every time you enter operations the game assaults you with country music.\t\t\t\t\t
