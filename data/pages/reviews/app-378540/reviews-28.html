\r\n\t\t\t\t\tpreface: I already beat this game on Origin back when I used to do EA Access so my time played here is a lie.

Feels a bit different than many in the genre. I like the focus on body parts and scavenging. It makes the souls grinding a little bit more about what you need than getting the highest amount in the quickest time. The customization is great as well. In Dark Souls, I always struggled with this and ended up with what's called an Artorias build. In the surge, you can change your build on the fly.
In lieu of character stats, you have armor stats and set bonuses. Additionally, your character has proficiency ratings for each weapon archetype. So as you practice more with one type of weapon, you will start outputting more damage with it. Which is nice because it makes you feel like you're getting stronger while you are getting better with each weapon's combo set.

The weapons themselves are varied although there aren't many if any options for long range. But you can poke enemies with your drone to come to you instead of fighting large groups so it's not a big issue. Even among the archetypes of weapons there are unique weapons which have their own combos and attacks so it pays to switch around weapons and see what you like. Don't listen to people that oversell proficiency. Those people are just lacking proficiency irl and need the game to compensate them. Don't get me wrong, proficiency is POTENT on some weapons. But some weapons just smash regardless of your finesse.

Story-wise it's pretty fun. You'll meet other survivors and help them out if you so choose and they'll move along on their own personal journeys while you endeavor to complete yours and audio logs, grafitti, and videos will help fill in the gaps as you forge ahead. It also has a good sense of humor in many of the minor stories told through your investigating.

The game features a duck and hop system for dodging horizontal attacks which will allow you to counterattack with a stunning attack in the form of either a superman drop or a flying knee. Additionally, it has a counterattack which utilizes their "perfect block" system for weapons that are functional as a shield as well as a big sword. Then of course you can press A for your classic i-frame distance-cover maneuver which is reminiscent of Control or Bloodborne moreso than Dark Souls.

Although there is plenty to praise about this game, it has its fair share of problems as well. Such as the cinematic kill cam. When you are performing a cut, the default camera will slow down and you will have to wrestle with it against the environment. This can be disabled in options, but it was included in the game and doesn't work as intended. With this setting disabled though, it's not an issue at all.

Another problem I have is that targeting body parts with weapons feels very unintuitive. Especially when you are still new to the game. Trying to do the dash attack when you have a high speed weapon will often have you do the sprint attack because the window for "at the same time" is around .2 seconds (or at least it feels that way) so when you are trying to learn combos you will get a lot of false negatives on execution. Which makes it take much longer than it should. Additionally, sometimes - especially during boss fights - the limb you are targeting will slip right past your weapon. This happens less as you learn your specific weapon's combos because you know which moves will get you a diagonal slash hit or a lunge when you need them.

All that said, I would recommend this game. It's a riot.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI like many aspects of the game, but am rating it down because of the insane difficulty, grind, and maze-like maps.

The story ain't too bad, and the setting is actually kind of cool. The combat is not the best, but it's satisfying, and meaty. Once you get the hang of it you can wipe the floor with most enemies easily enough.

The problem is that in order to get new schematics, and parts to build and upgrade those weapons/armor, you need to remove particular pieces off of enemies. You need a certain kind of alloy or part to upgrade your helmet that you can only get when you find an enemy who is also wearing a helmet, and rip his head off. Same goes for every other body part / weapon.

In practice this means that you will have to kill an ungodly number of enemies in order to upgrade your systems. Finding enemies ain't a problem because they respawn constantly.

However, the maps are also incredibly convoluted and difficult to navigate (terrible camera placement, and control also plays into this), which means that you will have to fight CONSTANTLY as you spend valuable time trying to figure out where the heck you should be heading.

Overall, I found the experience to be frustrating, and repetitive. The excitement you feel at finding a new weapon, or entering a new area is quickly dispelled by having to grind parts, and find your way through destroyed buildings.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t6.7/10. Fun combat that gets just a little old by the end, although the limb cutting mechanic manages to stay entertaining at the least. Sparse bosses, but they're diverse for the most part. For fans of that "industrial dystopian cyber-ruins" thing, or whatever. Exoskeletons, right?

My first impression was that The Surge would be a great klepto game, where I get to keep stealing things that are unfortunately attached to a moving target. But after a pretty short time, limb cutting turns into either a chore to upgrade all my equipment, or a quick way to end a fight without the rewards I initially thought it would have. I think I was a little let down just because I hadn't thought about what it would really be like, though, so not a big deal. Exo-theft is fun, and figuring out the weak spot on a jittery enemy is engaging.

Speaking of jittery, I said that the combat is fun, but but that doesn't mean it's not janky, to say the least. Enemies have very simple behavior regarding aggro, where they'll instantly turn to you if they notice you, and instantly turn and run if you lure them too far away. They also always turn to face you, so if you want to get behind, you have to wait for them to get stuck in an animation. The windup for enemy attacks is also very fast sometimes, to the point where I just couldn't get out of the way fast enough. If you decide to run through an area without dealing with enemies individually, they can pile up behind you, and you get stunlocked very easily in those situations.

As far as world design, these places are extremely compact. They make excellent use of vertical space, which is nice. Shortcuts let out to two, sometimes three different places. The maintenance tunnels you run through, with the exo-lifts moving you vertically, can end up making the world nightmarishly maze-like if you don't have a perfect sense of direction (even indoors, which... it's all indoors) or have every landmark committed to memory (which there a bit less of than I'd like, considering it's supposed to be a sterile corporate environment, even on the production floor). Ultimately, it gets pretty boring, but at least it's a consistent theme throughout.

The world building was pretty good. The characters were convincing enough (if you want to be completely brainwashed, I honestly really enjoyed seeing Don Hackett, the smiling face on every CREO screen throughout the game, even with the propaganda he represented), plentiful audio logs, relatively few side quests. I respect that it let the tone get pretty dark towards the second half. I really hadn't thought that hard about the implications of what was going on around me up until then. The music is good, too, builds the atmosphere well.

All in all, solid game, but noticeably buggy at times. Died to a few weird interactions (I think an object in the world moved and pushed me into the ground and the game freed me by killing me), some visual glitches, etc. Nothing unplayable, though, so most of them were entertaining.\t\t\t\t\t
