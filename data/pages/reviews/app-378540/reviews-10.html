\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI would recommend this game, but with some caveats. There are by now a great number of games that borrow elements from the Dark Souls lineage of games -- which is easily my favorite genre of games, I have come to realize --, but at the same time there aren't that many games that offer an experience which is almost one to one with the From games in terms of structure. Within this more restricted group of games, you have absolute world beaters (Dark Souls I and III, Sekirô, Ni-ôh 2, Bloodborne, Elden Ring), and you have the lesser gods (Demon's Souls, Dark Souls II, Star Wars Jedi: Fallen Order, Mortal Shell, Salt & Sanctuary, etc). And of course, there are games that just aren't all that good, below all these.

I situate The Surge as being on the same level as Mortal Shell on this inpromptu tier list. By that I mean that it is a good and, for the most part, satisfying one of those games. However, I would also recommend all of these other games over The Surge (aside from Mortall Shell. Really, I would say this game and that one are interchangeable in terms in overall quality). The Surge is a product meant for people who can't get enough of this formula of games, and have basically run out of these other games that are better than it. There are genre brethren, like Code:Vein and Lords of the Fallen, which also closely stick to the Dark Souls structure, but fall below the line of quality that makes them recommendable, in my view. The Surge clears this bar fairly comfortably.

However, regardless of this praise, which the game really does earn, there are also significant things which hold The Surge back for me, despite my overall enjoyment being great enough that I put in about 22 hours and finished it without too much of a sweat.

First of all, the combat just didn't really do it for me. This game is basically Havel: The Game. Both you and the enemies are heavy robots or half-robots, and this makes every combat encounter a poise fest. I usually go for quick weapons and dodging in these types of games, but I found no 'quick' weapons at all that were satisfying here, because the enemies are nearly impossible to stun and so will just casually wind up and hit you in the face as your are laying absolute siege to them. Your own character, for his part, is a slow and hulking bulldozer, and so I never felt nimble while fighting, at all. Instead, fairly early on I found one of the heaviest weapons in the game, and then stuck with that for the rest of it, so that when I did hit dudes, at least it did a noticeable amount of damage instead of feeling like I was doling out pin pricks. The combat therefore felt like a lumpy, slow mess at just about all times. I tried multiple times during my playthrough to switch back to ostensibly 'faster' weapons, but my character still felt like a tank while using them, and so I kept reverting to my heavy blade.

Despite this complaint, I still had enough moments of fun taking out enemies to carry me through the game. I traded blows with them throughout, and there was fairly little finesse going on at any point, but it was satisfying enough for what it was. However, I can honestly say that I deeply disliked just about all of the boss fights, for the same reason given above. There mercifully are only few of them, but the frustrations with the slow rhythm of attacking come out even more when the bosses basically have unlimited poise, and can hit you for a lot of damage. There were two boss fights where I saw no other way to victory than to literally hit the boss once, retreat, hit them once more, retreat, and so on. It was boring and long-winded.

The third issue is with the environment design. On the one hand, I really quite like the visual style they went for. All of the other Souls-likes named above fit squarely into the high-fantasy genre (whether it be a Japanese samurai motif, or a medieval knights-and-castles motif). The Surge, with its hyperindustrial and near-future setting, is a nice refresher among these. However, the entire game is made up of environments that have those same hypersleek, factory-of-the-future sensibilities to them, and it gets boring after a while. Furthermore, the way-pointing is inadequate considering what the game asks of you in exploration and risk-taking. I was quite often at a loss of where I was supposed to go after a story sequence. I would get told to 'go to x place', but then the game would not tell me how to find this place, or in which direction to even go to find it. You may say 'Dark Souls does the same thing!', but that is not true. Yes, Dark Souls does not hold your hand at all, but the genius of the level design in those games is that the environments organically bring you to new places, through subtle signposting.

For instance, though there are of course significant sections of Dark Souls happening inside or underground, even in those cases the developers always made sure to have very distinct sections to each level, as to give the player the ability to remember these places, and gradually form a strong mental map of how each part of the level is connected to the others. In those games, you will often come up from some hole in the ground on a ladder, and then see the many 'landmarks' of the level sprawl out before you. This makes it easy to orient yourself as the player. The Surge does not have any of this. There are very few signs in the level that help you to find your way, nearly all of it takes places inside, and many parts of the level look indistinct from one another. The final level especially is a nightmare in this sense. It is a long series of interconnected and utterly same-y tunnels, that give you no clue where you are in relation to anything else. You will arrive at a fork in the road in one of these cramped crawl spaces, and there will be elevators on both sides, which in turn both lead to another set of tunnels that also look exactly the same, with yet more elevators. I completely lost my bearings, and spent way too much time essentially feeling around in the dark in that final level.

I was a bit disappointed with the levels for this reason. Interestingly enough (or perhaps, logically enough), the DLC level is the one instance where the devs fully departed from what had come before in terms of levels. I had bought the deluxe edition or whatever, and so it was included in my game. I went to the dlc very early on, and was blown away by how different the aesthetics of that section were compared to the dreary industrial complex I had just come from. Not knowing it was a DLC, I expected this game to have quite a bit of environmental variety for this reason. However, when I left the DLC and continued further and further, I realized this would not be the case: it's reflective metal and annoying jingles all the way down in the main game.

Of course, there is a reason Dark Souls is so beloved: thanks to the sheer quality of the mix between well-tuned combat, amazing world-building, and straight-up mind-blowing level design, From Software effectively created a new genre in 2009/2011. It is normal that not every game that tries to emulate these elements manages to reach the same levels of quality in them. I would say that The Surge does a very admirable job in general: it is a still a good one of those games in my mind, despite the serious gripes I had with it, and it also carves out its own territory with its visual sensibilities, and some really cool design ideas such as the targeting of specific limbs to get specific build materials for your character. But the issues it has, did get in the way fairly regularly for me.

So yeah, The Surge is a solid 7/10. Fairly easy to enjoy, and worth it for that reason. But if you want to play a Souls-like, and saw a game in the small list in the first paragraph that you haven't played yet, I urge you to pick any one of those games up before you get going on this one.\t\t\t\t\t
