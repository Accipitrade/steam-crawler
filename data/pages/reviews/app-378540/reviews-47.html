\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOk, so I finally got around to playing this and I really enjoyed it for the most part.

Pros:

Story: While not incredibly original I still enjoyed the story. I won't spoil it but even though we've seen this type of story before it was well done.

Environments: The environments were appropriately atmospheric and downright creepy at times.

Combat: The combat felt good. The weapons felt like they should. Big weapons feel heavy and smaller weapons feel light and fast.

Level Interconnectivity: One of the things Dark Souls 1 is praised for is its level interconnectivity and The Surge has that in spades.

Enemies: The enemies were fun to fight and some were pretty challenging.

Cons:

Level Interconnectivity: I haven't spent this much time wandering around a level or levels trying to figure out where to go since Turok 2: Seeds of Evil on the N64. The first couple of shortcuts I found were exciting and then it seemed like every time I was finding something new it turned out to be a way back to the current hub. This was useful but also confusing, especially if you put the game down for a while and come back to it wondering which way to go. Also, this game could really use a map.

Enemies: Some of them can one-shot you regardless of how much health you have, at least until you get some upgrades later. Combine that with the confusing levels and you spend a lot of time just figuring out where you died. The first time a mostly normal looking enemy killed me I thought it was a glitch, then I thought maybe he had knocked me into some part of the environment that was killing me. Still, the combat is fun but frustrating at times.

Communication: NPC "I need you to go to a place." Me "Great! Where is this..." NPC: "I need you to go to a place." Seriously, please give me a little guidance as to where this place is.

Overall it is a good game and I am looking forward to playing the sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJanky. In the weirdest way possible. For a game so focused on combat, you'd really, really hope that the developers would put a little more emphasis on how the player character moves moment to moment. Instead, it sort of feels like you're trying to control a GTA character during a Dark Souls boss fight. Things are not as responsive as they probably should be. Now, that alone doesn't kill the experience, though. There are massive hit-box issues in the game too, so you'll find yourself getting nailed by things that you shouldn't be or you'll miss hits that should connect. Additionally, the targeting system of the game really needs a bit more refinement. Rather than just using the right stick like most souls-like games, it uses the right stick and the left trigger. You use the trigger to change enemies and the right stick is how you select parts of an enemy's body. This often works just fine, but sometimes (notably on bosses) it's a broken mess where you'll be trying to target a different appendage that's actually attacking you but the game will target literally anything else but the thing you want to.

I know that a lot of people have found some enjoyment in this and I think there's some interesting stuff going on, but for everything that I listed above on top of some bland environments and absolutely garish character design, I see absolutely no reason to keep going in The Surge. I've read the sequel is better, so I'll likely try that later.

And, for all you readers that are wondering about my cred in the souls-like genre, I've completed every FromSoft Soulsbourne game as well as platinumed Sekiro. Difficulty isn't really the problem. When the game works, it's actually pretty easy.

Update after a few more hours: it did not get better. I stopped at the Bed of Chao--I mean the robot with a billion arms. I'm really done with it now.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Surge is the best bad game I've ever played.
- Story
The protagonist (Warren) is a wheelchair bound every-man starting his first day at CREO, a massive corporation founded to stop climate change.
The story is largely a critique of consumerism and Silicon Valley culture. The game is populated almost entirely by robotic enemies, or mindless workers loaded down with working equipment. The game even "forces" you to act out its core themes by having Warren violently remove gear from enemies, only to equip it himself, or upgrade existing equipment. I found myself reflecting on this at times when I was killing the same enemies over and over, just because I thought their armor looked cool.
The CREO complex is also populated by several A.I, including Jonah Guttenberg which supports the central theme, and is a natural end point for the dire picture the game paints for humanity.
Another theme the game touches on is humanity as a collective conscience. The Rogue Process (the final boss) is an amalgamation of the thoughts (or souls) of all the workers present in the factory at the time of "the surge," that caused the game world's current state. The A.I is acting in a way that reflects its own interests, or rather the interests of "humanity" as a whole. With the end point being a rocket full of self aware nanites that will kill an estimated 95% of humans, while the nanites themselves proliferate as a new, and dominant life form. Basically a eugenics program run by detached elitists.
- Gameplay
The gameplay starts out very solid, and the tutorial hints are wonderfully utilized. They're sort of plastered onto various objects that you should naturally be looking at, and this occurs well into the game without ever being intrusive. You start with a single enemy in front of you, the core mechanics are introduced, both very basic concepts.
There is also a slew of weapons to collect, although the move sets for the 5 weapon archetypes are the same. You'll likely end up picking a single weapon type, and leveling it using the weapon proficiency system, with little to no reason to ever change weapons except to experiment with another type for fun.
Now for the bad.
The lock on ends up being a key barrier to enjoying the game at critical points.
Let me preface this by saying, it works well, and is actually one of the best lock on systems in any game as far as humanoid enemies, and is probably the most important core mechanic. I'm sure they worked on this quite a bit to make it fun.
The problem is attacking bosses. Not being locked onto a boss means you can freely attack, while you risk missing crucial information about the bosses attacks.
Being locked onto the boss means you're still going to lose that information, since the camera angles for large enemies are atrocious. The fight with Big Sister looks cool when you're locked on, but you can also attack the tower the boss is mounted on, and being locked on to the main body will likely cause you to swing at nothing fairly often.
There are also several difficulty spikes that ruin pacing. Black Cerberus I want to be forgiving of these spikes, as they end up being some of the best fights in the game, but it feels artificially difficult, which in turn breaks immersion, by reminding you you're playing a computer game and not in a world governed wholly by consistency.
These issues can ruin enjoyment for major portions of the game, and with the game being as small as it is, quite a bit of your experience.
However, the fluidity in combat, the combo system, injectables, armor bonuses, overall speed and responsiveness, all make for a very fun, tense, and challenging experience.
Except for EVERY DOOR YOU OPEN HAVING AN ENEMY SOMEWHERE TO THE SIDE READY TO JUMP AT YOU WHILE A STING NOISE PLAYS AND YOU DIE
- Level Design
A fully realized sci-fi factory. Perhaps too fully realized, as all the tunnels, and hallways end up looking exactly the same. This is realistic, but not good for landmarks, as there are a couple branching paths within these access tunnels. The tunnels are also problematic when you end up fighting enemies inside of them, and the game is full of tight corridors where you end up fighting enemies. It isn't interesting, and actually detracts from the wonderful combat the game has on offer. The depth of combat exceeds The Surge's contemporaries (Dark Souls, Nioh, Lords of the Fallen,) but never quite feels like it does due to the self implemented restrictions.
Unlocking shortcuts, and returning to Central Production B for the third time is actually fantastic use of level design. Returning to areas you previously weren't able to visit, and making progress through this sprawling factory is both rewarding, and makes the world feel fully actualized.
- DLC Both DLCs made me enjoy the game far more as a whole, although they're very short.
They stick to the core theme of dangerous consumerism in more fun settings, which relates the idea in a far more relatable way than a dystopian factory setting, unless you work in a dystopian factory, in which case, best of luck.
I highly recommend CREO World, as it has possibly the best boss fight in the entire game, and to a lesser degree I recommend The Good the Bad and the Westworld, just because cowboys are cool, and it's mission based with optional challenges, which some people will find to be a lot of fun.
-Final Verdict
7/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me Preface this by saying I HATE this game.

At the time of review I have spent almost ten hours playing this game, I have cleared the first area and made almost no progress in the second, as if I needed more proof that Dark souls has ruined melee combat in games I paid hard earned money to endure this manifestation of hell.
I cannot comment on the plot because as far as I can tell there is not much of one, you are a worker called Warren who until recently was wheel-chair bound, you had an exoskeleton grafted to your body and "something" has destroyed the facility you work at and near on everything in it is trying to kill you.
They usually attempt this by hiding around corners, In dingy pitch black environments or using guns from the far end of a corridor, sometimes the enemy just decides to kill you with a one hit attack that you cannot avoid since you trapped in the small tunnel it refuses to follow you out of.

And what are your means of self defence, non. you are not allowed to protect your self in this modern day crime against the Geneva convention, your options in regards to weapons are ones you can swing fast enough to hit the enemy on the rare occasions they are in range, or ones that actually deal damage. your armour does nothing as far I can tell and nothing can be upgraded to help you survive the mission you are stuck in because non of the components you need spawn in the area you keep dying in.

In regards to stamina, well you have non. Warren has been wheel chair bound for some period of time but he acts as if he has spent his life in an iron lung, dodging, and all forms attacks drain your stamina thimble, (I refuse to call something that shallow a pool.) As well blocking, not only deflecting incoming attacks but the act of raising your arms.

All of this might, MIGHT, be tolerable if it where not for the abysmal level design, lack of any map or objective screen, and the respawn of every enemy in your way.

So I am sure your asking yourself why I recommend this gift from the depths of Hades' itself. I recommend this to all of the masochists that enjoy punishing themselves, although I would suggest a whip might be cheaper.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBelow average game.
The developers made great progress from lords of fallen, bust still the game is not very enjoying. This game is sold as a souls like game, but for me it is more like a resident evil with a deep combat system, the way they used their sounds and music gave me some creepy vibes that usually games like resident evil would cause. I was very excited about at the beginning with this futuristic industrial setting, but honestly it ended up going nowhere, the plot is extremely weak, the game introduce us a bunch of characters that have zero impact, there is a part that I literally killed one of them(btw forced by the game to do it) and I felt nothing, side characters were so annoyingly irrelevant, well lets not talk about the mc, he has 0 development.
The combat and graphics were decent for sure, if anyone is only focused on gameplay you could still enjoy the game, but even then grinding in this game is very bad, makes things that you found fresh at the start very tedious during the long run. Also jumping feels really weird in this game.
In my opinion it's a game that you would play when there s nothing else better.\t\t\t\t\t
