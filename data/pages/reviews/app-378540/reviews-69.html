\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is *SO* good. The story, the combat, the setup, it's soo engrossing. It's important not to come at the game thinking it's one of these "everyone gets a cookie" MMOs. It is a harder game, that will punish mistakes when you make them -- and I love it! It's as closed as I've seen to that thrilling feel of open-world PvP games while playing Solo. At the same time, if you are the type who prefers to grind and "collect all the gear", i'll want to turn off the music (and medbay music), because what feels awesome and atmospheric at first can become a bit grating after the 100th trip to the medbay.

I did have a little trouble with video settings at first, as it kept saying the game needed to restart to change those settings, and then it would hang, or go super slow mo, or do some other weird thing. However, I finally got it setup at 1440p, windowed fullscreen, and it's been stable every since.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCHALLENGING
If you could describe this game in one word...

There is quite a bit of sound engineering and forethought in the battle mechanics of this game. You do not get to have guns, which is baffling to me considering how many of your enemies have ranged weapons. That being said there are quite a few unique and creative weapons and fighting styles which you unlock as you defeat enemies and recycle scrap.

The secrets in this game are actually rewarding, if you can kill them. (spoiler), and some of them start showing up in the first few stages of the game, so EXPLORE EVERYTHING.

You will have to kill the same enemy several times to get some of the cool gear that's out there, and those enemies are severely overpowered so there will be a massive amount of luck AND skill involved.

Check for backdoors, secret passages, smash all destructibles and kill every enemy, several times for maximum effect.

This game has a very Dark Souls vibe in some of the fighting mechanics, but it has a very Dead Island zombie atmosphere on top of that. The best part of this game is the part where (spoiler |||||||||||| ).

You will inevitably have to balance health consumables with energy mods and learn how to stack. Basic stuff, especially if you've ever played an in-depth RPG like Diablo,2,3.

Immortal Unchained share some things in common with this game in the dark grim futuristic vibe with a Necron (Warhammer 40,000) corrupted system overtone.

The visual effects and sound engineering leave little to be desired and are above par when compared to similar games.

There are glitches though, glitches that will kill you outright (drops) or lead to your death (getting stuck). It balances out when some glitches cause enemies to be stuck in place so you can just pelt them with attacks.

Some enemies have patterns, pay attention. Some seemingly attack at random, but its organized chaos. Prepare to take a hit, manage your heals, don't overreact.

A good follow up to this game is Immortal Unchained.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWould I recommend this to a friend? No.
The surge is a melting pot of clunky combat, grinding for materials like a JRPG, and neat mechanics poorly executed.
So let's talk about the bolts and gears of this game, combat.
In this souls-like game, you'll be slowly dodging attacks that lock onto you with inconsistent I-frames galore, and you will eventually receive the knowledge that you can do directional dodges, which is tied directly to your camera. How amazing.
So the neat twist, and or mechanic The Surge throws at you is that you can chop and sever your enemies appendages in the hopes that you can take their equipment for your own, and this is neat at first, until you realize this game has lots of grinding for alloy to increase your armors protective rating or to improve your damage output.
Once you realize the game's larger gameplay loop is a grind like a JRPG, the repetition dulls the experience.
The Surge is a frustrating experience that favors numbers over skill, and it doesn't truly let you have control over all your stats like Dark Souls. So avoid The Surge and either stick with Dark Souls or go for a more character action game experience, such as Devil May Cry 3, 4, or 5.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn short, The Surge is good but not great. The gameplay is fun, but there are plenty of faults, and the game is a bit short on its own. The atmosphere is decent, but not amazing, and the general story leaves a lot to be desired.

Let's start with the gameplay. The basic combat involves dodging and parrying enemy attacks while smacking their targeted limbs; once you lower their health enough, you can chop one of their limbs off in a satisfying finisher animation. You can get armor parts and weapons from humanoid enemies this way, providing a more engaging method of obtaining new equipment than souls-like farming. While fun, the combat also can feel artificially difficult at times, with enemies that deal jaw-dropping amounts of damage with hard-to-anticipate moves. It's also annoyingly easy to combo your way off a ledge, since most weapons have long, drawn-out katas that take a second to recover from.

In terms of story, the game is fairly mediocre. It has a decent dark sci-fi theme, but when most of the NPCs have all the charisma of a block of wood, it relies a bit too heavily on voice logs to convey the story. The beginning and ending also leave quite a bit to be desired, since it starts with a weird jump-cut from a botched surgery to running around a scrapyard. There's no clear explanation for how you get from point A to point B, and the short prologue where the protagonist is wheelchair-bound has a frustrating amount of unused potential. The ending, too, is a bit of a let-down, and it feels like we had no real influence on how things turned out. There's no closure and very little variety, with a cliffhanger scene of the protagonist crawling back to a wheelchair after the place explodes. The atmosphere is decent, with a passable variety between the various areas, but there are a lot of times where there's very little light for no good reason. Even with your flashlight, it's nigh impossible to make out enemies in these areas, and you often have to fight based on the glowing lights on their armor.

TL;DR: I would get this game on sale, and only if you're a fan of souls-like difficulty.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhether or not to recommend this game is… difficult.
When I started writing this, I actually had intended to click “recommend” for a couple of reasons, the first was… well… look at my playtime on the game! “Can the game really be ‘that’ bad if you played it from start to finish?”, I said to myself.
The second reason was that I can’t help but think that the fact this is a ‘Soulslike’ game (i.e. a Dark Souls clone) is part of why it doesn’t sit well with me, and that if I was unaware this was a Soulslike (by which I mean, unaware that Dark Souls exists), I might have enjoyed it far more.
I’ll elaborate on that in a second.
However as I come to the end of writing this review, I realize that I’ve only written criticisms, but despite genuinely trying, I can’t really come up with anything particularly nice to say about the game at all, other than ‘it has potential’.

So, I’ll start with the ‘Dark Souls comparison’ issues.
Thusfar, I’ve yet to play a 3D Soulslike that fully seemed to understand what it is that makes people love Dark Souls so much.
Firstly, they seem to think simply emulating the mechanics is enough… but the gameplay is only one of the many aspects that make the ‘Soulsborne’ series loved, there’s also things like the amazing environment and enemy design, the rich lore, the hidden mechanics etc.
This game fails on all fronts there, the environments in particular have so little variation (and are so mazelike) that navigating them is a nightmare, so much so that instead of discovering a shortcut being a “thank god for that” moment like in the Soulsborne series, you’re instead thinking “oh damn, now I’ve added ANOTHER identical paths for myself to get lost down later”.

On that note, the developers don’t seem to understand what a ‘shortcut’ is, while there are a few useful shortcuts from one Medbay (this games bonfire equivalent) to another, or from the end of a level (near a boss) back to the Medbay, the overwhelming majority of shortcuts instead link two points in a given area, but these shortcuts are usually neither a quicker route… nor a safer route… they’re just a ‘different’ route; they offer little to no advantage in distance/travel time, and are usually guarded by enough enemies that they’re at least as difficult as the original path you took, if not more.
As I said, all they really stand to do is make the map even more confusing to navigate… there’s genuinely been multiple times where I need to progress to the next section of the game, and have taken 3 or 4 paths from the endpoint of a section, all of which just lead me back to the start of the current section!

The issues with the environment just seemed to get worse and worse as the game went on, and I damn near through in the towel in the last level, in which I spent nearly two hours straight just running around in circles.
The solution was basically that, down one of the half dozen or so potential paths, down one of the half dozen or so branches from each path, there was a circuit (basically a switch) that didn’t seem to do anything (usually these have pipes of cables leading you to what they unlock or activate, but in this case they just went into the wall), which I eventually discovered opened one of the many locked doors, again located down one of the many turning down one of the many paths.

While we’re on the topic of the level design; elevators.
This game is overloaded with them.
Don’t get me wrong, nothing wrong with having elevators in your game, just like in real life sometimes they’re the best way to connect two different elevations… but if your game is set up so that literally every minute or two you’re riding an elevator… you probably need to rethink your design philosophy.
What annoys me most is clearly the developers are aware of how much time in this game is spent riding elevators, there’s a time limit mechanic I’ll be addressing later but while riding elevators, the time freezes. Though obviously this was probably some late development decision when they realized their level design was conflicted so massively with the time limit mechanic.

All that being said… this game doesn’t even manage to get the gameplay right!
Having your game littered with enemies that ‘can’ kill you if you let the swarm or ambush you can be fun, you learn the environment and suddenly they aren’t a threat any more and you feel like a god (though admittedly that’d be difficult in this game due to the aforementioned issue with samey, mazelike environments) and having bosses (and even minibosses) that hit like a truck is also fun (for the same reason), so long as they have telegraphed, learnable attacks you can learn.

So how does this game hold up? Well it decides to make EVERY enemy (as in, standard enemies you find on the map, not bosses/minibosses) able to kill you in 1-3 hits, and it also makes encountering multiple enemies unavoidable at times, so no matter how well you learn them and your environment, you’ll still end up dying a lot to (what should be) ‘weak’ enemies because they swarmed and/or blind-sided you.
This issue is worse than it sounds, as I actually completed the first half of the ‘Walk In The Park’ DLC, and the first 3 levels of the ‘The Good, The Bad, and The Ugly’ DLC before proceeding on with the game, meaning I was actually much higher level (with much further upgraded gear) than you’re supposed to be before proceeding to the second section of the game (that is, the section directly following the first boss) and yet I could ‘still’ be killed with such ease.
Granted I’m am playing with ‘operator class’ (i.e. light) armour, but to be fair I did test out a heavy set for a while to see if that was better and found that (as with most soulslike games and the Soulsborne series itself), the bonuses gained from heavy armour are (in most instances at least) MAJORLY outweighed by the detriment of slow movement.
Perhaps the mid range armour might have served me better, but I’m not replaying the game just to find out.

I also don’t think the developers understand how ambushes are supposed to work…an ambush is supposed to be surprising, that’s literally the point, so in a game you need to pace them such that the players let’s their guard down.
If you have an ambush around practically every corner (as this game does) it stops being surprising and just become obnoxious.

As for the bosses themselves, they actually do hold up for the most part… but with one major issue. All of them, even the giant frikkin’ bipedal robots, have the ability to swivel around 180 degrees at speeds that would make ballet dancers jealous, with no telegraphing or even so much as an animation….
In fact later, after I obtained an implant that slowed time, I discovered that they could even do this awkward swivel midway through an attack. To be clear, I’m not talking about an ‘attack’ that ‘turns the around’, I’m talking about them doing an attack that’s clearly and intentionally meant to go one direction, then I’d dodge through their legs or something and they’d swivel around so that the attack finished where I’d dodged to.
This is quite problematic because, as with actual Soulsborne games, often the best place to attack is from behind, the sides or between the legs (as enemies typically have massive ranges with forward attacks)… meaning this ‘ability’ basically makes it so no matter where you stand, or how well you know the boss, you’re likely to take hits.
Weirdly however, and I suppose ‘luckily’ given the context, pretty much all the bosses in this game (with the only exception I can remember being the final boss) actually seem to do LESS damage than most of the standard enemies that litter the map, further highlighting how poorly balanced those enemies are.
