\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet us get this out of the way: if you're not a fan of dark souls-esque gameplay this isnt for you.

That said: this game is built for a unique style. Definitely not for everyone. Its generally difficult/punishing. There is backtracking. There is farming. But it is a treat to play through.

Story: This is definitely a case of lore vs story. Theres plenty to pick up on from environmental clues to audio tapes that paint a picture of what exactly happened here, but this is not the focus of the game. The focus is the gameplay, and having just enough general context to push forward from here to there.

Graphics: Not amazing but definitely good enough looking. I personally am having some weird issues with refresh rate and resolution scaling (up to 120%) actually BOOSTING frames, but hopefully this gets fixed with an update. Overally, optimized well enough and looks pleasing enough.

Controls: This is another area you can consider "genre specific". Weapon movesets and animations are a huge part of the gameplay, and you need to plan out your attacks lest yee get caught in a combo because you clicked a hundred times and now youre stuck going through all the attack animations. The controls are nice and tight, just dont realize the kind of game you're playing and dont get trigger happy. It wont pay off here.

Sound: Does its job. The music is sparce but good enough, and the weapon/impact sounds are very nice. I find myself cranking the volume on my headset to hear the extremely satisfying crashes as metal smashes into metal, and it definitely helps with the "weighty" feel of the game.

Overall: I am still going through the game, I am on boss 3 out of 5, but it seems on track for a roughly 35 hour single player experience. I always suggest waiting for a sale for everything, but given this is a relatively small genre (soulsbourne-esque), I couldnt wait and needed to scratch that itch. I am not disappointed in paying full price, and am likely to buy any substantial DLC they release for this game. Deck 13 has learned alot from Lords of the Fallen, and while sticking close to the formula, this game provides a quality experience for those looking for more of their Dark Souls fix.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs soon as I watched the first trailers about this game, I knew this was a game I wanted to play. Sci-fi is my jam and powered exoskeletons are one of my favourite pieces of sci-fi gear; plus, I had always wanted to play a "tactical melee" game in such a setting (never felt attracted to the Dark Souls series due to it having a fantasy setting). Gave it some more time and eventually preordered it shortly before launch day.

Oh boy, was it a good buy.

As a piece of software, it's greatly optimized. My laptop is not powerful enough for the maximum settings, but I can run it on high settings at 1920x1080 (fullscreen) with perfect framerate. The settings screen is incredibly detailed in all aspects, and that feels great: you can rest assured you will make it work for you no matter your computer or gaming preferences. Compared to the sadly large amunt of modern games with limited settings, the developers of The Surge show they know the full extent and importance of the term "player experience".

Gameplay-wise, it is a very solid game with many unique features. Combat is fluid and very dynamic, and very varied as well, depeding on your gear, weapon type, drone module and implants. You will have to learn how to master all your available actions and abilities in order to make progress, especially from mid-game onwards and the boss fights. Make no mistake: it is a hard game and you will die countless times until you finally master how to defeat this new, apparently unkillable enemy that seems to throw everything you know about combat through the window. Do not mistake common enemies for easy fodder: each of them will be a challenge on itself, and some of them will even be (or at least feel like) minibosses on their own. Bosses themselves will require all your skills and wit to defeat. In both cases, don't expect to tank much damage: I'll say it again, you will die a lot until you learn, and even then mistakes will be paid dearly.

Being able to get materials, blueprints and weapons from your enemies is just awesome. It gives a lot of incentive to keep looking forward to see what's next, and to come back to defeat specific enemies just to get their loot. Exploration is also a core part of The Surge: levels are large, very intrincate and filled with shortcuts to Operations (your respawn, loot saving and upgrades base), and also filled with secrets, like loot, audio records (vital for the story), collectibles and even unique versions of common weapons (sidegrades): the incentive for exploration is definitively there. About gear and weapons, consider them to be all sidegrades: even though boss weapons tend to be better, generally you can stick to any particular armor or weapon you fall in love with just by upgrading it.

There's also the possibility to play New Game + and even ++ (and maybe even more), where you get to keep your previous gear and upgrades while the game gets MUCH harder with new enemies and other stuff I haven't seen yet (just started to play NG+).

The story is... serviceable. It is definitively a good one, although not a surprise for fans of the genre. That said, the way it's told -mainly through audio-records and some NPC interactions- keeps you in the point of view of a poor unlucky guy that just started his first day of work right when everything goes haywire, which is quite good in the sense of inmersion. There's also conversations to be had, side quests and some decisions to take, which is nice. A word of caution: careful with accidentaly killing your friendly NPCs.

In addition, the devs recently released the first patch of the game, including many fixes and features that the players had been posting on the forums, and have showed their will of keeping adding content to it in one way or another. These devs have not only made a good game mixing their own ideas with already working formulae from the genre: they are also showing they care for their product after release and will keep supporting it.

In summary: I highly recommend this game!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: This game takes the Souls formula and throws in a chunk of late stage capitalism, a twisted version of a silicon valley startup, and of course really satisfying limb chopping. It does these things well and is worth a buy to any fan of Souls-like games.


Overall, I'm extremely impressed with this game. With lords of the fallen, Deck 13 showed that they could emulate bits of the souls formula, but not really put it all together in a way that was polished or completely interesting. With The Surge, not only does Deck 13 make a game that emulates the souls formula well, but also adds its own pretty awesome twists to it along the way.

Let's first talk about the combat. It feels weighty and responsive in the way the souls games do. It is also increadibly punishing. I've read a lot of reviews and commentary on the game in fact stating that the game may be too punishing in an ill way, in that almost every enemy in the game can one shot or two shot you. This will be true if you do not care for your armor upgrades/health upgrades along the way. But even if you do, you are usually 3-5 hits away from death. Your character can hit enemies hard, but they can also hit you REALLY hard back.

I think in the game world, that makes a lot of sense. You start out as just a normal dude getting a factory job with average supplies and no combat training/proficency. If you're going to pit yourself against other people with the same/better equipment, I think it makes sense that you will die at a similar speed as your enemies do. I personally liked Deck 13's approach to enemy damage, but there was more than a few times I felt salty about an enemy that did JUST ENOUGH damage to kill me and force me to make a 5 minute treck back to my leftover scrap (souls). Overall I like that the difficulty is mostly due to enemies hitting hard and sometimes having strange hit patterns, but if that doesn't sound fun to you, that's fine. I would just not play this game.

One more thing to mention about the combat: it differs from the souls formula in that you can target individual limbs. While locked on to an enemy, you use the rights stick to cycle your lock on from the legs, to the body, to the arms, and the head. If you are locked on to a part of the body, you will get a prompt to attempt to cut it off after a few hits. IF the prompt is successful you will be able to salvage the gear you cut off the enemy and use it to form a blue print to recreate the gear with a 3D printer.

That is how almost all gear/weapons are collected in the game, and I love it. If you see an enemy with awesome armor, it means that you can have that armor, but you will be putting yourself at a significant disadvantage during the fight. Hitting unarmoured limbs kills an enemy faster, but will result in no loot. It's a really clever risk/reward system that ends in a slow motion limb cutting animation and then a few minutes later, your character 3D printing that exact piece of armor. It is also worth noting that you get the crafting materials you need for the armor by cutting of the same limbs on other enemies so building a set of armor becomes kind of a lengthy process. But luckily the combat is fun enought that I almost never minded grinding to get an armor set (with the exception of one set froma paticularly difficult enemy).

As far as armor/weapons go, it is also worth noting that you can level up every weapon/armor piece you find, just like in the souls games. I love this because it allows you to get really attached to a weapon and carry it through the whole game. You also get proficency leveling for using a specific weapon type more and more, which is a system I have mixed feelings about. On the one hand, it allowed me to get really good with my favorite weapon and constantly improve the damage I do with it. On the other hand it kind of locks you into a weapon type early in the game if you want to get the most out of this leveling system. Overall though, I love the way loot is handeled in this game as not only does it rewoard the player well and often, but also adds to the moment to moment desicion making in a fight.

Finally, I want to talk a bit about the story and world of this game. This game has cutscenes, dialouge, and a story that is far more obvious than the ones in the Souls games. These elements are done pretty well, with one character in paticular having an interesting story arch (only if you bother finding them though), but mostly the story is just good enough to not detract from the game.

The world building on the other hand is actually very well executed. Audio logs are always worth picking up, it is usually interesting to see what is playing on the T.V.s around the world, and this world feels oppresive in a way that is almost required for a Souls-Like game. The world you inhabit just sucks in a way that sets the tone really well for the rest of your actions because the fighting is just as brutal as everything else around you. In this portion it is also worth noting the level design which can visually get a bit repetative (it also looks totally rad in parts), but is laid out in a very satisfying maze like way, kind of like how dark souls one did it.

I loved this game. It is not as good as the Dark Souls games (AKA: some of the best games ever), but this game satisfies the hunger I have for the souls formula in a way that is fullfiling and suprisinlgly unique. Overall very worth the purchase.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed around 15 hours and got about 2/3 through the game i'd say. The game is pretty fun at first, but it gets kind of monotonous. Enemies are all kind of the same. You kill them the same way. Swing swing, doesn't matter which, maybe dodge (or simply back up) sometimes. I still don't understand the parry system. I've tried it out and think it's terrible. There's no tutorial and I should not have to resort to YouTube to find out how to use it and even then I still it is not explained well at all and isn't intuitive. I think most people ignore it, which leaves you with a pretty bland combat system. Whether you use horizontal or vertical slash doesn't matter 95% of the time so I find myself either horizontally slashing or slide-dash swinging which is a nice way to initiate combat. With Pax 2.0 I slide in and swing, and hit them 2-3 times and then use finisher. Pretty basic and I do that exact thing for 95% of the enemies. After you realize all this and get deep in the game, the combat just gets kind of boring and the enemies, being mostly the same, don't provide much challenge aside from getting big HP buffs later on. I feel like I am just rushing through the game now just to finish.

The thing that made me quit though was the horrible proficiency system. It prevents you from using any other weapons the entire game. I got the Pax 2.0 and while it is great I want to try other things but it's not worth the farming to level other weapons.
In the end, this is what killed the game for me... getting all these neat boss 2.0 weapons, and not being able to use them effectively. I got this cool staff that looks great, and a cool twin rigged weap, but my proficiency with it is level 1, where my main weapon is 18. No, I'm not going to farm enemies for hours to level it. Just no.
People said this was better than Lords of the Fallen? That's a stretch. Even though LoftF was an easy game, coming from Dark Souls, I had fun with it

TL;DR: Kinda fun at first but simple combat, bad parry system, simple enemies, simple story, stuck to one weapon type, gets boring after a while.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has gotten a bad rap for being "not Dark Souls". If we rated every game based on being "not Dark Souls", nothing would be good enough to play.

It's tough but fair, and chopping off limbs really doesn't ever get old. The game revolves a lot around fighting basic enemies, but they are tough and can hurt you badly if you are careless. You won't run into large mobs (3 or more) often, nor are there a lot of boss fights, it's about these solo fights and comboing your attacks in ways to avoid getting hit.

The level design is excellent, with a lot of shortcuts that loop you back to the bonfire/base. The maps are really interconnected well. Story is a rather bland sci-fi, I mean it's an interesting world and all but it just doesn't really deliver on atmosphere, which isn't helped by the fact the entire game starts to look the same since it takes place inside a facility.

Took me roughtly 20 hours to complete with minimal revisiting of old areas, although I did explore a lot (but the game isn't very open, it's pretty single-tracked).\t\t\t\t\t
