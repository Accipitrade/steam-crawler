\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Oh, it's just another Pocket Tanks clone" you might think. Well, you're wrong then.
Yes, ShellShock Live shares many concepts with Scorched Earth, Pocket Tanks and all the other old-school tank-duelling games, but it doesn't make it a rip-off, rather a spiritual successor.
The progression system
is done, in my opinion, just in a way such a game should have. There's a chance for you to get almost any weapon during a match, but you mostly get the weapons you already unlocked. Levelling up unlocks more weapons, and all the weapons are upgradable as you deal damage with them. This means that you are free to experiment with a wild range of weapons even at lower levels, but you still have a purpose to do better and level up. You don't have to grind for exp, levelling up doesn't take long at all.
The game isn't free, but that is very much a positive thing, because it allows to get rid of any sort of in-game payed features (yes, even the cosmetic changes can only be bought through playing the game) and minimize the amount of cancer.
Graphics
are quite nice, both somewhat simplistic and high-quality at the same time. The sound is also good, albeit sometimes a bit too loud. The game is pretty much stable (on Linux at least), it crashed only once.
The best thing about this game, however, is how flexible it is in terms of gameplay. Not only the frightening number of different weapons and maps to play with, but the number of different game modes as well, from simple deathmatches to sniping competitions and soccer. You can also play the game with basic ballistic trajectories, or make it increasingly harder by enabling wind and putting different restrictions on used weapons, whatever suits you best. These changes are balanced by increasing the multiplier on recieved XP and raiting gains.
Now, the raiting system at this point is kinda useless, apart from deciding if you really want to play with a team of low-rated people.
The game community
can be a pain in the ass. In certain hours the game is plagued by some, I don't know, 10-12 yo kids, especially German for some reason, who can neither play the game nor speak properly. Some people write that they'd been kicked from games for landing a good shot, but I didn't encounter anything like that. The best idea to solve this is this: If you see an adequate, good player, send him a friend request in Steam. This way you'll have a really fun time playing SSL. Oh, and learn basic ballistic physics, it really helps.
For a game developed by literally one guy – that's a job done really well. 9/10.\t\t\t\t\t
