\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe second instalment in the Quell series, Quell Reflect, builds upon the first game's framework and enhances the gameplay with new puzzle mechanics. Its look and feel isn't quite as melancholic but still nostalgic and relaxing in the same way. Aside from the steeper difficulty curve in comparison to its predecessor, everything else is in the same vein.

A few new mechanics, most notably the ability to control multiple drops in one level and the introduction of momentum-based moves, shake up the familiar puzzle gameplay. However, a handful of levels with up to 42 maximum moves to perfect are strewn into later sections in the game and can be tough nuts to crack. There's an ingame credit system that may be used to view the solution but working it out yourself without brute forcing it takes a considerable amount of time and dedication.

The underlying narrative is equally elusive as in Quell with only subsequent year dates and background images revealing a glimmer of a story. With a few new tracks added to the soundtrack, the music is once again soothing and a perfect match for a puzzle game such as this one.

It's overall a solid follow-up to Quell.\t\t\t\t\t

Quell Your Puzzle Addiction? (A Quell Series review)

Slide, slide.. slide away many enjoyable hours being challenged by some deceptively well designed, compact puzzles in the Quell Series. Your goal is to collect all the Pearls in the minimum number of moves & as a bonus, locate the hidden jewel found on each level. Simple right? Well the "Quells" can be deceiving .. as you will quickly find out.

Including the original Quell & the sequels, Quell Reflect & Quell Memento, each instalment are essentially clones the previous, introducing more & more varied puzzle elements to the rule set, increasing the complexity. Personally, I found the puzzle difficulty to vary considerably, with some perfect puzzle runs only taking a couple tries where others had me truly stumped for ages just trying to complete (let alone perfect)!

On average, progression was fairly steady throughout though. The best puzzles for me where those around 10-20ish moves, many still complex & challenging, but once the moves get 20+ it begins to get hard to keep track of the long sequences.

What I really liked in much of the puzzle design is the way you can break down the problem & make deductions from the layout presented to you. You may get a clue to which order you need to collect the Pearls based on their location/access or see obstacles you need to move or destroy in order, or what square you need to get your delicate raindrop to, or what to collect last.

Each little clue helps you formulate your strategy & is very satisfying to pull it all together to hear the chime of the perfect run. There is some great use of misdirection in level design & element placement, which keeps you guessing. The beauty & subtly in the designs is not immediately visible till you find the right path.

All instalments offer what must be close to 100 puzzles each. Each set of 4 puzzles makes up a window panel, with 3 window panels making up a year/tier (12 puzzles in total for each year, increasing to 16+ in Memento). You can only progress to the next puzzle once you have completed the current one. This can be a bit frustrating if you get stuck for an extended period. I would have liked to see the set of 4 puzzles in a window panel unlocked together so you could mix it up a bit, perhaps with a percentage requirement to open up subsequent sets or years in advance.

Still the games are, generally speaking, forgiving offering instant undo/back & retry at the touch of a button, encouraging experimentation, plus solutions can be purchase with in-game Qoins if you really get stuck. Although I tried to avoid using the Qoins, there were some levels that bested me towards the end of the two sequels.
https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=1384794903 The original Quell, I found to be the easiest since it offered the more basic puzzle mechanics & in-turn also had a nice, steadily increasing difficulty curve. That all changed when I hit Quell Reflect. With the introduction of multiple raindrops, golden block gateways, transporter rings & poison raindrops. The challenge really ramped up & found myself using a few Qoins!

Quell Memento continued to build on both of the previous instalments, with some creative new puzzle elements, such as lasers/light beams, illuminated squares, wormholes, roses that toggle spikes, power switches & ice cube blocks. These make the puzzles refreshing, different, greatly changing how you tackle each challenge. The downside to this is that now with so much element variety, many levels do become overly/unnecessarily complex lots of possible permutations.

Also refreshed is the UI & introduction of some voice overs telling a story (hinted at in the first 2 games). Some levels also offer a new timekeeper sub-level stages, each hidden inside a main level. These are particularly sinister ones, generally offering a hardcore challenge if you can find them.

Once I started playing I couldn't put the Quell Series down for long, playing through all three instalments over the course of a few of weeks. The controls are responsive, & the instalments are well presented graphically with nice sound effects & serene music. There is a solid amount of content & the puzzles are engaging, varied & well designed.

My brain recieved a good workout! Highly recommended!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Fun game where each puzzle board has two puzzles, the first solving in the fewest moves and the second finding the hidden gem on the board. I'd suggest getting the collection if you like this sort of thing to save yourself some money. If it's on sale don't pass it up.

I'm going to review Quell, Quell Reflect, and Quell Memento all in one because there's a lot of comparing I'm going to do.

Essentially Quell and Quell Reflect are the same. The layout, menu, feel, goals are all the same. If you have one and loved it then get the other. The puzzles themselves are different so it's just more of the same. Quell Memento feels very different and has a slightly higher degree of difficulty I feel. The feel of it is different as it now has a storyline, the stages are set up differently, and has different game components than the other two.

For Quell and Quell Reflect you are simply moving your ball(s) around with the arrows to collect all the pearls on the puzzle without dying from different types of traps. Quell Momento changes this by having some that are collecting pearls and others where you're changing colors of tiles. It's the same movements, but has more nuances to it. Quell Reflect adds multiple balls while Quell only had one so extra mechanic, but not really that much more difficult. Quell Memento might be better if you want a slightly higher degree of difficulty.

For each puzzle you will be solving it in the fewest moves trying to get the perfect. You can complete it with more moves, it won't stop you if you go over. So in that sense it can be a very relaxing puzzle. It has a perfect set of moves and occasionally you can complete the puzzle in less moves. This does not happen a lot. It's only required to find them in Quell Memento for the achievements.

Each puzzle board has a second puzzle built in it where you find the hidden gem. There are times when you see it and just have to navigate to it. So for each board you're doing it over twice. With Quell Memento, there are a bunch were you find a third hidden element which is a ring to take you to a secret board where you must solve that one twice for the moves and for a hidden object.

For Quell and Quell Reflect you will probably get all the achievements by just playing the game. There are a few like getting caught in a loop or dying to a spike a certain amount of times that you might need to just replay a game a few times to collect, but maybe not. I get those out of the way quickly as they are only like 5 or 15 times of something. It's no big deal. As long as you finish everything perfectly you will have all your achievements. Not so for Quell Memento. With Memento they added a bunch of other achievements, like forcing you to find a better than perfect board and completing it, changing a light bulb tile 200 times (that will NEVER happen through normal play), and others that you really need to go back and figure out how to do. I got lucky and stumbled through a few by chance, but upon completing the game I still had 4 more to go. I found the 200 times one really annoying to do. There's an achievement that forces you just to leave it go at the right screen to get an achievement. I don't really care for achievements like that. I prefer how they did Quell and Quell Relect much better.

Overall, I liked the games. I bought them in the Quell Collection and had fun with it. I don't see myself replaying it though. So if you're looking for that, I don't see it. I didn't really care for Quell Memento that much. I played it between Quell and Reflect and you could tell it was a later version, but I wasn't really thrilled with the changes to it. I didn't care for doing a board three times and then doing the secret hidden board twice. It became too much and I didn't know why not just add those boards as regular puzzles. Doing a puzzle twice was okay. By the third time I was really bored sick of it. And if you screwed up on the secret board in a way that brought you back to the first, you had to redo the first to open up the entrance to the secret board again. So there was just way too much repetition there. I didn't like the achievements as I already stated, and I found the story and cleaning off the tile to get to the stages menu just extra work I didn't want to have to do. I don't have complaints on Quell and Quell Reflect other than if you wanted a major step up in difficulty and a complete change in one from the other you're not going to get it. But I like them both equally and really enjoyed doing them. If you get stuck there are guides and videos to help you out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Quell Reflect" is very similar to it's predecessor, "Quell." The developer added two new play mechanics: movable pins and gold pearl/block pairs. Unfortunately, they also seemed to have made many more of the puzzles hard. It took me 9.6 hours to complete the game with 100% Achievements. BUT, I had to use a LOT of walkthroughs. Very frustrating. I'd have preferred easier puzzles, so I'm right on the border with the game. If you liked "Quell," and don't mind tough puzzles, then I have no problem recommending the game to you. If you don't care about Achievements (so finding that hidden gem and getting the puzzles done in the minimum number of moves doesn't matter to you), I again recommend the game whole-heartedly. But, if you're a perfectionist, a completionist, and get frustrated with tough puzzles, you might think twice about "Quell Reflect." Still, two out of those three groups should be happy with the game, so I'm sticking with my Recommended rating even though I wasn't overjoyed with the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou might like if: You enjoyed the original Quell and love laid-back zen puzzlers.

If it ain't broke...

The second instalment of the enchanting Quell series, offering up another neat collection of 80+ levels. It retains the wonderfully familiar mechanics, style and gorgeous soundtrack from before, but augments them with some fun new elements. Some levels now involve multiple rain drops, and others have gold blocks which can only be removed if you find a golden pearl first. They blend seamlessly into the existing game, enriching an already serene puzzler.

The difficulty has scaled nicely, creating a very natural progression from the first title - ultimately making it hard not to love if you enjoyed the first one.\t\t\t\t\t

The Quell collection is a triplet of decent challenging puzzle games. I recommend to buy all three parts in a bundle on sale. Basically you shift orbs throught logical buildup labrinths to collect all pearls with a limit of moves. In higher levels there are added some elements like poisoned orbs, spikes or portals. You can also get stuck in infinite loops. Each level contains a hidden jewel you must find in a second playthrough. The controls are sometimes buggy viewing the game is made for touchpad but it uses mouse pointer swipe instead of arrow keys. So you can accidently do a double move.

Quell Reflect is technical a remake of part1. the puzzles are not similar, but seem easier. Altogether 84 puzzles stacked by year numbers (?) 7 years x 3 blocks a 4 puzzles.\t\t\t\t\t
