\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBefore I continue, Inscryption is an experience best entered blind and with a mind open to not surrendering at the perception of a game over. But given how much I've played, I feel I've seen enough of the spoilers to know how to avoid them if simply saying "it's worth it" doesn't mean enough for you as a review. Because lets face it, this looks like a five dollar game at best. Some of the greatest deck building games around are free to play. So without further ado, I will explain without spoilers why both you and I were wrong about our initial impressions that this isn't a twenty dollar game.

Two or three years ago I fell in love with card-games because it was like learning a language; the cards being the vocabulary, the turns being the statements, and the strategy being its own kind of grammar. In playing a card game you engage in a kind of debate, posing and rebutting statements to learn how robust your arguments (or strategic decisions) truly are. In winning you learn what works, in losing you learn what doesn't. This is how people appreciate games like Magic, Yu Gi Oh, Star Realms, Dominion, and other games which vest their players with a measure of player agency in deciding how their decks are made. It isn't about beating your opponent to death because you know how to play better than they do (though many people misuse card games to do so), it's about learning and testing what you've learned to see what growing your decision making skills are truly capable of overcoming.

Inscryption doesn't have a tutorial because it is a game about learning a unique card-gaming language by exploring, winning, and losing. The most unfortunate thing about the demo is it demonstrates very little of this for its majority. Inscryption as a game challenges the player's ability to appraise the value of a card based on the environment within which it exists. Learning the game is beating the game, and this is truly a unique game worth beating.\t\t\t\t\t
