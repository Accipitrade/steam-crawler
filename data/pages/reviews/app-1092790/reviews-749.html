\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am so grateful I played Inscryption as blindly as possible, because it quickly breached my top 5 favorite games of all time.

The unique gameplay, reminiscent of popular monster-based card games and TableTop/pen-and-paper RPGs, while incorporating elements of roguelikes, classic point-and-click adventures, and singleplayer fantasy RPGs, demands adaptable thinking from the player in each act .

Therefore, as the game itself changes, at times appearing random but never breaching unpredictable, the process never gets old, and the challenge is always refreshing. This game allows you to believe that you will pass every milestone if you try again with a slightly different approach each time. Additionally, if the learning curve is too much for players, Inscryption is very forgiving in helping you progress further in the story, providing more features and buffs early on, teaching you mechanics as you fail.

My favorite of such features includes the splicing and buffing of cards throughout the game, such as with the use of the Mycologist, early stops at the sacrificial altar, and end-game card and miniboss customization . Early game adaptability lends extremely well to late-game deckbuilding and strategy.

While I showed up to play cards, it was the story, deep lore, and character design which kept me in it for the long haul. Inscryption toes the delicate line of creepy, captivating, and downright silly with well-written flavor text, easter eggs sprinkled throughout, the built-in found-footage and fourth-wall breaking ARG, and philosophical questions about the horrors lying beneath the surface of the game. And I haven't even completed the post-game content.
