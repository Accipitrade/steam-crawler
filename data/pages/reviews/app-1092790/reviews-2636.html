I remember seeing short playthroughs of the prototype ("Sacrifices Must Be Made.") earlier this year and have been keeping an eye on Inscryption since, and I was NOT dissapointed. If you have ever wanted to play card games against a spooky spirit in a dark cabin where puzzles await you both in and out of the game, this is the perfect pickup. I agree with the general opinion that Act 1 is its strongest point, though that does not shy away for the deeper card play that gets introduced in Act 3, and Act 2 opened up nostalgia for older games (I wont say more than that.) Honestly I would pay for full games of each act, they were that much fun on their own.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all, Spoilers, If you have any desire to play this game in it's best form just send it, second, if you're looking at this game because you want a Roguelite Card Game, this game is not that. As with all of Daniel Mullins games you'll only be playing the game presented on this store page for a couple of hours, possibly more depending on your RNG and how good you are at card games. I personally sprinted through the whole thing and encountered essentially no hang ups difficulty-wise, but your mileage may vary. That being said I still absolutely recommend this game.

There's a lot of blunders here, mechanics are introduced and taken away at such a rate that the moment you begin to feel you understand them you're whisked off to the next set piece and mechanics are switched out and left behind nearly hourly. This leads to a lot of the game feeling like it stops just as it starts. In addition some mechanics are just completely pointless within the context of the game like losing your money in Po3's game or the bounty system in general.

However, despite all these shortcomings, I still enjoyed this game immensely. The basic mechanics of each section are simple, yet have so much depth that more than anything I'm left disappointed this game is as story-centric as it is. You could easily make en entire card game off any of the four Scrybes and when they're all interacting together it's truly a wonderful game.

Inscryption is mainly a story that you're experiencing through the medium of the game I've described above. There is no true challenge to it and the game is essentially made for you to break it by manufacturing horrendously overpowered cards to brute force your way through it. Despite this I still found myself thoroughly enjoying the story. The interaction between discovering bits of lore within the game itself as well as Luke's antics in the real world is absolutely sublime and just what I've come to expect from Daniel Mullins. I didn't expect to be as invested in these characters as I was in just seven hours. Even the mass of goo locked in perpetual agony has his own side story to discover and it is absolutely heartbreaking. I found myself feeling genuinely invested in the ending sequence as each Scrybe offers you a hand shake as everything falls apart around them, In particular Leshy deciding that keeping score isn't important anymore when the scale gets deleted and simply asking you to play a few more rounds for the fun of it. Even Magnificus as furious as he was about the game coming apart still drags himself across the floor to try and shake your hand at the end of it all. The world would be better off if we could all show as much sportsmanship as these fake masses of pixels.

Overall I feel like this game is what every walking sim should be. It is a nearly perfect blend of game play and narrative that refuses to forsake either in the pursuit of it's goals. I highly recommend it to anyone looking for a good story or a good game and really that's how all these games should be.\t\t\t\t\t
