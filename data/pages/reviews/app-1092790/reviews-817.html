\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen the "best part of the game" boils down to just being a worse version of Slay the Spire... I can't help but feel a little let down. The presentation is undoubtedly bold and very impressive, but the mystery it presents is not compelling enough to slog through another 40 minute run so that I might pass the first part of the game. If you're lucky, maybe you can hit one of the many supposed "oh ♥♥♥♥" moments the game has to offer in as little attempts as possible and that can motivate you to play through the game further than I did and reach the, according to the community, parts of the game that aren't as good as the first part?
To put it bluntly, from what I played, the card game portion was pretty weak. The presentation and story are good enough to make the game worth playing, but what makes that difficult is the very fact that the game is a roguelike. It could probably still work without being a roguelike but because it is, the tedious card game with an hour or two of content gets unnecessarily stretched out for as long as RNG keeps you from clearing a run, with the rest of the game being gated behind it. And again, this is the portion of the game I see get praised the most by it's fans!
Having obtuse marketing is a double-edged sword. Presenting your game as one thing can heighten the experience when the rug gets pulled out from under the player. But problems can occur when the player never gets to the rug pull due to bad execution, leading to a frustrating experience instead of a fun surprising one. If the game's goal is to surprise the player, which it undoubtedly is even from just reading other reviews on this page, then it fails its job in ensuring that every player can get to the surprise part. By it's nature as a deck-builder, there's a lot of RNG at play and you can be put in situations that seem unwinnable, becoming frustrated when you lose and net zero progress because you've done all you can up to that point. It's not a game that lends itself well to the standard roguelike playbook it follows.
Inscryption is just not a good deck-building roguelike, and I get that it really is trying to be more than that which is worthy of praise and might be enough to get most people over it's subpar gameplay. If you don't care about roguelikes or anything else I've said, by all means try Inscryption, it really does portray itself as a unique experience. Unfortunately that wasn't the experience I got and it's a shame that the game is structured in a way that I, someone who spends 99% of their time playing video games, was unable to find the value in spending any more time trying to progress because, hey, it's a roguelike! You're supposed to lose over and over! Now start another 40 minute run and maybe if the final boss doesn't one-shot you this time you can continue the story! Sorry, no, My time is better wasted elsewhere.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPretty addictive.
The gameplay is fun and rather simple to learn (for the first act), and it's where the game really shines. Obviously, for a deckbuilder game, the cards need to be balanced, and I think they are for a casual player such as myself (I know my hours don't exactly show it but I both suck at the game and had to fully restart my save during act 3 lol) who doesn't really care that much about having the hardest hitting deck, just one that functions. The game does have some pretty overpowered card potential though, from clips that I've seen. However the simplicity of the system from act 1 soon gets changed for acts 2 and 3, with 2 introducing two new card types and 3 scrapping all but one new system introduced in 3. Personally, I don't like act 3's system, due to it forcing you to wait multiple turns to even start getting out damage, especially in the early game (again, this is all from a casual player, don't judge me). Even with these changes, it's still extremely addictive and not too overly complicated.

The acts and story are interesting, however I would say that the game does lose a bit of momentum after the first act finishes. Without spoiling anything, the game undergoes a drastic change of style that wasn't really needed. However, I have seen Kaycee's mod (which I haven't played yet but I fully intend to) that seems to be a return to the first act, which I love, but some might say that it doesn't count due to it just being a mod. Aesthetics aren't (or shouldn't be) all that a card game is about, so this one gets a slight pass from me. All in all, if you think it looks interesting or are into deckbuilder games or both, I'd recommend picking it up.\t\t\t\t\t
