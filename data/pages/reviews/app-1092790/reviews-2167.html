\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEvery genre in every art form has had to grapple with its own nature - to have to look itself in the mirror - in order to transcend its own limitations. Every medium, be it film, literature, visual art, music, or any other, has throughout history managed to look inwards, moving beyond the mechanics of style, skill and conventions and creating something that only that medium could convey.

Video games, as a nascent form, have long suffered the same accusations thrown at film throughout its early days, derided as cheap entertainment, lacking any higher depth or storytelling capability. Many games have experimented with the potential of the medium for narrative, exploring ideas of deep play and the inevitable ludonarrative dissonance that often follows.

I believe that in Inscryption we have finally seen a game that transcends its own limitations. This is a story that could not be told in any other form, could not be understood on any other device, and could not exist any earlier than the 2020s. Every aspect of the game is scrutinised, part of a meta-narrative within a meta-narrative. The 'game', defeating various NPC mini-bosses through deck-building and exploring the escape-room style environmental puzzles in the area around your narrator/opponent/kidnapper, is the engine that drives the plot, but it is only the engine. The real game is trying to figure out what the hell is going on.

Inscryption would be fun if it was just a rogue-lite survival-horror adventure across hand-drawn maps, bashing your head against some frankly unfair RNG and trying not to feel bad when the Stoat gets upset as you sacrifice him to play your wolf. It would be great, actually. The music, the animation, the sound design, the mood... all are impeccably done, the variability between runs is stellar, and the actual game itself is very enjoyable, owing in a big part to the charming (if deranged) narration of Leshy. The art style is fantastic, the puzzles are rewarding to solve, and the game-breaking combinations you can pull off using death-cards (looking at you Super Willem, my 1 blood 7/4 submerging multi-sacrificable monstrosity) feel like a cool add-on, not a cheap way to cheese the final boss. As you progress, you learn a lot about the game, and it teaches you pretty well about the different kin, and the best ways to put a good deck together.

If it was just this, it would be a fairly solid 8/10 game in a tightly-packed genre.

But it's so much more than this.

Inscryption is a game that never sits still. You don't get to visit every area, you don't get to try everything, and you have to live with your decisions. Cards die and don't come back. When you lose, that's it. When you think you understand the game, and you're getting comfy, it turns its own story on its head. When you think you understand the strange, intermittent found-footage, it turns it back on you. When you think you know how many Acts there are in the game, well, it has something in store for you then, too.

For a game that mostly consists (mechanically, anyway) of sliding cards into slots, moving a pawn around a 2D plane, and occasionally buying things from NPCs, this game had me on the edge of my seat practically the entire time. And not just in a stressed way - many of the boss fights (especially towards the end of the game) were some of the most unexpected, hilarious and awesome experiences I've had in video games. Without spoiling anything, I will never look my Steam friends list in the same way again (or game with my Hard Drive plugged in).

I only just finished it, but I'm already thinking about my next playthrough. I am certain there are a dozen different endings, a hundred things I missed, a thousand new experiences still waiting for me.

This is one of the most creative, exciting and thought-provoking games I have ever played. Every other game ever made has been laying the groundwork for this.\t\t\t\t\t
