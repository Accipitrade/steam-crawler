\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEDIT: They added in a roguelite mode into the beta which will eventually make it into the main game, so the crux of this review is now somewhat less relevant. Switching to reccomended.

Inscryption has been a game that in some regards I enjoy immensely and in others leaves me very disappointed. This stems in no small part from my expectations of what the game was and also my respect for the developer. I'm familiar with his previous work and thus had a decent idea of where this would go narratively, but expected that on top of his flair for story telling he would be capable of capturing the appeal of the roguelite genre. This opinion was bolstered by the excellent trailers and marketing the game used.

However the meta narrative that the developer put into the game deliberately turns the games premise on it's head numerous times. So much so that this is essentially a linear narratively driven campaign as opposed to a roguelite game.

Now I am not opposed to subversion in my video games, in fact even in this game I was regularly impressed at how the games rules were bent and twisted to delivery some of it's story. However subversion generally speaking has to overcome the expectations of the audience. This is done by A: Providing the core of what the audience wanted in a surprising way or B: Providing the audience with something different, but also far better than what they were expecting.

This game at almost all times opts for B, and in many instances it works to it's favour. Particularly in the games smaller interactions where stakes are low seeing all the interesting outcomes is very entertaining. But when it comes to the big gameplay altering decisions that are made as things unfold, there are some huge sacrifices made for the sake of the narrative to the point where I imagine many would feel falsely advertised to. Essentially the game subverts away what could be hundreds of hours of roguelite entertainment for 10 hours of a rather novel campaign. Not only does this affect the length of the game, but the games progression systems are all built to rapidly make the player essentially godlike very easily so that narrative thresholds can be reached in a timely manner by pretty much anyone. This works well in that regard, but the svere level of powercreep the player undergoes undercuts the sense of self improvement which is a large part a rogue-lite's appeal.

To put it simply, longevity was likely a concern for the developer when they made this game, but when compared to any other roguelite worthy of the name Inscryption is extremely short with very limited replay value. I can reccomend it to others as a fun short card game with a cool way of deliverying it's story (even if said story varies wildly in its actual quality).

But when it comes to speaking of it as a rogue-lite, a genre I have sunk literally thousands of hours into from only a handful of titles, then Inscryption is simply something I cannot reccomend at all.\t\t\t\t\t
