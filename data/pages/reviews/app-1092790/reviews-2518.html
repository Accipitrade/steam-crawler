\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll do my best to avoid spoilers of any kind, but in case you'd still rather not risk it, the tl;dr: I'm a fan of almost none of the genres represented in Inscryption and I still find it a fresh take worth experiencing at least once. I'd say there's trigger warnings for self harm and the game ends on something of an anticlimax, but nothing in there that leads me to overall regret buying the game. Actual review as follows:

"From the developer of Pony Island" isn't the big boast Devolver Digital thinks it is. From where I see it, Pony Island is utterly pretentious trash that could easily have been a free Flash game for all the difference it makes, drawing on concepts established by much better games without really doing anything interesting with them. The reason I bring this up to start with is that the gap in quality between Pony Island and Inscryption is like night and ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ day. And I don't know what black magic Dan Mullins casted to get so much better just two games later, but if even a cynical bastard like me can get a kick out of this game you can bet this will be amazing if you DID happen to enjoy Pony Island and its themes, which Inscryption very much evokes anyway.

As I alluded to earlier I'm not very much into card games, so I can't speak TOO much on how well the game's internal meta holds up. However, with the notable exception of a sudden, temporary diversion midway into the game, actually prevailing in key parts of the game's meta has a tendency to depend on unashamedly breaking it, which it gives you the keys to with varying levels of consistency throughout. On one hand there's a certain satisfaction when the stars align and give you all the best traits to mix together into one card, but once you have a death card that can hit three spaces for four damage each - which is relatively tame compared to some of the other absurd ♥♥♥♥ I've seen people pull out - the game no longer has any counter to you.

And I know what the themes of the game are like - you're trying to win and escape at any cost, after all. It just feels like a shame that many of the game's most intense moments can't be won simply through a decent deck and a good understanding of the game's otherwise original, well thought mechanics. I'm not saying this to be hyperbolic - the game straight up kills you if you attempt to progress past the Fisherman before you've been introduced to bones and death cards, which honestly comes off as kind of patronizing and rude.

Probably the biggest shame about this game though, is that it ends long before it's seen the full width of its potential. The most balanced and developed portions of this game's meta - the aformentioned midgame - is only a scant few hours long in a 12-15 hour playthrough, laden with dozens of brand new mechanics that had yet to be adequately or even partially explained. Even putting the meta aside, Inscryption also teases you with the prospect of whole entire games for each of the main Scrybes and then abruptly ends on the second without even so much as a final boss, which is a huge anticlimax compared to the masterful buildup that Leshy's campaign culminated in.

Maybe there's more modes yet to come, or maybe it's meant to be some kind of sequel hook, but I left this game feeling like there should have been more of it. That's really the worst thing I can say about Inscryption - it's great, but there needed to be more to it. This is also somewhat of a failing of the game's structure too - unlike other rougelites like say, The Binding of Isaac or Void Bastards, where you consistently build on a series of unlocks in a grander scheme than a single run, you effectively lose access to that section of the game entirely once you beat the penultimate boss of it. In that respect, being a linear narrative instead of a series of Scrybe worlds which the balance shifts too and from constantly, and a central world where all of the fruits of your labor pool into a deck you can define yourself, reeks of a HUGE missed opportunity.

But enough of my whinging. Despite my gripes, Inscryption is still an incredibly unique and well developed title, miles above what I was expecting from its talent, and I would still readily recommend it to almost anyone, even if card games are usually not your thing. It's a breath of fresh air that this industry desperately needs more of.\t\t\t\t\t
