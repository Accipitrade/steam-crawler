\r\n\t\t\t\t\t\tFirst, I want to start by saying that Inscryption is one of those games that really is best experienced without any previous knowledge, like previous users have mentioned before me. However, I felt compelled to write my thoughts for anyone on the fence of buying this unique game, and to give a broad overview of what you can expect if you absolutely need to have some information in order to experience the game for yourself. Combining a card game with a psychological horror aesthetic would at first seem like two clashing ideas. However, Inscryption really turns playing inanimate playing cards into a gripping experience that had me hooked from beginning to end.
\tThe core gameplay centers around playing and collecting cards in order to defeat mysterious figures as you traverse various ominous locales. Inscryption’s core card gameplay would be enough on its own to be its primary selling point, but what makes this game stand out on as a truly exceptional game, as well as a psychological horror experience, is its supreme attention to detail. Inscryption turns every card game trope and staple on its head. Instead of playing cards based on arbitrary mana, you sacrifice woodland critters to play your creatures. Instead of dealing arbitrary life points to your opponent, you place gold teeth on a scale when your creatures deal damage in a kind of life-or-death tug-of-war (with the option to even pry out your own tooth to, quite literally, tip the scales in your favor). Your hands shake as you battle mysterious figures to the chilling ambient soundtrack - each battle genuinely makes you shudder with horror for fear of your opponent reaching his calloused hands across the card table to end your run. The challenging difficulty, even for a card game veteran like myself, also adds to the menacing presence of Inscryption. Challengers will relentlessly throw creatures at your direction, forcing you to analyze your decisions, and calculate appropriately. However, if you are not well versed at card games or strategic thinking, fear not as Inscryption is still very winnable, and can be experienced purely as a horror experience if card games are not in your wheelhouse. The game rewards cunning, outside the box thinking that lets the player get the upper hand in the long run. For example, to spoil one innovative idea, a player can collect the card “ringworm” along their journey, which the game cynically states is essentially worthless. As you go deeper into the journey, you bump into campfires that allow you to upgrade your cards, at the risk of getting eaten by hungry survivors that reside at the campsite. My first instinct was to purposely upgrade my “ringworm” card until the survivors ate it, which actually causes them to not show up at future campfires and allow risk-free upgrading due to their intestinal complications - sorry about that! Exploring the mysterious cabin allows the player to solve various puzzles or riddles which can yield cards or items to aid their journey, which is beneficial as well and offers a great respite from card dueling. Inscryption rewards this kind of thinking consistently, but it also allows bountiful ways to complete the journey with various archetypes of decks you can build, branching paths that offer different challenges or unique loot, and plenty of ways to outsmart the game itself.
\tThe game is rich with world-building, lore, and a unique fourth-wall breaking narrative, that I will not spoil too much, as once again, I do think it is best left for one to experience for themselves. The gameplay and narrative complement each other so perfectly- this is not just a walking simulator with a forgettable card game, or just another card game with a scary theme. Nothing is quite as it seems in Inscryption, but that mystery is what really intrigued me to keep pushing forward and unravel the mystery. The game is varied enough with great card battling (with tons of creatures, enough unique abilities to fill a rule book, and challenging boss encounters), and immersive exploration that there is never a dull moment. The game constantly throws novel ideas or story elements at you, so even though it is technically “rogue-like” in many ways, you never have to worry about monotonously grinding away for a chance to see something new. I was constantly eager to discover new game-breaking items, meet ominous NPC’s, and even just learn about all the mysterious rules of the game (which is not as straightforward as it initially seems).
\tI want to conclude by saying there are so many things I want to say about my experience with this game, but I don’t want to give away all of the tricks, secrets, and twists. The game feels like it is deliberately trying to cheat and break you down, but it also gives you the tools through broken items and the ability to inscribe overpowered abilities to your cards, so it feels like you can fight right back, which was a large part of the fun for me. At the end of the day, I would just say treat it like a horror experience if you are hesitant about the actual gameplay of this game, because the game wants you to eventually overcome to experience the narrative. I can’t reiterate enough how polished this experience is, from the visual effects, to the spiders crawling across the board, to the endless amounts of secrets littered everywhere, to the highly intricate card game embedded within this experience. Do yourself a favor and experience Inscryption for yourself, I promise it will be like nothing else you have experienced in recent memory.
