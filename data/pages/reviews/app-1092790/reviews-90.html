\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has caused an exception in my game dev rules.
Rule 1 is no jetpacks. Rule 2 is NO JETPACKS, etc. Rule 3 is don't use literal card game mechanics [as a means to lazily circumvent the strengths of a computer, which is to literally automate random chard / dice roll mechanics] *UNLESS YOU'RE MAKING A LITERAL CARD GAME, THAT'S GOOD, AND DOESN'T/CAN'T EXIST IN REALITY SUCH AS INSCRYPTION.
(Rule 4 is don't use your scrawny gamer dev hands, in particular, but also on a broader general sense as prod graphics for things. Not even ironically. Not even Doom gets a pass on this.)
Amended...


I have personally vouched for this game as game of the year, across all games including all triple A titles. There's only a few games that compete with this quality in the reality time range. Mostly it's just The Last Of Us that everybody argues "could" be a better game but is also obviously like comparing a tank squad against a guy with a slingshot.

The game is designed to be fun and fair. I think there's some cheats in your favor happening behind the scenes to silently make sure you (almost never?) get stuck in a technically unwinnable state. The story is interesting but there's a lot going on. The puzzles aren't overly difficult but are decently designed. The game design shines when implied things actually happen, despite not always being listed as a function on the card. For example, a wolf cub becomes a regular wolf that does unexpectedly more damage after some time instead of being a +1 wolf cub. There are other, better examples of this in the game.

There's a few different perspectives the game takes place in. Depending on the perspective / genre, the rules change. This is an excellent attention to detail that I'd otherwise expect devs to get frustrated trying to create a literal copy into. It's handled here by what makes sense and what's more balanced. It's very creative.

I'll have to look around if I missed some option or used something broken, but I'd like an option to make the game more difficult. I didn't lose a battle until my 3rd character loop when I just got almost no cards and a lot of other bad luck.

Some of the cards are extremely overpowered. It's fun, but it seems overly easy to make something OP. In the first act, I had a unit that attacked 3/4ths of the board with a 1 cost that instantly killed all units. If you're able to defend for just a couple rounds, you can stall long enough to get the strongest units. In act 2, there's plenty of 0 cost units that can be infinitely regenerated (even within a single turn) and then upgrade multiple units to do unlimited damage with unlimited health. In the 3rd act, most units have 1hp so all you need to do is have 1 damage units by any means possible and take them out. Or defend against the occasional super unit who does 30 damage with a 0/1 unit until you can get a bomb or counter attack / death / sentry unit.

Act 1 probably has the best mechanics with the items and planning your route. Act 2 is pretty easy due to infinite money being easily obtainable with many combinations, but the open world is fun. Act 3 has limited units and money, but the units get upgraded pretty quickly to be very overpowered. All these works fine for a single playthrough, but it'd be fun for a re-balancing on some cheesy things if there's ever a followup.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is fantastic.

You start out playing a roguelike card battler. Every round is new and thrilling, and as you discover secrets your deck will become more powerful until at last you beat the final boss. Beating the final boss isn't the end though, after you've won at least once another secret gets unlocked, which will allow you to defeat the final boss a special way.

Does the experience end after beating the final boss? No. After beating the final boss you unlock the "New Game" button which suspiciously wasn't available from the beginning. Using it starts up A WHOLE NEW GAME. No longer is it a 3D roguelike card battler, now you're playing a deck building 2D RPG. Yes... that's right, it doesn't just have another game mode, it has ANOTHER GAME.

AND IF THAT WASN'T ENOUGH, once you beat the 2D RPG, you unlock A THIRD VIDEOGAME which is a sort of combination of the previous two while introducing new mechanics.

All 3 games have unique, fresh bosses with unique gimmicks to learn. While it's incredibly exploitable once you've learned it, I think that makes it even more fun. There are secrets all throughout, and achievements which while challenging to acquire, will not suck years out of your life trying to obtain.

The game is dripping with atmosphere. With 3 distinct art style, outstanding music, and unique character designs. The story isn't the best thing in the world, but what it lacks in narrative depth, it makes up for in charismatic characters. (The dialogue is hilarious, even if a little macabre)

Anyway, after you beat the base game, you can unlock Kaycee's Mod, a gamemode which allows you to play the first version of the game with more cards, more mechanics, more starting decks, bonus skull challenges, new items, and more music.

Inscryption is a wealth of content. Even if (like me) you're not a fan of deck-building card games, there's still so much to enjoy in this experience. I can not recommend it enough.\t\t\t\t\t
