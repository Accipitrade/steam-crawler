(Spoiler Free) Inscryption is a must-play game. This game flips your perception of it so many times, and every time it catches you off guard and surprises you. The card-play mechanics are engaging and pretty in-depth, (coming from someone who typically doesn't like card games) the graphics are neat and cool to look at, and the story is one of the most interesting and engaging stories I've ever followed in any game. The atmosphere is immaculate, Leshy's cabin (and other locations") are endlessly intriguing and mysterious. The secrets are plentiful as well. Overall, great game, and I cannot recommend it more.

(Spoilers) Inscryption is one of the, if not the best indie game I've ever played. Acts 1, 2 and 3 are all pretty fantastic in their own rights, and the card-play mechanics continue to get more varied and interesting throughout all of them. The intrigue and lore surrounding the four Scribes, their domains, the characters and items inside of them, is all fascinatingly huge. I love going back and finding different parts of the game that were hidden in plain sight, like Grimora's island being on the globe in Leshy's cabin. Even the real-life sections with Luke Carder slowly uncovering the dark secrets behind Inscryption are fascinating. It's absolutely amazing the attention to detail Daniel Mullins put into the world of Inscryption. There is one part that I'm indifferent on though, and that's Kaycee's Mod. Kaycee's Mod is an addition to the game that turns Act 1 into the main focus, with more secrets, cards, items, etc. And I do like it, but at certain points it just feels unfair for the sake of it. I don't know, I can see why people like it so much, and if you just want to have fun with it, then It's a great thing to play after you're done with the main story. Overall, I cannot recommend Inscryption enough, this is certainly a must-play game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe popularity of rouge-likes has inspired the indie scene to produce several titles in the style of pseudo-randomized, challenge-run oriented, count-and-pray video games. The genre boasts many projects adopting retro-games' philosophy of simple ideas executed in complex formats. This has brought out the best in noteworthy indie developers, and Daniel Mullin's, “Inscryption,” is a strong example of creators inspired by the success of the genre on the Steam platform. Notably, Inscription has taken the turn-based card game and used it as a vehicle for ARG-esque narration which its predecessors, “Pony Island,” and, “The Hex,” were famous . This blend makes for a unique experience and opens the window for how pseudo-randomized experiences can be used as narrative tools.

\tInscryption starts the players on a maze of forked paths and a starting hand of cards. The goal is to reach the end of the map, clear the end boss, and proceed to the next map. To accomplish this, the player navigates though several encounters against Leshy: two glowing eyeballs who act as gamemaster. Like many GMs, Leshy enjoys confronting the player with random, oddball NPCs who offer chances for new cards, upgrades for deck, passive field-bonuses, and pliers for ripping your teeth out. Between the battle encounters, the player can get up, stretch their legs, and interact with the secrets of Leshy's DnD cabin. I'm a huge fan of the glowing cow skull.

\tThe mechanics of combat play like a simplified Yu-Gi-Oh. Monsters are summoned to the field through sacrifices of which the cost is denonoted on the top right of each card. There are two energy resources in the game: blood, which requires (x) amount of cards on the field to be sacc'd, and bones, which are tokens gained each time a monster is removed from the field. Costs are concordant with ability. Simply, theoretically better cards cost more resources to summon to the field. Monsters on the field can only attack cards vertical to them, unless otherwise noted, and attacking empty spaces result in life-point damage represented by a scale on the player's left weighed with golden teeth. Praactically speaking, if one side takes more than 5 points of damage without dealing equal or greater than points to the opponent then that side loses. These mechanics lead to fast-paced, tempo driven gameplay that offers the player enough tools to feel fair. The simple, small numbers betray a nuanced tactical challenge as players seek the most efficient paths to lethal.

\tThis nuance is enhanced by the sigil mechanic. Several cards bear sigils, which grant passive effects. The flight sigil allows monsters to attack life-points directly regardless of cards in front of them. The deathless sigil returns a card to the player's hand, rather than the graveyard, when killed. Some sigils give your cards better resource generation, while other are defensively oriented. The engaging aspect of sigils becomes apparent with events that allow players to attach a maximum of two sigils to any card in their deck. Keen-witted players can quickly abuse these mechanics to achieve what the way-too-serious community of rouge-like likers call, “op broken runs.” Driving this attitude is Inscryption's freedom in experiementing with sigil combinations. The inncouous squirrel card can become a defensive wall with the Stink sigil; death-less cockroaches become offensive titans when granted the fledgling sigil, &c. The players is consistently given the chance to answer the essential syngergy driving question of, “what'll happen if I combine (x) and (y)?” The game design encourages these meta-breaking strategies, as evidenced with the Ouroburous card existing the way it does, and the satisfaction of discovering these strategies forms the core of Inscryption's gameplay loop.

The aesthetic is delightfully eerie. The card art is simple but charasmatic. The haunted cabin vibe comes across well; after several hours of gameplay, I still get spooked pretty often by Leshy's eyes peering out at me in the corner. The quiet melody of the soundtrack's twang and ambient breeze heightens the intended, isolating effect while feeling cozy. It's a murder slasher house with the ambience of a warm, dimly lit session of DnD. The themes of violence is undercut, to good effect, as you get attatched to your animal card friends and ponder quietly over your theory-crafting.


\tThis is all part of the first act of the game. Without spoiling too much of the other acts, unfortunately the ingenuity of act 1's gameplay takes a backseat to the narrative of Inscryption, which is remenicent of 2000's era creepypastas like Marble Hornets or Ben Drowned. Luke, a giant dork who reviews cards on the internet, recieves a spookey haunted video game in the mail a-and there's scary binary on the walls and the game glitches creepily at the player by going, “BLEEEEEH!!!,” and so on. Frankly, it's cool if you like this ARG-style storytelling, but these kinds of stories were pretty lame on SomethingAwful and they haven't aged well in current year where most ARGs are annoying advertising stunts.

\tAside from my personal tastes, the detriment to this narrative style is how it teases the player with unspoken promises of more game. Maybe if you run the scary binary through every binary translator on google, you'll find a secret clue that leads to a secret ending! Perhaps if you play specific cards in a specific order in act 1, it'll unlock new dialogue in act 2! What's the correlation between the Daus and the weird, obscure german game that inspired the game?

\tThis kind of theory-crafting was prevelant in the secret beta discord during the game's beta phase, in which I participated during October 2021. While this mystique was sort of cool for a week, it got tired once I realized there was no hidden game behind this National Treasure scavenger hunt. Inscyption made me re-experience the shame and disappointment of realizing that Slenderman isn't real, and I had allowed myself to be immersed in a smoke-and-mirrors show tantamount to a lot of goofy lore fun-facts. Further, given that the game past act 1 focuses primarily on this ARG angle, it makes the rest of Inscryption feel lackluster compared to the ingenuity of Leshy's game in act 1. That said, the finale's kinda neat even if it couldn't resist blowing out my ears with scary static.

\tThat said, as of December 14th, a new mini-expansion called Kaycee's mod was added, in beta form, as of current date. This mode focuses entirely on Leshy's act with 30% less haunted satan floppy-drive and 70% more game. New cards have been added to the pool, the map events have been tweaked to challenge players, and run-modifiers can be added to scale the difficulty. Kaycee's mod is great and offers more incentive once you've exhausted articles about Karnoffel and just look up whatever the point of the ARG was on Youtube.

\tI'm not saying anyone's wrong for like the ARG. If you thought it was, like, totally rad and meta, then good for you. I can admit that there were aspects to the narrative presentation that were sorta neat and, aside from all of that, the core card game is one of the coolest systems to be released on Steam in years. It all comes down to taste, and I didn't hate the ARG enough to not recommend the game to anyone who thinks card games are cool. I'm a little disappointed that the devil didn't come to my bedside to challenge me to a weird version of Dungeon Dice Monsters with my soul bet against forbidden knowledge, but I'll get over it. Gripes aside, Inscryption is one of the strongest titles of 2021 and a good time.\t\t\t\t\t
