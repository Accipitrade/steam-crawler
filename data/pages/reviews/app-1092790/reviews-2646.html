\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is hard to review or recommend due to the nature of how the story plays out. I'll try to avoid spoilers but in order to convey why I'm not recommending this game there is some things I can not avoid talking about in this review. -Spoiler Warning-

The first half of Inscryption is easily one of the most atmospheric, fun and memorable experiences I've had this year. Basically you play the role of a hostage who is forced to play a deck building rogue-like with their kidnapper. The dialogue, game mechanics and genuinely gripping suspense had me feverishly playing to see how far I could push my deck building skills in order to beat a game master who was not always playing fair. Unfortunately the game takes a big twist halfway through that, in my opinion, severely hurts the pacing and tone. As I got further and further into the second half of the game I was always waiting to see if I could go back to the first half, with some expanded mechanics or maybe some more interesting puzzles to solve. While Inscryption does attempt something similar to this, it feels like the card based rogue-like gets lost somewhere in translation, and instead the game focuses on being more of a parody or love letter to other card games.

If your familiar with Pony Island, Daniel Mullen's previous game you may already know that this game will not be what it seems on the surface. When the other shoe does drop there are no doubt some very exciting, fun and surprising moments Inscryption pulls off, but the issue is that the deck building elements get sidelined for the story. If you read this review and are interested in trying this game to experience a very crazy story, with a card game to boot, I WOULD recommend this game. If you are buying this game because you are looking for a new Slay the Spire or Monster Train, I sadly CANNOT recommend this game.
