\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA mixed, but overall positive bag

As a deckbuilder, it starts out quite exciting, as it layers on mechanics on you slowly in a masterclass of mixing story narrative with gameplay mechanics. However, this soon hits a plateau, and for anyone who's play some of the more popular deckbuilding games out there, the overall mechanics and pool of cards will seem very shallow.

(A few spoilers below, but not much. Moreso for gameplay mechanics than story)

I can immediately tell that many people are going to be instantly turned off by the sudden shift in Act 2. Personally, at first I actually bought into the change in mechanics (although reading the UI during card battles definitely took getting used to), and I was delighted by the promise of brand new gameplay systems. Few things surprise me in games anymore, but Act 2's shift certainly did. However, that delight soon turned to annoyance when I realized how shallow each of its new systems are. Act 2's trading card mechanics feels very unfleshed out, with a small overall card pool, and very limited ways to explore and expand your deck to customize a playstyle to your liking. As well, the new deck motifs introduced are very interesting at first, but there's only 1 or 2 overall "strategies" to each deck type - honestly, even if you had no idea what you were doing, you'd probably be able to beat it just through getting bigger numbers than the enemy.

In Act 3, when you revert back to Act 1's mechanics, it becomes painfully apparent how shallow the deckbuilding elements are. Act 3 is a total slog to go through, as you lose the intrigue of discovering new gameplay mechanics or cards - by this point, the game's shown pretty much everything it has to show you, save for couple "newish" modifications you can make to your cards. The overhead map is replaced from one of branching risk/reward decisions in Act 1, to a linear walk to the next fight map in Act 3.

When you see what the game is trying to go for as the Acts progress, you definitely can admire its ambition. As well, you also can't help wondering: if it focused on and fleshed out just one of the Acts mechanics, would it have made for a better game? At times, it feels like the game is trying to be subversive for subversion's sake, and it doesn't quite pull it off.

The overall meta-story, to me, quickly lost its intrigue. The creeping feeling of on unease in Act 1, as you work to discover the real game behind the card game, falls apart as the game essentially tells you what's going on, removing any leverage on mystery it had, and losing all narrative tension. If you've played games like Undertale and Doki Doki, among others , you've seen this sort of narrative structure done before, and done better.

Still...the brilliance of Act 1, how it seamlessly merged storytelling with gameplay, cannot be denied. You just end up wishing the creator focused more on what he had there, instead of splitting his focus 4 different directions.

tl;dr Slay the Spire meets Undertale, except not as good as the originals, but still a good experience overall\t\t\t\t\t
