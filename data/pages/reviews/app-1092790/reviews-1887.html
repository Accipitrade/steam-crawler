\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeware spoilers contained in this review.

I, like many, was drawn into this game from everything I saw in Act 1, which is what is generally the only portion of the game that is advertised. I noticed several negative reviews stressing their dislike of the game because of the game differing across acts, and what is termed deceptive adverting. I was not expecting the turn the game took in Act 2, but I was willing to follow along to see how the story would unfold. I was especially interested in the interweaving of other media: found video footage, bits of data like certificates, the glitchy effects. I was especially fond of how the story in Act 1 is skillfully revealed through the melding of several genres (escape-a-room, deck-building card game, game board, rogue-lite, etc.), and the intrigue of cards themselves having relationships with you as beings that are more than cards. I also found Act 2's worldbuilding intriguing, what with how it resonates with Act 1 and reveals more of the Scrybes and their relationships. I found the characters' relation to the very game itself--and the mysterious, Lovecraftian OLD_DATA--especially fascinating.

However.

Act 2 was when the gameplay mechanics begin to communicate less to the player; puzzle solving becomes less intuitive. The training dummy is the first place where I encountered this, as it requires a very specific approach that eluded me for days of playing sessions. The bonelord "offering" puzzle also frustrated me, especially because I ended up building decks without a focus in bone cards, which kept me away from the solution.

Act 2 is also the point where the narrative starts to not only grow less clear in terms of exposition, but also must increasingly rely on poor delivery of that story events. The Youtube streamer videos in particular struggled to build an appropriate sense of creeping horror and suspense due to the cringe-worthy acting; while perhaps the goal was to make this character unlikeable, it did not create appropriate narrative stakes for the ultimate situation regarding the OLD_DATA and P03's desire for "transcendence."

This leads into visual/sensory and procedural choices made within the story that undermined the delivery of a premise that started off quite strong. Visually, glitching and glitch-type visuals appear laced throughout the game, often indicating some aspect of data corruption, and this corruption making its way into the "real world." However, these glitches come in a variety of shapes, sizes, and frequencies; even further, after a certain point, there is a "saturation" level with the glitch use, such that glitch use that might be indicating something more important either narratively or related to a puzzle solution is missed.

This leads to some of the most abtruse puzzles in the game, those including ciphers, decoding, and actual game file manipulation. Upon research, I found that some puzzles relied on knowledge of morse code; others relied on decoding strings of numbers and letters and then having the where-with-all to employ a coding terminal feature within the game to manipulate certain text files. These puzzles also require knowledge of the developer's previous games, sometimes very granular bits of knowledge. It seems that this style of puzzle creation is part of this developer's modus operandi, and I suppose that is fine; there have been an increasing number of games with puzzles, hints, or game mechanics that draw on the digital environment of internet, files, and coding that we inhabit in our contemporary moment. I do wish though that such puzzles were more accessible to a wider audience. Perhaps their very elite-skill-set gated-ness is their appeal, though... after all, many of these puzzles are not required for finishing the game itself, "merely" for arriving at the hidden ending version. Further, the blurring of the lines between what is game and what is not also plays into this style of puzzle-making, which is one that "troubles" the player, what with actual system files being held hostage. Perhaps there is a horror thrill to this for some, like the monster coming out of the movie screen and into the room. For me, however, who plays games to find brief respite from a world already horrific enough, this spilling over of game-world consequences into reality, as narratively appropriate as it is for this game, it is not my cup of ludic tea.

Ultimately, I agree with another review's expressive statement that this game writes more checks than it can withstand us as players cashing them. Act I promises players so much, in not just game play itself but in story. Act II adds more threads to this, even as it begins to stumble; Act III returns to Act I's stylizations, but somehow feels far more hollow. The shifting between not only genres but also media and technological eras has so much potential, but this game struggles to not just unify its disparate parts into a whole greater than the mere sum of those parts, but to transition successfully from the unified whole that Act I is to the rest of the game. Fan art and player reminiscing across forums and online communities seems quite skewed to Act I, testifying to its success as something not just fun to play, but something that connects us to the story and makes us want more. But as for the rest of the game--for me, It is an especially sad moment when playing and realizing that, because of the game's narrative-based format change across acts and the loss of what drew me in initially, I am not having fun, I am not invested, and I just don't want any more of this experience.\t\t\t\t\t
