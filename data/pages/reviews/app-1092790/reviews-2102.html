\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs others have mentioned, Inscryption is a card battler with some light escape room elements.

The first section starts in a cabin in the woods and is very strong. It is powerfully atmospheric and includes card battler elements very much akin to Slay the Spire, and an escape room puzzle.

This section of the game is pretty special. I would say that on distant reflection it doesn't live up to all of its promise and that the balance issues that pervade the rest of the game are also present here, However, if the game had just included Section One and then ended, with a price adjustment I would be giving a positive review.

There are two subsequent sections. Here is what you need to know, with as few spoilers as possible:

- I enjoyed the second section thematically. This section kicks off a change in genre that leads you to view the experience you have had thus far in a different light. It also expands the kinds of cards and strategies to which you have access. It reminded me a lot of the Pokemon card game in a good way.

- However, the card expansions you get are, for the most part ... not very good at all. For want of a better term, you expand from two "suits" to four, and two of those suits are not good; it's hard or impossible to base your deck on either of them exclusively to create a winning strategy. Most of the cards you add to your original two suits are also not especially usable.

- By the end of this section, I ended up feeling kind of cheated. I put a lot of effort into collecting cards and most of the cards turned out to be, well, if they were cool they were not usable. And most were not cool.

- Despite the collectathon nature of this section there was no way I was going to be able to fill my card binder, because you can't retry encounters. Deliberate thwarting of my materialistic impulses? Perhaps. However: in general, a game needs to be fun, and this started to be one annoyance too many.

- I haven't finished the third section yet. In the third section, you get restricted to one of the new "suits" from section two, and lose access to your other "suits." Remember: the suits that you can't base your deck around. I love challenges but this restriction does not add the good kind of challenge. The balance in this section is downright bad.

- One of the especially poor design decisions here is that unlike in the previous two sections, where there was at least some way to remove cards from your deck, I have not been able to find any method to routinely do so here. Your strategy needs to evolve as you progress but it cannot.

- This section brings back the escape room elements, but their implementation is extremely shallow to the point of almost feeling unfinished.

I am reviewing now instead of reviewing after finishing the game because I find the third section to be dull and actively un-fun. To be honest, the graphics in this section also really hurt my eyes, which is a major part of why I haven't pushed through.

I am deeply disappointed by the lack of creativity in the third part of the game, which in no part delivers on the creativity of the second third, let alone the first third.

Given the major shifts in style and theme between the three phases of the game, it is worth asking whether the various parts of the game could work in isolation. I would gladly pay for an experience like the first third of the game. I would be willing to play the second third with a friend or for a very low price/free online. As for the last part, it costs me nothing to keep playing it and I still don't want to.

Therefore, I would recommend that those who want the best experience playing finish the first section and then turn the game off. And think about whether 3-4 hours of spoopy gameplay is worth the asking price.\t\t\t\t\t
