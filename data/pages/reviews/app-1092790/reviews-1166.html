\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBreaking the fourth wall can break immersion, and I can't think of a better narrative game to demonstrate that.

This game is a gimmicky slog. It's too busy being impressed with itself for being able to read metadata of arbitrary files to notice how blatantly inconsistent it is with regards to the fourth wall. Are you meant to be TheLuckyCarder? If so, breaking the fourth wall to read *my files* doesn't make sense. Was this supposed to be the game the robot released onto steam greenlight? That doesn't any sense with the random reactions we here from Carder. These fourth wall breaks are pretty much the primary selling point of the game, and they actively detract from the experience by taking you out of the story and reminding you "hey, you're playing a game where the stakes don't actually matter". The one exception to this is the files which it promises to delete if you lose, but if anything that all but forces you to alt-tab and back-up those files if you actually care about them. That's not immersive, that's obnoxious.

The card game design is nothing to write home about. The first version is actually fairly cool and fun, the idea of paying for cards with sacrafices is creative and it's fun building up cards to be insanely OP. However, then you get to pixel-land and the game introduces 40 new mechanics that are not nearly as well fleshed out. Finally, Robotland expands on the least interesting gimmick of the four and, quite frankly, looks awful. The environmental puzzles are by far the best element of the game but they only make up about 15 minutes of game total across 14 hours of total play.

Overall 3/10. The ingredients for a very good game were present, if the game wasn't so hell-bent on trying to be the next Undertale. We already have plenty of half-baked creepypastas being made into narrative games, honestly I would've been happier if you had just made a really good card game.\t\t\t\t\t
