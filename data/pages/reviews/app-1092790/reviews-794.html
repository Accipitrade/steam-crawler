\r\n\t\t\t\t\tScore without Context: 9/10 Gigs of my fanfiction exposed

The Context: Some games require a pretty specific dialogue about how they work, are designed or played in order to get the point of it across to folks. Inscryption lies on the other side of the coin, where vagueness is preferable to descryption. A lot of what makes it unique would be spoiled by summary, rather than teased. Heck, even acknowledging this essentially tips off that it isn't your typical card-based roguelite. It is a very good example of one, however.

You begin in a room cloaked in darkness, are told the basics of the game, and are essentially let loose from there. What follows is a mix of puzzles, riddles, and discovery all tied together with the framework of the card game. All that's left is matching wits between yourself and your mysterious opponent sitting just out of reach of the light. Gathering cards, strengthening them, trimming your deck to your preferences, passives and more all make an appearance, but always seem to have their own twist compared to the usual cadre of deckbuilders.

That carries over into the moment to moment gameplay as well. Most battles are extremely reactive with plays being shown a turn before they occur, and events and surprises abound in each boss battle. Your opponent constantly enhances this, whether through new mechanics, dialogue, or secrets that hint at a deeper truth to all of this. The room itself becomes part of the experience, not just set dressing for your journey through the biomes. Adaptability is key, as no one idea overstays its welcome before being replaced by something new.

Not all of these things work, but that's the exception rather than the rule. Some of the twists might not land with you, but there's enough there that something new would replace it soon. I think it earns a lot more than it doesn't over the 15-ish hour adventure, and is worth it to go in blind for any deckbuilding lovers.\t\t\t\t\t
