\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am 2/3rds of the way through Inscryption and the game is a perfect example of why I wish Steam allowed for a more nuanced recommendation system than a binary yes or no.

The TL;DR: Inscryption is an amalgam of multiple games. One is a great game screaming to be developed into an amazing game. One is... spoilery, but the long and short of it is that it does not follow up that great game very well in my opinion.

Unfortunately this game is nigh-impossible to review in-depth without spoilers, so be warned.

-----------------------------------------

Inscryption: The Roguelite (Act 1) is a great game that I wish were longer and more fleshed out, perhaps with ideas from the similar-but-different games that follow it. It begins very well-paced and engaging, mechanically-sound, audiovisually and thematically stunning, and there's something new around the corner to keep you going. This is all readily apparent if you watch an hour of gameplay footage, and you don't need me to elaborate on this. It's great and it speaks for itself.
If, however, you're a card game veteran, you may find yourself breezing through the game at a certain point and be left wanting for challenge. The pacing started to drag for me after I defeated the "final boss" for the first time (on my fourth or fifth run, about four hours in) and I had effectively solved this game (Act 1, specifically), with little new content left in it, and some repetition grinding on me (the game even pokes fun at itself for this). This is what I mean by "a great game screaming to be developed into an amazing game." There is a ton of potential here and I desperately want more of it - more replayability. I then moved on and continued the story.

Inscryption: The CCG (Act 2) is an unfortunately fundamentally flawed game that is made less enjoyable by a far less user-friendly experience than the game that preceded it.
In my opinion and admittedly limited experience with Act 2, the Inscryption system does not lend itself to a constructed deck format nearly as well as it does a limited/roguelite system, and stumbles upon pitfalls that many CCGs before have. Notably, Act 2 inherits the biggest bugbear from Magic: The Gathering - resource-screw - and amplifies it, making dead draws and casting costs absolutely brutal, and your opening hands extremely swingy (with only an unlockable mulligan option). This is in direct contrast to Act 1 where starting with only a pseudo-random hand and having the choice of drawing squirrels versus drawing a random card was a sublime experience that lent itself to great, tense decision-making. This is tempered by you being able to immediately retry any duels you fail, but this itself just means there are no stakes whatsoever to anything that happens in Act 2. You just waste a mildly annoying two minutes or so, and try again. I could accept that Act 2 is meant to be more of a "chill" puzzle game than Act 1, but RNG elements fly in the face of that and make the whole ordeal frustrating. There's not even an attempt at a flashy boss battle at the end of Act 2 that might test every skill you've (supposedly) learned while playing it (the closest you get is whatever Scrybe you fight last) - it's a predictable scripted death, quickly and unceremoniously segueing into Act 3.
Act 2 is also dramatically less user-friendly than Act 1, with a pile of new mechanics that are not explained nearly as well as Leshy does in Act 1. Your only resource to truly learn new mechanics beyond trial-and-error are low-res, pixelated tooltips that you now have to *scroll* through because the font won't fit all the text on the screen. The four duels in each dungeon (one of which is a boss with a unique mechanic that you will probably die to the first time because it destroys your board without warning or something) that pull from each mechanical suite are a far cry from actual tutorials. I still only barely comprehend how the circuits mechanic works. I feel there are interesting individual mechanics that could be cherry-picked and plopped into Act 1's roguelite framework. So with that said, I do not feel that I have mastered Act 2, or even grok certain mechanics in it at all, and I have no desire to return to it.
Beyond that, the graphics and aesthetic are a very clear downgrade from Act 1 here. Like, I get it, I can appreciate kinda-scummy pixel art, and it's there for a reason, but... it's still scuffed, and still a big downgrade, and just doesn't really help my frustration with Act 2 in general.
The music in Act 2's pretty alright, by the way.

Inscryption: The RPG: Son of the Roguelite starts off iffy but becomes quite enjoyable as you progress. It also leads into a satisfying ending. I liked it well enough.

Inscryption: The Story is an okay and generally well-acted spooky mystery story with an ARG thing going on.

Inscryption: Kaycee's Mod is the infinitely-replayable roguelikeification of Act 1 that everyone and their mother was asking for, and it's quite enjoyable even in beta.

TL;DR: Act 1 is fantastic, Act 2 is a mess, Act 3 is good, the ending and story are good, and Kaycee's Mod makes Act 1 replayable and challenging and makes recommending Inscryption much, much easier. 4/5 stars?\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is clearly a wonderful work of art, but it is not made to my taste. It's a love letter from the creators to games that mean a lot to them.

I only continued this game for the purpose of beating it (not worth). I know it's unusual to not like this game, but I genuinely spent over half of the time playing this game just mad at it. I really wanted to love this game, I just can't. The parts that I struggled on, I could not figure out why I was struggling. It wasn't until I realized that this game is just about throwing yourself at a wall until RNG lets you bust through it eventually, or if you're lucky, immediately. I can honestly rant for a long time about this game, but I'll try my best to simplify my thoughts below.

**CONS**
(just my opinion I know not everyone will agree and that is wonderful if you love this game and think I'm wrong)

- The game immediately steers away from what I liked about it in the demo/introduction.
- I got so tired of new card mechanics being added throughout the whole game without introduction.
- You lose your decks often, and just have to learn a new one.
- The ending sucks so hard, and leaves story lines incomplete.
- You're suppose to die over and over and over. I didn't get that from playing the demo. I love dark souls because you learn when you die, in this game there is nothing to learn other than get a different starting hand.
- There are no options for story in this game. Many times I'd be forced to do something I didn't want to do, even where it seemed there could be options.
- The art style of a specific part makes cards a headache to read.

**PROS**

- I liked some of the characters
- I liked the visuals ~70% of the time~
- The beginning of the game is absolutely magical. Love it
- Having a strong deck is fun (until they strip you of it quickly)


My overall feelings for this game are confusing. I am really disappointed, that's certain, but I do think there's something special here. Give this game a try if you are ready to spend hours reading and going over card details. If you just want to get straight to the meat of it you will be disappointed. This game also seems to do a good job pleasing the board game community. They incorporate many mechanics from board games and other card games, which could be fun if you love those.

This game is not a bad game, I just personally hated playing it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a cardgame with a story, took me a day to finish it, at least I think I finished it since it decided to turn off. Loved it. All of it. Music, atmosphere and the fantastic card mechanics! Damn, it really propels you forward, wanting to learn more and try out various builds. Love that they keep it all familiar, yet fresh, throughout the entire game.

Oh and as for the horror, there is no jumpscares here. Don't worry about having to rip your headphones of your head in mach 10 or waking up your neighbours with your screams. It's a creepy game but not a scary one.

The game is divided in to two sections, first is the card game and second is when you can walk around, escape room style.
I was expecting the first part only, where you can spend a really long time just playing cards with the crazy eyes. But I discovered the story and boy did it take me for a ride. I couldn't let go of the game at all, which is why I didn't get any sleep
at all. It really was the perfect game to play when you are home, alone and got a cold. Ok imma go get some sleep and then play it again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't write reviews in general, but this is one of the best games I've ever played. There's more heart and soul put into this game than 10 AAA games combined, it's just gripping. It's art. Avoid spoilers, don't look into the game anymore you than you absolutely must. Here's what you should know -
It is primarily a card-based battler (think Slay the Spire with a story that will keep you guessing more so than anything like Hearthstone or any other trading card game) - it does have some puzzle elements but nothing that will make you want to quit the game, though it definitely has some abstract moments.
It's gorgeous, the art style is amazing and varied.
It is not a very hard game but you do need to put some thought into your moves.

I loved this game. We need to support these kinds of developers and Devolver are amongst the best in the business as far as publishers go. You can tell the team behind this had fun/really cared and it definitely translates into the experience.
