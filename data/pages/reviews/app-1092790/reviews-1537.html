\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI cannot recommend Inscryption for a number of reason. I understand the developer's intentions, but I believe anyone purchasing the game should know them in advance.

WARNING THERE WILL BE SPOILERS AHEAD READ AT YOUR OWN PERIL!!!

1) Much of the game's entertainment value comes from not knowing what you are getting into. This is why you will see so many reviews which explain little about the game. If you already know much about the game, it loses a lot of its appeal.

2) While the game advertises itself as a roguelike it has very little replayability. After completing the game once you have seen everything it has to offer. As of the time of writing this I played for 7.6 hours and completed the entire game and feel no compulsion to go back to it.

3) Fairly light spoiler but still a spoiler What is advertised on the store page is only the first "arc" of the game. A big reason for this negative review is the first arc takes about 2.5-3 hours to complete. So by the time its done and you hit the first twist in the game, which will tell you if you will enjoy the rest of it, you are past the refund deadline. This strikes me as extremely deceptive and bad business practice I have no desire to encourage.

4) I found the second arc of the game to be exceedingly boring and a massive divergence from the gameplay of the first arc. The third was more entertaining, its great if you like making broken things, even worse than the second if you dislike the game being too easy.

5) Difficulty Only the first section is particularly difficult, though you will end up running into a lot of autolose or effectively auto loss encounters. These being ones where without extremely specific combos you will be unable to get enough of a board presence or inflict enough damage to win before you just die, such as the enemy having 5 power on board at the start of your first turn or having a board where you have several spaces taken up by obstacles barring you from playing large creatures while the AI plays entirely in the unblocked squares, and, being the AI, ignores the cost of every card it plays. Even better is when your obstacles don't stop fliers and they do play in the open spaces, but only fliers which you likely cannot kill. Some people like that, I find it to be bad game design, especially because many times even with perfect play you will not see any of the combinations that let you win these encounters. And if you do, every fight is trivialized. Every other section is exceptionally easy and has virtually no consequences for failure.

6) Ending The ending was just flat out confusing and I found myself struggling to finish it. The gameplay became entirely a story device with a series of battles that were both impossible to win and impossible to lose, they only ended after a fixed amount of turns. The game also just ends with no questions answered and more questions given. It very much feels like a large section was left out or they intend to make a sequel but at the present time there is no indication there will be.

To end on a positive note I will say the game was not released then abandoned or left largely unchanged as often happens. The developer is currently actively working on an additional game mode, but it is clearly very, very early in development. At present it adds insufficient content for me to recommend it even still. In the future, perhaps that will change and I will be able to change my review to positive in good conscious.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't normally like to sit down and write steam reviews, since I usually have better things to do. But Inscryption sucked me in like almost no other game has this year, and as such, I feel compelled to review this experience. To keep whoever is going into this blind, I won't talk about any specific things in the story.

Inscryption is a roguelike deckbuilder, and if that immediately turns you off, don't let it. I don't really enjoy card games myself. I don't care for Hearthstone, and I always found myself bored with trying to learn trading card games. But Inscryption's deckbuilding mechanics are something special. Most of the fun I had with the game was when I would find ways to make my deck absolutely insane with card synergies, overpowered deathcards, and putting sigils on cards that feel like they're too broken to belong together. The best part is, though, that Inscryption's fun comes from abusing the mechanics. The mechanics are designed to be abused. The game ENCOURAGES you to abuse them, even. Your opponents don't pull any punches, so neither should you.

As for the overall story and structure, there's a lot to say, but I'll leave most of it out for the sake of spoilers. The game follows a three act structure. The first act is definitely the strongest, that almost goes without saying. The atmosphere, roguelike deckbuilding mechanics, and sense of mystery all are at its peak at the very beginning. The second act is without a doubt the weakest part of the story, as it flips just about everything you were used to from the first act into something entirely unfamiliar. If you don't like it, then just push through it. It's extremely short, and you'll be glad you did. The third act is where the deckbuilding becomes the most complex, as well as where the story starts to break apart and become more meta. I had several moments where my jaw completely dropped as I was amazed by how well it does the seemingly worn out "meta indie game cliche". The deckbuilding here takes some time to get used to, but it's much more complex and I found myself enjoying the increased challenge and higher skill floor and strategy requirement, rather than just steamrolling the game with a broken deck that I got lucky with. That's just me, though.

Finally, I have to talk about the secrets and hidden lore. All I can say is that there's a lot of it. The game ends, and you find yourself wanting more, and having even more questions than before. You feel like you've only scratched the surface of what lies beneath the mystery of Inscryption, and personally, I want to go back and see as much as possible. However, therein lies my biggest issue with the game. Some aspects of it are simply TOO cryptic. I want to go back to the game and explore secrets, but it's nearly impossible to know exactly where to look without spoiling myself for them.

That's all I have, and all I feel I can say without soiling the experience for anyone looking to try this game. If you're on the fence about Inscryption, just do yourself a favor and buy it. Stop scrolling through reviews, don't look anything else up about the game, just buy it and immerse yourself in one of the most addictive and story-rich experiences I've seen in a really long time.\t\t\t\t\t
