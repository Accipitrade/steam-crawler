\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst few hours, I'm hooked. Relatively unique mechanics, great theme, cool narrative, lots of little things to discover. Then the shallowness of the system starts to show. Battles are mostly easy, but at certain parts of the game, losing has the annoying consequence of replaying several of those easy battles. It's just too tedious. While I am invested in the story and the meta-narrative, I just don't care about the deck building or the combat 8 hours in, so it's tough for me press on.

Shame, because I loved Pony Island. Even though the gameplay in that one was much simpler, it never overstayed its welcome.\t\t\t\t\t

This review will have MINIMAL spoilers, but i will keep in mind that some people don't like those and i will mark spoilers with black boxes.
WARNING: THIS GAME IS NOT A REAL ROGUELIKE. IT HAS ROGUELIKE MECHANICS BUT IT BARELY QUALIFIES AS ONE.

10/10 artstyle(s, there are several)
10/10 deckbuilding Every single style the game shows off is incredibly satisfying. MASSIVE amounts of variation in both deckbuilding style as well as scenarios.
10/10 storytelling/story. I won't make any further comments on this besides that your expectations will be completely twisted and turned around. You will NOT expect what this game will throw at you dozens of times.
10/10 price
10/10 in general, this game is literally flawless. If you like roguelikes, cardbuilding games or psychological horror games, please do yourself a favor and try this game for a couple of hours.
Minor spoiler regarding that comment : even if you don't like the first style the game throws at you, the game will take on a wide variation of content. That's all i will tell you

Go get this. Easily one of the best games i've ever played.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis review will have a few spoilers in it, so avert your eyes if you care.

I really like Inscryption, I especially love the visuals and the theme, but personally, I really don't like the ARG elements that are included inside of the game.

It just reminds me of those Abstergo parts in the Assassin's Creed games; they just instantly take you out of the game and ruin your immersion. Hearing the guy randomly commenting on a battle I just lost or a card I picked up makes me roll my eyes. The video player parts are basically the same, but you have the choice to just go straight back into the game if you want, which I appreciate.

I really do enjoy this game and I don't even think the ARG is bad, I just wish there was some way to separate the gameplay from it, because it just kinda ruins the vibe for me while I'm playing the game.

Everything else is awesome though really, I adore the 3D parts of the game and hope that there is more of them in the future!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWarning: Review contains gameplay spoilers but no major story spoilers.

I am a huge fan of roguelike games, especially deckbuilding roguelikes similar to slay the spire. The first part of this game scratched that itch and I was hooked on it. Sadly, once you beat the first part of the game it pushes you into the different sections of the game. It becomes more of a hearthstone/MtG type game where you have a deck already built prior to going into fights, which I personally am not a fan of. Apparently once you beat the game you are able to go back and choose a section to play again, but I can't bring myself to continue playing the second act due to the dull gameplay.

I thought this game was going to be a cool roguelike game with some crazy story twists, but it turns out to be a story game with a short roguelike section.

If I end up coming back to this game it will be to wipe the save data and play the first chapter again.

If you are into the different gameplay types, you might enjoy this game. But if you are looking for a strictly rouguelike game with long-term replayability I would look elsewhere.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA solid card game that's dripping with a malicious atmosphere and crammed with dubious secrets. Inscryption’s gameplay forms a crisp and rewarding web of logic that remains enjoyable to engage with throughout the majority of its playtime. Details such as the way cards and pieces roll onto the table or how the camera lurches during actions complement the game’s tricky and mischievous tone. The card concepts start out simple and quickly scale in complexity but in a way that’s thankfully intuitive.

Despite my best efforts, there were plenty of times where I could tell that I'd lost the match right from the first hand. I'm not too adept at card games, but this was regularly disheartening. Even with how powerful good luck can be, weaseling out of a stacked scenario can feel impossible without godly foresight or heavy preparation for that exact scenario.

Looking beyond the card gameplay, the meta/creepy “Daniel Mullin” elements have a handful of highlights and intriguingly unsettled me on multiple occasions. However, the overall drama might feel unimpressive to anyone familiar with this sort of indie space. Either way, outside of a few road bumps, Inscryption ended up being a real treat as well as a glimpse into a genre I’ve haven’t spent much time with.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOK. Here is a game that has blown my mind. I rarely fall into a game, much less a deck-builder, so deeply. Even less do i find myself liking horror games. I HATE horror games! Jump scares are a cheap scare tactic that have zero depth. Regardless here comes the review part.

First let me start with the first thing. If you like deck-builders. Get this game. Full stop. Don't read anything more, don't go find online reviews, go into it as blind as you can manage.

Next let me say. if you liked pony Island. Play this game. Full stop.

Now if you *don't* like deck-builders or you find yourself liking deck-builders but bounce off a lot of them (like me). Play this game. Go in blind, give it a shot. It is not like other deckbuilders and the endless grind to get more cards to get a synergy is absent here.


If you need more than either of those two statements (for instance you don't like deck-builders at all). Keep reading.

So the review proper starts now.

Inscryption is, at it's base level, a story game. It has extremely well thought out deck-building mechanics but they take a backseat to a story that is told through dialogue, puzzles, collectibles and other subtle forms of storytelling. The game borrows a lot from games like five nights at Freddy's, The Black Watchman and Control for it's themes and style while managing to keep it's story fresh and interesting. I always found that when a game or book goes into the supernatural and everything is on the table that it's hard not to guess where it's going. When i began playing the game i thought it was a game about a crazy guy finding people to kidnap in his hut in the woods and force them to play card games then kill them. Without spoiling too much i was SO far off base it wasn't even funny. What plays out is a story told in part from in game lore, partly from found footage sections that pop up and, from what i can tell partly in an ARG style hunt that spans the interwebs. Now i say all this and you are probably going "but what's the gameplay like?" it's a deckbuilder! it's unlike any other deck-builder i've played but it still is a deck-builder. The game starts by having you slowly build up a cards and die in a series of runs. Now i am by no means a master deck-builder player but it took me like...4 of these runs to beat the final boss. However in between rounds you can get up and explore you cabin, solving a series of puzzles that, while not too difficult, are memorable. The game really plays this angle straight and you think you are finding a way to escape from the cabin but what you actually end up doing is finding a way to reset the game so that you can play the REAL game which one of the four bosses took over and made his own. The real game is still a deck builder but you discover by playing this new, completely different game you've only been using 1/4th of the available cards and mechanics. This part of the game is where i struggled the most. Suddenly having 4 times the cards you started with is overwhelming and, for deck-building noobs might be where they quit. However being able to try again with no dying and no "starting over' mechanic from this point forward was a masterstroke. Fail a fight? you can re-do your deck and try again right away without even having to travel anywhere, just click on them! genius. Anyways things get crazy from this point of the game but throughout it all the card game, deck-building aspect keeps building on itself and you get to master a ton of different aspects. The only thing i wish is that we got to play entire sections of the game with each boss and each subset of cards. Maybe that'll happen in the future who knows. Regardless Inscryption isn't an experience i'll soon forget. \t\t\t\t\t
