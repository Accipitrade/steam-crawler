Inscryption – A

Updated Dec. 28: Modified review to account for new update from December 14.

Who This Game is For:
Those who enjoy roguelike games or card games that have a story to it. Horror fans will also enjoy this game. Think Slay the Spire story mode.

Is it worth the cost?:
Yes. But I would recommend waiting until sale of 20% or more if possible.

Click Here to Follow My Curator Page for more reviews!

Summary:
What a damn treat this game is. Inscryption, developed by Daniel Mullins Games, is a single-player card game with a horror story built around it. In this roguelike game, you build your deck, and go through the map as you pick up on clues to discovering the mystery surrounding this card game. Use items and manipulate your cards to become an unstoppable machine and then get your picture taken when you die so the old man across the table can gloat in his victory. When you want a break, move away from the table and solve puzzles around the room to unlock new cards and further the story. I can’t say much more since I don’t want to spoil the story, but it was a damn treat. This is a story game first with a roguelike card game built around it. The story only took around 8-9 hours to beat, and with a replayable roguelike mode out in beta, this game is definitely one to add to your deck.

BREAKDOWN:

Graphics – A:
Inscryption is unique and has two different styles in the game. First, we have the 3D section. For 2 of the 3 acts in this game, you will be in a 3D environment, while the remaining act will take place in an 8-bit format like the NES but with much better coloring. Each of the environments are well-detailed and incredibly unique. There are 2 main 3D locations, both of which are quite small but still fun to look around in. The atmosphere of these locations are one of the highlights, which I learned after playing this game at night and remembering how much of a scardey-cat I am.

Once you are sitting, you’ll be able to see the details of the playing board and your cards. The cards themselves are each quite unique and have easy-to-understand layouts. Some of the cards will be animated and talk to you as well. There is a filter over the screen during these sections that gives it a slightly pixilated look to it. This didn’t bother me though and helps make the animations feel more alive. The models of the other characters in this game are phenomenally made and genuinely freaked me out.

Gameplay – B+:
At its core, the game revolves around a card game called “Inscryption”. You start with a hand of 4 cards and are essentially playing against the person across from you. Each card has a HP and attack stat as well as different abilities. These abilities can range from attacking multiple slots, hurting anyone who attacks your card, or even destroying the card after it attacks once. That last one has a symbol I kept forgetting which made me question reality when my cards magically disappeared. Most cards also have a special way of being played, like sacrificing cards on the field or using the bones of destroyed cards to summon new ones. These attack whatever card is in front of them and if there’s nothing there, they do direct damage. For every unit of damage you inflict, a weight is placed on a scale. This scale sways back and forth as you take damage or the enemy does. Your goal is to tip the scale all the way towards them to win. There are also items you can receive that affect your card game, like giving you an extra card to sacrifice or pulling out one of your eyeballs to weigh the scale in your favor.

In the 3D environments, to get to these battles, you go across a map that has different events on every space. Some give you new cards, some give you the chance to upgrade your cards, and some give you the chance to sacrifice and mutilate your cards to make others significantly better. None of this is shown on screen, but you’ll know it’s weird when you see the two-face doctor and he can take duplicate cards and sew them together. Once you get to the end of each map, you will fight a boss. Each boss has unique modifiers and events happen to spice the game up.
Outside of battles, you can get up from the table you are playing the game on and walk around. There are intricate puzzles to solve and each one either furthers the story or adds unique cards to your deck. Doing this is highly encouraged and usually necessary to keep the story moving along.

In the 8-Bit environment, you walk around and fight different enemies to beat 4 bosses. The card game itself is the same, but there are no extra events and you get new cards by finding packs. Packs are either scattered in chests or received when beating enemies or a boss. You can then edit the deck with the cards you receive.

Story – A:
I am not going to go too much into story since I believe it is one that should be experienced. The story is great. It is a horror-themed story mixed with some thinking to really get the full picture of what is going on. The story is told through figuring out puzzles and using the hints given in-game to paint a full picture. There are other aspects to this story that are shown through the 3 acts of the game, but I really don’t want to spoil. Just know it is a great horror story and revolves around the card game “Inscryption”.

Replayability – A-:
The story section of the game took roughly 8-9 hours to complete, but there isn't much of a reason to replay the story. Though on December 14th, a new update was pushed into beta giving the game an unlimited roguelike mode. With different cards, challenges, and starting decks to unlock, this adds a major reason to come back to the game. I'm curious to see how this will look in a full release, but I am very optimistic.

Developers – A-:
I have not seen many responses from the developers on Inscryption, but Daniel Mullen Games has 2 other games that have been released: The Hex and Pony Island. I unfortunately haven’t had the opportunity to play either, but the reviews for both are overwhelmingly positive. There have been some minor patches to Inscryption, but they were usually for a couple of bugs here and there. I wasn’t hoping for added content though, which is weird because I usually do. The game they released feels feature complete and offers exactly what I hoped it would. The game is published by Devolver Digital as well, who have also published games like Gris, Loop Hero, Death’s Door, Enter the Gungeon, and many more amazing games. Overall, the devs seem to be on top of bug fixes and appreciative of beta testers. I am personally very excited to see if a sequel comes out and looking forward to discovering more behind the story.
