\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat a game.

Like many reviews before me, play this game blind! Avoid the spoilers! This is an experience that is best enjoyed at your own pace on your own terms.

There are settings in the options to turn off several of the filters if the visual noise or CRT lines hurt your eyes. There's also an option to turn off certain flickering if you're photosensitive. I love seeing these options in the game because although it really adds to the aesthetic, there are those who aren't me whose eyes can get tired from looking at such effects.

I won't go into details about any of the story beats. My review doesn't need it. This game roped me in and enthralled me for the entire duration it took for me to beat it. The game knows what it is, and confidently rocks it. The aesthetic is tight and cohesive. The sound design is amazing and immersive. The gameplay itself is smooth and easy to play quickly once you know what you're doing. The story itself made me cry at points, in a way that were I to play the story again from start to finish, it would hurt just a little.

Every single card element across the entire game is lovingly thought out and iterates on itself incredibly. But this is not Slay the Spire. This is not your next roguelite, though it does give you the option at the end to play it as such. It's unfair to the game, however, to act like it not being a game with infinite depth of replayability is a flaw. That's not what this game is. I can understand wanting more depth because parts of the game are incredibly fun! The card game is so thoughtfully woven together with the rest of the game's stories that there were little things that made me go "oh crap" with realization at multiple turns.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is all that I hoped for and more. The premise itself looked so unique and interesting, and it certainly didn't disappoint in that department. I'm not saying the game is perfect, or is as addictively fun as something like Slay the Spire, but it is a fun, creative, unforgettable experience that was obviously created with a lot of time and effort.

You begin the game in a cabin, confronted by a mysterious figure who seems like an absolute lunatic, and are seemingly being held captive in order to play a game with him. The game itself is simple, using energy mechanics to play cards like most deck building games do. The twist is your energy to play cards is by using the blood of your sacrificed creatures (some are zero cost to get the sacrifice train rolling). You play along the board your captor has created, and play along with his weird role playing shenanigans with an awkward smile on your face until you can figure out how to get out of his cabin. There are encounters scattered along the board which give you the opportunity to modify and enhance your deck, and new features are added every time you die that give the game a new spin which keeps things fresh. You are allowed to get up from the table where the game is being played in order to explore and investigate the cabin itself which holds secrets and the keys to escaping. The puzzles are fun to figure out and well executed, and always had me applauding their clever implementations. The art style is also very unique and well designed, as are the characters you meet throughout.

I will not spoil anything by elaborating too much further beyond this initial introduction to the game, as a lot of the fun of the game itself relies on taking your first impressions of it and turning them on their head. But I will say this, you are in for more than you might expect out of this game.

Now as for my problems with the game, I found the mechanics of the card game itself to be a little too dependent on the luck of your initial draw. There are not very many ways to manipulate your opening hand or to draw more cards, and your deck has to contain at least 20 cards. This lead to many battles being largely dependent on my opening hand and winning or losing felt less about skill and more about luck, which is never very fun. That said their is a VERY large pool of creature abilities and it was fun to try to synergize my deck with different themes.

My second issue I had was the poor (and kinda cringey) live action cutscenes that were placed in the game to fill out the storyline. I understand what they were trying to do, but it really falls flat and takes a lot of the atmosphere and immersion of the game away. I feel it didn't need that story arc at all and the characters living inside the game did a fine enough job to convey the main plot points.

My only other issue with the game is I wish they would have continued the story further for all characters, as it was kind of a let down how the game ended when it did. It almost seemed like they could have or had already finished the remaining portions of the game up to a certain point but maybe were too crunched for time to completely follow through with it? I'm not sure but the ending felt almost like a teaser for what could have been and that was kind of a bummer. Hopefully it will be released later as DLC if that was the case.

Overall I really enjoyed Inscryption, and I appreciate all the hard work and dedication that was obviously put into it...................... 9/10 :)
