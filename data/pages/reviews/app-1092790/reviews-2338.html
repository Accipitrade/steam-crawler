\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a serious mixed bag. There will be heavy spoilers down below...

I do recommend this game. I believe it's a good, but flawed game. My main issue with the game is the game feels incredible... Under explored. Every section of the game is unique in it's own way, however, these different sections all feel like they needed more. The first, and best section in my opinion (tragically), was also the most fun, had the best atmosphere all around and really was a wonderful part. it was the game at it's best feeling like a fully thought out game. I had a few issues, for example, the final boss of this felt almost underwhelming. Like a joke. It really took away from the tension of the fight. One of the highlight's of this part was the escape-room styled puzzles through it. It was always fun to explore and figure out solutions. The secrets and easter eggs in this game were a large enjoyment.

However, and, heavy spoilers ahead, that's where things take a turn.

The game's meta-narrative starts taking front stage and we enter the weakest part of the game, part 2, a top down adventure pixel game where you explore the 4 main entities of the world, who they are and their realm. You learn about their friends, and most importantly their mechanics. These mechanics are very mixed overall. Leshy's is the strongest mechanic by far, and the most enjoyable. Grimora's was fun, however it felt slow, despite it being capable of some crazy infinites. PO3's mechanic was bland and uninteresting, while still working well. The final mechanic, the Mox, was by far the weakest of them all, and in part 3 also felt extremely underused and clunky overall. This section of the game is a massive downgrade from the first part, and the once amazing gripping atmosphere is replaced by simple pixel art. As someone who loves top down, classic adventure games, I greatly disliked this section. The atmosphere, one of my favorite parts of the first section, was gone in exchange for a flat, uninteresting UI in battles that barely had any real flavor behind it all. The battles were fast and not-memorable, though the music was very good (a constant plus for the game in my opinion). Once you beat all 4 of the bosses, we enter part 3...

Part 3, Po3's world, was a largely mixed bag. For once the atmosphere had returned and the ability to explore your surroundings with puzzles. Things feel a lot better now. However, things still feel industrial and very set, and PO3 even mentions how he dislikes flavor over function. This is a fine thing for a character to believe, but when it's reflected in an entire section of the game, it lead to much of it feeling like it lacked. Like it was purposefully created to not have all this unique flavor with it all. There was some there with the robots and the cool environment, but it was... Less interesting. The gameplay was a highlight here being very fun throughout, though the roguelike progression of the first part was missed on my part. The issue with this part as a whole and reflective of the entire game was the mechanics- There was way, too many of them. It felt like after every single boss there was a brand new mechanic being pushed on me without enough time to edit my deck to try and incorporate it, multiple times. The mechanics didn't even mesh that well outside of a few token cards. It all felt like it was underexplored, and if we had more time here it could have worked. And, then, you fight a boss and proceed on to what should be the end of the section. The boss from before had little buildup, and felt like a miniboss, so you're expecting some sort of massive conclusion battle. And... Once again, no. it's a sudden end that made all of my deckbuilding through my run feel pointless. I felt like I was left wanting, and kept expecting a sudden large ending card game, but nothing. Your deck is taken away and you then enter the game's epilogue.

Now, to it's benefit, the epilogue made the game's meta narrative feel a lot better. The epilogue has you go through the other 3 main bosses, and have conversations and games with them. The games with everyone are incredibly fun and the dialogue was very rewarding. Hearing Leshy tell you good game, and shake your hand retroactively made me respect him and he easily became my favorite character. The conversation as a whole reframed the entire first section as something not to escape from, but to enjoy with him. We also get to see fully fleshed out flavor for both Grimora and Magnificus. This was such a treat, but also pushed into my biggest gripe with the game. I wanted more. I wanted to really play with both of them, and have their own sections. It left the pair feeling like they were practically abandoned. And, then, we see a wrap up to the game's meta-narrative...

The Meta-Narrative is a story about Luke finding the game cartridge, and being harassed by the company who 'created' it. It's all done with a real actor, and, is my second biggest gripe with the game. The meta-narrative outside of what's happening *inside* of the game was just incredibly... Boring. Every time we got to see Luke I just wanted to get back to the game. It felt bland and uninteresting. It's very, telling, actually- When the game's meta narrative is the weakest part OF the narrative, then there's a serious issue with the game as a whole. If it had all been removed, and we instead focused on the inside of the game and left it to the 'player' instead of some random dude named Luke, I feel the game would have been much stronger. To reiterate, the Outside Meta-Narrative with Luke felt boring and bland, but the game's Inside Meta-Narrative with Po3 trying to upload the game and everything felt very solid.

After I finished the game, I really truly did enjoy it. And I was left wanting. I wanted to explore Grimora's section, Magnificus's, to fight a real Po3 boss, to fully explore all 4 of the main boss mechanics, to spend more time with each part and solve the puzzles in the relatively short escape room portions. The entire game just left me feeling like I needed more of it, and it left a bad taste in my mouth. If this entire game was just Leshy's first section, with more content, more puzzles, and a narrative solely on him, I probably would have enjoyed it more. But, personally I just wanted to see the other 2 have their own sections. And it really was a big blow to not get real games for all of them, and instead we got underexplored, underutilized mechanics.

...And, one last thing, because I had no other place to fit it. The Ouroboros card was fun to use but incredibly overpowered to the point where I wish I didn't have it, but it was still given to me. As a magic the gathering 'spike' I couldn't not use the strongest available options, but it trivialized any game I drew it in.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first time I take some time to write a review for a game. I bought this game expecting another chill deckbuilding roguelike and ended up amazed and shocked at the details. This game is an homage to card games, blending known TCGs like MTG, YGO and DM into a simple yet fascinating language. I particularly enjoyed the fact that once you get used to some rules and interactions, the game invites you to reevaluate your strategies and be creative in order to survive. Additionally, there are some puzzles solving and escape room aspects that, despite not being my kind of game, were intriguing enough to keep me looking and trying. The story starts pretty straight forward and then adds layers upon layers of lore. Finally, I'd like to mention the way the game interacts with the player. I felt really thankful for the experience.\t\t\t\t\t
