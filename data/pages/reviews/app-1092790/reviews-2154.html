\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is a game without an audience.

If you're interested in what you see in the ads, a crazy guy larping at you in a rustic cabin as he walks you through a roguelike card game as your game-master, well I'm sorry to say that that's not Inscryption. That's just a fake game of which the style, plot, and roguelike elements are completely abandoned a third of the way into the adventure. After that point, the game becomes something far closer in style and tone to Daniel Mullins' previous games, and this creates a clear divide.

People sold on the concept in the ads won't like this game because the thing they paid for is a small sliver of the adventure. I've heard people over and over say that Act 1 is the best part, that it "goes downhill" after Act 1, which is depressing to hear, but understandable if someone thought they were getting into a roguelike card game.

And people who are Daniel Mullins fans looking for more of that signature style, like me, will find themselves forces to complete a roguelike card game to get there. A game they don't care about the mechanics of, don't care about the story of, and yet is still difficult enough to impede their progress and stop them from getting to the real game until they get good at a completely unrelated game they give not one ♥♥♥♥ about.

So who is this for? The only audience left are people who find roguelike card games and Daniel Mullins' games to be equally engaging, which I have to imagine is quite the niche crowd. The 20 bucks is just not worth it for a game you will likely hate at least a third of. To come from a dev I respect so much, this is immensely disappointing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHate that I have to suggest against this one. This game hits you with stylized charm immediately then keeps you around with intrigue about the setting and situation you find yourself in as the player. It even manages to keep that for a long while through right up until you finally clear act 1 which only last as long as it does because the game will force you to lose MULTIPLE times if you do too good on the first few runs. The incredibly sound design and atmosphere the game sets up with the very tiny number of speaking characters and puzzles at this point creates something far greater then the sum of its parts only to throw them out the window.

When it finally blesses you with being allowed to clear act 1 you go into the second where there is a complete aesthetic downgrade. The game suddenly drops down to what feels like a rushed product missing tutorials for a lot of new mechanics and simplified battles that present little to no challenge at all even with RNG against you. At this point it is relying on you still playing just on the hope it gets good again by bringing back the strengths that Act 1 had. And it does... somewhat.

The game brings back the amazing visual style with some slight changes for good reason as well as some more fun puzzles and spaces to move through with but just like the first act this one eventually sends you into the final section of the game in what feels like another rushed/unfinished way. Suddenly the game is rapidly approaching an end before you can even feel like you have come close to taking it all it could have offered, going so far as to tease whole new stages and styles and characters and more lore you could have dived into before just being pulled away and telling you it's over and you have nothing more to do.

I can't recall the last time I've felt so positively compelled to experience what a game is setting up only to reach the end and feel nothing but disappointment so intense it makes it hard to even look to all the good parts of a game and say it was worth it at least. This game would really have been something special if it was given the time it needed to build all it's acts to the same level as 1 and 3.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game design decisions in this game are absolutely baffling to me. The first third of the game is fantastic. You're playing a light lane based roguelite deck builder that is centered on (to what is known to yugioh players) as a tribute mechanic. This is where you need to sacrifice one monster in order to summon a stronger one. In most card games this is usually a pretty bad mechanic, as you many times lack either the monster that needs to be sacrificed or the monster that sacrifices. You rarely have both when you need them. But Inscryption in its cleverness has two decks you can draw from, one deck for squirrels which can be sacrificed and another made up of the powerful creatures that require sacrifices to be summoned. This creates a lot of interesting decisions on what deck you draw from and how you play your cards.

This fantastic little deck builder is then presented in the form of a roguelite. You work your way through three different maps that each end in a boss. And these battles are a ton of fun, each boss has a kind of gimmick you need to be able to play around. While once you figure out the gimmick you should never lose, this part of the game is a blast. Fusing cards also leads to a lot of interesting decisions. You can tell there was an enormous amount of love and effort put into the first third of the game. It oozes personality.

Then all of a sudden, the fun stops. For some strange reason the creator decided that the audience may be having too much fun so he thought it would be a good idea to make the game worse in every single way. In fact, it feels almost like a straight up bait and switch. The game downgrades it visuals to what can only charitably be called rpg maker level graphics. And with this comes worse sound and a very slow UI. You also no longer have two decks - you have 1. So we're back to the problem of not having the right cards you need when you need them. This makes the beast deck absolute garbage. To top it off all the roguelite elements are totally gone as well. Now you just fight things until you draw the right cards and win. But hey I guess you can build decks now? Too bad theyre all the same because aggro is clearly the best strategy. The creator took something that was so great and somehow made it dull. I hear there is a third part but part two was so bad that I honestly stopped playing. I have no desire to keep playing through this atrocious bait and switch - I dont care if its supposed to be "meta."

As someone who has sunk 500 hours into Slay the Spire, I recommend you stick to the demo. The game has zero replay value and there is no endless mode. The demo as far as im concerned is the real game. Inscryption feels like it could have been something great if it wasn't just a stupid gimmick. The bait and switch legitimately made me mad. I feel like I was robbed of $20.\t\t\t\t\t
