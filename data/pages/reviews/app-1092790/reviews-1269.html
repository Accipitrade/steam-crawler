\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr: I recommend the first part but not the other two due to their worse gameplay.

The audio is great. The visual is good although a bit too dark at times, which makes it hard to see. The story is meh. The meta narrative gets old really fast. After all there is not a second "fourth wall" to break.

The rest of this review will be exclusively focused upon the gameplay, which is the main reason why it comes down to a negative. There are mainly three parts to this game.

The first part is what you see in the trailers which is great. As a roguelike, it is forgiving as almost everything resets when you die. It eases you into learning the rules with a (not yet meta) story. It has a good combinations of skills and luck, which is what a good card game should be all about. You could also play the entirely optional minigames which gives you some small advantages in the main game. If you could get this game on a sale then the first part is fully worth playing.

The other two parts are only hinted at in the trailers, and as other reviews pointed out, they are significantly worse, and I will attempt to explain the case purely from a gameplay perspective.

In the second part, the most significant change is that you are drawing from one pile instead of two piles in part one. In part one, there is a squirrel deck where you can only draw squirrels, which is essentially a free-to-summon base card; and you have another deck where you have everything else. In part two however, you instead have to build your deck (similar to Heartstone) to control the portion of those base cards. This makes the core gameplay a lot more luck based than skill based. This is somewhat remedied by reducing death penalties (you can retry instead of starting all over like a roguelike in part one) and reducing the difficulties, but overall this results in a less engaging gameplay.

This is further complicated by the fact that there are four different sets of cards you can choose from (with different base cards), and the game only introduced you to 1.5 of them (the beast set and a little of the death set) during the first part. You would need to learn the other 2.5 by reading the rules (and guessing the implied or the unclear) and playing the minigames. While the game explicitly encourages you to build a mixed set in one of the dialogues, I find it very hard to make it work as the good cards tend to need a lot of support from their respective sets.

In the third part, the core gameplay shifts to the tech set at the beginning and the two-pile returns, which is good. What remains gone, however, is the roguelike element. Since there is not a way to alter the deck like in part two, if you make a mistake during your deckbuilding events, too bad you will be stuck with it for the rest of the part three.

Perhaps realizing the fourth magic set has not received much attention, towards the second half of the part three, the game introduces the magic elements to your deck. This results in a weird gameplay as towards the end, your base cards are ridiculously buffed with secondary effects, circuit effects and gems; on the other hand, only a small portion of your cards benefit from the circuit/gem effects as you only get to alter your deck to a very limited amount after these elements are introduced.

Looking back at this game, it seems clear to me that the other three sets (other than the beast set highlighted in part one) clearly have potentials (which is also hinted at in the epilogue of the game), while the worse gameplay does not treat these sets as fairly. Sadly, the gameplay might have been much better if the developer did not insist on having a meta story. As the developer is working on an endless expansion of part one, I personally would look forward to a similar treatment for the other three sets (and perhaps also storylines for the other three Scrypts as well).\t\t\t\t\t
