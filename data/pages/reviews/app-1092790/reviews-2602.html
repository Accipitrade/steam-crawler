\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe current clime of card based video games is rough as shale. If it isn't the lowest, most awful form of pack based gacha and purely competitive multiplayer focus, it's instead a roguelike that uses cards very thinly, or simply as a veneer for abilities and characters. Being someone who grew up with card based games like the Pokemon TCG on Gameboy, I longed for a similar adventure of deckbuilding, rare chasing, pack opening fun, without having to worry if my strats were up to competitive snuff without opening my wallet. Needless to say, in the end, my standards drooped low enough that I would take any properly card based game if it was done well.

Enter Inscryption, like an entire tanker of water dumped on my humble expectations for a mere glass.

For card lunatics like myself, Inscryption has everything. You collect cards, later you can open packs, you can build your own deck, but there is also a good roguelike section that lets you play with the cards in a different fashion. Instead of pack opening and deckbuilding, you're instead encouraged you to craft your own overpowered cards and set ups out of obviously randomized prizes and events. It's intensely exciting, without it ever really feeling like there's a single strategy you MUST use to win. Unfortuantely due to the deckbuilder and the roguelike essentially being two seperate modes, the consistency of the gameplay is a little bouncy, and you'll doubtless come away preferring one over the other, (though luckily after you've done everything, you can indulge in either one at your leisure). Overall, the experience as a whole was like a breath of fresh air; a single player card based adventure that, after each run, left me itching all over for just one more turn, one more roll, one more card to add to an iteration of my favourite deck... anything to prolong the experience.

So that's the card game aspect, the actual gameplay. But I also know that most people won't bleed cardboard if cut, so what about the rest? Funnily, it's what most people would come to the game for; Inscryption's daunting atmosphere and creepy story are phenomenally done, and anyone playing the demo can tell the sheer shadowy oppression, the theme of 'red in tooth and claw', the cruelty you yourself sometimes have to apply to your cards... It's all masterfully crafted, like a fine oak table smeared with a freshly hunted deer carcass. As you may well have heard, some of this finely tuned atmosphere is abandoned partway through, at least for a little while, but if you've enjoyed the game up to that point then there's plenty to play with, to keep yourself busy before the game redescends into its murky depths.

In all, Inscryption is well worth full price. Its a game with a terrifying story and mood, seated comfortably on a bed of a well crafted card game roguelike. The dismount, aka the end game, may not hit too firmly, but by then, you'll just be hankering for another playthrough, so it's not a major concern.

Now to patiently wait for either DLC or a full on sequel. I'd take either at this point, I just want MORE!\t\t\t\t\t
