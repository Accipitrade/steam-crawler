Personal notes:

1. This game is best played without knowing any help from outside source.
2. Do NOT get too attached to your deck. Trust me.
3. The marketing and advertising of this game is kind of misleading unfortunately, but perhaps it is for the greater good (You'll see soon enough when you try it).
4. Don't mind those who reviewed the game's length around 8 - 10 hours. Just enjoy the narrative and that feeling of dread as it slowly haunts you. Every players will have a different playtime amount, and it doesn't mean you're bad if you played it long enough.
5. Do NOT play this game if you're a newcomer to video game world. I know, it sounded very gatekeeping, but these kind of games are best enjoyed by someone who understand video game at somewhat deeper level.

This game is some of the best money spending I've ever done, really.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you played Daniel Mullins' other games, you know what to expect, and he delivers the goods and then some. It's his most ambitious project by far, both in scope and in actual gameplay mechanics. It also means many of his shortcomings are there, and the bigger scope just makes them more obvious. Still, a recommendation for me, even if you only play it for its first 4-5 hours.

It opens with the player character trapped in a room with a mysterious and capricious game master, who forces them to play a card game in the vain of Hearthstone or Magic. The card game itself is quite engaging, with all the depth, synergies, OP strats and RNG you know and love. It's also breakable, exploitable and relies on luck more often than not. It also means battles drag on sometimes because the RNGesus doesn't feel like helping either of you today.

So, a Slay The Spire type deal, right? Maybe with some Hades-style story? Yeah, about that. Saying as little as I can here, it's split up into different scripted sections and has a definite narrative ending, after which the game is basically over. The game uses this structure to have fun with its mechanics. The basics stay, but the specifics are always changing.

In terms of production value and the sheer number of unique assets used, it's definitely impressive. Some parts are admittingly better than others; the starting section, as you might have heard, is the best one. After that, the game slips and slides between a slow grind and trying to capture the gameplay and magic of that first section. It almost gets there, but you can't experience something for the first time twice.

The bad news is that there isn't much incentive to replay it once you've exhausted all of its content - which is kinda weird to say on a game like this. No multiplayer, no endless mode. I can understand people who are upset about this, but considering the effort that went into creating that content, I think it's worth it. You get a lot of bang for your buck as far as I'm concerned, too.

So should you get Inscryption? Try the demo and ask yourself if you'd like to play 12 more hours of it in different strokes of paint, or if the mysteries interest you in any way. But even if you don't end up loving it, at least appriciate the effort that went into creating all of it.\t\t\t\t\t
