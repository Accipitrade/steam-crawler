\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is very fun, but feels like a series of missed opportunities by the time it's finished. Short review; the gameplay + art is engaging and the storyline is adequate. Spoilers from here on out.

After the first act of the game, it massively opens up the possibilities for deck building, introduces two more mechanics for putting cards on the field, and does a bunch of meta commentary about the game itself. Pretty typical for a Devolver game, but the execution seemed pretty sloppy to me. Nothing is too engaging because it's incredibly easy to break the game in a myriad of ways and the stakes no longer exist, both because you can retry immediately with no (or at least, very little) penalty, and because of the aforementioned difficulty plummet.

It doesn't over-stay its welcome, but the 1st act has by far the best material in the entire game, with what I found to be both the best art-style and svelte game mechanics. It's not that the other 2/3rds of the game are bad per se, they just don't live up to what I was expecting. It feels like the developer crammed every last idea they had for a card game all into one deck-builder, and the whole twist being "wowee the game itself is evil!" is very overdone at this point. It's probably just up to differing tastes, but I would have vastly preferred if the game stuck to its guns with the cabin in the woods and ditched everything 8-bit and beyond. \t\t\t\t\t
