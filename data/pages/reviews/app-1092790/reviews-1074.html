\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is the kind of indie game that creeps up on you. It actually becomes more enjoyable as you continue to play it, despite starting with an incredibly attractive premise. The card game mechanics work extremely well throughout the game, getting significantly more interesting as the game throws twists and turns your way. The setting for this card game is suprisingly drawing, keeping me invested from start to finish on my second attempt at playing. I started this game up for the first time in October of 2021, and completed it when I picked it back up in January 2022, and before the end of the month I had completed every achivement this game has to offer.
I simply do not have enough good things to say about Inscryption. Thank you Inscryption devs for showing the industry the importance of creating an interesting world for your game to take place in.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a game I recommend with caveats - there are certain aspects of the game that are absolutely brilliant, yet there is one major aspect of this that completely threw me off.

At its core, it is a solid deck-building card game. However, its infusion of elements that you'd generally not expect in a game as such that truly elevate it - a sinister atmosphere, puzzles and rich lore.

In Inscrpytion, you are pit against a series of opponents, each with their own rich personalities and play styles. Along the way you meet other interesting characters, make important choices, which in turn move the story forward and help you build your deck. The card battles are often a well crafted game of tactics - it keeps you on your toes, forces you think carefully & adapt your play to the evolving battlefield and feels rewarding when you beat an opponent.

Outside the card battles, the journey that you embark on reveals details about the world you've been dumped into without a warning in bite-size pieces. It also presents you with a number of important choices that require careful consideration in order to build a deck that has the right synergies. And yes, you really need to understand how different cards and power-ups come together to make a powerful deck.

Interestingly, there is the completely unexpected mechanic of an escape room style puzzle solving layered on top of this, which blends wonderfully with the core mechanics. You get to step away from the physical table where you're dealing cards and look around the small room(s) that you find yourself trapped in. Uncovering clues and solving puzzles to find an escape route is extremely rewarding in its own right.

The icing on this cake is the sinister atmosphere that the game manages to build, right from the way you're thrust into the game's setting to the amazing music to the gorgeous art style.

However, the strong opening act is its own undoing. Without giving out much, the game does a bait and switch on you, altering mechanics, art styles and themes as you move into the second act and does this once again for the third and final act. Lore is a common and powerful binding thread across the three acts and so is its core mechanics of card play. However, these changes themselves break the immersion that the game manages to build in act 1, not to mention that the following acts do not measure up well to the first act in terms of substance either. They're good, in their own right, but they do sometimes feels like standalone mini-titles, which are vaguely tied in by the genre and lore. Towards the third act, I found myself simply wanting to power through the game to uncover the lore and get that 'Game Completed' tag.

In a nutshell, this is certainly an experience worth having, especially the first act, but go in with the expectations that the game will suddenly morph into something different, twice!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a big fan of card game / deck building games, stepping into this - I knew I was likely to enjoy this. I had personally never heard of this Developer before and hadn't played their previous games, so I had little idea of what to expect. I was mostly attracted to the game being described by others as an example where this particular developer is good at hiding a games true nature behind a simple mechanic.

Inscription is a hard game to summarise... and for this reason, I would just say to anyone thinking of picking this up to do so when the opportunity arises. The game has some great twists and turns and blends Deck building with some puzzles and escape-room mechanics. The rules are simple yet original and the game finds a way of changing it up as you progress without it becoming unfamiliar.

I really enjoyed this game and hope the developer receives the recognition and support they deserve. I have picked up the other games they have made to give those a try and support them further.\t\t\t\t\t
