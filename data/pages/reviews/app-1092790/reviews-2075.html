\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI expected a game that put mechanics over story. Instead, I got a game with a thorough mechanical exploration of a solid gameplay idea and a lack-luster plot that peters out without much of a resolution.

Overall, it starts out incredibly strong but gets progressively worse - the gameplay goes from great to good, but the story takes a nosedive.

1. The surface is great
(no spoilers)

The surface-level Inscryption is an atmosphere-first brutal roguelite.

Boy-oh-boy, the atmosphere is so thick you could cut it with a knife. The palpable sense of unease and mystery is enhanced by exceptional modelling (which, surprisingly, features TONS of generic models), imaginative use of animations and music, and flawless visual design.

The atmosphere alone would carry the game on its own, but its the card combat gameplay that's the real star here. It takes minimalistic numbers and turn rules, and elegantly combines them with maximilistic smorgasbord of glyphs (card keywords represented as icons), almost all of which are immediately grokkable. It's tense, it's strategic, I love it.

But, being brutal, it definitely does not care about balance at all. On the one hand, with no mulligan, it is extremely easy to have a bad initial hand and a bad first draw, which - even after burning all three of your hard-earned special items, which also are a subject of a bad loot draw - can still leave you dead, either ending your run outright, or ending your run at the region boss (since that extra life would be transformed in a vital resource card). On the other hand, Inscryption gives you a green light to rip its belly open, break its bones, and then reassemble them into a planet-cracking victory machine. Between sacrificial glyph stacking, totems, and memento cards, you can create some absolutely joyful monstrosities (and that's without touching the unique special cards with hidden mechanics).

The latter seems a conscious and fully intentional decision on the part of the developer, to allow people who aren't into roguelites to progress into the rest of the game.

Speaking of which...

2. The shallows are good
(mechanical spoilers)

...while my expectations for what Inscryption would be like after the first twist weren't specific, I certainly did not expect to get an Evoland.

It almost seems like Daniel Mullins came up with the board and glyphs first, tried out four different systems for card costs (blood, bones, energy, mox), and then sewed up the prototypes together.

The two most promising - blood and bones - were the star feature of the first part of the game, the roguelite, which I already covered.

All four of them are featured as separate decks in the second part, styled as a Game Boy Color TCG tie-in. While it lacks both the atmospere and the craziness of the first portion, the novelty of building a 20-card deck from four different sets is enough to cruise through the ~15 battles painlessly.

Energy card cost as seen in, say, Hearthstone, is featured in the third part, styled after a generic level-based game run by a narcissistic True Gamer™ alpha-quarterbacking douchebag that couldn't roleplay if his metallic butt was on the line. Despite (intentionally?) being the weakest part of the game both on the mechanical - both the control over the deck and the gleeful powering up are gone; card cost and overall structure are generic; there are gotcha moments that require you to always have trash cards to offer up for mandatory trade/burning up/deletion - and the narrative level, it's still enjoyable, due to having both solid base mechanics and well-made setpiece encounters.

Bones and mox are featured separately in little vignettes at the last part of the game, and I can certainly see why they weren't extended into full standalone part - the former would be pretty barebones without serving supporting role to blood as in the roguelite part, and the latter simply sucks.

Speaking of things which suck...

3. The depths are bad
(light story spoilers, with blacked-out full spoilers)

...if I had bought the game solely for the story, this wouldn't be a review, this would be a rant.

The story within a story about the haunted game is a pretty straightforward, functional plot: four cantankerous Boss Sage Gym Leaders semi-regularly screw each other over, until finally one goes too far, which prods another to commit a murder-suicide genocide-suicide by deleting the game itself. Even though I have to admit that the Great Transcendence was nicely delivered, this story is just servicable and nothing to write home about.

The overarching story reminds me of the discussion about the endings two popular games. Just like the ending of Mass Effect 3 (that is before BioWare clarified that no, its more interesting reading is not true) it sits in that grey zone where I cannot tell whether the author is incompetent or extremely subtle. And just like the ending of The Witness, it leaves me unsure whether it's a failed serious story or a succesful practical joke.

It follows an arc that sets up little and delivers on none of it (Mr Punny Name Kohdok finds the possessed game, receives some gentle pressure to return it, experiences some mild paranoia, faffs about until the deletion uncovers a .zip file with blackmail material, then gets shot in the head). The weird stuff seems to be nonsensical and to have no point. The story-within-story is basically tangential to the overarching one, because the self-aware game file seems to serve no other purpose than to be a time lock on a .zip file and is not explored any further.

But most importantly, the reveal of the Great Secret of the story (ie. what's in the .zip file) is censored in the worst possible manner: we don't see enough of it to get a good understanding nor closure, but we do see enough of it for the reactions of the characters to seem disproportionate and contradict each other.

Having not played Daniel Mullins' previous game famous for a dark mystery twist, Pony Island, I genuinely can't tell whether he went for eerie horror and missed the mark horribly, or whether he was trying to troll his fans with a parody of a "haunted game" plot.

In conclusion

If you want to play a thorough mechanical exploration of a nice card game and don't mind a mediocre story, then Inscryption is a game for you.

However, if you wanted a nice juciy mystery or a mind-boggling plot - feel free to skip it, only frustration awaits you.\t\t\t\t\t
