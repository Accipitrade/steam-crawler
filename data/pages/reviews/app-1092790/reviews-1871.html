\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe core deck-building gameplay is super fun to mess around in and never gets stale because of the steady influx of new mechanics. Just from a strictly gameplay perspective, this game has a ton of of it, all good, often great. The cards are cute, the functions and gimmicks easy to understand and manipulate, some of the music and a lot of the aesthetic of the game is memorable.
Every other gameplay element surrounding the fundamental deck-building gameplay is another matter entirely, and without spoiling anything, truly make for a unique and frankly haunting gaming experience. I will be recommending this game to a lot of my friends, practically forcing them to play it, not because of how good the deck-building aspect is, but because of how mind-blowing the actual game surrounding it can be at times. 8.5/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's difficult to write a review about a game like Inscryption without spoilers. It's a wild ride that is at times terrifying and yet veers away from creepy to become something genre-subverting and unexpected. Designed with a deep knowledge of the card / roguelike genre and its tropes, the worst thing I could probably say about it is that it is so deeply tied to its story that you won't see replay value from it.

The game shifts and upends your expectations. Giving away any more than that would be a disservice. But if you've played and enjoyed a variety of card battlers, you can be sure this will have you opening your eyes to the possibilities of these games. Nothing stays fixed for very long, though the shifting of the rules (almost) never feels too out of hand - it mostly manages a rhythm that leaves you feeling in control of choices and learning from every battle.

A few broad descriptions without ruining the experience:

- A strategic card deck-builder that blazes its own trail. You might have played something like Slay the Spire or Monster Train, and gotten used to a certain build strategy in these games (pumping up rage/attack builders, or spikes that return damage, etc). But Inscryption eschews these for a range of new ideas, quite a few of which I hadn't seen before in any form. I want to write them in but honestly they're better discovered organically. And when the game starts to break its own rules, it can seem like winning is impossible but somehow synergies can emerge without even planning for it.

- Extremely story driven. The opening act feels like you're playing for your life against the Nothing from the Neverending story. A real menace is present and losing has some actual game-influencing consequences. Things never stay fixed for too long, though...

- Bonkers synergies and rule-breaking in later game stages and bosses. It's almost like a demonstration of the designers cutting loose and saying, 'what if -this- was a thing?'.\t\t\t\t\t
