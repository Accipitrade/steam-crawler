\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr it's a pretty fun deckbuilding game with some innovative mechanics, but it is often inconsistent. also some parts of the plot are in my opinion sort of dumb.

PREFACE:

There's an ARG attached to this game. I didn't bother with it. I also have not 100%ed the game.

GRAPHICS:

Inscryption has three kinds of graphics styles which are connected to the gameplay. The first is a 3-D low-poly sort of thing, which I think looks neat and conveys the character designs really well. The second is a 2-D top-down pixel view which isn't mindblowing, but is still nice to look at. The third is an FMV sort of thing where there are real people acting out scenes. I don't really like these, but this is more of a problem with the narrative these scenes are a part of (see the plot section).

The design of the world you're in depends on the section overall. The first part is a log cabin with rusty tools where the game is played mostly on a paper map, the second part has several worlds based on various themes like robots, skeletons, and magic, and the third is some sort of factory. The third part is the worst graphically, in my opinion because the "playing area" is almost entirely blue which makes distinguishing things hard.

PERFORMANCE:

The game lagged quite a bit during the 3-D sections, but it never crashed.

GAMEPLAY (COMBAT)

The game is a deckbuilder. The amount of control you have over your deck varies; initially your deck is not saved between deaths, but you cannot remove cards from the deck and you are at the mercy of RNG to get good ones. Then, you get near complete control over your deck where you can choose what is in it (in this part you don't lose anything, including decks when you die). Then after that, for the rest of the game you cannot remove cards from the deck minus a few moments but your deck doesn't reset when you die. It's fairly inconsistent. Personally, I though the first system was best given the roguelike nature of the game, with the last system being the worst, because it seems to only retain the annoying parts of a roguelike.

The game throws some novelties at you depending on where you are in the game, though broadly sacrifice seems to be a theme. This is strongest in the first part of the game where your deck is entirely built around sacrificing animals for mana cost, and you can remove teeth or cut out your eye to deal damage. The last part of the game sort of does this with one boss mechanic where you can turn a file on your computer into a card and it gets sent to the recycle bin if it dies, but obviously you can just recover it (you get an achievement if you delete the file forever from the bin).

Aside from sacrificing animals, the later parts of the game allow you to get mana through either bones (where any time a creature dies it creates mana) gems (where you have to place a weak creature on the battle field) or energy (where you just get it based on the turn). The second third lets you experiment with styles, where some cards relate to both mana types, but the third third of the game forces you to use the energy system, retaining a few small parts of the gem system, which is fine, but boring.

The first part of the game has a system called "death cards" where when you die you can basically merge three of your cards together into a card for future runs. This is really cool but it vanishes after the first third of the game, mainly because the death mechanics change.

GAMEPLAY (OTHER):

The 3-D sections of the game let you get up and explore the room the game is being played in. You can find some secrets like new cards or item types (some of which are seriously overpowered or ridiculous - there's one that turns every empty slot into a bomb) by solving puzzles, and the game encourages this by making you get up from the table to advance the plot (usually by finding an item for the gamemaster). It's a cool innovation, and it's a sort of logical consequence of realizing deckbuilding videogames are based on real, actual card games where you can get up from the real life table and walk around for toilet breaks or whatever. I'd say that the 2-D section is weakest entirely because you don't get to do this.

Some of the mandatory puzzles are good too. There's one where you have to look at your settings to find a password.

The way the map works over time changes as the story progresses. At first, you constantly go forward by selecting points in the map where stuff happens (you fight a battle, you sacrifice a card, etc.). The 2-D section works like a JRPG where there's an overworld and you walk to points on that overword to explore the sub-words. In the third section, you move from room to room by clicking on arrows but you can click on things in the rooms to get items or upgrades. The third section was hardest to navigate, mostly because of a lack of distinguishing markers and everything being the same color, as mentioned above.

PLOT:

The game seems to be an homage to those old creepypastas where someone finds a haunted videogame attached to some tragedy and then the game starts bleeding into reality (think Sonic.exe or Ben Drowned). It's tagged as a horror but I'd probably call it a dark comedy based on the aforementioned creepypastas, because it seems to lean into the tropes in a very over the top manner, especially in the third half where the villain makes what is basically a lenny face while revealing his master plan to take over the game world. This is where the FMV comes in, because the FMVs form the "frame narrative" of the game, which is about a card collector finding a videogame (the one you play in the actual gameplay sections) by digging in the woods. I've read a bit about the ARG and it seems to be even more insane. The over-the top nature of the FMV things doesn't seem to work very well with the rest of the narrative, which is suprisingly grounded, absent the meta-"it knows it's a videogame" aspects, which is mostly about fighting against your gamemaster, whoever that may be.\t\t\t\t\t

This is the Best Game of 2021

Inscryption is one of those games I was going to shrug off near the end of 2021. But after the game started to gain popularity and Markiplier began his Let's Play of the game, I decided to play it for myself and I instantly realized why many people were hyping up this game. It never shows you its full hand; and that's the best part about it. Just when you think you've finished the entire game, you're actually just scratching the very surface.

The story begins ominously in a dark cabin alone with man with a maniacal love of card games. It's up to you to build up your deck and beat him at his own game. But if you fail, he kills you, causing you to restart from the beginning. The lore of Inscryption is extremely complex and even has its own ARG attached on top of the actual game itself. Don't expect to have lore thrown at you either, this game encourages exploration and finding secrets. The mechanics in the game become more and more complex as the game goes on, adding on the difficulty and the flexibility of what you can do. The art-style this game as is as beautiful as it is off-putting and blends together perfectly with the atmosphere. The music is mystifying and helps with the horror aspects. The characters are what make this game so uncanny and unique, My favorite character is by far, the Stoat. His smart-ass demeanor and cute design really made me love and hate the little bastard! One things I disliked about the game is that the game heavily relies on RNG. Just like games such as Darkest Dungeons, many parts of Inscryption are randomly generated every single time you die, with major exceptions in mid and late game. That being said, you'll have to be cautious with every choice you make. The puzzles in the game also are a minor complaint. Many of the puzzles are fairly challenging but there's a minority of them fall under being too easy or too cryptic.

Overall, 2021 hasn't had many interesting games that could really get my attention but Inscryption took that and subverted my expectations of what separates a good game from a great one. If you're still reading my review, do yourself a favor and buy this game immediately. Don't look at the trailers or watch someone else play it first. You NEED to experience this game as blind as possible for the best experience.

9/10 It really makes you feel like a Stoat
