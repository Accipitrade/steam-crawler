Cabin in the Woods
I've beaten the demo twice in anticipation, which never happened to me before. I recommend checking it out first, naturally. What can you reliably expect from the full game? An elaborate meta-narrative in the finest tradition of The Hex and Pony Island. Prepare to feel sorry for cards, experience empathy towards villains, find peace in failure, and doubt in victory. That and quite a few hours of engrossing gameplay I promise, but everything else in this story is subject to change.

It begins in a cabin in the woods where you're held hostage by Leshy who makes you play the game that uses his living cards. Leshy is a complicated creature from the Slavic mythology who, I think, got a reasonably authentic representation here. This is more or less how he looks, and what he does, or would do nowadays, also isn't that far off - beasts and mind games are definitely his field of expertise. If he reminds you of a grabby homeless man, well, this is because, most likely, some legendary hermits were at the root of the myth, to begin with.

NASCAR||Formula 1
It's completely unique for a card game to put such strong accents on its setting, characters, and atmosphere. I would cautiously compare this bold approach to gems like Ring of Pain and Hand of Fate when it comes to humor and personality. Beautiful in all of its manifestations, the goodness starts off being soaked in a bizarre, stylish, almost bleak fairy-tale atmosphere reminiscent of Darkwood. These are surface-level comparisons, a feeble image in relation to the full picture, but I don't mind - it's a firm hook.

People ask ignorant questions like "is it better than Monster Train?", and to me, it's like taking a Formula 1 bolide and saying that "it's better than a NASCAR car". I mean, they're both cars, they share a lot of elements that can be compared, right? But anyone would understand how inappropriate a direct juxtaposition of these two things is. Likewise, these games have different goals and use different means to achieve them. I, for one, always turned 180 degrees, built up speed, and then rammed into everyone in my NASCAR simulators.

"A ghastly spectacle but the soul of the wolf cub now lives in the adder."
Inscryption is a deckbuilder where you'll be playing unlimited cards per round on a grid and sometimes examine your surrounding to solve puzzles like it's The Room. These are the only constant mechanics, the connective tissue, but I won't be going into detail. Suffice to say that I found the core card game as well as the puzzles extremely enjoyable. Besides, the game teaches you its mechanics gently and patiently, you can always click RMB to get a tip on just about anything and check the Rulebook at any time.

The enemy doesn't necessarily adhere to the exact same rules as you do and I loved how thematically coherent it is. Battles usually start with your adversary having a few cards on the board, bosses have more lives than you are allowed. So, don't expect a fair fight, it's an abusive relationship where you're a pawn doomed to sacrifice your brethren - spill their blood and conjure their bones. Leshy gets all he wants for free because he's in control. Sometimes it may seem unfair, so I've prepared a word of advice next.

The game has so much to offer, don't give up. If you think it's too hard for you early on, just pray for a milk run. The first act is the hardest part of the game, but it's balanced in a way that makes breaking it easy if you pay attention. Counter-intuitively, cannon fodder is valuable, don't dismiss weak creatures. In droves, they will be very useful on most of the bosses, including the "last" one. Bosses tend to destroy/transform/steal your first wave of troops, so best make them ants, rabbits, and bees. Finally, always go for Mantis Gods, all game long.

We've Come a Long Way, Psycho Mantis
What happens after the first act? You have to see it to believe it, or just play enough games from this developer to already foresee something like that. And I don't mean it in a derogatory sense, this isn't some mundane ass-pulling - even when you know the twist is coming, it's gonna be a clever surprise anyway when these people do it. After Inscryption, I was left wallowing in ambivalence. I'm not so sure if I've beaten it, I'm so lost that I don't even know if I've won. And I love it! Or do I?

From now on, any game attempting something like this would have to go completely out of bounds and take serious risks to be on par. But most importantly, within all the brilliant twists and competent use of a great variety of complex meta-schemes, there lies an exceptional game that cuts above and gets you involved through its ingenious gameplay rather than by other, less relevant means. Infinitely commendable. I recommend Inscryption so hard that I almost fell from my chair while writing this.


P.S. I must note that the guy in the videos boasts a killer chin, almost as good as Bruce Campbell's.

P.P.S. Please, add the ability to roam around with a mouse in adventure game mode. This way, the whole game could be played with a mouse, which is important to handicapped people, consistent, and convenient.

The game does kinda rush towards the end with arguably less quality content towards the end of the story. Still a fun game with some neat moments. Replayability still strikes me as something this game lacks, however, there is certainly a set up for more content to be created. I'm not sure what the dev has planned but it was an enjoyable 10 hrs of game, hopefully some more stuff gets thrown in to bring me back in the future.

Initial Review: (6 hours~) The card game, while not ground-breaking, has some neat mechanics that are fun and easy to pick up. The whole card aspect of the game is just fun. Then there's some puzzles thrown in escape room style. None of them are really complicated, but some do require story progression to unlock the "hint" or tool necessary to access them. The art and thematic feel of the game is great, really fits the mood to the story. The music is ok, it's creepy but after a while it started grating on my ears.... chalk that up to preference tho, and it can be turned down so it's not a deal breaker by any means. Lastly, the story is interesting, and pretty well written. It's relatively straight forward/predictable but still deep enough to entertain.

My only concern is replayability. Given the "one and done" style of the puzzles and a mediocre pool of card variety.\t\t\t\t\t
