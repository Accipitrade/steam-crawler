\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the kind of game you have to play blind, making it difficult to explain what you're getting yourself into.
Essentially, it's a roguelike deckbuilder with a presentation similar to Hand of Fate.
You play against a creepy dude, collecting new cards and upgrading existing ones as you go.
If you lose (and you will), you go back and have to try again.
Obviously, there's more to it, but the less you know, the better.

In my opinion, the game started off really strong, then slowly fizzled out as I progressed.
It tried to shake things up, but the way it did just wasn't my cup of tea.
The ending was good though. Sort of.

Definitely worth experiencing.
Just be aware that, even with all its bells and whistles, it's still a card game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI disliked Daniel Mullin's previous games (Pony Island and The Hex), as I found them to be stretched out gimmick games that thought they were far more clever than they actually were. If you're going to parody something, it can't just be a half-baked "bad-on-purpose" echo of the original.

Inscryption is an echo that lives up to its inspirations. While most deckbuilders and incremental games are designed to be played for hundreds of hours it's a 10-hour rollercoaster of rule setup and player mastery. Everything is an escape room, with the player encouraged to find exploits and break things. It also nails its comedic timing.

My only caveats are:
1. There is a metaplot (everything regarding the live action segments). It is bad and doesn't get resolved. You're better off ignoring it.
