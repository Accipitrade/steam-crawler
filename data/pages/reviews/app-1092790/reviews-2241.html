\r\n\t\t\t\t\t Inscryption is a horror card game with puzzles, adventure and mystery woven throughout. It appealed to me first as a card game, and then as a narrative experience. From its Ludlum Dare origin - Sacrifices - we can see that the card game works in principle, and in Inscryption we see it, with fully realised features, carry a more complex narrative.

The core gameplay revolves around a creature-battle card game, with lane based attacking and defending. Usually a placed creature will attack the lane opposite it and deal damage to that creature or to the player if the attack goes unblocked. Resources are limited: sacrifices must be made to field the strongest creatures (with a blood cost), block opponents attacks with creatures (whether they have enough health points to survive or not), and make it through to the next fight by dealing enough damage points to tip the scales. The game board telegraphs the opponents moves - we can see what creatures will do, what battle effects they have, and what will move onto the board next. The player can devise synergies and spot pitfalls in their own deck and in the opponents cards and exploit them.

As the player clicks through the map they will move through different environments with different events in play. They will meet with traders and trappers, hungry-looking survivors, and bloodstained dolmens. An encounter may leave the player stronger or weaker than before in various ways. Items are available to turn the tide of battle - from the Squirrel in a bottle to the Pliers and more besides - whether you need an additional sacrifice to play your Wolf for game, or you would lose without tipping the scales using the pliers. Items can be given after events as a reward or a commiseration, and are picked up readily so the player need not be too precious with them (and perhaps should not).

The adversary is the master of the game, a pair of eyes in the dark, and performs as the NPCs and bosses, introducing creatures and features and narrating different scenes.The shader used in the cabin gives everything a grimy grainy texture - an inky dark pool of shadows where the game master resides, a faltering light from lamps and candles. Card art is simple, single colour (though this changes with enhancements and events) but serves well to illustrate mechanics and themes clearly. From somber and atmospheric music in the cabin, to boss themes, dark buzzing-murmuring voice effects, and visceral rips and tears, music and sound design complement the themes. When the narrative pivots the music and design follows suit, to great effect. When not card-battling the player can get up from the table and move around the cabin, where there are various clues and curios to interact with that may provide cards, currency, or progress the story and add to the lore.

Exploiting the gamemasters design weaknesses is just another way to play the game - we know that some cards and strategies are optimal and edge out other lower tier playstyles entirely. Inscryption isnt complex enough to be competitive (not least because it is designed as a single player game) but the variety is enough that the random deal of cards and pickup options will give some flavour to any deck used. There will be interactions and combinations of enhancements that I had not encountered or considered but I found at least three neat tricks that made for very effective plays and supports. Some were just broken, but required enough in-match/in-run setup that they felt earnt not given.

The main loop is simple enough to start with, and familiar to most roguelike fans - traverse the
spire? the lands and face enemies and bosses while expanding your deck with useful creatures and events on the path towards the final confrontation. As the player fails and tries again, additional elements, resources and outcomes are added, and the players attention is drawn to allies(?) who want to help them break it - and we find that there is more to discover, and that the roguelike loop is not the mainstay of the game.

I would recommend this if you are a fan of metanarrative horror media, digital card games, roguelikes (in that order, after experiencing the game). It does not have the same kind of replayability as other roguey-deckbuilders but offers a level of narrative complexity which warrants further investigation and contemplation. I replayed immediately to catch more of the things I missed. Get this!

8/10 STIMULATING\t\t\t\t\t
