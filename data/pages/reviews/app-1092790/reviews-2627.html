\r\n\t\t\t\t\tQuick Review:

-First act (cabin) is spectacular, but lasts only a couple hours
-Game has huge tone and gameplay shifts and gets worse the further along you are
-Story driven experience with a lackluster and unsatisfying ending
-True ending heavily teased but incredibly obtuse (no one has found it yet)
-Overall, not really worth $20, 8.5 hours in total length including searching for secrets.

________


I wish there was a thumbs sideways option - I'm choosing thumbs down in hopes people seeking a real review find this one.

The game starts off phenomenally, with a great atmosphere and an increasing complex card game. The plot is simple: you're trapped in a cabin with a crazy man, and he's forcing you to play a card game. Some of the cards talk to you, and there's puzzles in the room to solve. Unfortunately, Inscyrption would be a far more compelling purchase if it stopped there. After only a couple hours into the game the tone and gameplay shift dramatically.

A new world opens up, and all of the characters and questions you had from the cabin are answered in a satisfactory manner. However, this is the last time the game will give you a satisfactory answer.

The greatest detraction is the fact that you aren't the player. It's a character named Luke, and you're controlling him. This really destroys the mood and detracts from the amazing atmosphere which existed in act I. He'll comment on things just uncommonly enough for you to forget he exists, and it pulls you out of the game every time. There's also a ton of extreme tonal shifts in the third act which are questionable (i.e. horror game, but occasionally some bounty hunters show up speaking like a 1950's idea of a cowboy).

There's an overarching story but it's done in a way that's intentionally extremely vague. It's essentially a creepypasta, so if you enjoy that genre you'll probably like the game. I don't and thought the story was lacking. Basically, Luke (you) dig up an old floppy disk haunted by the ghost of it's programmer. The ending is crap and doesn't answer any major questions.

Let's address the elephant in the room: there is clearly a true ending, but no one knows how to get it. I consider that to be bad design, as a real game would allow the player to discover it on their own. Maybe the story is actually tied up nicely, but at this time it's unknown. I can say that apparently current clues include an alternate reality game involving a youtube channel set up for the game and the developers previous titles, so I can conclude it's impossible to figure out by yourself in a reasonable time. I also think it's a bit scummy to require the player to play previous games to understand the final puzzle.

Overall, I don't think the game is worth $20. Funnily enough, if the game stuck to Act I with the incredible atmosphere and just was a rougelike card game I would be far happier with it. Maybe the true ending will change my mind, but for now I'd call this a youtube game - it's best experienced by watching videos and not by playing it yourself.\t\t\t\t\t
