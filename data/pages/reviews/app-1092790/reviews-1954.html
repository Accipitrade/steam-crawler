\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you want a quick and unjust tagline for Inscryption?
The Blair Witch Project meets Yu-Gi-Oh.

This is an important roguelike deckbuilder. Its the most innovative take on the genre I’ve enjoyed in years.

The tutorial is fairly paced. You learn that you’re interacting with unreliable tutors ready to manipulate the board in their favor. Once you’ve progressed past the opening stage, the point-and-click aspects begin. Fights come fast and often. You’ll spend five rounds or fewer in each, so the frequency doesn’t bog down your pace and enjoyment. Take breaks. Get up from the table. Puzzles are in every corner of the room. Some reward you with resources, the others with lore. Every detail matters and experimentation is rewarded. Investment in the game and your attention is critical to solving puzzles and progressing. Puzzles can also satisfyingly be brute forced.

Playing Inscryption treats you to a mix of media and meta-twisting art choices. We start with low poly heavily atmospheric 3D graphics. While being spoiler sensitive, I can mention that the art style moves through interactive found footage and nostalgic non-isometric pixel art.

Please play this. We have consistently disappointing AAA releases. Indie studios have relentlessly pushed gameplay, artistic stylings, and conceptions of what constitutes games and interactive media. Inscryption is the most fun I’ve had with deck building since the Slay The Spire. It’s a great use of your time and you’ll never forget the twists and brain teasers along the way.
