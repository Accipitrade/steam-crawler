\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a truncated review to fit Steam's character limits. This review was also written before the Kaycee's Mod release. Full review is at Dragon Quill. Comments are enabled there.

Inscryption is an attempt at metafiction that falls completely flat because it’s a story about playing a person playing a haunted video game, not actually playing a haunted video game yourself. In general, I found the metafiction segments tedious and uninteresting interruptions to the actual game. This is a shame, because the gameplay is actually very good.

When you first load up the game, the “New Game” option on the title menu is glitched out, but “Continue” is available. Pressing this places you in a dark cabin across from a creepy dude completely hidden by shadow, who wants you to play a game with him. This game takes the form of a series of card duels, with the following twist: In order to play cards, you must sacrifice cards already on the field and pay for the new card with their blood. The only cards you can play freely are useless squirrels, which have no attack power and only 1 hit point. Some cards are instead paid for in bones, which are generated whenever one of your creatures dies for any reason. To make this even creepier, some of your cards talk and react to the choices you make.

These sacrifice mechanics are actually the thing that got me interested in the game, as it was mentioned in a game design series I follow. It goes beyond just the cards to permeate everything in the game: In between card duels, you encounter nodes on the map that allow you to empower one of your cards by permanently sacrificing another; your “healing” items are a pair of pliers and a sacrificial dagger you can use to rip out your teeth or eye to weight the victory scale; and so on. I thought it did a great job of injecting an unsettling horror into something as innocuous as card games, something horror media often tries to do but fails at.

Additionally, you can at any point actually get up from the game table and explore the game master’s cabin; though you’ll quickly find there’s no escape, there are a number of environmental puzzles to solve, often using information obtained in the “main” game, which in turn give you additional cards and bonuses to use in the duels. I found this a really interesting mechanic that emphasized the bizarre artificiality of the card game and aided in immersion — we can feel the protagonist’s helplessness in how they can, technically, walk away from the game at any time, yet they can still only push against their restraints so much.

All of this was really, genuinely enjoyable to me. It’s a fun, interesting roguelike with a delicious undercurrent of horror and tons of secrets and mysteries to uncover.

It’s also only a third of the actual game.

See, after you beat the game master, the metanarrative gets its grubby hands over everything. Turns out you’re actually playing as a trading card enthusiast named Luke Carder, who found the game’s disc buried in the woods outside his house yada yada you see where this is going, it’s a haunted video game creepypasta. No, I was actually enjoying the creepy video game, thanks, please let me play it instead of making me watch your painfully cliche indie film. The rest of the game is completely different and largely ditches the horror aesthetics.

I won't get into too many details here, but I found the ending extremely underwhelming. We don’t even get an actual reveal on what’s so scary about the ~spooky haunted video game~ unless you also follow the related ARG, so the game just ends on a whimper. (Also, the big reveal at the end of the ARG? Satan did it! Which is the "a wizard did it" of horror. Wow, utterly uncritical and vanilla reference to your inescapably dominant religion, much horror, very scary.) Horror writers, I get that you feel very clever having read TV Tropes’ article on “Nothing Is Scarier“, but that’s not an excuse to not write a resolution. “Nothing” by itself is not actually scary; you need to provide the shape around that nothing to give us some idea to speculate on.

So essentially, I think I can describe this as an utterly uninspired bargain-bin creepypasta story that for some reason had an actually really good game built as a tie-in. This would have been better if the first act had just been a full game, or even if they made each act into a full game instead of their ideas getting discarded just as they were picking up steam.\t\t\t\t\t
