\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter I beat the first section of Inscryption and I was watching the videos with The Lucky Carder, I remember saying to my friend “This game can be succinctly summed up as ‘a love letter to card games’”. Having finished the game now, played through Kaycee’s Mod a little bit, and thought about it for some weeks more, I can confidently say my original impression holds true. Inscryption is the ultimate love letter to card games, and to a small extent table games generally.

I can’t be super illuminating in this review because, as near countless others have mentioned before me, this is a game which is best enjoyed with as little knowledge prior as possible, but there are some aspects which I’d like to talk about.

The first thing that stands out in Inscryption is the atmosphere. From moment one the game’s theming and atmosphere is set in stone. And it rocks it in all ways. The cards, the abilities, the puzzles, the opponent, the bosses, the music (god the music!). Everything. It’s all perfectly bundled together and each part is reinforced by the others at all times.

And then Inscryption has the absolute biggest cojones in the world because as soon as you start to feel like a god it just flips it all around on you. Incredible stuff.

Everything is spectacular, including the difficulty for the most part (although twice I was ”too soon”d and it left a bit of a sour taste in my mouth). However, Inscryption’s biggest fault is that the first section of the game is simply the best! And in my mind, although I love the rest and think it’s notable and enjoyable, the first section is untouchable in my eyes.

I’m less bothered by this knowing that it was likely a strategic choice to make that part the first section because it’s the most unique and gripping, but I am also less bothered because the developers smartly realized that the initial section is so good it deserved its own infinite roguelite and Kaycee’s Mod is a very satisfying way to quench that thirst.

Special praise is deserved for Kevin Saxby, who either was/is a tabletop gamer himself or studied a lot about what makes us love card games. If you told me Kevin Saxby was not a card gamer himself I wouldn’t believe you. His performance is perfect, and that’s also a credit to anyone who worked on the script because it is perfect as well. Kevin does a fantastic job, though. One of the best for me in recent gaming memory.

My issues in Inscryption are few but were ones I encountered a number of times throughout my run. Number one is the camera while at the moment of deciding what to draw is less intuitive than I would like, and there are times in the game when you cannot appropriately see the scale and thus not have the best information possible when making your draw.

The other issue is one of inconsistencies of being able to decommit from actions. At certain points, like when you can sacrifice cards for bonuses, after initial selection you get one final chance to take back the card, but at other times you don’t, and the game isn’t exactly clear about when that is or why that is, and it can be a bit dissatisfying.

Overall though Inscryption was probably the best game I played in 2022 and wasn’t really close, either. 2022 was not a stellar year for me as far as the games I played, but Inscryption would be competing for the top spot in almost any year of my gaming life. Inscryption is a transcendentally good game, and I recommend it to anybody that has even the slightest interest in TCGs/CCGs/Tabletop games or even roguelites generally.

9.5/10\t\t\t\t\t

Is it worth full price? I would say yes, somewhat so. It felt a little easy for my taste and I was able to plow through most of the puzzles with relative ease. I would wait for this game to go on sale, but if you really want it, it is worth it's salt. The soundtrack, overall character design, and the ruleset of the game all make for an overall amazing, if sometimes frustrating experience.\t\t\t\t\t
