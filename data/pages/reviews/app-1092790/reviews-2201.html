\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis review begins with a preamble that, despite its utmost objective earnestness, will likely be interpreted as a “conventionally lazy cop-out,” even under the most forgiving circumstances. And so, the conundrum begins... the challenge of reviewing Inscryption — or, really, anything from the Daniel Mullins Games catalog — is two-fold:

1) As the game is best experienced without foreknowledge of any sort, a thorough examination — either abstract or conceptual — would necessarily rob gameplay of story-telling effect and thematic surprise...

2) Any singularly- or literally-focused evaluation of the game’s chimera-like assemblage of disparate elements would be insufficient in capturing the manner in which the amalgamated-adventure transcends its baser sum.

More difficult, still, is offering a spoiler-free description of the game’s final act that adequately conveys how this simple, indie deck-builder establishes a sense of frank and unfettered terror: providing the player a grim, barefaced glimpse into the yawning maw of never as we — the helplessly sentient — embrace the existential scramble for… something... Anything… while the inexorable swirl of blinding aught pulls us into its final, irrevocable nothingness.

Zero stars — there are no lights in shadowed corners, industrial chasms, or abject oblivion...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption came out of nowhere and absolutely floored me. It's certainly my favorite game that's come out of this year that I've played, and one of only a handful of games I would push pretty much everyone to play with no real reservations. Although a deck-builder is the best term to describe it, it also does little justice. At the same time are puzzles in your environment, an overarching metanarrative that I won’t spoil, and more. All throughout, the game will throw you in on so many loops, some of which change almost everything you think you know about it. That includes mechanics, story, presentation, and so on, all in tandem with each other, while keeping the same solid and well-constructed undercurrent. It genuinely surprised me, in a time where I don't really get surprised by games anymore. And the transformations were so total that I was deeply engaged with the deck-building gameplay the entire time, even though I was never interested in the genre before. It kept me on my toes and needing to adapt to new scenarios, and are some of the most creative game design decisions I've ever seen. But of course, the card gameplay is intended to reflect where you’re at in the story, which itself was an emotional rollercoaster. Some moments I was really creeped out by a card game. Others got me laughing harder than I can remember from a game actively trying to be funny. And the ending had me on the verge of tears. It was a wild ride that I never saw coming. I think that with Inscryption, Dan Mullins has cemented himself as one of the most innovative and versatile indie devs out there. This is a truly excellent game, and deserves the self-described title of a love letter to video games, since I think it really does represent the best the medium has to offer in all its aspects.\t\t\t\t\t
