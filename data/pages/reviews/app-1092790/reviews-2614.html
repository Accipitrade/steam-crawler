The best I can say, balancing informativeness with discretion, is that while Inscryption is, as the Steam store page and promotional media suggest, a deck building card battler game with a horror theme, it is also significantly more than that. This game has earned its "Overwhelmingly Positive" review rating.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOkay, this review will have two sections to it, a non-spoiler version and a spoiler version.

Non-Spoiler Review:
The gameplay is really good, excellent mechanics that are all satisfying to play in a card game, and the whole escape room and puzzle vibes to some of it is really good.
The Story though, starts strong I think, but then it sort of falls flat towards the end. I was able to follow along I think, but it left me with a mix of questions that I think should of been answered at the end, but wasn't, as well as a feeling of true confusion.
That aside, I would still heavily recommend this game, just try not to get too heavily attached to the story.

The Spoiler Filled Review:
Okay, so again, the gameplay is great. The card game is solid and I love the fact that you can not only build a deck that synergizes well, but also can generate some really broken cards if you know what you are doing. The puzzles and escape room vibe builds into this as by doing them you can unlock more cards to collect in the game, items, or other things that otherwise could help you in each chapter (I consider this game to split up into 3 chapters. The first chapter is everything inside the log cabin which you can see images and videos of on the store page. The second chapter has this old 8 or 16 bit rpg sort of feel to it with no escape room but an open world (NOTE: this is where the rogue like aspects cease to be). The third chapter has you in a new escape room, but instead of the log cabin, is a robot factory, and still maintains some of the free roam feel, but only in the card game. To note, cards can have a cost (blood, bones, whatever), attack power, health, and some emblems which give the cards special properties (such as an emblem with spikes meaning that if it's attacked, the enemy takes 1 point of damage as well).
In Chapter 1: You have a rogue like fee to the card game (you make 2 mistakes, you die, have a death card generated based on what's in your deck, and are made to start from the beginning), and an escape room filled log cabin. To progress to Chapter 2, you have to actually complete the puzzles around the log cabin and then beat the roguelike card game's final boss. The only types of cards you have to deal with either cost blood or cost bones. You have two decks for this chapter, a main deck which contains your more important cards, and a side deck which contains squirrel (1 hp, 0 attack, cost nothing). Your main deck can contain as many cards as you collect on your travels. You can only draw from 1 deck 1 card a turn, so between that and the items, you have to think about your turn and the next few turns. This is honestly the strongest section of the game both gameplay wise and story wise. I love it.
In Chapter 2: The rogue like ends, and instead you're left to free roam. You are told about the four sages that rule the world, and are introduced slightly to the other 3 decks that didn't appear in the first chapter. Robots, Mages, and Undead. Mages require mana crystals be placed on the board to be played, but the mana crystals buff their health and attack power. Robots try to encourage the player to build circuits with their cards, and by doing so can buff each other (they also cost energy which like Hearthstone, your max energy increases by 1 per turn). The undead deck is a rush style of deck, and only costs bones. At the start of this chapter you are told to pick one starter deck from one of the four sages. For this chapter, there is only your main deck, consisting of a max of 20 cards; it will tell you how to select what cards go into the deck the first time you attempt to challenge someone. Once you beat all four of the sages, then return to the starting area to challenge the final boss of the chapter, the final boss gets interrupted and you move into Chapter 3.
In Chapter 3: You are now in the robot factory, the mechanics from chapter 2 will still come into play, but you are limited to robot style of cards. For this chapter you have a main deck (containing your general important cards, and can contain as many cards as you collect), and your side deck that contains Empty Vessels (these might seem like just beefed up squirrels at 2 hp, 0 attack in the beginning, but they get better as you progress) The card game still has the same general map layout as chapter 2 as well. You are again challenged to go about the map, challenging instead of the sages, some super powered robots. After beating each boss (other than the robot factory one), your side deck of Empty Vessels will then be given an emblem of your choosing (I think out of a subset of 3 randomly selected ones). In addition you will also unlock the ability to purchase special emblems relating to the zone you just completed back at the shop in the center of the map. If you win enough battles, you will gain a star rating, meaning that in some of your future fights, a special "bounty hunter" card may appear to shake things up. The higher your bounty, the more difficult the bounty hunter card is to deal with.
Special Note: One of the bosses of chapter 3 actually gave me a sense of dread, as it asked for an old file off my computer to examine, and then generated a card from it for the match. It specified that if I played it and it died, then the game would attempt to delete the associated file. For those that don't know, the oldest files that naturally occur on your machine are in the Win32 folder for Windows. I had better options to play at the time, so I didn't put it to the test. However, if it had actually deleted something *from that folder* I would of been livid regardless of if it was just a language related file or not.

Now, the story part:
Ugh. Okay, let me first note, there are two layers to what's going on in the game. First there's the in game (IG) portion, and then there's the real life (RL) portion. The IG portion is what you actually play through, and experience as a player. While the RL portion is a series of video clips you get access to at certain points in the game, in order to show you how this game was found, and what's going on to the person who found it as a result. Sort of to try and imply a number of things about the game's origins and why it's "oh so taboo" that it exists.
I really liked the story in the first chapter, it was clean and self contained. After you manage to beat the final boss of chapter 1 though, they try to bring in these video logs of "the Lucky Carder", a card game streamer in that world that opens card packs on stream and focuses on building decks. At first I thought that was pretty cool, where are they going to take it? As you go on through the chapters, talk to NPCs, collect all the collectables, so on, you learn that the company that worked on Inscryption's IRL card game (in that fictional world) might be run by the devil, and has some dirty secrets buried on the video game floppy disk that The Lucky Carder found in the woods. It is implied that you are playing as the Lucky Carder. The IG NPCs are some what sentient, and retain memory between games. Some where along the line, the Lucky Carder IRL starts having some agent from the card company start pestering him over the missing floppy disk (because he emailed them), and he ultimately winds up dead. Some where in chapter 2 or 3, it becomes a confusing mess to follow, and the theming becomes a mess as they throw horror some what out the window. While I know what's happening, the clues don't mesh well together for it to be satisfying.
The story has so many conflicting themes in it, it's really not even funny. It just became a confusing mess in the end. Again however, I would still recommend this game, the gameplay is solid, just try not to think on the story too much.\t\t\t\t\t
