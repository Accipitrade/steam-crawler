\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is incredible.

I am not normally a fan of deckbuilders, and I often will grimly tolerate roguelite, but within the genre, that sometimes feels like a ploy to stretch out a thin game a little farther.

Not this game.

It is difficult to say too much about this game, as most everything about it is spoilable, but I will do my best. Inscryption starts as a traditional deckbuilder roguelite, with the joy of the game coming from explorations of the mechanics of each stop on the map. Learning what cards synergize and how to maximize boons and prevent catastrophic damage from a bad round. It's very good, with a lot of depth. There are multiple styles of deck, e.g Bird, Elk, Wolf, etc, which can be thought of as families of cards with similar playstyles. In addition, map events range from transposing attribute sigils from one card to another, to deleting cards from the deck for benefits.

This is not the whole game, though. This is the part where I must tread lightly. The game begins to zoom out, steadily, taking wider looks at itself, each time representing fundamental shifts in style and genre. It begins to dabble in escape room style puzzles, which loop themselves back into the original card game. It zooms out to contain fmv sections, telling a metanarrative about playing the game itself. This pattern repeats, growing more intense and purposeful each time.

I will not detail the mechanics of the spoilable sections, save to say that the game is very respectful of your time, which is a feature I find rare with many contemporary titles. The game is so jam packed with momentum that I finished the game in just a few days, playing for hours on end with few breaks, losing myself for hours at a time.

The ending is as powerful as the rest of the game, leaving me feeling afraid, happy, sad, and remorseful all at once.

Bravo; innovative and powerful. Pick it up, you won't regret it.\t\t\t\t\t
