\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI bought this at 18€. I was uncertain if I would like it, but due to the nature of the game and the overwhelmingly positive reviews, I bought it with an open mindset.

It's not a bad game. If there was another way to rate the game I'd give it 5-6/10. I'm pretty sure I've missed some underlining revelations and insights that other players have dug into, but for me, the game overstayed its welcome. I played it for 16.6 hours, not all actively, to complete the story, and enjoyed the first 5-7 hours moderately. The mechanics are decent and the setting is sort of interesting, but it's just not enough for me to recommend this.
The game changes its nature during the course - some bits better, some worse. In my opinion, the game is seriously overhyped. For those who enjoy a bit of mystery, 'meta gaming' and the devs previous instalments, it's probably worth the investment in time and money. For me, it was a mediocre experience and for 18€, it gets a thumbs-down.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn this review, I’ll go over the game one step at a time, to keep a better overview. Spoilers ahead!
The game starts within a spooky cabin, a glowing pair of eyes your opponent, with a pair of hands occasionally coming from the darkness to either explain, interact with the game… or end it. The cabin itself contains riddles, and several advantages for the actual card game itself to be found. Everything is atmospheric, and I love it. The gameplay isn’t shabby either, with simple designed card, and the premise that almost every one of them requires sacrifices to be played, a mechanic that is both easy to learn, and hard to master. Between rounds of the actual card game, the player looks upon an overworld map, allowing him to plan ahead, and make decisions about what events to go to, gaining new cards, getting rid of old ones to gain an advantage, and more. The first act resembles the typical card-based roguelike, although it’s certainly the best within its genre. It’s simple enough to not make it frustrating, and complex enough that you constantly feel like you are learning and improving. Additionally, I can’t praise enough how bolt and unique of a decision it was to combine horror and cards in a single game, a decision that makes this chapter than out within the genre, and what originally drew me towards the game.
You read it correctly, it only made that chapter stand out as starting with chapter 2, it is gone. As is almost everything I just praised. Chapter 2 introduces a ‘new game’, available after unlocking the ‘new game’ option. Graphically, it looks like a classical pixelated game, and tells the story of how the player character is on a journey to replace one of the 4 scriptors, which are Leshy (the glowing pair of eyes from before), and 3 people that had their souls locked in playing cards in act 1. To be frank, at this point I could not care less about this bit of story, and mostly kept going because I wanted more gameplay like I was presented with in act 1. Instead, several mechanics are introduced, or removed. Taken away were all of the ‘planning ahead’ steps I previously praised, and added were 2 new mechanics concerning the handling of the cards within matches themselves. Further, the player gained full control over the composition of his deck. As getting your deck where you wanted it to be was a big part of planning previously, this made the game far too easy. In total, this part is pretty forgettable.
Speaking of which, act 3 is back to first person, with a little environment to explore. Sadly, this new environment doesn’t present the same amount of escape room mechanics, but that point, I’m willing to let go. What I’m not willing to let go is the forceful switch to a simpler mechanic for the actual cardgame. From now on, a battery is in control over how many cards you can play, with 1 power available on turn 1, 2 on turn 2, and so on. It is a staple of the sub-genre, that has been seen and done before, and usually makes a game too easy. Sadly, this does not change here. Due to this, you are never out of options, and can always play at Least 1 card, which makes the game less strategic. Map navigation has become a chore, as you can only see one slot at a time. It’ not a hinderance or difficult, but it Is quite annoying. By itself, this is fine. It does not hold up to the standard the first act set, but we have not touched upon my biggest issues with this act yet. The first bigger issue is, that from now on, every single area introduces a new mechanic to the actual card part of the game. They are either inspired by the scriptors from the previous game, give some of your cards a boost, or introduce some energy conduction mechanic. It boils down to cards looking more complicated, but don’t change the game too much. You need to watch your enemies plans, but they don’t shake things up as much as they are supposed to. Ignore them on your side entirely, and you’ll be fine; Were you to focus on min-maxing your use of every mechanic however, you would certainly be overwhelmed by how much there is to watch out for, which also nulls any usefulness. The difficulty is sadly further reduced, so as to validate this design decision. My second large issue is how meta (in terms of ‘how to make a game creepy’) the game becomes here. This game being from the same developers as pony island, I had expected that a little of it would be present. I have no issue with a game going a little meta in this regard, and it greatly fit the atmosphere of Pony island, when a steam friend suddenly sent me a message in a language he didn’t speak, confusing me greatly… a brilliant use of a 4th wall break. Here however, almost every boss breaks the 4th wall. One boss used my steam friends as his cards, another asked that I use the data on my hard drive to play. Yet another wanted me to design the boss battle, which was only a very minor meta moment, but also made the fight very predictable. Unlike Pony Island, this destroyed what atmosphere had remained. With me as a person being involved, it ruined the setup of me being the finder of the game, introduced in segments between acts. Yet in other parts, I’m addressed as ‘James’, who Is the character from said segments. The game can’t seem to decide if I should be James or myself, ruining immersion in the process. To make it clear: I’m not saying that the 4th wall break, and use of me as a person couldn’t have worked. I Have seen it work, and would have likely metaphorically crapped myself, if Leshy would have pulled this on me… but the way it was used, it was detrimental to the atmosphere, rather than being constructive.
At the end of act 3, one of the scriptors decides to delete the game, leading you to play with 3 of them again, as they prepare for death. I only cared about one of them. Leshy, whom just wants to play, doesn’t care about scores anymore, and just genuinely cares for the cardgame, which was what lead him to make his act so immersive. Him reaching out and shaking my hand as he died was the only bit of story that interested me since act one, as he clearly enjoyed our game together as much as I had. The rest just didn’t matter. I didn’t care. It was to wrap up the story that was only begun on act 2, which I already said I didn’t care for.
Let’s touch upon one last issue I have. That one is the ‘Lucky Card Gamer’ part, which is a series of segments presented between acts. I think it’s meant to make the player go dig around for some ‘outside of the game’-lore, but if I’m honest, I bought this game because I want to play it. Not because I want to go on a treasure hunt for some obscure lore that has nothing to do with it. As such, I thought the segments were annoying and immersion breaking. Frankly, if these parts wouldn’t have been there, and I would have been addressed by whatever steam name I have, the 4th wall breaks would have worked a lot better. But as is, this is a lot of what dragged the game down.
In total, the game could have been much more than it ended up being. It could have been just a psychological horror card game, and it would have been great! Just Leshy, tormenting you in peace. Going ‘you’re the first person to finish this campaign! Let’s do another!’ in full enthusiasm after beating act 1, and presenting new boards, bosses, and cards… maybe even new puzzles in the cabin, or a few of the 4th wall breaks sprinkled in, as the player and Leshy keep playing together, and the player suffering under it. Instead, the developed went and got too ‘fancy’ with the game, and put so much ‘complexity’ into the story, that it utterly ruined everything the game had going for itself. Every effort was taken to make the game creepy in a very meta way after the 1st act, which sadly did the exact opposite, and ruined everything there was.
Don’t get me wrong, I LOVE the first act… But a quarter of the game being fantastic still doesn’t justify a double digit price. Sadly, I can’t recommend it because of this.
