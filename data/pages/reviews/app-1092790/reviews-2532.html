\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDaniel Mullins first released Pony Island, a bad game from a time when internet culture was just insufferable. Then he released the Hex, a much, much better game that is actually pretty great and has inspirations and satires of other cool games, from street fighter all the way to the beginners guide. So we had Daniel's bad, and his good, now we have his 'fine'. It's alright.

I was pretty compelled by the story, but sometimes the story takes a major back seat to the subplots/gameplay so they can run their course organically, which sort of creates a somewhat randomly long or short time frame in-between story beats where it feels like nothing is going on plot wise, you're just playing the same game you were when the story was happening and making the gameplay seem a lot more important. Kinda boring by contrast.

There's a lot of randomness in the game not just in how the gameplay works fight to fight but actually in how the whole experience will go for you for the next couple hours depending on some arbitrary decisions. The most egregious was halfway through the game you are given 4 choices and if you pick wrong you'll be stuck playing a game you don't want to having had no indication of what you were choosing before you did. That's a total drag and yeah I am someone who drew wrong there.

The story however is pretty good, it hits home if you are somewhat interested in game design or making your own games towards the end as really it's a story about games, a very mature one that we've needed for a long time about the medium itself. Maybe this is because I 'made the wrong choices' or didn't figure out one of the puzzles along the way or something but it does feel like certain subplots/side characters go unresolved and it leaves a feeling of wanting more. Can't say i really gave a ♥♥♥♥ about luke honestly . There are a lot of characters we only saw a glmpse which maybe upon replaying the game we'd get to see more of but thats a big ask with such a long entrance.

It's a good game, it's definitely a flawed experience and there are some things I wish Mullins restrained themselves on doing and yet a lot of stuff that seems neglected, but you should still play it.\t\t\t\t\t

You will like this game if (pick any):
- You like good card rogulike games with interesting mechanics.
- You like indie games with a lot of cool little details and features.
- You like these games and similar to them: Pony Island, Hex, Undertale.
- You just want to play a good indie game.

I think the game should have a "Leshy Card Game" endless mode. Would be a nice DLC.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is a real contender for my GOTY...from the visual design to the atmosphere, it's the total package and a love-letter to card games as a whole. You don't get more clever, more engaging than this. With each hour I played, more and more, the game showed it's proverbial hand to me, goading me towards a finale that stunned and surprised. I would recommend it to anyone since it's a very different kind of game, one that anyone can enjoy even if you don't like cards.

Shifting more towards it's narrative, there's layers to the game not only about the ethics of your actions but the ones of those you can't control. Losing control, a lot, remains a theme that carries throughout all of Dan Mullin's games. I'm eager to learn more about them all and where they fit in Inscryption's story & universe. Pony Island & The Hex are both games that stick to a thematic presentation that works for the scope, but Inscryption pushes beyond that. With what feels like an unlimited budget, every facet of the game is stuffed with personality and effort, stripped from artistic limitations. It's allowed time to breathe, to grow, to expand upon it's ideas, give people a lot to think about and dwell on. It's the perfect length, too, spending about 10 Hours in a more casual playthrough. I think I'll be diving into it a couple more times, make different decisions.

I dunno what else to say other than to recommend it, go play it and try to avoid any spoilers lol
