\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: While the gameplay is god-tier at all times, the main campaign is 80% storytelling and 20% gameplay. If that's not what you're looking for, there's an official expansion that's basically 100% gameplay, and it has enough content for various-long sessions of play. Nonetheless, I still recommend playing through both with open eyes.

Full Review:

I write this review now because I've seen most first-timers having a similar (and somewhat negative) reaction, and I believe that this game is far deserving of more.

As of May 17th, 2022, this game consists of two major components: a three-act campaign and an official expansion.

As expected, the campaign is what most players are coming for. But here's the thing, said campaign works mostly as 80% storytelling and 20% gameplay. Do not worry, the gameplay is god-tier and you'll see that it's universally acclaimed by both the card-game and roguelike communities.

But the fact remains that the story takes the center stage. Without entering into spoiler territory, it will pull down your rug. It will take you out of your comfort zone. It will make you feel as if you were playing an entirely different game. And you can rest assured that you'll experience these feelings with each passing act.

That being said, the story's quality is as high as that of the gameplay. It's highly creative, and not like anything I've ever seen before. Think of it like a meta-based mystery that develops as you play the game, with you (the player) being entrusted to make your own connections and interpretations.

If you ask me, it truly made me feel as if I were experiencing this series of events with my own eyes. I was not the player, I was the protagonist.

But given the nature of this narrative, most people have been highly judgemental of it. A significant amount of players agree that "the game goes downhill after act one" and that "the game should have been purely about act 1". But I think they're missing the point.

More than a game, I believe that Inscryption should be seen as an experience. An experience that you can interact with. Think of it like Doki Doki Literature Club, another critically acclaimed title that used similar narrative devices.

If, after reading this, you're still looking for sheer gameplay, the game also presents an alternative: the Kaycee's Mod official expansion. Now, that's like 10% story and 90% gameplay. Again, if that's what you're looking for, you can play it with no strings attached to the main campaign.

Nonetheless, given that it expands on the official lore, I still recommend playing both aspects of the game with open eyes. Let yourself be carried away by the works of the developers, and enjoy yourself in ways that are rarely seen in the gaming industry (especially today).

10/10, have already played more than once. Long live Mantis God.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you want to check this game out because you're curious and haven't seen more than people's praise of it, I would recommend you stop reading reviews now and go play it.

That's what I did and It really did surprise me, all around quite fun and kept me entertained for the time I gave it. As a player of irl card games it really did tug on a few heart strings in certain places, especially towards the end. The gameplay was interesting and brought several unique aspects unique to a digital card game that I hadn't seen before. The characters you meet along the way were great fun, and seeing their secrets and their interactions was sweet. The deckbuilding felt quite sleek, a lot of the time you could keep your decklist quite short and it would still pack a punch.

I do reserve a few criticisms however. As much as I can understand that the game features digital horror and pays homage to the classic 'haunted cartridge' creepy pastas - I feel like some of the choices they made were a little cheap: often times there would be harsh static and glitch sounds, as well as lots of visual stuff that comes at you very suddenly which I understand is the point of it, but a lot of the time it went on for way too long and at some points I had to take my headphones off and look away, even with the volume set to the lowest.

The video sections that centre around Luke were not to my taste at all, the first few were good and set the scene, but by the end I was just clicking them to get through them in case there was a secret or something that I ought to see. And Act 3 of the game lost my attention a little bit, it dragged on a little too long for me.

All in all I had a great time with this game, but seeing how many achievements I had missed I would normally feel compelled to go back and hunt them out, especially with roguelikes and card games like this. However, for this game I can't quite put my finger on it but I have no ambition to do that here - make of that what you will.
