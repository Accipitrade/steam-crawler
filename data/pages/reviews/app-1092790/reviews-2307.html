\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLoved the game initially. Seemed to have deep lore with interesting characters and fun and new ways to catch my attention regularly, up until the first big part of the game happened.(won't spoil the game..)
Honestly, I was expecting this game to go a similar route as Slay the Spire(A strong recipe that works well..) With it's own twist on how lore and cards/gameplay evolve. It would've been sick, with limitless expandability and countless hours of fun exploration/gameplay. They could've even made good use of the other (later) strong characters in this game.

Personally I have no idea why they took such a perfect platform with such an amazing idea and let it slip like this..

Instead, the game is a one-off story with many edgy/sharp(personally uninteresting) turns that you'd only return to if you were interested in exploring.

Graphics are Astounding. Smooth camera work with amazing details. Easily my favorite atmosphere(dark cabin) for any card game ever so far. 9/10

Sound/BGM only added onto the graphics initially. Very much placed depth into the scene, and with a good pair of headphones it felt as though I was there. It did end up being slightly repetitive in overall tone, with little ways to branch out and use new sound styles. 8/10

Gameplay Was exciting, fun and new. Definitely New. After the first big event it fell apart quickly. Instead of exploring for cooler ways to play, the game started becoming a speedrun to the next event in hopes it would get better again. At the end of it I was only happy it was over and am still reminiscing over the first few hours of gameplay. The right idea with the wrong execution. 5/10

It's biggest downfall is that you can beat the game in a short sitting if you know what you're doing, blind. Which was the case. For a strategy enthusiast it's almost ironically worse. The game is too short, and worked itself into a corner unless they add a whole new game mode... Replayability: 1/10

Overall, it was an okay game. For 20 bucks, this did not follow my usual 1 hours per $ payoff metric... beating the game very quickly and feeling very incomplete because of it. If you love card games, go for it. Other than that, I'd wait for it to be on sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat game if you:

1. Enjoy card games, including draft roguelikes and traditional constructed formats.
2. Enjoy single-player story-driven games as opposed to gameplay-driven survival roguelikes like most in the genre.

It's a great ride, very enjoyable. Simple to dip into too, I was able to get my younger brother into it. Later parts take very sharp turns which can throw people off not necessarily in terms of difficulty, but just overall experience, expectations, and taste. I enjoy a huge variety of card games and I also enjoy single player campaigns, so this was very fun for the most part. I don't agree with all of it, but I look forward to see if it inspires anything else in the future. I'd advise from here on to stop reading any reviews that go too in-depth, including the following spoiler part, and try to enjoy it for what it is if the previous numbered list applies to you.

SPOILERS AHEAD

Parts 2 and 3 are kind of a slog. While I can get behind the game modes, they feel a bit tacked on to the superior product. There really isn't anything like the atmosphere, exploration, and fun that is in part 1. You get Undertale vibes when Stoat and Stinkbug talk to you and plead with you to not sacrifice them or warm them by the fire. Puzzles are simple but walking around the cabin, finding them, and how they're presented is really nice. And while the card game in part 1 is relatively simple compared to others, like most card games, there is still a devil in the details and sequencing; it's fun to be caught off guard every now and then. The whole presentation of it being a survival roguelike draft game was great, it gave the player plenty of meaningful choices on which branches to choose, allowed them opportunities to break the game, and oozed atmosphere and flavor the entire way.

Part 2 is alright but it didn't have the same feeling as part 1. I wanted to rush through it so I can get back to the survival roguelike experience in part 1, fuse monsters and make broken cards etc. Part 2 had singular and isolated moments of triumph that felt good: like making a broken deck or finding out how to farm infinite foils-- but they are isolated and never to be experienced again because once you break the game, you're done with the part. Part 1 had me and my brother cackling at creating a 14/14 Euriyale or from slapping triple strike on a 0-cost submerge+airborne 4/3 memento card that eventually won the game. We pushed our luck with the campers trying to make stronger and stronger creatures, and were excited with the rare cards from the boss spoils. I was playing this game with my brother throughout part 1, he's young and has absolutely NO interest in card games whatsoever, but genuinely enjoyed playing with me. Come part 2, he was bored out of his mind and couldn't keep his interest. It's not nearly as easy to share a victory or feel like you are contributing equally to a traditional deck builder because he's just so much more inexperienced; a lot of the time is spent reading and experimenting, without the gambling and fun that is in part 1.

Part 3 is fine but it feels watered down. The most annoying part of this act is that you barely get to cut any cards in your deck, and on top of that, when you die you don't get to start over. So you end up with a huge pile of cards, some of them failed experiments, others plain bad, and only a few being pretty fun or useful-- but since it's a pile you don't get to see them often. There wasn't much gambling like with the campers, nothing particularly flavorful like with the sacrifice stones, or anything as exciting as the twin Dr. Frankensteins. I suppose it's reflective of Po3's personality, that is of a snobbish spike who has no interest in flavor, lore, or the expression that cards can be. His world is barren, cut and dry, very cold, and because everything is so methodical and mechanical, you're left with feeling like a robot yourself.

Part 4 was cool if not just for the duel disk and the emotional high points.

Despite my gripes, I did overall enjoy the game. I think it's a fantastic and novel idea to have a single-player campaign driven card game that isn't like some weird arcade fighting game mode where you're just playing against AIs who have a handful of lines and little to no background. Games like that or like Yu-Gi-Oh! where you are playing through cuts of the story have so little detail and feeling in them, leaving you wanting wanting to skip everything so that you could just enjoy the gameplay. Inscryption, at least for the first part, asks you to take it slow and rewards you tenfold. I haven't played a game like it, though I can imagine Griftlands being similar. I am a bit mixed and torn in my feelings however.

On the one hand, Inscryption being a single-player story-driven campaign is a fresh take and I enjoyed it very much. But on the other, I really wish there was an endless mode for just part 1, with more mechanics to unlock and harder challenges to experience like Slay the Spire. It's a very bold take on the genre and I'd like to see if it inspires something a little more fleshed out. All in all, I recommend this game highly, and especially I recommend part 1 to people who are even new to card games. Its gameplay is simple to grasp, and you get all the fun and excitement of learning it, drafting, and growing steadily in power until you break the game.\t\t\t\t\t
