\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is a roguelite deckbuilder that ran face-first into an escape room, and it's the first game I haven't wanted to write a review for, because you should go in with no prior knowledge and just play it. Perfect for fans of puzzles, horror, card games, and mindbending metafiction, Inscryption is one of the best, most original games I've ever played.


But if you insist on knowing more...



The Plot

Your character is locked in a cabin, playing a card game against an unknown foe. Eventually--maybe after you pull your first tooth to tip the scales in your favor--you realize this card game may have rather high stakes and enormous consequences. But, like all roguelites, you try, try again. In fact, you'll need to, because as you fail, you learn. And learning is important in Inscryption, because the rules keep changing.

That's true in multiple ways: each round of the game, each boss, you find yourself facing new tactics and moves from your opponent, and as you progress the story, the game itself changes, too. It might feel like an uphill climb, but soon you discover that you, too, can have some tricks up your sleeve--and some friends in hidden places.

Explore the cabin, solve puzzles, and build your ideal deck to defeat your opponent--and, in doing so, unlock a broader world and two more incredible, mind-bending, psychologically twisted acts of the story.



The Card Game

You're taught to play a strange card game by your mysterious captor. Play a free squirrel card on the board and sacrifice it to gain 1 blood--blood that can pay the toll to play a more powerful creature. If your creature attacks a blank space on your opponent's side of the table, you get points based on its attack power. If it attacks a space occupied by a card, your creature attacks the other card. Creatures may have more than just attack and defense, however: you'll quickly learn that sigils on the cards can make them strike in multiple directions, move after a turn, kill another card instantly, or more.

You take turns scoring against one another, with each point weighing down a side of a scale on the table. If you completely tip the scales, you win. If your captor tips the scales, you lose.

With only four open slots on the table and a limited number of cards in your deck, you need to hone your strategy and be cautious with your plays to defeat your captor. But as you win each round, you progress through a map that allows you to make pit stops where you pick up new cards, empower old cards, trade, or make some gruesome experiments out of your creatures.

Eventually, you'll reach a boss. Your captor will attack you with new tricks, use creatures you've never seen before, and pull out all the stops to defeat you. And if you succeed? Well... let's just say there's not only one boss.

The way the card game evolves throughout Act I--and the whole game--is ingenious. You'll laugh, you'll cheer, you'll yell at your computer in frustration. At one point, my jaw even dropped. Who would have thought cards could be so exciting?



The Gameplay

Inscryption keeps you on your toes. At first, I struggled with the card game. A lot. And you might ask: why bother playing a game where you aren't good at.... well, the game? But the beauty of roguelites is that each time you fail, you have a chance to gain a tool that will help you succeed. Over time (and many attempts), I learned to really enjoy playing the game within the game.

Because essentially, that's what the cards are: you're playing a game with a card game in it. The cards are a vehicle for a larger story--one that you discover as you begin to explore the somewhat rudimentary 3D environment around you. And I promise you'll be drawn in the more you play.

So far, I've only commented on the gameplay in Act I. That's because the beauty of the following acts is how much the game changes, and I refuse to spoil any of that for you. Suffice it to say, while the following acts of the game still revolve around your skill at cards, everything changes radically--from the story, to the style, to the secrets.



That's It.

That's all I'm giving you. Anything more about your character, your opponent, the premise, or the card game would not only ruin the story, but frankly, it wouldn't make sense unless you play. If you're still not sure, play the demo--but I wouldn't waste your time, when you could be working on building your optimal deck...\t\t\t\t\t
