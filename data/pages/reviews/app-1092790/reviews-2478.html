I had some aspects of it slightly spoiled for me, which you can gather from most other reviews. And those aspects I actually quite enjoyed, despite the fact I think I may be in the minority opinion here. Its a rare occasion where the story of a game grabs me and truly holds me as firmly as Inscryption has, and personally having been in a massive rut for story based games lately, I think Inscryption is totally worth playing.

Now I will get into some minor spoilers and mechanical aspects I really like.

I do wish that outside of part 2 the gem abilities were more used, as well as if it were possible to bring the aspects of the other characters into that original gameplay loop, of the map, creatures, & bosses. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust finished my first full run of Inscryption (at 17.5hrs played); took a little less time than I'd expected, but I'll be continuing to play for a bit because, honestly, it's been a joy to play. I'm sure there's still a lot of interactions I haven't seen yet, as well. I highly recommend knowing as little as possible about the specifics of the game, because learning it is part of the experience. I avoid talking about them here, but there's a tl;dr at the end if you want it.

I'm only moderately experienced with deckbuilders (Megaman Battle Network, Slay the Spire, Griftlands) and I found it slightly on the easy side *in terms of results*, but I have still found the gameplay and mechanics very engaging. More importantly, I feel like it improves on some of my least favorite things about that titan of the genre, Slay the Spire (which I will spoiler out, in case you would like to skip):
In Slay, I came to feel that the first floor experience was a brutal tax on my fun - Strike and Defend cards composing the vast majority of every starting deck means most of the first 30 minutes of a run feels almost completely deterministic except for how much damage I get nickel-and-dimed into taking by enemies doing 6 damage into my 5 block, or just poor luck drawing a full hand of Strikes when I need to Defend, and vice versa. First floor card acquisitions are a brutal roulette as well, because (after some difficulty is accumulated) only *some* cards are strong enough to help a deck survive the Strike and Defend deckbloat through the second floor, and many of them depend on synergies with other cards for their strength, which obligates a blind commitment that luck will continue to give you cards that build toward the same strategy. At higher difficulties, a bad first floor basically means I need to restart and do the whole determinism dance over again hoping for better draw and card acquisition luck to be able to proceed on to the fun part of the game - and my next first floor will feel *almost exactly the same as the one I just suffered through*. I deeply enjoyed the character mechanics in Slay, but having to play through a deterministic first floor every run just for a chance to scale into the satisfying lategame synergies made it very difficult to continue having fun past a certain difficulty.

I'll refrain from talking about any specific mechanics, but in Inscryption I do not suffer those struggles at all. The system feels very compact, with consistent rules and, in my experience, unusually good communication about how things work. Due in part to those strengths, I'm not sure I ever felt robbed by a surprising outcome - I think this is also because of Inscryption's (imo) fantastic pacing. I'm so used to the burden of a frontloaded learning curve that I was utterly surprised by how much of the complexity of the game is neatly couched in what at first felt like simplicity. Balancing unit math, thinking ahead, and making good decisions are complex enough tasks that I feel challenged, but I also feel adequately rewarded for playing well - this being another difference from Slay the Spire, where so much of a successful run felt out of my control. I also feel like almost every choice in Inscryption begs actual consideration and forethought and, though that may change as I continue to discover strong strategies, that is the reason why I said it "at first" felt simple - taking into account the depth of the decisionmaking and risk-weighing, I feel "compact" is much more appropriate, as it accomplishes much with its concise mechanics.

Mechanics aside, Inscryption's flavor, setting, ambience, and je-ne-sais-quois are also simply fantastic, and honestly a reason all to itself to play this game. It's just not quite like anything else I've seen - I feel the word largely "compact" applies here, too, as it accomplishes a great deal of environmental storytelling with simple, efficient strokes (and a few wildly indulgent strokes as well). Though, I should also recognize the sheer amount of love and consideration that must have gone into those strokes - perhaps it only *feels* effortless. I'll say again that the aesthetics are fantastic and good reason even on their own to play the game, and leave it at that.


Tl;dr: Play the demo, and if you enjoy it, there's a lot more inside the full package. More mechanics, more flavor. Also: I think that, as with almost any experience, from food to videogames and beyond, it's best to go in with as few expectations as possible.\t\t\t\t\t
