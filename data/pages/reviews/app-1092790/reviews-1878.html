\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNO SPOILER VERSION:
You're locked in a cabin with a weird guy who won't stop staring at you and are forced to sacrifice squirrels.

8.5/10

One of the first card games that I had ever played that taught me quick to learn or lose. It might not be as challenging for veteran card game players, but I certainly felt the gears turning in my head. Getting the right sigils on my cards to pull off plays I didn't think were possible was a lot of fun, at one point I achieved undying squirrels and unending sacrifices. Being forced to wager your body parts in a touch situation to push through was always a tough decision to make, and all the while there was an overlying mystery that I wanted to get to the bottom of. Winning the battles are satisfying, strategizing around the map paths is fun, and overall I really enjoyed it!

SPOILER VERSION:
Alright this is where I bring the score down. I do not care at all for the live action 'youtuber' we're supposed to be rooting for. In fact, I'd be getting immersed in the game only to get yanked out when this guy says something out loud or even worse, when you're forced to watch all these video clips. These parts of the game just annoyed me more than anything.
The pixel version of Inscryption was okay, if not too much to take in at once. It wasn't anything special, and I often found myself throwing myself at battles only to lose and get zero punishment for it unlike in the first part.
Botworld was fine enough too, but it lacked the overarching dread of Part 1, where it felt like there was an actual weight to sacrificing things. I appreciated the return to the escape room full of puzzles, but it's such a far cry from Part 1 it was hard for me to enjoy past thinking: Thank goodness, we're finally back to this! The boss fights in which it pulls from your friendslist and your computer files are delightful, a glimmer of the fun of Part 1. Past Botworld, it was alright. If Inscryption was going to be split up into multiple parts, I believe it would have benefited from getting full levels for each of the four main 'Scrybes'. Grimora and Magnificus got completely glossed over, and I would have loved to immerse myself in the unique mechanics of either.

Storywise, I think I would have been far, far more interesting to throw out trying to create an ARG entirely, with this 'Luke Carder' guy. When I believed the game was talking to me personally rather than this character, it was more fascinating. Leaning on the fourth wall breaking mechanics utilized by other games like Oneshot or Undertale would have done a lot for the story, instead of essentially removing the player from being a character in this world and replacing them with Luke. I think the best way I can sum that thought up is, storywise, Luke is playing the game, not you. And the game suffers for that.

All in all, it was an enjoyable experience I went into completely blind and totally recommend! If you can get past the drawbacks, what you see in the trailer and Part 1 is so phenomenal it's worth slugging through the rest.\t\t\t\t\t
