\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is divided into 3 distinct acts. In my experience, Act 1 takes ~5-6 hours, Act 2 takes ~1-2 hours, and Act 3 takes ~2-3 hours. The first act is genuinely incredible. It has a fantastic atmosphere, unique mechanics, engaging puzzles, and a phenomenal difficulty curve. Once the game shifts into Act 2, a sharp shift in tone and difficulty frankly ruins the experience.

-------------------
The Tone
-------------------
Where the Act 1 is focused on horror, Acts 2 and 3 are focused on pursuing a mystery. As such, Acts 2 and 3 contain a lot of "meat" of the game. They contain most of the major plot points, characters, and puzzles. However, I can't shake the feeling that these acts lack the same polish as Act 1. They feel almost like DLCs. Act 1 is a fairly complete experience. Sure, some questions are unanswered, but I would argue that if all Inscryption was WAS Act 1, it would be just as beloved. Act 2 and 3 pursue these unanswered questions, and as such, it has an extremely different atmosphere. It is much like the experience before and after learning how a magic trick is done. You can find enjoyment in learning how a magic trick works, but by learning how it works, it's much harder to be impressed with the trick when you see it later on. Just as the enjoyment in learning how a trick works is much different than the enjoyment in being wow'ed by a magic trick, the enjoyment of the horror in Act 1 is much different than the enjoyment of tackling the mystery in Acts 2 and 3.

-------------------
The DIfficulty
-------------------
Acts 1, 2, and 3 play different variations of the same card game.

In Act 1, you slowly build a deck, beginning with 4 cards, and you summon cards by sacrificing pre-existing cards. As you progress, your deck becomes stronger and larger, but so too does your opponent. As such, most battles are a delicate balance of attempting to maintain a solid defense while also having just enough wiggle room to summon stronger cards. Stronger cards require sacrificing more cards, and so you must consider if you want to sacrifice your defense just to play offensive cards. Wiping out here is a huge blow. You must rebuild your deck from scratch, and it takes upwards of 15-20 minutes to traverse the board to be in the same position as before losing.

In Act 2, you rapidly amass dozens of cards that you then use to build a deck of 20 cards with. To summon cards, you use one of four currencies (earned in 4 separate and distinct ways). As such, much of the battle is spent attempting to earn a specific currency so you can summon whatever your deck's heavy hitters are. If you lose a battle, there is absolutely zero punishment. Your deck of cards is entirely unaffected, your progress is not reset, and you can challenge your opponent literal milliseconds afterwards.

In Act 3, you slowly build a deck, beginning with 4 cards. Playing cards uses an energy system; I'll do by best to explain, but it's rather unintuitive over text. I found it to be fairly intuitive in game, though. Turns give you an energy level equivalent to itself. For example, Turn 1 gives 1 energy, Turn 2 gives 2 energy, Turn 3 gives 3 energy, and so on, up to a max of 6 energy. Cards cost a set amount of energy, between 1 and 6. On Turn 4, for example, you can summon any amount of cards, so long as the sum of their energy levels does not exceed the 4 energy you start the turn with. You cannot stockpile energy, though. For example, if you end Turn 4 with 1 energy remaining, you will still start Turn 5 with 5 energy, not 6. As such, much of the match is spent stalling time until you can summon the stronger, high energy requirement cards. Losing a battle here is almost as meaningless as losing in Act 2. Your deck is entirely unaffected, and it takes approximately a minute to return to the same position.

These rather simple variations on the same card game, where you use a currency to summon cards, completely obliterate the tension of the game. In Act 1, tension is created by the uneasiness of the environment, the fact that the cards quiver in fear whenever they are about the be sacrificed, and the knowledge that you will lose progress if you lose a battle. The stakes feel tangible, and so getting invested in the game is easy. Unfortunately, in Act 2 and 3, there is no fear of losing progress. Strategy goes almost entirely out the window in Acts 2 and 3. It is so incredibly easy to shut your brain off and brute force battles, and brute forcing works way better than it should.

-------------------
Conclusion
-------------------
The first act is one of the best experiences I've had in gaming, and it's a shame that the rest of the game was so... different. I just can't bring myself to recommend a game that willingly changes its identity away from what made it so good.\t\t\t\t\t
