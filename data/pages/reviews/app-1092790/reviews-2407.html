\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGist: It isn't actually an escape room/card game/rogue-like, and the advertising is wholly dishonest.

This was a disappointing one. The first 1/3 of the game has a great concept, really tight mechanics, stellar atmosphere and this really interesting blend of escape-room/roguelite genres. This is the part of game that is advertised. If the game has ran with this and developed it, it would probably be one of my favourites. However, the game that the marketing shows you is only about 1/3 of the total. As you progress, the gameplay alters completely and deteriorates in favour of unimpressive meta elements ("what if the enemy cards where named after your steam friends?!") and mechanics are swapped for less developed ones. The game is also way too easy (and constantly gets easier) which means that the more promising mechanics don't even require you to engage with them (I'm tasking about conduits and gems mainly, for those who have played it). I think the game director may have even realised all of this because one character lampshades it by telling you to stick with the "hackneyed" mechanics. The concept of the story is interesting but perhaps as should be expected the ending only ties into an ARG without much real conclusion to the intrigue of the plot.

Overall it seems like this game got carried away with itself by thinking that it was more avant-garde that it really is, and relying on the player being wowed enough that they didn't notice or mind that the game they had bought and sat down to play had been a front for a "ludo-narrative art experience" or whatever you want to call it. In reality, the meta wow-factor for which Inscryption sacrifices its gameplay and the expectations of the audience doesn't stand up in an age were most people have played Undertale. I'd give act 1 (the advertised spooky card game) 10/10, act 2 6/10 and act 3 4/10.\t\t\t\t\t

As a vaguely horror-themed deckbuilder game, there's a lot to like about Inscryption. However, there are several points in the game in which the mechanics shift around and certain new characters are introduced, and not always for the better.

Worse than that, however, is the tendency of the developer to add spooooooky meta content- futzing with game files, combing through it for hidden messages, ARG nonsense- once again reading its ugly head here.

I should have seen it coming given The Hex and Pony Island, but I was really just here for the deckbuilding and card slinging, not the depressing/frustrating CreepyPasta stuff that an otherwise fun game is saddled with. I never asked for it, but it comes with the territory, I guess.

But if pretentious CreepyPasta file-combing is your thing (and it is for a good number of people), you'll find nothing to dislike about this title.

Now, I have to acknowledge Kaycee's Mod. It's the 'Endless' mod we've all been screaming about, and for the most part I'm here for it: starter decks, randomized maps, optional challenges, new unlockable cards and inoffensive background story stuff you can get if you want... it's pretty much exactly what I would have wanted. At times the challenges are SO challenging that one has to rely on good RNG to make significant progress, but it's still fun.

One thing I'd like to know though, is how they calculate 'misplays'. Like... is there a winning scenario for EVERY permutation? A perfect move for every situation? I'd like to see what a run with zero misplays looks like.\t\t\t\t\t
