\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, I want to say that this game is a masterpiece and easily among the best releases of 2021 (not that there was much competition). Go play it. For those who are already familiar with the game, my few points of criticism lie below. Basically all of this applies just to chapter 1 anyways, and is such a minor part of the game that I really don't care.

The first campaign, while masterfully presented, has a few shortfalls. In my first attempt, I managed to beat the first phase of the angler, only for him to spawn a wall of grizzly bears for no apparent reason. This is bad game design for several reasons. First off, up to this point it has been established that the dungeon master takes great pride in his art, and that he values immersion over all else. The fact that the dungeon master unironically pulls a "rocks fall, everyone dies" radically undermines this characterization. A DM as talented and dedicated as they were would have prepared for this contingency so that they could kill the player in a way that didn't feel cheap. Not to mention that grizzly bears have nothing to do with fish, making it thematically odd for the Angler's fight. The player is virtually guaranteed to die to the Trapper on their first encounter anyways - why the rush? It also takes almost all of the suspense out of death for no good reason. This also happens because the first campaign is too difficult and random. Horror is ruined by familiarity with consequence. Once you die enough times, the sequence ceases to be something interesting and macabre and becomes routine. This also makes your speaking party members cycle through their dialogue just a few too many times. This difficulty also causes pacing problems. The campaign is so hard, and the trapper so effective a roadblock, that it seems very likely that most players will win not by just barely scraping by, but with absolute steamroll decks that take almost all of the tension out of the final battle, because only absurdly powerful compositions can ever possibly defeat the trapper. I think it would be better to let the player get a peek at the final boss of the campaign, even if their chance of success is so miniscule, just to add a feeling of progression.

Puzzles in the game are either insufficiently explained or leave the player no room to feel smart. The former issue can thankfully be overcome with brute force, as is the case in tile puzzles. As a general rule, finding the solution to a puzzle you don't understand should make you feel something like "Oh, so that's how it works" or "I can't believe I didn't think of that!". The worst possible reaction is "Wait, why is that right? How can that be right?", which is exactly how I felt when I unsealed the Lonely Wizbot. I gave up trying to intuit the actual answer and sequentially attempted all 128 possible configurations until I unlocked it. Thankfully it was earlier than later in the sequence of answers. I had assumed the game was meant to mimic the actual gameboard, but in my "solution" both units which weren't in (what I assume to be) my hand were clearly blocked by other units on the field, so I have no clue why they dealt damage at all. I don't know if I'm an idiot or the game is being inconsistent, but either of these problems could be fixed by being more explicit about the mechanics of this minigame. However, for puzzles based more in lateral thinking and discovery than logical problem-solving, the game throws up its hands completely. Early in the game, you are told to look in the book for a code to open the vault, but it's literally right there. They just write it out for you - 273 - no working out the order, no deciphering hidden information, nothing. Initially I thought it'd have to do with the 3 numbers at the top of each page, or by finding patterns in the text itself, but this opportunity was missed by the developers. The idea to uncage the wolf really didn't need to be hinted at by party members - this would have been more interesting and fun for players to figure out on their own, as they almost certainly could have. Party members in campaign one are too helpful in general. It would be more atmospheric if the dungeon master took active steps in preventing them from helping you, either by shifting the cabin so that they didn't recognize it enough to give you much advice, or by outright silencing them.\t\t\t\t\t

I'm not 100% sure what to think about Inscryption. Most of all, I think it is a technical achievement. The core card game is very fun, engaging, and creative, and the music and visuals are stunning. There are many avenues and strategies to explore which keeps the gameplay from feeling too stale. To be honest, I can't help but feel like the card game that is the basis of Inscryption would be very fun as a real-world trading card game or as an online arena-style deck-builder. But of course that's not REALLY what Inscryption is all about, is it?

Setting aside the excellent game design and art direction, the heart of Inscryption lies in its twisting story, which is set to catch you off-guard and dodge your expectations the whole way through. What begins as a simple card game expands to be an escape room which contains a card game nested within it. And even then it zooms out even further to become something much bigger. It's a nested puzzle box of ideas and games and story. I will not lie by saying that I fully understand the story that was told here, but it was certainly interesting and surprising nonetheless.

There were plenty of things that I liked about Inscryption, but it wasn't a perfect experience by any means. The story was confusing, the game dragged on just a little bit too long, and the difficulty bounced all over the place. Sometimes it felt like the game was cruel and unforgiving, but mostly, depending on what power-ups and decks I ended up with, it felt like the game was way too easy, with many games ending before my opponent could even take their first turn. Of course, that was often due to good luck with my first hand, but there were many game-breaking exploits (which I'm sure were intentional by the game devs) that I was able to take advantage of to bend the game to my will. I will say that FINDING those exploits and builds was pretty fun on its own, although it did feel like it made the gameplay too easy. I still can't tell if that made the game more fun or if it rendered it a meaningless non-challenge. SPOILERS: specifically the power to have cards return to your hand when they die is completely overpowered, especially when it's applied to squirrels and high-power death cards But of course the game is built with those powers in mind so it's not necessarily a detriment to the game, just something that gave me pause.

I'm not going to go much more into detail here. Overall, I'd say that Inscryption was an enjoyable experience, if a little plodding at times. It kind of feels like it went too far in its desire to change the game into something new, and I kind of wish it went even farther with its meta aspects. There's a lot I loved about it, but it left me wanting a little more substance in the over-arching story. If you end up playing it, I hope you have a great time with it.\t\t\t\t\t
