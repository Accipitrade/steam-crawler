\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscyption is an incredibly game that tells an extremely intrigueing story that has you coming back for more, and greets you with its great card play and deckbuilding mechanics that make winning feel great and make losing suck less.

Inscryption has an incredible story that makes you feel really cool and secretive about as it delivers this story in a manner that makes it closer to the satisfaction of figuring out an ARG (although Inscryption has its own —Already been solved but you can follow along) without the banging your head against the wall part. However, this story is subsidized with the great card play and deckbuilding that makes it rewarding to both outplay your opponent and to absolutely pummel them with a card that you made overpowered. The game, especially in Act I, has its roguelike elements really shine through as the game allows you to break its own mechanics and it provides a sense of satisfaction for that, rather than the feeling of cheating it that is provided by some other roguelikes. Despite the gameplay being so good, Daniel Mullins does not hold his punches as there are tons of cool twists with both massive gamplay changes (only for the better) and there is awesome story progression that accompanies it. As a Mullins game, there is the wealth of secrets that follow in its trail and hunting for them is a great feeling once you find something that you could’ve played the entire game through and never before seen. This all, not to exclude its stellar soundtrack, culminate into one of the finest indie classics that I have ever played, with its ending being something that I still think about.

TL;DR: Inscryption is a masterpiece of a roguelike with stellar storytelling and great gameplay that all mark it as a master stroke of a Mullins game and of indies in general. Now goodbye dear Review Reader, buy this game, and allow me to shake your hand.\t\t\t\t\t
