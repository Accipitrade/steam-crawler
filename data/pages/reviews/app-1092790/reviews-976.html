\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe first third of the game is great. Why is this? Probably because it's the most polished part IMO with voice acting, "different bosses", and an easy to learn hard to master style. Not to mention the game is marketed through screenshots and video of the first third of the game. Do you wonder why? Well it's because after the beginning you are in a different game. It's very strange. The artwork was great, which doesn't follow in the latter. Essentially the game is about a group of sentient gods who are trying to overcome each other with cards. I feel like the game didn't explain this very well in the later stages. The artwork and overall confusion lead me to become lost with the overall message behind the game. Originally I was thinking this game was a simple rouge-like card game as pictured. That changes in the second part of the game and becomes increasingly confusing as everything changes. Honestly, I don't know why it's gained so much traction as it's marketed for only a 1/3 of what the entire game is. It's like I bought a 1/3 of the game and the rest was just a bore/rubbish. I am glad to see they are coming out with Kaycee's Mod which allows for endless mode but it's still buggy. The game is going to take awhile to get to where I believe it's good. A big downside is, once you play to understand the game it's kind of done at that point. You become too overpowered in endless mode to really get any thrill. Even in endless mode it quickly can become boring. There is not a lot of repeatability until Kaycee's Mod is complete with new features. In a lot of ways Inscryption is like Doki-Doki Literature Club with the innocent beginnings and chaotic endings. Doki-Doki just explained it better, worked better, and made sense amid chaos. I hope to see this game become better. It needs some work!

TLDR: I would wait to buy! Game is incomplete!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs someone who played through Witcher 3 multiple times almost exclusively for Gwent content, this was a very fun experience.

Spoiler Free information below for anyone considering this game....

1.) This game is, in fact, a deck builder, but a very large majority of the game is around finding tricks to actually make your deck even stronger outside of just the cards. Do not expect just a pure deck building experience.

2.) The actual card game you play is extremely one-sided, but it's one-sided for both parties, meaning you can be completely obliterated in one turn, but you can also completely obliterate your opponent in one turn. This can give a very polarizing effect of the game feeling way too easy, while also simultaneously feeling way too hard. As long as you keep in mind this is not meant to be a pure deck building experience like mentioned above, you'll be okay.

3.) There are a good amount of puzzles, and while there are enough clues to honestly solve all of them, a significant amount can be brute forced leading to the a large amount of puzzles feeling very unrewarding. Try to solve everything legitimately for a more rewarding experience.

Would recommend for sure, 20 dollars is actually absurd for what this game offers.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI dont like card games. Slay the Spire, Pirate Outlaws, etc., etc. Not my thing. It felt like there is only so many ways a card game can be designed, and all of those methods were designed years ago. I was not familiar with the developer, Daniel Mullins. The buzz of a mysterious game where-not-all-is-as-it-seems and the involvement of Devolver Digital is what caught my attention.

It was the aesthetics, story and style that kept me at the table. Also a sucker for a game that prominently features a narrator. The first portion of the game alone is well worth the price.

Enough reviews focus on the general mechanics and positives of Inscription so here - spoiler warning is a take on the most common complaint about Inscryption. It does not reveal any specific aspects about the story but rather the presentation:

The game is divided into three Acts. The transition from Act I to Act II is jarring because of its presentation. It changes from what is seen in the store screenshots to Gameboy-esq pixel art. In my humble opinion, that really is the fundamental difference between Act I and II. In Act III, the art is more contemporary and the same as Act I. Nevertheless, there is a lot of hate directed towards Act II. It is true that Act II also introduces some additional game mechanics the thing is, I continued to play Act II just like I did Act I, and I finished it without a problem. I did not bother with the new cards and their mechanics. The game played exactly the same for me as it did before. If the art had stayed consistent between the Acts, my expectation is that the furor would have been much less.

Hats off to Daniel Mullins for making a card game feel novel again.\t\t\t\t\t
