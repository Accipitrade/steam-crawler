\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is a rather controversial game due to a lot of the community feeling like what was advertised to them and what the demo had playable was not representative of the complete game. While this may be arguably true, I still feel that Inscryption is worth your time and money, with a few caveats. NOTE: This review contains spoilers.

What first drew me to Inscryption was the atmosphere and aesthetic. I'm a sucker for anything card-game related, and also a massive horror fan; to find the two genres blended together in a neat little package was a dream come true. I actually pre-ordered the game after playing the demo, something I very rarely do, especially for indie games.

Upon playing the full release for several hours, I felt that the game had a lot of interesting and fun mechanics that I hadn't seen much of in other games. Mechanics such as infusing sigils from one card to another, cards that upgrade permanently between runs, escape room style puzzles that add stronger cards or boons to your starting deck all added depth and strategy to an otherwise straight forward game. I was having a blast and felt that Act 1 had a very enjoyable difficulty curve.

SPOILER WARNING. Then came the infamous Act 2. After defeating the final boss in Act 1, the gameplay takes a momentary back seat in order for some in-game lore to take place, followed by the game itself "rebooting" into Act 2. Gone are the horror elements from Act 1, gone is the cabin, the darkness, the atmosphere. You're now playing a 2D sprite based card game akin to the GBC Pokemon Trading Card Game, a game which I also thoroughly enjoyed completing as recently as 2019. While I would have loved to play a game like this normally, I didn't really want to play it right now. What I wanted to play was a continuation of Act 1, the primary focus of the marketing materials and the demo that I had become enamoured with. It was a little disappointing at first, but the game itself had not become "bad", so I played on.

The change in atmosphere aside, there was one notable issue I had with Act 2; the difficulty and strategy of the game severely drops. Whether it be from bad balancing or AI, or from the developer consciously deciding to make Act 2 and 3 easier than Act 1 in order for the story to take more of the spotlight than the gameplay, I found myself breezing through Act 2 with an auto-made deck and not quite enjoying myself as much as I had in Act 1. If I had to estimate, I spent 3-4 hours playing through Act 1 repeatedly, enjoying myself the whole time. I auto-piloted through Act 2 in about 1-2 hours.

Act 3 returned to a style more closely resembling Act 1, for which I was thankful. It wasn't quite as atmospheric or "horror" themed any more, but was much closer to what I expected the game to be during its entirety. Act 3 was also easier than Act 1, but I found it very enjoyable again as new mechanics were introduced such as respawning items, a shop available at any time, and the new buffs available to your cards. I had some absolutely busted cards toward the end of Act 3 and I felt that my strategy was rewarded.

One issue I had with Act 3 was its online connectivity features. During one of the boss battles, the boss attempts to connect to the internet to receive a card made by another player. Over the multiple attempts I had at fighting the boss, it never successfully received a card from another player, instead defaulting to a scripted card. I'm not sure if the game never actually does receive another card from other players and is just made to look like it could, or if it just could not find any cards to receive for whatever reason. It also attempts to upload a card you design mid-fight to other players, which my game failed to do each time also. It did manage to scrape my friends list and turn them into cards successfully, so I'm not sure what the issues were with the other features. I would have enjoyed that boss fight and the act overall a bit more had these features worked correctly.

The finale was very short and to be honest, not all that interesting aside from some glimpses of acts that could have been, as you partake in very short fragments of games against the other two scrybes that did not have their own table-top segments in the game. I would have loved to have played through their acts rather than the Act 2 currently in the game, although I understand it would have meant significantly more effort from the developer and ultimately wouldn't have been the story he wanted to tell.

The meta story consisting of real life video footage woven in between gameplay was interesting but nothing ground breaking unfortunately.

At the end of the day, if you know what you're getting into when you buy Inscryption and understand that it will NOT replace games such as Slay the Spire or Monster Train as your go-to roguelike deckbuilder, then it is absolutely worth a purchase. I had a great time with Inscryption, even if it was not entirely what I would have liked it to be.\t\t\t\t\t
