\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption, from all the marketing material I saw, was advertised as a spooky rougelike deck building game with some puzzle/escape room elements. And it is, kind of. All I can say spoiler free is that once you reach the end of the first act it throws all of that out and becomes a very different game. One that I did not enjoy, despite trying very hard to keep going.

I feel as though I've been tricked into spending more for this game than it's worth because whatever is after the first act is just not fun for me. If it had been better communicated that there's an abrupt tone and gameplay shift after the first part then I might not be writing this review now.

In short, if you're going to twist the gameplay almost entirely, communicate that to your customers or at the very least make it not an incredibly frustrating tone shift as well. My advice? Pick it up, play for less than 2 hours, then refund. You'll have a better experience than if you push to the second part of the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDon't watch any trailers. Don't read about this game. The more blindly you can play this game, the better.

That said, I want to write an actual recommendation of this game since it's one of my greatest gaming experiences in a long while. But telling almost anything about this game would risk spoiling the weirdness and scale of it.

I absolutely loved this game. But who else is it for? It feels like a simultaneous love letter and parody of card games - especially deck builders. It's a game that knows it's "just" another card game, and plays with that presumption. And it plays with the presumption that YOU know and love card games. The game plays as much with you as you do with it.

If you like card games, meta-creepyness and in general just thinking about game design, I think you should definitely give this game a try. It is jaw-droppingly impressive for such a small studio. The story and atmosphere hooked me, the gameplay is simple and engaging and I feel like the designer made a game that just ... "got it". The game left me more appreciative of games, game development and the people in my life I play games with. It reminded me of why I like playing games in the first place, and especially what has hooked me on board/card games.

I loved this game, and in some way, it felt like it loved me back.\t\t\t\t\t
