\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed the demo, loved it, bought this on day 1. I assumed the entire game would be like the demo. Turns out the entire game changes into a different game. Nothing in the screenshots advertising the game or the demo showed that it would radically change. Even the logo of the game on the store page is from the first part of the game. I beat the game and it was interesting, but I was pretty disappointed. I expected the game to be... like the advertisements. Its primarily an arg/meta-story game and I expected it to be more focused on being a card battler horror roguelite (like the first 3 tags for the game are)


spoilers below


The start of the game is just like the demo/screenshots/trailer. Youre in a haunted cabin and are forced into playing a card game with some creepy thing. You sacrifice animals to summon cards and progress forward on a branching map. Eventually, you can stand up from the game and explore the haunted cabin. In the cabin you can solve puzzles to find/unlock new cards, find new items, and progress the story forward (both the in game story of dealing with the creepy guy in the cabin and the meta-story of a real world guy discovering the game on a floppy disk). I loved this section of the game. Everything after this was meh

After beating the "final" boss and progressing the meta-story along, the entire game changes. Its no longer a spooky 3d card building roguelite in a haunted cabin in the woods, it turns into a 2d deck building pixel rpg. Its not even a roguelike anymore (beyond opening rng card packs, I guess). If you die you lose nothing, theres no random perks, theres 1 map (overworld+detailed towns, in true pixel rpg fashion), theres no random enemies.

After beating that section, the game goes back into 3d but is now scifi themed. You no longer sacrifice animals and rip out your own teeth, you shoot robots with guns. It still has a sort of rpg map with fast travel. Its still not really a roguelite. If you die you lose money and get sent back to a checkpoint so you can progress through the same map with the same enemies to get your money back. You explore a scifi cabin to unlock cards/items/story, but its really not as spooky as the weird cabin from the first part of the game.

I went into this expecting slay the spire + horror + a story. If youre like me, youll be disappointed. The focus of the game is the story, not the card game underneath\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption has an amazing premise and a fantastic start, but it is one of the biggest examples of missed potential in all of video game history. The combination of deck-building, board games, escape rooms, and puzzles makes for such a unique and immersive experience that's also surprisingly scary; but it's the story which ruins it and will disappoint essentially every single player no matter who they are. It's difficult to explain why without going into spoilers, but even if you get a brief view of the gist, I'd say it's not a good enough story that you'll be worried about missing out on a "surprise," since there isn't really any.

Starting out, it's set in a cozy, atmospheric cabin with a mysterious opponent. I couldn't put the game down at this point, and just wanted to keep trying out the rogue-like progression and building new decks. The card game itself isn't phenomenal, but it's enough to keep your interest with the variance between games, and the surrounding mystery. The escape-room attributes are okay, but they're mostly unnecessary, and just help inject some soul into the experience. Everything here from the graphics to the music, sound, and gameplay is great; and lives up to its "horror" status. The only bad portion is when you have to watch these cringy live-action videos about a guy named Luke, who is our central plot character. You're essentially watching him learn about Inscryption, but it's so badly acted and out of place, it makes me sick. You're better off skipping these videos entirely; as THANKFULLY, Luke will only comment on the game itself once in a while--though every time he does, it completely ruins the immersion.

Once you're past this, however, you're greeted by the opportunity to start a new game, and you see sprite-based visuals... And you know it's going to be terrible. This section of the game is terrifying, a badly-designed reference to gameboy-esque titles like the Pokemon TGC, with garbage graphics, mediocre gameplay, and you're just BEGGING to go back to the gamemaster scene in the cabin. If you don't drop the game here, after about an hour or so you finally return to the original setting, but now it has a technology-robot themed motif. It's not as good, but it's sufficiently different and anything is a welcome relief from the sprite-based section. Similarly to the first, you can have a lot of fun here, though the game mechanics are changed a bit.

But the real horror is only just beginning, because the idiot who made this game then turns the meta-ARG mechanics up to full power in a desperate attempt to save time and he butchers his own game. At this moment, he then has the audacity to show you gamemaster scenarios for the other two major characters based on chess and Yu-Gi-Oh mechanics. This is such a tease it makes me want to give a negative review and spit on this game forever. I can't imagine someone programming those sections, playing them, and then deciding, "The game is good to go, I don't need to make them into full-fledged campaigns." This is the missed opportunity! Inscryption could have been one of the best games of the year, it would have been remembered for ages, and would have been just incredible if it had four full campaigns for each of the gamemaster characters. Instead, you get two with a lot of garbage blocking the way from your enjoyment of them, and then you get mocked with what could have been. This is when you realize the story is just god-awful, it's pathetic, and it makes no sense. You can't have a meta-narrative about Luke AND about my computer and MY experience at the same time. It's like someone didn't even bother testing it or thinking about it, there's just no taste. It's a shallow attempt to draw attention on cliches that everyone is already getting sick of, and then he doesn't even do it right.

So if you have a lot of patience, and you can handle a HUGE disappointment, then buy Inscryption and enjoy yourself for a few hours before the developer slaps you in the face. The only other major flaw about the game is that it's often too easy and it has to artificially lengthen itself with forced death because you too-often win on the first turn. But that's NOTHING compared to the abomination of the plot and mechanics that just steals a nearly-perfect card game experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is very much a work of love with lots of effort clearly put into it. It was a fun and exciting experience from beginning to end with plenty of twists and turns that have come to be expected from this developer. My biggest criticism is that this is most likely all we will see of Inscryption. One thing that should be kept in mind before jumping in is that this is a NARRATIVE roguelike. There very much is a story that you can't necessarily ignore if you want to continue and it WILL change the experience.

I do have some minor criticisms. The RNG can end up having a snowballing effect that can, at times, make it feel a little unfair, albeit the CPU has their own somewhat unfair tricks at times so I dunno. I think something like a "hard mode" option that lessened the impact of certain things like the bonus candle you can find would have been an interesting option. As it is I got to the end of a run on my fourth try. The third act honestly wasn't that fun and dragged on due to the low stats of most of the cards you were allowed to use after having such fun deck variety beforehand.

Still, I would certainly call this game this Dev's current magnum opus.\t\t\t\t\t
