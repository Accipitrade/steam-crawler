Third time's the charm!
Daniel Mullins has little to nothing to prove that he is a brilliant game developer and designer, where the key strengths in his games are the meta factor and an unpredictability that engages players with questions to find answers for. I got sold with Pony Island, got further impressed with The Hex and allowed myself to pre-order Inscryption.

Without giving away spoilers, I can safely say that your character finds himself in a dark shack facing a strange man, who plays a card game with you that might decide your life and death. Inscryption's gameplay core lies in DnD-style RPG adventure, reminiscent of the video game Hand of Fate, with the card dueling as a medium for battle. There are times you'll be getting off the table to run quick errands or check your environment for something that will help you reach your goal. But as you progress along, you'll notice bit by bit that nothing is what it seems... (duh)

Even though the premise changes as the game progresses, the adventure/card gameplay core remains and evolves, which I think helps keep Inscryption fresh through-and-through. My least favourite experience would be in the middle part of the game which, while not as punishing as the initial part, the wide range of cards makes it less appealing to me to sort out which makes the best deck for me to progress. But after finding my preferences, it is smooth sailing onward to the end, even with some trials-and-errors involved.

If you are interested in checking Inscryption out, why are you still reading? Go play it or check Mullins' other games I've mentioned in before-hand, if you like out-of-the-box games done right!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was under the assumption it would be like Duel Links where it would be an actual card game because I didn't want to spoil myself and bought it after a quick look on the Steam page but this is a story-based game with 3 acts.
Don't go into this thinking you're going to have your deck all the way through the game.
No, you're going to have to start from scratch at least 3 times.
The first and third acts are good but there's a section in the middle that's all "Retro" and "Pixel" that put me off a bit.
I don't want to spoil what exactly happens but it's really good for what it's worth.
The first section of the game is like a limbo, where you will have to re-do the boss battles and keep dying until you have a strong enough deck to defeat the final boss of that part.

The story's pretty much like a Creepypasta where a guy gets a game from a car boot sale and it's "cursed".
No, I'm not making it up. It is like Sonic.exe. But it's not crap somehow.
It's pretty good and I can appreciate the diverse game mechanics and all that.
The card gameplay itself is very fluid and satisfying. A bit "grindy" at times but what do you expect?
All in all it's the first game in the past few years that's had any heart and soul put into it and is actually GOOD.

Just a shame it's not a proper trading card game.
I can understand why there would never be a multiplayer, the meta would be hellish to balance lol.
8/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo those expecting a regular roguelike experience (then again, with how popular the game has become, I wouldn't be surprised if you knew the big deal already), I recommend familiarizing yourself with the developer's other games and their "gimmick". A lot of people have been disappointed by the lack of a real roguelike, and while Kaycee's Mod is slowly fixing that void, it'd be a shame to miss such a nice game because of those subverted expectations. I especially recommend The Hex for reasons that will become apparent the more you think about the ending.

Inscryption is an excellently-made game with a gripping, addictive card system and a story I look back on with growing fondness every time I boot it up. While one might find the meta-narrative aspect a little overdone and hackneyed, the story of the game Inscryption, both in itself and outside of it, concerning the development and the shady figures involved with its invention, kept my attention the entire way through. I don't consider myself a big deckbuilder person; I have a hard time keeping track of strategy and synergy, as well as inability to truly put effort into my decks outside of getting the game to sort and choose for me. I did not go through that with Inscryption; the system is easy to learn and hard to master, providing a lot of elbow room for fooling around and finding your own ways to challenge yourself or break the game in half.

I wholeheartedly recommend Inscryption to anyone looking for anything shaped like itself; a weird, atmospheric, fourth-wall leaning game that's a lot more than it looks.\t\t\t\t\t
