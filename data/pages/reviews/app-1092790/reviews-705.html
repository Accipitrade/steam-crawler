\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst of all, you should avoid spoilers. This game is best enjoyed on a blind run. I have kept my review spoiler free, in the sense that I will try to tell you why you might want to play this game without mentioning any specific part of the game's story that isn't already evident from the trailers on the store page.

I recommend this game to anyone who's a big fan of trading card games and is in the mood for playing a game more for the story than the mechanics. There's inspiration in this game from many different tcgs, including YuGiOh (both the card game and the anime, especially the concepts of having to sacrifice your weak cards to bring out stronger cards and some cards being sentient.) There's also a surprising amount of references to the culture of trading card game players. Being a tcg fan, this game resonated with me personally in ways I wasn't expecting.

Although I'm sure a lot of Slay the Spire fans would enjoy this game, I wouldn't recommend that you go in expecting a similar experience. Inscryption is an aesthetically beautiful game with a fascinating story that probably couldn't be told in any medium other than a deckbuilding rougelite/escape room puzzle game, however it is very much form over function. It doesn't have the same balance or high skill ceiling that Slay the Spire does. You typically win a run by finding one of the game's absurd balance-breaking combos, and the base game has very little replayability once you reach the ending. Most rougelites are like that to some degree, but in this game there is just two or three op cards and combos that plow through large chunks of the game. Also, a lot of the story progression is tied to the puzzles rather than the card game. However, the Kaycee's Mod expansion does add some content and replayability in the form of a rebalanced challenge mode that focuses specifically on the deckbuilding roguelike aspect of the game, so you should definitely look into that once you've completed the main game.\t\t\t\t\t
