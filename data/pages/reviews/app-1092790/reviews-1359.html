\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption's biggest achievement is how it envelopes a rather mundane card game in a bizarre narrative so off-the-wall it’s hard to even begin to describe. Here, you’re trapped in a cabin out in the woods, by a sadistic megalomaniac who directs you through an adventure told through a deck of cards. You don’t see his face -- only his demonic eyes; pinning you to your seat, beaming, as he asks you to mutilate yourself for currency, accepting his ad hoc game rules.

The way he speaks makes it very clear that this is your last game. Ever.

The best part about it is that while it’s abiding to some pretty standard genre mechanics, the narrative around it is anything but conventional. After a short introduction you’re suddenly allowed to get up and stretch your legs. You get to peek, nudge, and roam around the cabin, giving you some insight. Not just into the game, but your predicament too. Cards are just one facet of storytelling here, where both wins and losses are used as an excuse to expound and add color. You get to explore and solve puzzles -- letting the world of cards color your surroundings, and vice versa.

Before you know it, it feels like you’re playing a different game altogether, where the stakes have been raised and the cards themselves almost seem secondary. To say that it’s subversive and meta is the understatement of the century. But if you know about Daniel Mullins’ previous titles, you sort of know what to expect here.

Highly recommended if you enjoy card tricks.\t\t\t\t\t
