\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI think my short review of Inscryption would be that it's overproduced where it shouldn't be, and underproduced where it should have been.

My even shorter review would be: "Slay the Spire that you'd only play if you were forced to by a murderous weirdo in a cabin in the woods, oh wait..."

Because it really is just worse Slay the Spire. In every place where StS managed to be concise and clear, Inscryption wastes your time on clicking through dialogue and tedious animations.

I think the most annoying thing about Inscryption, to me, was how often it coughs up a fight where you can't actually... do anything. Right off the bat, a fight where all the enemy creatures hide underwater or fly, so you can't touch them, all you can do is hope to win a damage race. It leaves most fights feeling very one-dimensional.

The mechanics aren't fundamentally bad, but on the very limited 2x4 grid, there's suddenly not a lot of space for square-shifting creatures, or the ones with split attacks, and the limited hands and draws means that after turn one or two, you'll mostly just be doing one single thing every turn, because there's only one thing you actually can do, which makes most fights very deterministic affairs.

Especially because most of them wind up as damage races and you either have an early lead or you're early behind and those almost always turn into a win or a loss, respectively.

Being that it's a Magic-alike that *only* has summons, and you can't manually move summons around between squares or anything, you're also super-limited in what you can actually do with the battlefield.

It's a game that would've been better if it stole more from other games. Like if it had just been "Magic, but there's actual positioning," that would've been a pretty strong sell. Instead it's so stripped down that I rarely felt like I had any decisions to make, either I had one very obvious one that won me the fight I was in, or there was nothing I could do because I'd drawn a trash hand right off the bat and the opponent was going to be doing 50% of my health from turn one.

What I noticed, though, was that the few cases where the game was actually fun and offered me choices, was when I drew something like the "Cat" cards early, something that gave me more resources and thus meant that I was more guaranteed of being able to make an action every turn, and sometimes even a decision between a cheap card and an expensive card. Having items to deploy also helped make fights more interesting because suddenly I had something that was kind of like those Sorceries from Magic, which did other stuff than just plop another creature on the field.

But the problem was that getting those things was very random and in no way guaranteed.

So, long story short, the "simple" changes to Inscryption to make it interesting, would in my opinion be:

Expand the battlefield(deeper, wider, or both)
Give the player more resources or rebalance casting costs so players are more guaranteed to have a valid action every turn
Either make items guaranteed(like, say, have some of them be "permanent" items that you get X uses of per game) or add some cards that have non-summon effects(though in that case more resources so you could get in a non-summon and a summon every turn, most turns, would be nice)

As a rarity, since it's almost always one of the first things I comment on, I can't even comment on Inscryption's soundtrack. Was there even one? My brain just sort of edited it out, so it was either very understated or insanely forgettable.\t\t\t\t\t
