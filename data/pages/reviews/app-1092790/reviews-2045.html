\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of those things where it is a spoiler itself to tell you I can't tell you anything without spoiling. If you've looked at the game enough to see this, you'll know what it is advertising, and you have probably seen the other reviews talking about the acts of the game. The first act is what you expect. Leave expectations behind there. I had seen the reviews with cryptic warnings like this, and I still did not see that coming. Nor the next one. The next couple were more foreseeable.

The gameplay is good. It is not top tier great, perfectly balanced, or a game you will play as a roguelike deckbuilder for weeks. But that gameplay itself ties into the story, which is the proper way to use a game in storytelling, rather than art not-a-games that clunkily try to tell a story with the gameplay getting in the way. Inscryption does a lot with atmospherics and tone, along with tying it all together.

Inscryption is fairly short, on the order of 10 hours, longer to 100%. That speaks well to a lack of padding, and that really is about as much depth as it has.

There is significant clamour to take one or more parts of the gameplay and extend it/them into full-length games, either by extending them in Inscryption or making a spinoff. You may see something about "Act I endless mode" by the time you read this. That would not be a bad thing, but it will take more gameplay testing and balancing that is not strictly needed in the base game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is more than a game. Since my purchase a few days ago, I have been playing it absolutely non-stop. I watched a few Let's Plays of it, and was really into the idea of the card mechanics and the atmosphere. After purchasing it to try it out, I found that was only the literal tip of the iceberg. There is SO much here! The game unfolds in so many brilliant and unpredictable directions. I frequently felt like I was battling something that was a step ahead of me.

This game has a number of fabulous card mechanics, that feature many changing and interacting rule sets. The mechanics for each of the decks feel very unique. I found myself growing very attached to my cards, and got a lot of joy out of developing different strategies for the different decks. The game makes the wise choice of constantly putting you in situations where you are picking up new cards, or modifying old ones. It keeps the game feeling fresh, as you are always making small tweaks to your deck and strategy in order to keep up with the influx of cards. They all seem really well thought out. There were certain cards I tried to avoid or didn't use often, but to me that's a sign that there's depth to each deck, with there being varied strategy within each type. I was still able to see the potential reward for mastering use of those cards.

I don't know if they did it on purpose, but the game modifiers really lit the spark for me in terms of making me feel like a real strategist. On my second or third playthrough, I accidentally made a totem that returned my squirrels to my hand after they perish. It seemed useless at the time, a result of my limited choices in the early game, but it ended up making me such a powerhouse, pushing me almost all of the way through the first 1/3 of the game, until I finally lost a hair-raising battle. I realized that there are tons of creative ways to give yourself an edge.

This game scratches an itch for me. It was challenging the whole way through, while still feeling rewarding all the time. I had no problem accepting every failure, seeing what kind of secrets I could tease out of the game by playing through it again, and again.\t\t\t\t\t
