\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat game. I loved the story and how you slowly reveal bits of story as the game advances and solve different puzzles. But the rouge-like card game aspect of the game was so addictive, I almost wish there was a mode with no story. It seemed like just as I was getting the hang of things or had just gotten some cool cards I really wanted to use, I'd finish an act and have to start from scratch. So it'd be cool if there was like an endless arcade mode or multiplayer capabilities that just expands on the base card game and lets you play around. Or maybe a DLC where you can experience the other 2 scrybes being in control of the game. They already have some of the basic assets necessary from the conclusion from the game. They'd just need to flesh things out a bit more than the brief glimpses they gave us. \t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI appreciate such an ambitious attempt at cohesively blending so many genres. It works on a lot of levels, but falls sadly short as well. The potential here is massive. The flaws are magnified by the idea of "what could have been" for me. Still, weirdness and experimentation are valuable in their own right regardless of execution. The envelope has been pushed. This game lives and dies on how good the card game is at any given point, though, and they aren't always solid enough to carry everything else.

The first third of the game is incredible, full-stop. The card game mechanics are tight and satisfying, which makes the weirder elements (tone, setting, genre-blending) the icing on the proverbial cake. Some of the puzzle elements are not explained well enough, however, which results in the bane of puzzle gaming design: trial-and-error or brute-force solutions. Still, with the card game carrying the majority of the fun, this is a minor issue.

The second third of the game is the worst part by far. The card game becomes an RNG-fest, and the experimental elements feel too similar to other games that may have done them more justice. The pacing slows to a crawl, and I felt like I won or lost on my opening hand. I felt like the deckbuilding option was not helping because the cards simply aren't synergistic enough to warrant all the different things they could do. I almost gave up at this stage, honestly. However, couching the weakest third in the middle is a good move. I just wish it had been balanced more.

The final third is redemptive, but never recaptures the magic of the first third. I like the shift in card mechanics, and the reliance on RNG had mostly vanished. The return of experimental elements from the first third is also welcome. The new elements (souls-like type stuff) works really well. Losing isn't overly punishing but rather creates more time to practice and learn different strategies to win intentionally by "getting gud."

I found the underlying narrative fascinating enough to explore it when I didn't have to, so that's a good sign for any game narrative. What it all added up to, I can't say for sure. There seem to have been ARG clues scattered about that I didn't have time to explore, but I always welcome that type of gameplay for others if it's well done.

The takeaway for me is that there's tons of potential for this particular mix of gameplay styles and mechanics, especially concerning the first third. Prospector is best waifu. Talking cards is a really engaging narrative/hint/tutorial mechanic. Escape the room games rock when well designed. Mysteries abound.

Love the ambition here, and I'm thoroughly impressed that it held together at all. Just might've needed more time in the oven, which unfortunately isn't always possible. Daniel Mullins will do great things in the future, I'm sure. I'm glad this game is becoming so successful because it means that the designer(s) will have more resources to try something new and potentially brilliant once again.

Keep on shining, you mad scientist.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile my overall experience with Inscryption was positive and fun, i feel i must give this game a negative review not because it is bad, but because the game you are purchasing is not the entire game.

it's hard to explain more without spoiling anything, but if you are looking at this game and thinking that playing a roguelite card game against a creepy shadow man in a cabin, featuring escape-room elements and everything else pictured on the tin would be fun, then i must advise that you look elsewhere. While everything listed IS in the game, it is not the entirety of the game. After a point, the game becomes much different, and many players who have gotten to this point have found themselves disappointed.

You can basically think of Inscyption as being almost like Doki Doki Literature club. It looks like one thing on the surface, but quickly changes into something only tangentially related at best. The game is still fun of course, but it's not the game you initially paid for.

If you're looking for a roguelite card game with a talkative and interested GM, i'd recommend either of the "Hand of Fate" games. While the second one is a direct sequel, i don't believe you need to have played the original in order to fully understand it.

If you've played the developer's other games Pony Island and The Hex, or other similar games like Doki Doki, then you might want to check out Inscryption. Just understand that what you see on the steam page is only the tip of the iceberg.\t\t\t\t\t
