\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game got a lot of praise and this would be completely justified, when considering only the first act. Unfortunately this game includes three separate games, one great, two terrible.
The first one is a fine card game with great narration. The balance is horrible, but that's intended, as breaking the game is a story theme.
The second game is far too easy, as it ditches all progression loss on failure and lets the player retry, until even the most stupid plan works. A lot of new mechanics are introduced, but understanding of none is required, as retrying with a conventional glass cannon deck works all too well.
The third part brings back progression loss. Also, it takes away every possibility to change to a different deck. This might mean getting trapped with an unplayable deck, which isn't all that unlikely, as the third act introduces a lot of new mechanics, which aren't explained at all. Yes, these mechanics were also in the second game, but weren't explained there either. I don't know, how this could make it through testing.
This review might be considered to be a spoiler, but I think, it is better to get some parts of the experience spoiled, than ending up with a game, that spoils itself.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPicked this game up because a few people said it was game of the year. Didn't enjoy it at all, but I kept playing because I was wondering if there's something that shows up later that makes it fun. Ended up playing through the entire game but I still didn't find anything that was fun for me. I like Slay the Spire, strategy, cards, rpg, all that stuff. This game felt like such a slog. I was finished learning the gameplay mechanics a few hours in. The game introduces a few more things in the later hours but it's more like a variation of what came before- it doesn't require any new learning or thinking. So basically I was done feeling any challenge from the game at around 2-3 hours and the remaining few dozen card battles were just wasting my time. All sorts of random, somewhat interesting enemy powers, but the player's powers are very simple. The answer is almost always: Hit Them Very Hard. Specifically I took a bifurcated strike unit, overclocked it, and just went for face as soon as it was playable each match. Won pretty much every match, and I didn't have to turn my brain on at all. I think that with strategy and card games there is an idea that the more you play the better you can get, and if you really enjoy the challenge, games are just going to keep getting less and less fun as you improve your skills. I dunno. Or maybe Inscryption is just too easy and there's no difficulty setting anywhere.

And beyond the gameplay, I also wasn't really interested in the story. (Story spoilers ahead, obviously)

The stupid Lucky Carder. He's so annoying. In general I enjoy a more straightforward story. There is no resolution, no clarity, in Inscryption. Characters will talk about ♥♥♥♥, make spooky foreshadowing statements, but generally nothing is ever explicitly stated. There are deep lore dives on the net that put together all of the unclear babble that the characters spout, but I hate stories that can only be understood with heavy analysis like that. It's really just hiding the fact that the game is about a very simple idea that isn't really a narrative: some random card game enthusiast found a creepypasta game at a garage sale, and now he's haunted, or some ♥♥♥♥ like that. It's enough for a short story, but why then does my steam record say that I played for 9 hours? If it were shorter, at least I could've wasted less time trying to find out what the game of the year was all about. The only thing I can say that was somewhat interesting about the story were the characters in the 2nd half of the game. The frame narrative with the Lucky Carder was boring, but I somewhat connected with the characters introduced in the 2nd half of the story.

Obviously there has to be some reason that several gaming sites listed Inscryption as their game of the year. I believe it's due to the weird ♥♥♥♥ in the game. The game includes live-action video, and lots of 'meta' interaction directly with the player. I think one of the most interesting mechanics was how it swapped the 'graphics' a lot. Like sometimes the game just completely looks different, even if it still plays the same. It never looks amazing, but it does seem like there was some work put into it, especially to render the game multiple different ways, by a small developer. It's an interesting idea to think about- how games can actually look completely different, but still have the same underlying game mechanics.

Unfortunately, for me, game mechanics are still really important. I remember when I started the game, I made it to the Angler boss and was beating him, but then the Angler said something like, "No! Too Fast!" and instantly killed me. That pretty much set the mood for the rest of the game: too easy, and any losses were basically scripted. Not really fun. I have absolutely played better card games.

Inscryption has some interesting mechanics and explores some cool ideas that most other games don't, but it doesn't break any ground in terms of card game mechanics. I'd be fine if people were calling it 'cool indie game of the year,' but for me, it wasn't a fun game, and it didn't have an interesting story. It was a weird and unpleasant experience, that felt like a waste of time, and I don't recommend this game to people looking for a good game or story. If your special interest is reading spooky creepypasta stories and wading through hours of unpleasant and unengaging card battles, then have it, I guess, but I don't think that applies to most people.\t\t\t\t\t
