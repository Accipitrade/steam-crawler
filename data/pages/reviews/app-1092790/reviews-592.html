\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSpoiler free review: It's good. I think it had too many ideas and is a perfect example of why not adhering to a core principal can bring a great experience down to simply a good one. Would recommend but temper expectations.


Spoiler Review:

I think Inscryption has one of the best first few hours of any game I've played in awhile. The dark, mysterious setting, obvious secrets you need to unravel, puzzles to solve, and the promise that losing only makes the next time easier. I love card games, I enjoy deck builders, and I adore horror. No brainer for me. The issue is that this is only one part of three. Had Inscryption revolved entirely around the themes of its first act it would have been a homerun, but it doesn't.

Upon beating Leshy you're immediately confronted with the first shortcoming of the story; the meta narrative. This is no longer a dark and spooky story about playing a game with life or death stakes... It's a video game as experienced by some kind of video creator named Luke Carder. Weird, but it could loop around in a satisfying way perhaps.

Act 2 completely ditches the low poly style for an 8 bit free roaming deck builder. In terms of the narrative, it's central to introduce the various characters that will serve as the focus for the rest of the experience. You're set up to take over one of the Scrybes, a mythical creator of cards.

You're immediately assaulted with 3 other decks, play styles, and mechanics that are kind of incompatible with one another. The gem deck needs resource cards, beasts are what you play in the first act, undead use bones, and the machines are all about building circuits or something. I ended up mixing gems and beasts as a good mix of utility and power. Overall the gameplay was fine, presentation was lackluster and felt like a downgrade.

After beating this the meta narrative shows its true colors. This is where I kind of lost faith in the meta narrative. Weird employees knocking on Luke's door, bad things happening in the night, it all presents itself like a generic creepypasta. I don't fully understand the intention but it feels like it goes nowhere and just wastes time.

Act 3 is a sort of return to form. You're locked up with P03, forced to play a game and help with what it dubs "The grand transcendence." Key mechanics change though. Death is just a set back, you're forced to play the machine cards, and there's not a lot of secrets or puzzles to find. I found this part particularly frustrating because occasionally you'd just roll bad RNG and be stonewalled at an encounter 3-5 times.

Towards the end you're presented with the culmination of everything; the 3 main scrybes are trying to take down P03 in order to stop its plan. The plan being.... To release Inscryption? There's some fiddling with your files and computer tomfoolery, perhaps the ultimate plan is to take over the world by taking over computers via Inscryption? It felt underdeveloped.

Naturally you help the Scrybes overthrow P03 and start deleting Inscryption. The finale really confused me. There's time taken to develop assets and scenarios for the Scrybes you don't face in the 3d world. Graveyard chess and a full blown Yu-Gi-Oh Duel Disk hologram battle that are invalidated after maybe 10 minutes of gameplay. At the very end you simply see Luke trying to destroy the floppy disk and then getting killed by (presumably) a GameFuna employee in order to take the disk back.

Post-Game you're given the option to run through a challenge based version of the first act which is really what I wanted from the beginning. It's a good saving grace.

Overall, there was a decision to focus into a meta narrative that undermined the worldbuilding and characters of the actual game being presented. I don't think this was the right decision. Meta narratives are very tricky to pull off and when your game is two completely separate parts (The game you play vs home videos you're watching in between acts) it may be a hint that the pieces aren't connecting properly. Luke's random comments also took me out of the gameplay when they came up.

I liked it, a lot of this reads as negative because I know it could have been much more. What is here though is worth buying.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYeah, this... this was quite the ride! You really don't have the slightest idea where this game is gonna take you, and you shouldn't, that's what makes it so interesting. At the core it will always be a simple trading card game (think something like Hearthstone, but against the computer), but that's not the point, that's not what you play it for. The card game aspect is passable and the rules change regularly enough that it doesn't get boring (in fact, "beating" it often just means figuring out how to abuse the current set of rules in gamebreaking ways, which can be quite fun). But the real treat is the story with its never ending WTFness and always wondering what the hell will surprise you around the next corner again.

The ending is particularly satisfying, giving you a nice little "what if?" treat that you'll be wanting for at that point but without dragging it out longer than necessary. And strangely enough, it's actually quite sad. Never thought I'd feel sorry for the old bastard at the end.

Good game, Leshy. Good game.\t\t\t\t\t
