The first part of the game is brilliant and mixes a card game with elements of an escape room in a way that's both genuinely new and cleverly executed, but after you escape from the room it goes very downhill and gets more than a little dull and repetitive. After winning the game you get to unlock playing the first part again but under much more annoying rules which make it no fun any more. It's definitely worth the money but just play the first chapter and then stop: life's too short.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really can't think of the last time I felt so conflicted over a game. There is a lot of good and there is some really bad bad. I think much of the praise it receives is because of some trendiness of certain types of stories in recent years. Part of the problem with reviewing this game is that it expects you to know nothing going in.

To prevent spoilers, I am going to explain my thoughts in three separate sections. 1. The basics, no spoilers. 2. Minor spoilers. No plot details, but we'll discuss style and subject. The game is best experienced without this knowledge. 3. Heavy spoilers. Plot details.

1. (Spoiler free) You will be playing a card game with rogue-like progression. The card game itself is incredibly fun but never meets its full potential, especially in terms of difficulty. If you're like me, around the time you fully understand how the game works, its over. There will be no significant challenges for you. That isn't to say the gameplay isn't fun - finding new ways to absolutely break and devastate your opponents can be nearly endlessly entertaining. But it will not really test you. The rogue-like elements are cool for the first few runs. You will probably die 3-5 times even if you are good at it. But the card game is only half the game. After all, you are in a creepy cabin with a creepy (serial killer?) man with whom you are playing this card game. You are able to leave the board between battles and explore the cabin. There are some fairly good puzzles to solve. I'm terrible at puzzles so I found them to be intellectually stimulating but, if I'm being honest, you will probably find them relatively trivial. It is also quite possible to beat the game having never solved several puzzles and your experience will be much worse for it. I really should note that the game is quite charming and the gameplay is engaging. If it wasn't, I wouldn't be so upset by the lack of challenge or the missable puzzles. If you like the gameplay then you may at one point find yourself saying "Wait, seriously? That's it, I can't keep playing?". (I will not further contextualize in this section) Don't worry. There is an infinite post game for the rogue-like card game after the story is over.

2. (Minor spoilers)
Alright, let me just get this out of the way. This game is a meta-story. It is one of those very popular sort of games right now in which the story of the game is about a person playing the game. I am aware that Incryption's creator is known for these sorts of projects and it doesn't affect my opinion. If you love this sort of thing then you will love the twists. Personally, I have become exhausted of this method of storytelling. The acting during the "cutscenes" is about high school theater quality. The overarching story is interruptive and unnecessary. A serial killer playing a magical card game in a secret cabin was fascninating enough. I played the game because I loved the premise. The story is divided into three acts and the initial concept is only used in the first act. The second act tries at a "retro" style game and is by far the worst section of the game. It is also the longest. Or maybe it just felt the longest. The card game reaches new lows and the story drags. Act 3 is a bit better but still falls flat.

3. (SPOILERS) (PLOT DETAILS AHEAD) (YOU HAVE BEEN WARNED)
So, what is Inscryption actually about? Yare not playing Inscryption and you are not playing as you. You are playing as a vlogger name Luke ("The Lucky Carder") who picks up a haunted videogame adaptation of Inscryption. The "real life" story is told through badly acted videotapes. The beginning of the actual game story involves Leshy, a murder-y guy cloaked in shadows, who has taken over the in-game world and is killing you repeatedly inside it. Every time you die, a new card is created based on you. That card may be appear in your next run. It is a really cool idea for a rogue-like and I loved it! You are able to escape Leshy by completing a couple of the puzzles in the cabin and then beating his in each stage of the rogue-like. After you escape, Act 2 starts and you are in the "old" code. For some reason, this digital card game contains an older retro version of itself. There are four primary characters. Leshy (who we've discussed), an evil robot, a crazy wizard, and an old lady who talks to the dead. You choose one of them who you will seek to defeat and replace in their domain. The gameplay here is insufferable. The graphics are a really lackluster take on retro. New mechanics are introduced but they aren't fun.

Upon completing the "old" game's story, the game corrupts again and the evil robot takes over everything. Interestingly, if you were attempting to replace him he has some special dialogue for that. Based on that, I assumed that the game had four paths following Act 2. This is not the case, which is fine. But the actual conclusion to the game is lackluster.

Act 3 involves the evil robot playing a similar game with you as Leshy did earlier, only now you aren't allowed to leave the table. And everything is blue. Like, really blue. This segment of the game is a constant eye-sore. Act 3 does introduce gameplay that is a sort of refined combination of the previous two acts with real challenge and fun mechanics. (Such as customized cards, which you can send to other players over the internet!) Eventually evil robot will let you leave the table to complete tasks for him. The other characters are secretly hiding in his lair and planning to (kill?) overthrow him. You will eventually beat the robot and then the old lady decides to destroy the world/game so that no one will ever suffer through this again. You play some final additional variations of the card game with each of the characters. There are some neat ideas that are prototyped here. The wizard plays a parody of Yu-Gi-Oh with you. All of these ending games are basically cutscenes. You can't win or lose. You are meant to be listening to the characters' final dialogues here. These are genuinely good closing remarks by each character. Leshy left as a somewhat contrived friend, but I still felt attached and hurt by his death.

I never even mentioned Kaycee, who I guess is the creator of the in-universe digital game. She died working on it and at first it seems like she may haunt it. Then she is implied to be the "good guy" of the story and there is some great darkness hidden in the old code. Neither Kaycee nor this evil force will ever be completely elaborated on because meta games thrive on theorist communities and Inscryption will need this to make its story coherent.

Final thoughts: Inscryption is awful. I hate it so so much and I have never had more fun with a digital card game in my life. I cannot recommend it enough. It is stupid, cringey, and painfully bad for the majority of its main story, and it is one of the few games I would consider rating a "10/10". (If I really believed numeric rating meant anything) I can't wait for you to hate it with me.

Or love it. I don't know, a lot of people really talk about this game as a masterpiece. It is a fine stance to take and sometimes I feel that way too. I think that you are unlikely to ever forget Inscryption. I think you will want to tell someone how you feel after playing it. I think it is the sort of game that makes people who rarely write any sort of serious review pour their heart out in essay-length ramblings such as this.\t\t\t\t\t
