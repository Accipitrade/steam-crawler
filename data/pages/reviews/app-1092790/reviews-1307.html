\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is very engrossing. The game makes you want to keep playing just so you can understand the plot better, and the rules of the card game keep changing, making things fresh and difficult again just as the card game was starting to become boring and easy.

The game is separated into three parts: the first part is roguelike -- if you die, you lose your whole deck and you have to start over from the beginning. The second part is very forgiving: every opponent only needs to be defeated once and you don't lose any progress when you die. The third part is somewhere in-between in terms of dying and difficulty: there are checkpoints and some enemies respawn if you die before reaching the next checkpoint.

Don't be discouraged if you die a handful of times in the first part -- the game gives you better cards and more power-ups after you've died several times. Indeed some of the game's secrets can only be found by dying, or by losing an eye, etc.

A common theme in the reviews is that people didn't like the second and third parts of Inscryption as much as they liked the first part. The Steam achievements corroborate this -- 75% of players have the achievement for beating the first part, but less than 50% of players have the achievement for beating the second part. And there are *a lot* of reviews with less than 10 hours of game time, which means that a lot of people went ahead and wrote a positive review even though they never finished the game. While I acknowledge that the atmosphere in the second and third parts isn't as rich as the atmosphere in the first part, the card game actually becomes more interesting (and more difficult) toward the end of the game and I personally enjoyed the additional complexity and difficulty.

I highly recommend this game, and furthermore I recommend that anyone who didn't finish parts 2 and 3 go back and finish the game.\t\t\t\t\t
