\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was not expecting this game to be so captivating as I played well past the time I was supposed to go sleep.

It's a card deck building game with a rougelike element to it set in an escape room with a creepy (but not really scary) Saw x Dungeons & Dragons like game master antagonist, and PLENTY of puzzles and mysteries to unravel. There will be a lot of things you will miss out on your first playthrough, if you are going into this blind as I did.

The cards' designs (mostly inspired by woodland animals or urban legends) are unique and their special abilities called 'sigils' and the cost to play them make each one of them special and they can be tailored for any player's strategy. I always found myself looking forward to the next card to be found in each run. The talking cards and their personalities were charming tidbits of the game.

The soundtrack and sound effects for the game fits very well with events that unfold. Each sacrifice is felt, bone tokens pile up noisily, and the scale heralds satisfying victory (with clanking teeth fillings, if you're lucky) or dreadful defeat followed by the blowing out of a candle's flame. The boss battle music mixed with attributive sound effects are both eerie and fitting.

Without giving away too much of the story, I would say that Inscryption (on the surface) is really great short game. However, as you begin unfolding the mystery it turns into a very different game altogether with new card playing elements yet not the same thrill or level of intrigue when first starting out, which is a shame. In short, I believe it that Inscryption would have been better without it being given an overdrawn story that dilutes the player's immersion with a half-explained ending that I would search for answers on Google later.

Overall, I would definitely recommend this game for those who love playing tabletop games, escape rooms, or eureka lovers, in general. I'm on my second playthrough now and I imagine that I will be on my third one shortly after. 9 out of 10.\t\t\t\t\t
