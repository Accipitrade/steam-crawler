\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis review is going to be as spoiler-free as possible, but any description of this game, like most Daniel Mullins games, is some form of spoiler. Like Pony Island and The Hex, it's best to go in with as little information as possible.

Inscryption is, to my mind, the best game Daniel Mullins has made so far- it keeps all the fun, clever meta stuff, while adding lots of lovely detail and building in a set of genuinely deep card game/puzzle/deckbuilding mechanics. If your main complaint around Pony Island and The Hex was their lack of engaging gameplay, you should snag Inscryption right now. If you're not sure whether demonic horror, video game meta-commentary, and strangely emotional endings are your jam, then snag Pony Island. That game is only a couple of hours long, and it'll give you a little taste of the sort of experience you're in for.

If I had any criticism, it's that the FMV segments don't add all that much IMO. I would have been happy without them- they don't really mesh all that well with the game's tone, and stopping the gameplay to transition to watching videos feels a little jarring. I appreciate the humane design- give the player a clear point where they can turn the game off and come back later- but I wish it was done some other way. The low budget and production value, as well as the somewhat cheesy acting, really break down the great atmosphere the game had built up to that point IMO.

Overall, strong thumbs up for pretty much everybody who can tolerate a puzzle game. I devoured this game in one sitting (sat down at 6:30pm, stood up at 3:30am) and I'm already hungry for more from Daniel Mullins.\t\t\t\t\t
