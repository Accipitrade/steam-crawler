\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis has the distinction of being the first highly recommended game that left me wholly unimpressed.

I love card games and deckbuilders in particular. The card game portion of this game felt incredibly shallow and repetitive. The victory condition (dealing 5 net damage) often has little to do with what your opponent is playing so there's very little back and forth or situational strategic variety. Pursuing any synergy will quickly get you to a point where you easily win regardless of what the opponent is doing, often on the first turn. The core gameplay loop just felt like the same monotonous game of solitaire. For ten hours.

I like puzzles. Most of the puzzles in this game amounted to searching around for a combination then entering it into a safe, with a few puzzles based on the card game's rules for variety. Overall the 'escape room' layer of the game was fine, just not compelling enough to warrant the slog through the card game.

I like atmospheric horror. The first act of this game did this well enough. The narrative interludes and the meta-narrative that is expanded on in the later acts did nothing for me. Some of the game's sound effects were just grating and there were several 'RIP headphones' moments that feel like the reason QA was invented.

I like interesting stories with twists. The gameplay took a few interesting turns, but the story ... if I gave you "You're trapped inside a video game that seems to have taken on a sinister life of its own" as a writing prompt, I bet 90% of the people with the patience to read this far into my review would come up with something indistinguishable from Inscryption as a quick first draft. Let's spoilerifically show all the boxes it ticks even in my very limited sampling of the 'sinister tech/creation turning against us' genre and adjacent horror or dystopian media:

Video logs of someone getting progressively more unhinged, culminating in death: Literally every horror game, literally every found footage horror movie, that Alasdair Beckett-King sketch.
A company trying to suppress its rogue tech: Deus Ex, Devs, The Expanse, Short Circuit
The presumed helper is shown to be even worse once it usurps the bad guy: All the Dead Spaces, all the Bioshocks, Portal 2, Ex Machina
Forced to do what a rogue AI wants you to: I Have No Mouth And I Must Scream, Portal series, System Shock series, The Matrix, Upgrade
You're dragged into a game you want no part of: Tron, Westworld, Wargames, Sandkings
Things glitching as the world unravels: DDLC, Eternal Darkness, Batman: Arkham Asylum, Wreck it Ralph
Meta moments where the game pulls from actual filesystem, friendlist, etc: DDLC, IMSCARED, Eternal Darkness
Tech as an inroad for the demonic or possession: Event Horizon, Dead Space, The Ring, Poltergeist, Christine, Kairo/Pulse, Nightflyers
A sinister force trying to broadcast/upload itself and spread unchecked: Tron, Demon Seed, The Video Dead, Wreck it Ralph, Wreck it Ralph 2.
You haven't actually won: All of them. Except Short Circuit. And maybe Wreck it Ralph.
Cleaving to genre conventions is fine, but Inscryption doesn't go anywhere with them. No shocking twists, no memorable characters, no juicy writing. None of it left an impression.


I would still tepidly recommend Inscryption to gamers very different from me: people who are willing to overlook problems with gameplay or challenge or depth in writing in order to experience something with unconventional and inventive elements. But if you're discerning (and why wouldn't you be, there's 50k+ games on steam alone, at least a few hundred of which aren't cheap asset flips or furry erotica) and the games that stick with you are the ones that were firing on all cylinders, give it a pass.\t\t\t\t\t
