\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm usually not a huge fan of card games despite the best efforts from friends and coworkers. Inscryption still roused my curiosity thanks to it's unique presentation and narrative.

Inscryption is a deck building card game where you are trapped in a cabin by a malicious entity who forces you to play a board game with them. The game takes place on a linear board in which you move your character piece forward, collecting, upgrading and exchanging cards for various boons until you reach a combat encounter which is fought using the current cards in your deck.

Cards have offensive and defensive stats along with various abilities that can be exchanged or upgraded between battles. The game slowly grows in complexity as you progress and although there's an element of randomness due to the shuffling of your deck, this can be somewhat mitigated using various resources and lifelines collected as you move your piece across the board.

The entity will eventually allow you to leave the table between battles and let you explore and solve puzzles within the limited confides of the cabin from a grid-based first person perspective. Finishing these puzzles will award you with new lifelines, boons, cards etc and will eventually be crucial to make progress in the overall narrative.

The game never really gets stale and keeps hitting you with unexpected twists both from a narrative and mechanic perspective. The game does an excellent job at introducing new mechanics and then expanding upon them and exploring new features that eventually change the rules of the game. In fact, my only issue with the game is not being given enough time to fully explore the different mechanics introduced later on in the game, but I guess that's where the replay value comes in.

Overall, the game is much longer than I initially anticipated, but much shorter than I would have wanted. I felt like the ending was a bit sudden despite the game just having introduced new environments and mechanics and when the credits started rolling I found myself wanting more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor context, I had only played Mullin's Pony Island prior to this game. I was aware that he was known for being meta with his content and subverting expectations. Where I feel that type of subversion worked with a 2D sidescroller, subversions with this one are a tad more complicated.

Like a lot of other reviews are saying, the atmosphere in the cabin is phenomenal. It's grotesque, mysterious, piques your interest, and makes you want to uncover secrets. The rules of the game slowly build up, and nothing truly felt overwhelming when I was making my way to the final boss. It definitely had that 'roguelike' feel to it from the start: bosses that initially feel unfair or complex that you can eventually overcome by developing strategies and making better cards.

The problem, and as you'll see with a lot of the other negative reviews, is AFTER you defeat that final boss. Spoilers ahead.

The game takes a dive and has you explore a 2D pixel world that is more along the lines of a traditional deck-building game. Where previous mechanics could be introduced slow, now they all come at once. Where the first act was dripping with atmosphere, now it's stuff that, while lovely to look at, make me think of a game like Mother 3. It also starts the horror indie game... thing. You know what I'm talking about: the ARG, the accessing of files, the consequences bleeding into the "real" world. It's not really taking itself too seriously (the guy's name is Luke Carder for f*ck's sake), but I feel like my cynical side is talking when I say I've seen this before and it's been done.

Then we go into Act 3. We're back into the 3D environments now. Hope that you were using some of the new mechanics introduced in Act 2, because one of them, the battery charger, is now your primary way of getting cards out on the table. Also, it teaches you (somewhat) about new mechanics like conduits and gems. You know. After you just went through all of the prior act. The return to 3D is welcome, and the environment is still great to explore, especially with context picked up from previous acts, but it's more sci-fi and less horror. Your mileage may vary on the level of success this has.

Then, after you defeat the game's campaign in Act 3, the game just kind of... ends. Dissolves into that chaotic mess that I recall from Pony Island and wraps things up in a nice bow. I might go back through the game and get all the achievements/easter eggs, but I think I'd have to take a moment to let things settle.

TL;DR if you're expecting a roguelike deckbuilder along the lines of Monster Train or otherwise, then you'll probably feel ripped off, even with the context of this being a Mullin game. If you don't care about that and just want to be along for the ride, then this is definitely something fun to do.

Regardless, I had fun for what I got.\t\t\t\t\t

Still a 10/10. Unmissable experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust completed the first round a few hours ago, but I am still lost in the surprise of this game, Inscryption.

This game exactly does what a game should do. That's the reason why there are so many positive reviews here. It utilises our curiosity just right, from the trailer above, to every corner in this game. This trick is the core of Inscryption. Indeed, curiosity is the beginning of everything and it is also the only mood guiding me when I play this great game. When you are attracted by the trailer and start wondering what it is going on, you are completely trapped by this game. If so, I boldly suggest you purchase Inscryption and you will not rue I swear.

Let's talk about the design of Inscryption first. There are 3 acts here and each act has its own theme, including the art style of card battle, deck-building mindset and even the board. Everything is consistent within each act. Each act has a different theme, but card battle is still the main melody of Inscryption. Some mechanics are creative and fun in card battle game. I particularly like the mechanic creating a new card by yourselfwith each time you lose in act 1, to help you further in the following.

Because I think Inscryption is a story-rich puzzle game, I will not mention too specific here. The rogue-lite card battle system is the main body of Inscryption, but actually Inscryption is a puzzle game. This game really know what is attracting players in a game. Real players keep exploring something new and fresh and digging up the hiddens with or without cues, just like Ready Player One. As I said, curiosity is the beginning for all (This make me remember The Stanley Parable). Without curiosity, you are still sitting there playing a card battle game with a stranger all days. You don't care who your opponent is, what happen when you lose in battle and why there is no "new game" button in menu. If you wonder the answers of above questions, you will surely reveal the hidden truth. Inscryption cleverly keep presenting some pieces of truth in front of you (from the trailer) and no one can ignore them continuously. Finally, you open the Pandora's box as well, just like what Lucky Carder did in the story. Every player is Lucky Carder and Lucky Carder is everyone here. What we are doing in this game is just chasing the hidden truth with pieces of cues, and when the truth is revealed, we are immersed in huge sense of accomplishment and excitement exactly.

Just enjoy this, Marvelous.\t\t\t\t\t
