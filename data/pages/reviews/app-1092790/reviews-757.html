\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhy is the Stoat talking to me? What are the knick-knacks littered around the cabin? Why is the New Game button disabled on the starting screen? You will find out. You will feel every twist and turn—and there are many, oh so many—as the world of Inscryption slowly reveals itself to you.

From the start, I was enthralled by the tight, simple, yet profound card-battler gameplay. The game changes in myriad ways, but that core is never really altered. And that, I think, keeps the game compelling: that the same formula can be dressed up in so many ways to carry you through the story.

The STORY. I'm not much of a story guy in video games, but this one compelled me. Without spoiling too much, you might have heard that the game is divided into three acts. Perhaps it's a controversial view, but I liked each of them the same. I did progress much more quickly through the latter portions of the game; I finished Act II in two sittings and Act III in less than a week, after taking multiple weeks of off-and-on gameplay to conquer Act I. In some sense, I wish those latter acts had been longer—but they're also when the story of the game becomes more apparent, so I was more attuned to progressing and finishing the story than to simply playing a card game in a cabin as I was in Act I. Fortunately, once you finish the game, you can play Kaycee's Mod, a modified version of Act I severed from the three-act story.

It took me just shy of fifteen hours to finish the game. I've been too busy to fire up the game since then, but you can replay the base game, of course—perhaps trying to uncover more secrets or try new meta strategies—or delve into Kaycee's Mod and its numerous challenges. Through those means, the game provides replay value beyond its already-rich story.

One suggestion: go into Inscryption as blind as possible. This game's story is nothing without its constant twists and turns, each one masterfully done. The wiki and subreddit are replete with spoilers, and I think the game is transparent enough for you to be able to develop your own strategies without their help; once I discovered a few core strategic principles, I was able to do fairly well in the rest of the game. A possible exception is the cabin puzzles in Act I; it took some Googling to find out I'd missed a key item when I thought I'd solved all the brainteasers I needed to progress.

Inscryption has earned every single one of its plentiful accolades. Play it. You won't regret it.\t\t\t\t\t
