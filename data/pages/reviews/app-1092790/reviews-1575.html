\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of the best games I have ever played, to the point it lands squarely in my "play this before you die" category.

I have experience with Daniel Mullins prior, as I played his genre hopping mystery game "The Hex" on a whim when I was sorting through my games library. The Hex is a densely layered creation that established Mullin's experience with ARGs and hidden messages. If you have not played that game, you should, because it has substantial links to Inscryption's worldbuilding.

Inscryption feels like the culmination of Mullin's experiences building his last two games, and the result is a perfect representation of his craft. It is, on the surface, a deckbuilding strategy game very similar to other seminal games in the genre. You immediately get a sense there is more than meets the eye after a couple minutes of playing the game, however. The cards start communicating with you, the "game master" drops cryptic messages, and soon the game world opens up as you are allowed to stand up from the table. From there on, it is off to the races. You begin to piece together the world both from interacting with the cabin, and playing the game itself. The game is separated into 3 acts, with a final coda. Each of the acts maintains the core gameplay loop of the deckbuilder, but your perception of the game world is drastically altered.

The game is not what it appears to be, and as you play, your experience will drastically change. Do not expect what's on the tin. Your understanding of the game will help guide you through the journey, and will help you understand key parts of the mystery. Read and pay attention to everything, and you will be rewarded with a transcendent narrative and greater overarching mystery for you to sink your teeth into.

This is a game where I feel I cannot truly review it without spoiling things. That is frustrating to me, but I don't want this review to be a synopsis or a guide. Play it. Let's reach the Great Transcendence together.\t\t\t\t\t
