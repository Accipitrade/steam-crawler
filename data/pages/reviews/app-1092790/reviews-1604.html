\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEnjoy playing card games? What about escape rooms? Or RPGs? With Inscryption, you get all three combined into a delicious, breath-taking package.

You start off as an unknown player in a dark cabin. A pair of eyes appear in the dark on the other side of the table. You’re not really sure what’s going on…and the game begins. While Inscryption markets itself as a horror card game, it’s so much more than that. There are no jump scares or horrifying demons trying to eat you up (or are there…). But the immersive setting constantly gives the sense of something not being quite right, and you definitely need to get out!

Inscryption has many twists and turns, most of which I did not get into for this part of the review. A lot of the fun lies in the surprise! Find out for yourself, or if you’re still curious, I’ll dive a bit deeper in the next section. Overall, I’d give Inscryption a 9/10. Excellent gameplay, art, and storyline. The only thing that lets it down somewhat is that later on some of the “randomised” elements that made it more fun get a bit lost, and some of the usage of mixed media could have connected better to the wider game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption can be described as a outstanding deck building card game with a beautiful chilling atmosphere that encapsulates all that we ever wanted in a game. That is, until the curtains unveil, and we meet the second and third act. Whilst the basics of the cards stay intact; the cards get a (frankly) jarring overhaul each act. Whilst some may like this aspect the majority of us will feel cheated, as did I when I expected a slay the spire-esque game with a twist of its own. However ended up utterly disappointed and unable to refund after I experienced the joys of the first act whilst taking my time. Which ended up being a costly mistake as after finishing the act I was left unable to refund due to too many hours played. Overall I would recommend this if your mainly interested in the ARG aspect of this game and its somewhat interesting story. Otherwise if your like me and only liked the first acts style, i'd wait for the off chance the developer makes a endless mode for the first act (and is heavily discounted might I add) otherwise, its a hard pass for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption's first act is as good as other reviewers claim it is: atmospheric, creative, thought-provoking, and much more. There's fun to be had by trying and adapting your strategies around the cards and totems you find along the way. It's a roguelike at its finest (and I hope to keep enjoying it with Kaycee's Mod). That's what is leading me to recommend this game.

Arriving on the second act, however, the roguelike mechanics were left behind, and so was my fun. I loved pondering what path to pick as much as I HATE the idea of spending dozens of minutes picking card combinations that will most likely not even work at the end of the day. Especially when there are so many cards to choose between and no limitations from the game to try and guide you on what cards you should pick for your deck to be minimally functional.

I didn't want to force myself to play act two, so I decided to watch a playthrough of the rest of the game. In my opinion, the story goes all over the place, but at least gameplay-wise, act three seems to do act one a lot more justice than the second. Anyway, just be sure to note that the game's variety doesn't always mean good things beyond the first act.\t\t\t\t\t
