\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWriting this review after finishing it last night with a lot of strong feelings. TL/DR will be this: I recommend this game if it is on sale as the Act 1 of the game is very much worth it. The rest of the game I have a harder time vouching for.

The game starts off so strong it's ridiculous. The concept is refreshing and unique and has that easy to learn, hard to master element that is so crucial in card games as well as the allure of the cabin elements that offer fun puzzles and discovery on a first play through. With the addition of the Kaycee's Mod support in the main game, I can understand the glowing reviews as that is the strength of the game. I am uncertain exactly how much replay-ability this section offers as the game was clearly designed to push players past into the other sections but I may revisit it and experiment with the game more in the future.

SPOILERS BELOW
The rest of this game is what suffers the most for me. After a stellar opening that is challenging enough to keep you gated for a while as you learn the mechanics and forcing multiple runs with Leshy and his mechanics and cabin puzzles the game throws you into act 2. The initial sell here is honestly quite good. Forbidden from starting a new game until the option unlocks here is quite interesting and discovering that the card game has quite a few more mechanics to expand on is exciting at first but falls apart quickly. The conduit and gem systems are clunky and frankly not interesting enough to play with. Act 2 finishes quite quickly as you can find a mechanic that abuses the base system quickly and face bash into all challenges until your deck wins naturally. Act 3 is where the game completely loses me.

The PO3 section is an unfun slog that feels more like a chore than a compelling card game guided by a narrative. It's a large section of the game that constantly acknowledges how parts are not fun, unfinished, or bad. A game accurately pointing out its faults does not fix them or make up for its own downfalls.

Some of the mechanics of Act 3 are interesting in the moment with the ability to build cards and modify existing ones but it still falls short and the amount of fights becomes a labor I completed to finish the game. The bounty hunter system seems created as a mix to add variety but also artificially lengthen this section even more. Overall, this section was more of a headache than anything, while clever moments were present, they were quickly washed out by the slog of the game.

The Finale is mostly a story section that feels unearned aside from Leshy's game as that's where the heart of the game was originally. His world disappearing as he begged to just play even if no one was keeping score was actually touching and felt well placed. And then you play wizard yu-gi-oh with duel disks with my least favorite system present in the game for far too long as the game glitches and breaks.

Speaking of the story, It does not know what it wants to be. Again it is strongest at the beginning and the reveal of Luke Carder as our narrative voice is interesting, a haunted card game on a floppy disk raises questions and his paranormal experiences that we view through his recording footage became a driving force on me playing more in the back half of the game. But the game will trade narrative strength for gimmicks in Act 3, using personal files and info of the player to make jokes and bring steam friends in as enemy cards but I thought this was Luke Carder?? Why are his recordings showing my files and my friends appearing? It has the air of unedited ideas being shoved into an already bloated game without consideration for consequence on the narrative.

If you made it this far, thank you for reading my ramblings. Perhaps this game was effective at what it did simply for the fact I've put so much thought into it but I'm unsure. It's like a delicious ice cream sandwich that had mayonnaise added to it halfway through. It's hard to think about how good the ice cream sandwich was when I started, because now I can't stop thinking about the mayonnaise. \t\t\t\t\t
