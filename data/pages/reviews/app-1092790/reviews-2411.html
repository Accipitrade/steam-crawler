Inscryption starts as a deck-building / rogue-like game in a disquieting setting. You're forced to play the card game with.. well.. all you can see is a pair of eyes and.. maybe if you get close enough.. *ʷʰᶦˢᵖᵉʳˢ* ᴵᵗ ʷᵒⁿ'ᵗ ˡᵉᵗ ᵐᵉ ˢᵃʸ ᶦᵗ.. After a while you are allowed to leave the play area, to walk around the room you in which are being held captive. Not too long though. Sit down boy (and/or lady). You need to play the game. This stout isn't going to sacrifice itself.

Strangely enough I'd say that the main focus of this game isn't so much about that mysterious card game. The focus of the game seems to be towards breaking through the reality of Inscryption being a game. It's reaching out to the world outside of it. Then it tries to go beyond that. And further. It might even start to reach g̴̛̱̝̞̮̰̜̎̉͑͠ļ̷̡͚͙͔͎̟͓͍̩͚̱̖̦̹̊̽̈́͊͑͝i̷̢͙̩̙͈̤̖͈̟̘͛̒̈̇̿̕͘͝t̴̛̛̻̲̠͗͒͑͂̀̀͂͒͐̀c̴̢̡̲̝̺͕̼̣̔̂̎͗̌̊͌́̀̊̌͘͝ḩ̷̡̢̱̘̫͖͍̈́͂́̽́̋̆̃͜͠. This game is about playing a game, that makes you aware of the world outside of the game. Characters that start realizing that they are in fact fictitious beings. This is what made this game so unique and stand out from the others. The narrative kept me hooked through the entire game. The further you progress, the more surprises the game will throw at you. The card game itself is easy enough to learn- though it might be a challenging start for people that don't play card games often. And since it's a rogue-lite like game, please realize: you're gonna die a few times. It's okaay, you're supposed to. ᴺᵒᵗ ᵐᵉ ᵗʰᵒᵘᵍʰ, ᴵ ᵈᶦᵈⁿ'ᵗ ᵈᶦᵉ, ᴵ'ᵐ ᵃ ᵖʳᵒ ᵍᵃᵐᵉʳ. The game blends various genres as you progress further. Each time there were fun and challenging new additions- yet nothing too complicated, easy to learn. Puzzles were challenging at times, but if you keep playing you'll get hints eventually. That reminds me: If you play this game, and you should, promise me you won't look up anything. (We all know that only nincompoops use guides- you're not a nincompoop are you?) Just skip something and come back later. It'll be better for your brain, I promise.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is a roguelite with its own creative gimmick, that slowly builds on that and becomes something far greater. In the most basic, non-spoiler way I can describe it, it starts with a simple roguelite and simple gimmick, as well as some puzzles thrown in. It then becomes a short, 8Bit styled card RPG that throws out the rogue aspect of it and becomes a much easier difficulty. Finally, it once again shifts into a partially-roguelike chapter that combines almost all gimmicks together.

Gimmicks: 8/10
Inscryption keeps you on your toes with constantly disappearing and reappearing gimmicks, as well as a creative ability or idea for each boss. Though at points it can get a bit overwhelming with around four ways to play cards, it allows you to make your own strategy for each chapter.

Quality: 10/10
This game is really something in its varying quality, switching from 8-Bit style to realistically pixelated to a simple IRL camera and back, with such grace that it feels like the lore was a real event that was recorded and captured. I absolutely love the style and quality of all 5 chapters.

Bosses: 11/10
The gimmicks for the bosses were truly creative and amazing, and I loved almost every single one of them, especially during the factory chapter. The changing card strategies required to easily destroy the boss keep you awake and alert as you never really play a major battle with the same rules. Each boss has a different personality that somewhat corresponds to their gimmick, such as an unintelligent fisherman that will take your cards (referring to them and nearly everything as fish), but a constant urge to take only the freshest one.

Strategy: 8/10
While trying to use all of the card tricks at once can be a bit overwhelming, these can all be used separately and to your own liking, making a bunch of strategies for everyone to use.

Music: Around 6/10
This is probably just not my kind of music, so to be honest, I wont include it in the overall rating. It just feels more like, in most cases, either a simple beat with a simple song or ambiance (Save for Magnificus' theme in the 8Bit chapter, I really like that one :D)

Lore: 8/10
The lore is definitely the good kind, though Im sadly that kind of person that can't find it that easily. I wont go into detail on the story, in-game nor irl, but I will tell you that the story can confuse some until they truly dive deep for it.

Child Safety: 13+
The game, if not already obvious, is a bit dark due to the sacrificial beginning and ominous characters in chapter 1. However, I should also add that one of the bosses could be possibly acting a bit sexual towards the size of your files (Yes, I mean files, like on the computer), though it's a bit hard to read emotions when it's a computer that says it. There is one major thing at the end, and big spoilers: In the end, when one of the IRL main characters, Luke, opens the door to yell at a worker from the game company, there's no words before, no signs, she raises a gun and shoots him in the head. There's no shots of his dead body other than his hand, but there is a lot of blood and it's very obvious that he did indeed die. The age minimum can vary, but I recommend 13.

Overall, 10/10, I would definitely recommend this game to people that like puzzles, as well as card games and a bit of mild horror.\t\t\t\t\t
