\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNow that I've unlocked all the achievements (which isn't necessary I just do it for games I really enjoy *hint hint*) I can finally write a review for Inscryption.

TL;DR if you're anything like me, a complete amateur at card-games who quit Hearthstone after 40 minutes cause it was boring and overly complicated, as well as someone who doesn't routinely play Roguelikes OR card-games and prefers to shoot rather than think, this game is 100% worth your money.

For starters, unlike Hearthstone, Inscryption doesn't immediately bloat your deck with 30 cards and 28 different card sets, you start with 4 bog standard cards. They have no special features, no high stats, and no real redeeming qualities, and yet they are your best and most versatile cards. You slowly learn how you can play these 4 starter cards, and adapt them to different deck types, playstyles, and builds.

Sigils add additional benefits to each card, and can be customized as you need, or even combined for truly devastating results. Need a card with high health to jump to open spaces preventing the enemy from damaging you until it dies? There's a sigil for that. Want a card that can hit 3 units at once killing any opposing units it touches in one strike regardless of health? There's sigils for that. Want a unit that has no cost to play but can be sacrificed for blood infinitely AND returns to your hand to be played again upon being slain in combat? You guessed it, there's Sigils for that. The combinations are endless, devastating, and occasionally broken. In addition, there's a rulebook you can easily and frequently access, meaning anything that looks different, new, or strange, can be immediately read up on and played to your advantage.

But what's the draw? The story? How does Inscryption get you invested and continue playing? Honestly the entirety of Act 1 (of 3 total acts) is the draw. All the screenshots you see on the store page are from the first act and continually get you interested and invested in the game as a whole, asking a whole bunch of questions and never really getting answers until you hit act two. You start it up and wonder, "Who's this dude in front of me? What's the deal with the blinking door? The statues? The talking cards? What in the heck is going on?". The answers were not only interesting, but somewhat unforseen as well. Especially the ending, though the acting overall was a bit cheesy.

But criticism always follows positive thoughts, and mine like many others is that acts 2 and 3 just weren't as impactful as act 1. Act 1 really sucks you in with its atmosphere and environment, so when it changes in 2 and 3, it feels somewhat hollow by comparison. That's not to say Acts 2 and 3 were bad, just that it felt by comparison something was missing to really keep you invested unlike Act 1. In addition Act 3 was not as well done in my opinion compared to Act 1. It felt almost mandatory to grind for Overkill Points to bolster and have a varied deck, and having to manually click and walk across practically empty landscapes to continuously grind in the mechanical oppressive atmosphere that was set up was almost unbearable. In addition Act 2 is supposed to be your tutorial into Act 3, opening up and learning about different options and playstyles that weren't previously available in Act 1, but by the time you complete Act 2 there's really no point to any of them as none of the other styles offer enough benefits or variation to be worth choosing over the standard deck that was handed to you in Act 1.

All in all, Inscryption is an incredibly solid experience that offers all skill levels a fresh, gripping experience that can be replayed endlessly. Fans are even making an even harder version of Act 1, which I have been playing, and the increased difficulty is a welcome addition. 10/10, well worth the price and time investment.\t\t\t\t\t
