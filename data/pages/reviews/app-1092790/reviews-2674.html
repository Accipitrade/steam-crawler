\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInscryption is my new go-to example of the advantages that a game holds over other forms of media when it comes to telling stories. Many games will focus either gameplay or story and let the other take a lesser or nonexistent role but Inscryption is possibly the first I've ever played that sacrifices neither and delivers an unforgettable experience with both. My only criticisms of this game are both tied to it's length; the first being that I feel like Daniel Mullins used great restraint when it came to revealing details of the story to players which kept the mystery exciting and kept them guessing at the nature of central plot, and in fact may have used *too much* restraint because it seems like many plot threads and characters were left undeveloped. The second is that I finished my first playthrough in about 24 hours and need more.

Edit: Kaycee's Mod completely addresses my second criticism of this game and alleviates the first to a sufficient degree. With the inclusion of Kaycee's Mod, Inscryption is possibly a perfect game. I cannot recommend it more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe first section of the game is so good that when it switches to the other sections (a gameboy-esque RPG maker version with similar card mechanics and a repeat of the first section in a different setting with different rules and very little of the charm of the initial premise) it feels like falling off a cliff. Not in an interesting or thrilling way, either.

It feels like Mullins had a genuinely good game with the Sacrifices Must Be Made idea, developed it fully, but couldn't help himself to smash it into his increasingly uninteresting overarching Gamefuna ARG. If you're interested in poorly acted FMV video cutscenes that leave secret codes you need to go on reddit to solve or have somehow not yet gotten tired of the increasingly cliche 'What if the game characters knew they were in videogames' that every third indie game seems to be rehashing these days, maybe you'll get some enjoyment out of it, but if you genuinely enjoy the first section completely on it's own merits from the demo I strongly recommend not purchasing the full product.

Mullins and his studio feel like they're becoming the M. Night Shyamalan of their corner of the industry, insistent on mandatory twists that no one expects (and are increasingly less likely to want), and this is their 'The Happening'.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wish I could give this game a neutral rating, because I thoroughly recommend the first half. As-is, consider the red thumb as a warning to those looking for a roguelite card-battler.

Up until half way, the game matches and exceeds what you'd think of when you see the trailers. There's a constant stream of new challenges and mechanics, held together with a fantastic atmosphere and a compelling mystery. After that section finishes, though, both story and gameplay take several quite sharp turns. They're often pretty interesting, but really struggle to live up to the atmosphere and tension of the first half. It doesn't help that the slowest and least interesting of these new turns is right after you beat the first half.

I also feel that [SPOILERS] the found footage meta-story really didn't land. I've seen other reviews praising this game as a great horror experience, but while the gameplay could be very tense, the narrative starts to wrap itself up in a somewhat generic "cursed/intelligent videogame feat. shady corporation" which didn't work for me. The in-game story has a strong ending, but the final twist of the real-life story felt pretty trite. I honestly feel that it would have been improved if all the real-life acting was cut, with you being the one to canonically move events forward.

This might be the weirdest game to talk about that I've played all year, because it's almost a victim of it's own success. The opening is so fantastic that it totally undercuts almost everything that happens after it. Again, I'd leave a neutral review if I could.

If the trailer has you interested, I would still give the game a go, especially on a sale. Just be warned that the game has a lot of content that you might not find as compelling.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is much much better for the first few hours, and then it takes a sharp nosedive in quality and just throws extremely repetitive battles at you for another 6 or so. While the first section wasn't as dense as something like Slay the Spire, it was a decent-ish deckbuilder that leveraged atmosphere quite well, and the story elements were well integrated into gameplay- it was common for something to come up in the game and interrupt runs, which I thought was really interesting. One of the big problems i think that a lot of these dual theme games have is that there's more like 2 separate games that you switch between every now and then, and the first part of inscryption gets around that very well.

Then you finish the first part and get thrown into a hamfisted Pokemon game with some meta elements, and it just draaaaaaags on. I would recommend this game if you've got the money to just quit after beating the first part, because I thought it was really cool and worth my time. But that's 3 hours of play (maybe more if you're new to deckbuilders), and that includes how the game periodically throws roadblocks down to make sure you don't progress too fast.
