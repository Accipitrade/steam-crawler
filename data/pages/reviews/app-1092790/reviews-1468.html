\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a reviewer, Inscryption is a maddeningly frustrating game, one that is just as excellent as it is impossible to review without spoiling. I will try my best in a bit, but I'll just say now that if you enjoy Slay The Spire-style roguelike deckbuilding and internet creepypastas, you owe it to yourself at the very least to stop reading now and check out the free demo and see if that is enough to hook you in. The less you know about the game going in, the better.

Inscryption is an extremely effective piece of interactive storytelling, recreating the kinds of fascinating yet unnerving early-2000s urban legends that floated through internet forums via word of mouth. You play as a person trapped in a decrepit log cabin in the middle of the woods, and the only other person in the room is a mysterious shadowy figure who is essentially forcing you to play a card game with him. This part of the game functions similarly to Slay The Spire, where you can choose between several branching and reconnecting paths to add new cards to your deck, empower your existing cards, or engage in combat encounters. If you fall in combat, you lose all your progress and will have to start with a new deck on a new map.

The card game aspect of the experience is surprisingly unique and well-crafted. Almost every creature in the game requires you to sacrifice one or more creatures already on the board in order to play it, and every turn you have to choose between drawing a free-cost fodder creature or drawing an actually useful creature from your main deck, which means that card draw is something you have to actively think about to avoid having a full hand of useless fodder or having too many creatures and nothing on the board to sacrifice to play them. Additionally, damage that creatures on the board deal to each other carries over between turns, meaning that even after playing your best cards you need to consider when they are so damaged that you should sacrifice them to play something else to replace them.

Finally, victory is calculated by a weighing scale that can tip towards either side rather than a common health point system, where you need to deal enough damage to the enemy in order to tip the scales fully towards their side in order to win. Any damage you deal to the enemy will push down on their side of the scales and consequently lift up your side, which essentially means that damaging the enemy is simultaneously an offensive and defensive play. This means that you always need to seriously consider whether it is better to block damage with your creatures or take it directly; on the one hand, blocking damage with your creatures means that you have fewer sacrifices on the board to play more cards, but on the other hand taking damage directly puts you closer to death and puts the enemy even further away from defeat.

Now, all this is well and good. You have a card game system with a surprising amount of depth and strategy, and you are fighting for your life against the mysterious figure who has trapped you in the cabin. But then you look down at your hand, and one of your cards looks you in the eye and says "screw this game, we need to find a way out!" This is where Inscryption reveals another one of its layers. At any time in between encounters, you can physically get up from the table where the card game is being played, and walk around the log cabin, whereby Inscryption becomes a point-and-click escape room game. You will need to gather information from some of the cards in your deck, performing actions in the card game that will affect the cabin, and solving puzzles in the cabin that will affect the card game.

So far, everything I've talked about can be found in the demo for the game, and this is honestly only scratching the surface of Inscryption. The story and gameplay continue to evolve as you play, you eventually learn about the truth behind the cabin and the mysterious figure, and even the very nature of the game itself evolves to keep pace with the unfolding events of the narrative. All this culminates in a finale that left me with ice in my veins and more questions than answers, like any good creepypasta. But if you are determined enough to find more answers then Inscryption still has one more ace up its sleeve, one last hurrah that most people who finish the game will never even realise: an innocuous trail of breadcrumbs that leads outside the game itself, effectively allowing Inscryption to escape the confines of a video game and into the real world.

If I had one criticism for this game, it is that there is not enough of it. Even though there are many secrets and easter eggs that will take you multiple playthroughs to discover, the main game itself can be finished in about 10-15 hours. That is not to say that I felt cheated by its short length; Inscryption is exactly as long as it needs to be to achieve what it sets out to do, and I only wish that I could go back and experience it again and again. Also, even though the story continues to build upon itself over and over into a beautiful crescendo, I have to admit that my favourite card gameplay is from the beginning section of the game, and while there are many interesting mechanics and gimmicks introduced in the later areas, none of them can recapture the magic I felt as I was exploring the possibilities of the initial card game for the very first time. Thankfully, the developers are taking steps to solve both of these issues. There is currently an open beta for a pure roguelike mode of the game which takes the pure card game aspect of Inscryption and turns it into its own game mode, allowing you to keep experiencing the game for many hours to come.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn absolutely phenomenal Game, both in Narrative and Gameplay.
Don't go in Blind, we Gamers deserve to know what we're paying for.

Inscryption advertises Itself as a "Card Battler RogueLike," whatever that means; however, the Gameplay that remains consistent throughout is an authentic CCG (not TCG, as far as I can tell, Kaycee's Mod doesn't have Online PVP or Trading available, YET - here's hoping for it).
The four Scrybe Play Styles actually balance balance out in a very unexpected way; and while Act One somewhat guides you though some puzzling wins(Act One's Card Games more thematic Puzzles than actual Duels), the Card system carries over to the other two Acts seamlessly, and provides legitimate challenge as one would expect from a real Card Game.
Keep in Mind the Duelists' Moves are scripted, yet your own hands are randomized, so the strategy required still stands - Inscryption is a Real Card Game, incredible how the Dev Team accomplished this, no Wonder the Mod Expansion was in such high Demand.

Story Spoilers and Act Descriptions Below.

In Act One, the Inscryption CCG itself is narrowed down to only Leshy's Beast Cards with part of Grimora's Bone Cost System, Streamlined into a roguelite card puzzle, all presented as a Tabletop RPG set in some Imaginary Appalachia/Weird West Gold Rush Setting (like Malifaux, for those TableTop WarGamers out there).
Leshy is the Game Master, and you must recount your journey to his Cabin, as you are trapped in his Cabin.
The better prepared your Beast Caravan, the farther up the trail you reach until you finally defeat Leshy and earn his Meat Cake to eat. (Meta Reference to the Original GameJam Story "Sacrifices must be made", wherein the Player is a Father and Son who are trapped by the Hillbilly Leshy after surviving an unknown Crash.)
Act One is already an addictive, albeit basic Game all on its own - I've put in 9 hours straight just replaying the Part my first night.
Since I don't care for ARGs, Acts 2 & 3 really bog down the Initial Face-Value Story and advertised gameplay, which really should be expanded upon, BY A LOT - there's AAA RPG Potential here.


Act 2 Opens up when Leshy realizes the Challenger can't eat and is in fact Luke Carter controlling the Game Avatar.
After a scripted struggle for his Camera, Leshy is defeated and a New Game is unlocked.
The Inscryption Videogame is not a TTRPG but in fact a GBA-Graphic CCG RPG.
Personally, I hate the Retro Pixel Look as some cheap visual shortcut - HOWEVER I was so invested with the story, I was more than happy to play along for more of that smart CCG Gameplay.
There aren't a lot of Achievements to unlock in Act Two, but the "Secrets & Easter Eggs" are straight-up trash.
Walk-Spamming into every Wall to enter Story Integral Areas is NOT clever Game Design.
Don't bother with the MakeWork and risk burnout, just watch a YT Longplay Game Movie of it.
There's good CCG Gameplay to Enjoy instead.
10/10 Would play an Expanded CCG RPG of just Act 2 with all four Play Styles.

Act Three is more TT RPG with P03 (Poe) as Game Master.
There are 3D Visual assets during the OLD_DATA_ deletion Sequence that suggest Grimora and Magnificus also have their own TT RPG Segments(Which Kaycee's Mod better include in Final Release).

10/10 AAA CCG RPG AND TT RPG Potential right here.
All these Hipster GameDevs need to cool it with being "Meta" and stop ruining what could be genuinely phenomenal Games if just played straight.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is described on Steam as a roguelite, with some horror, and some meta-narrative. It is that, for the first four hours. Then suddenly it loses all three of those things, and becomes a weird fantasy RPG of sorts (just, with card battles). Apparently it shifts yet again later, but I really do not care to see it.

While the beginning is a roguelite, it's probably the most "lite" of any I've ever played. I found it difficult to lose until the game decided I needed to lose, for plot reasons. But then once the game decided it was time for me to move on from the roguelite section, it got even easier.

Personally, I think that it is valuable to know going into it that you're essentially buying 3 card games (that are at least related to each other). Also, the entire Store page is only representative of the first few hours of the game (or longer, depending how good you are). If you understand that, and still want to play, then by all means. It's certainly not poorly-designed, it's just not what I was expecting. And I don't care enough about the 0bl1v10n.exe-style meta-narrative to keep playing through stuff I'm not enjoying.

There is also a mod by the developer that makes efforts to focus in on the first few hours of the game, and flesh it out, etc. And that's definitely admirable. I'd buy it, were it its own game. But I personally won't recommend a game based on whether its mods are enjoyable. Especially when the Store page is misleading.\t\t\t\t\t
