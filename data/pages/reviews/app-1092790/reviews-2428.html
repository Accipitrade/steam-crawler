\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs someone who loved both The Hex and Pony Island, and who has a lot of experience playing card games professionally, I was ecstatic for Daniel Mullins' new game: Inscryption. Now that I've beaten it, I'm sad to report that though it's his most ambitious, it's clearly the weakest of his games, yet nevertheless still worth your while:

-The big overarching negative here is that the "cursed game" trope that Daniel's games are known for doesn't really mesh well with ... well, so much GAME. Where The Hex and Pony Island put the focus on the trope, Inscryption dips its toes in those waters just to jump back out and be what's frankly a rather shallow and simple card game. The card game isn't really allowed to breathe and build any depth because the game clearly wants you to move forward with twist after twist, with each new act changing up the card game mechanics in turn. So rather than have one really great card game, each act has slightly different renditions of the same simple mechanics, hoping that the variety will prevent you from getting bored with it all. And it almost works! Almost.

-While many reviewers think the first act (which is a strict roguelike card game a la Slay the Spire) is the best, as someone who has a handful of world records in that game, I actually felt the opposite. Within a few runs, I'd completely broken the balance, sometimes in comical ways (I made a few cute infinites), but mostly in ways that were more disappointing than fun. Every run can be beat the same ways, and enemy encounters aren't varied enough to test your deckbuilding like in Slay the Spire. Feels like there have been dozens of Slay the Spire inspired games now, but they've all missed the point of why that game is so great. Anyway, I won like 7 runs in a row and it felt like my brain was rotting. Every fight was over on turn 1; the enemies didn't even get turns. The balance is completely busted. This is true for the other acts, but act 1 is particularly egregious. But hey, like I said before, it pushes you to move on: there are a handful of meta scaling mechanics like Ouroboros, Champion Cards, etc, that are there for players who can't seem to win. So even if you can't figure out the rather dry ways to break the game, the game breaks itself to help you move on. Not fun.

-I don't know if I'm an outlier here but as mentioned previously, the game was insanely easy for me. Even after act 1 I practically never lost, consciously choosing to "win some other way" after I'd found yet another simple and effective way to win every encounter.

-Okay, so those are some damning points, so...why is the game "worth your while" then? Is it the story? Well, sort of? I liked the characters, the vibe, and variety of environments and artstyles. It's apparent that a lot of work went into this. There's a lot to love here, not coincidentally because it was clearly lovingly made. But, like I said, because it's not paced as well, the story is nowhere near as strong as Pony Island or The Hex. Also, uh, it's a bit...pointless? It crescendo'd strongly in its final stanza, but unlike the other 2 games, you're kinda left wondering what the game was all about. And it's tough not to make those comparisons; so much of the narrative direction and style leans so heavily on what made his previous games great. Nonetheless, it's still cute, has charming moments, and its share of spooky ones. I enjoyed seeing where it was taking me, even if I still don't know exactly what for. I have a feeling this part of the game will grow fonder and fonder in my memory with time.

-Some very good tracks. Jonah Senzel did his part and then some. I loved The "Scrybe of Magicks" in particular. Banger.

-The worldbuilding is stellar. Very strong creative direction. And every new environment adds another wrinkle. Loved it.

-Ignore my hours played. I had it sitting idle for a while. You'll get 12-20 hours out of this one.

Okay so it's flawed. But man did they try. It's easy to forget this is an indie game with a small team. There's so much packed in here. It falls short of the games it'll draw comparisons to (including Daniel's previous games; thus the curse of being a genuinely great game developer), but on its own it is still a solid game. So even though this review might seem overly critical, I'm still happy I bought it, and will buy Daniel and co's next game day 1, without hesitation. Wouldn't be the worst if he gave the whole "cursed game" thing a rest though; I know there are other stories they can tell. A helluva try. If this is your "worst game" then you're damn good at what you do.\t\t\t\t\t

Though you can say some of the late game isn't as perfectly executed as the initial setup, i do think it's worth experiencing, and it's closer to the cardgame RPG i've wanted than almost any other for a long while.
