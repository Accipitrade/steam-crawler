\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIncredible game! Really throws you for a loop and lots of secrets to be had.

I wont spoil anything about this game because you really should experience it for yourself, but there's a LOT to this game than meets the eye. Lots of things to explore, clues to find, secrets to uncover: and that's just the beginning. IF your into card games and spooky theme this game is for you. It breaks the boundaries in many ways that leaves you guessing about whats really going on. I will post an update after I finish this game, but this game doesn't disappoint at all.

Kudos to Daniel Mullins Games for making an incredible game.

Update 1: Just finished the Game, excellent and great ending, or so I think, because I'm completely unsure if its the true ending or if there's more going on. Almost perfect but id give it a 8/10. Some parts were very awesome and allowed you to create perfect card combos to beat your opponents. Maybe once or twice it felt like a bit of a grind because I kept losing, maybe because I didn't understand the card combos as I accidentally clicked to skip said explanation. I think if I could recommend anything its this:

Take your time and look at all the rules. You will win if you can pull off great card combos, and bless RNG for card draws.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to start off this review that while I loved the part of the game that is advertised, I cannot give personally give it a recommendation for spoiler-y reasons that I'll get into later.

Inscryption starts off as a very enjoyable deckbuilding roguelite, especially for those who enjoy actual card games which is what it is as opposed to simply being card-driven like Slay the Spire. The amount of ramp and crazy combos you can pull off is simply insane; with good deckbuilding and the right amount of luck you can go from barely surviving each encounter to one-shotting bosses with armies of cthulhu monsters fed by infinitely self-replicating squirrels. The sheer variety and depth of card mechanics is also surprisingly really good, so much so that I wouldn't mind seeing a physical card game based on this come out in the future.

There is also an "game beyond the game" element seen in a few other roguelites, with the escape-room puzzles you can fiddle with whenever not in an encounter. Completing them will provide boons to help you in your encounters as well as progress the story, both of which persist across your multiple runs of the game. On top of that is the "deathcard" mechanic, where at the end of each failed run you create a card using stats and abilities from cards in that run's deck. These can get *incredibly* busted, but having a bunch of overpowered ones may just come back to bite you.

Wrapping it all up is the game's amazing atmosphere and design. Everything about it, from the card design and writing to the artstyle and soundtrack, all blends together to form an experience straddles the line between chilling and comfy. That is, when you aren't pulling out teeth just to get a slight edge in a card game. I really wish the trailers for this game better justice of how eerily cozy the ambience of this game can be.

HOWEVER, there is one big problem, and that is everything I said above only applies to the first part of the game. I'll try not to go into too much detail, but I'll give a spoiler warning here nonetheless. Once you finally beat the game and select "new game", you start not from the beginning of what you just played but in an entirely different game reminiscent of the handheld Yugioh RPG titles. You complete some puzzles and a few bosses in that before you are thrust into yet another game similar to the main roguelike, only it's not a roguelike and is inferior in almost every single way from the first part of the game. Everything from game mechanics to the atmosphere just pales in comparison to what you were playing before, and how the story/metastory develops from this point on completely undermines all the horror elements in the first part.

It wouldn't be so bad if the actual roguelike deckbuilder that was advertised wasn't so short. There are only three bosses (four if you include the final one) that are fought in the exact same order each run. Not only that, but if you know what you're doing and are able to get a decent deck going the game is pretty easy and just becomes busywork you're doing while progressing through the escape room stuff. With how broken some of the combos are, especially the infinite loop ones, it would have been nice to see later stages, encounters and bosses that actually warrant such power in your deck rather than it all leading up to two other not-as-good card games and an obnoxious metanarrative.

So, what we have here is a game that advertises itself as a deckbuilding roguelite with escape room elements, that is instead an escape room puzzle game with card game elements; a genre that cannot really be replayed masquerading as one that champions replayability above all else. I realize that there being more to the game than what it says is part of this developer's schtick, but I honestly just wish I could have more of what was on the tin as that is what I signed up for. Sure, there are some neat concepts in the other two parts of the game, but none of them are enough to make me appreciate the detour.

I really wish I still could recommend it for the parts that I loved, but there just isn't enough of it for me to do so.
