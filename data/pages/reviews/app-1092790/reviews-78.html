\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPresentation:
8/10
A wonderful presentation that oozes in atmosphere. The games, especially in the first act, feel raw and draw you in, like a wonderful thriller where you're scared at how curious you are to see how the hell you're going to get out of this one.

Gameplay:
7/10
Inscryption has managed to create its own card game structure which works, quite well even, but gets repetitive by the end of its circa 8-10 hour runtime. While you're never completely bored with the new mechanics and flavours introduced, the overall structure you're sitting through is the same over and over, and the repititon only serves to highlight that other successful card games (Hearthstone, Magic) have only achieved that success through an absolutely incredulous amount of balancing and planning.

Inscryption has a bit more to offer than just its card game, but since this is what you'll primarily be doing, you must be excited about the prospect of learning, mastering and putting down an entirely new card game just for couple of sessions of gaming.

Narration:
5/10
While I started out engrossed with the narration, it plunged into typical Daniel Mullins corrupted-media storytelling. I love what Daniel Mullins is able to create on his almost complete own, but I've become disillusioned with the narratives he tries to tell. It's a serviceable story and you'll be mildly entertained, but do not expect deeper reflections or a turn of events that will actually surprise and engage you.

Overall:
7/10
While Inscryption is lacking in several departments, the presentation and the fun I found in mastering a new card game upweighed the negatives for me. The game manages to be extremely mechanically surprising, giving several "" moments as the player is allowed to break the boundaries they had thought were established.

I played it in two sittings on a single day, which contributed positively to my experience. It became a personal journey, and despite feeling tired from the nth card fight by the end, when the credits rolled, I was content.

Inscryption is, despite its flaws, a small piece of art. Daniel Mullins manages again, like in Hex, to create software that surpasses software, and while he fails to write an engaging story, he does still surprise and provides an experience you will not be able to find elsewhere.


Play If:
  • You like to learn a new card game which lends itself to be broken and abused in the greatest ways, to do just that, testing yourself against a campaign of fights
  • You like narratives about corrupted media
  • You have an interest in avantgarde video games and want to experience something special that only this medium can provide, and you wont find elsewhere
