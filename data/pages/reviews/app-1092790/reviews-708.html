\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of those games that's hard to find a review for because everyone does the "BROOOO I can't say anything you have to go in completely blind" thing. And I get it, especially for a Mullins game, but that's not very helpful for potential buyers. So I'll try to give a decent review without actually spoiling any of the story.

Inscryption is split up into four acts. All of them are based around the same card battle deckbuilding gameplay. The actual card game is very easy to learn yet it gains a lot of depth as more elements are added. There are some significant differences in terms of mechanics that get added or removed, but it's a card game throughout. Some reviews call this a roguelike, which I don't agree with. The first act has roguelike elements but they don't stick around for long. This game is about the story first and foremost.

There are a few hiccups in the story, choices that I personally wouldn't have made because they seem kind of cliche. But seeing things unfold is so exciting that you won't care in the moment. The actual dialogue writing, as opposed to the plot structure, is incredible. From the moment I first opened this game to the moment it ended, I was either playing the game, thinking about the game, or asleep. The sound is fantastic. The visuals are fantastic. Every half-hour or so the game will throw another curveball at the player, but it never feels confusing or cheap because the core gameplay remains consistent. At many points the game feels goofy or just plain strange in tone but the ending left a pretty big emotional impact.

Inscryption was easily GOTY 2021 for me. I would recommend it to basically anyone. Just don't go in expecting a roguelike. If you want the pure roguelike experience, you've got the wrong door, Loop Hero's two blocks down.\t\t\t\t\t
