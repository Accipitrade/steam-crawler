\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStrongly recommended for people who are into these types of games. If you like slow, plodding, methodical, difficult tactical turn-based RPGs with a lot of reading, this is one of the best one of those I've ever played. The intro eases you into the mechanics in a way that's incredibly friendly to beginners, so once the game opens up I felt empowered to explore and unravel the mysteries of Sucia island. The setting is completely unique, and although the moment-to-moment writing is nothing special, the world-building and presentation of the backstory were fascinating from beginning to end. If you're interested at all in the old style of punishing but rewarding computer role-playing games, Geneforge 1 is an exemplar of its genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR - A breathtakingly original, open ended, nuanced, ahead of its time, extremely well written sci-fi yarn about people meddling with overwhelming power and the fallout of using it to create living (intelligent and self-aware) tools of servitude wrapped inside a horribly paced, convoluted, imbalanced, frustratingly buggy, puke green shell.

Pacing and balance is the main problem with Geneforge: it takes way too long before anything interesting happens, locations become stale before you get anywhere relevant to the story, enemies and obstacles are painfully repetitious (and lazy- how many freaking maps are nothing but endless turrents/mines put behind every corner and bend)... the list goes on... talent trees are unforgiving, leveling up is far too grindy (especially after the game stops rewarding you exp for completing quests), core abilites level up purely on random exploration, inventory management is painful, towns are far too big, most abilities are pointless, enemy difficulty goes from too easy to downright stupid so fast you can't even see it, looking for specific quest or story NPCs in the sea of identical NPCs is annoying, save/rest scumming is a core mechanic... you get the idea.

The one redeeming element of Geneforge- and it's pretty bright considering- is the extremely sharp and hauntingly subtle storyline. The idea of the shaper knowledge changing your persona as you grow in power, putting you on top of several groups all vying for their own freedom in a hopeless situation is expressed with efficient detail (not a word too few or too many). All the major factions rest in grey areas where you're simultaneously intrigued and repulsed. Getting the second hand story of everything else happening on Sucia island mirrors your own journey with surreal flavor; you can't help but be taken aback and become invested in the various outcomes.

Unfortunately, even the brightest parts of Geneforge are not without serious issues. Allying with the different groups doesn't alter the general game in any real way, there are too many strange side factions introduced so late you can't really care, there is way too much back and forth running around, the Geneforge itself is haphazardly shoehorned into the story, the main antagonist is fun to read about but doesn't make an appearance until the end of the game, and again, everything takes way too long. It might sound as if I'm nit-picking or being overly critical but due to the amount of wasted, pointless time in Geneforge, little annoyances become overwhelming.

I am familiar with Spiderweb Software and yes, such a huge game is an achievement for such a small dev team (in the early 2000's no less). Moreover, I don't necessarily wish to tarnish Geneforge's positive Steam reception, but reviewing the game has to take into account both the time it was released as well as the time it's currently being played in. While Geneforge has a wealth of content (something to be noted and prized in the early 2000s) most of it feels stretched thin like too little butter across too much toast.

If you're a fan of early PC western RPGs (ie. Fallout, Baldur's gate, or other Spiderweb games), and you've got the patience, you will most likely enjoy Geneforge (especially for it's story). If you're not (regardless of what age group you're in), you may not find much to compel you here.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a longtime fan of Spiderweb games in general, I have to say that Geneforge is by far my favorite. While the gameplay itself is pretty simple, and (let's be honest) quite repetitive. The heart of this game is in its story. Most notably in the moral crux that it presents, which pretty much boils down to:

If you have the power to create and end life with the literal wave of a hand, are you morally obligated to grant your creations any rights that you yourself cannot take away?

Some creations treat you as a god, some see you as an equal, and some hate you because they never asked to be created. It's an interesting and thought-provoking concept that's well worth the price. It feels more like a book than a video game, and that's not a bad thing.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFancy being stranded on a remote island? Ever dreamed of creating an army of compliant monsters willing to die for you? Geneforge can fulfil the dream.

Geneforge is a masterpiece and one of the most extraordinary computer role-playing games I've played so far. The concept and the setting are truly unique here. I love fantasy creatures, isolated islands and hence the ideas of being stranded on a mysterious island and having my own army of monsters. The gameplay mechanics remind me of Fallout 1&2, which is also great.

I look forward to Hi-Res versions of Geneforge Saga and hope that music will be added to the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNice idea for a game, but poor execution. It is an early version of an indie company game so it's not bad. It has potential, but this game just isn't polished enough for the big leagues. The graphics are just good enough to tell friend from foe. Music is non-existent. The sound is either a 10 second loop of kids making noise, or birds chirping. I just muted the game and ran my music in the background. Gameplay is okay for a while, but then it's just boring clearing out the extra areas. For the price it's okay, but I'd recommend avoiding this game. This company has slight improvements in it's other games which are almost the same.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played all of Geneforge 1 in 2005 or 2006, right around there. More recently, I bought the game on Steam about 4 years ago and played some of the beginning. What do I think of it? It's a good game--I can't deny this.

But in many ways it doesn't even come close when compared with some of the titles I feel to be truly great. When I played it I compared it specifically to Final Fantasy 6 and Chrono Trigger, and the first Dungeon Siege, which were basically the only RPG's I'd played. Like most typical RPG's your character increases in strength and abilities by killing enemies and completing quests. But the classes differ vastly.

Your character can be one of three classes: a Shaper, who specializes in creating living creatures directly from magic; an Agent, who is far more focused on using magic spells to inflict the actual damage; and a Guardian, whose specialty is dealing direct physical damage, mostly with swords. I tested Shaper and Agent, but in the end enjoyed Guardian much more--the other classes seemed playable, but I think Guardian is easiest from my recollection, and didn't require as much micromanagement. The gameplay is good, and in combat your character gets action points you can spend to move, fight, and use items and abilities.

Combat makes up a lot of Geneforge, though you'll probably also spend lot of time talking to characters in the game, picking up items and selling them and trying to amass enough money to purchase the best items.

But the best part of the game is the story. You will learn more about magic, and the Geneforge in particular, a powerful piece of magical machinery that can greatly enhance those who use it properly. You'll learn about the different magical creations in the game, learn about the different factions in the game (four of them IIRC), and learn about the history of the land you've been stranded in. And much of the heart of Geneforge is freedom to choose--your character build, your faction, your ending. If you sufficiently improve your character, you will be able to roll over most enemies.

Anyway . . . if this review sounds a little bland, perhaps it's because the game itself is kind of bland as well. There are people who absolutely adore the whole idea of turn-based strategy, the idea of tactically micromanaging the actions of each of your party members, and carefully choosing their equipments and stats. I used to be one of those people, when I first started playing RPG's, but less so today. And if you are one of those people--I suspect that you will LOVE Geneforge.

But these days, I suppose I feel I need more from a game. I think all the micromanagement has gotten to me over the years. But also, Geneforge has very little atmosphere or enjoyable character. It has virtually no music, though it does have atmospheric sounds. I can't remember the name of a single character from Geneforge--whereas I can remember the names of all the main characters from Final Fantasy VI and Chrono Trigger, even though it's been even longer since I played them. Geneforge's environments are mostly brown--deserts and dirt, though there is some variety thrown in. And--while the ability to create life in Geneforge is quite awesome--I didn't even feel that this aspect of gameplay was very fleshed-out. The story in Geneforge is definitely interesting, and there is a huge volume of writing in the game, so if you're a big fan of reading in your games, I can see it appealing to you. But frankly, I still feel that both FF6 and CT have significantly more interesting stories, characters, environments, and of course excellent music.

Geneforge is a good game. Some people love it. You may. Someday, I may go through and play the others in the series. But . . . I didn't find a whole lot be excited by in this title, and I feel its impact is far less than it would have been than if, for example, the creator had even expended minimal effort to create a few characters that are really memorable.

7/10\t\t\t\t\t
