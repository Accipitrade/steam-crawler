\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is the first Spiderweb Software game I've reviewed through Steam (and the first one I completely, 100% finished after two false starts years ago) so maybe some background is in order about this eclectic developer's output. Jeff Vogel, Mr. Spiderweb himself, has always been able to focus in on his strengths (writing, gameplay systems) and paper over his weaknesses (art, sound), recycling assets and engines to pump out entries for a handful of settings. This resulted in a huge catalogue of very similar old school RPGs, with gameplay that'd historically fit in somewhere between Ultima VI and Baldur's Gate. Some people, like me, don't mind this at all—I play a pile of early 90s DOS rpgs, so quirky systems and archaic graphics don't phase me as long as the scenario is good (and usually, with Spiderweb, it is). Some people, very understandably, very much mind this. That's totally fair, but it does mean Spiderweb in general is not gonna be for you.

I mostly bring all this up so any future Spiderweb reviews I write don't need to rehash the same bits. Yes, the game looks terrible—in particular the snot green ooze border is masterfully awful. But if you're already willing to move past this, let's talk Geneforge proper.

One thing I appreciate in Spiderweb is that the settings are never just your average swords and sorcery. Geneforge is the series that exemplifies this best, with a strange biopunk world where humans play god, create new life, and it goes just about as well as can be expected. The first entry does a good job of setting up the wider ethical conflicts and power struggles that'll recur later, but also does a good job of teasing out a standalone deserted island mystery. What happened here? Who's still around? What do they want? Geneforge is very good at dripping out answers through exploration and adventure rather than straight exposition, and while a couple of the reveals fall flat in this entry, I still enjoyed putting together the pieces.

The other good thing about Geneforge as opposed to, say, Avernum, is that Shaping new life opens up some interesting non-traditional RPG options. Geneforge forgoes party members, requiring you to fill out the party roster with some bizarre conjured up monsters. You can still tackle things solo, or you can be like me and make a useless wet paper bag that hides behind his invincible army of snakeworms. Normally I'm not much for pet classes, but not many RPGs go all in on summoning so I gave it a shot here, and it creates some very interesting tactical gameplay.

Many maps were easy, and I just rolled through as my octopus monster squad melted everything that crossed me. Some maps are a lot more dangerous, and I had to carefully manage my 'mons like they were the secret service, hustling my VIP player character to the nearest exit. A couple levels are downright awful—the power core is a contender for my least favourite dungeons list—but clever use of resources can eventually drag you through.

It's a fun loop—you slowly clear your way across the massive number of areas on Sucia Island, scavenging together gear and items from the countless ruined buildings to let you push further and further on. Geneforge gets a bit muddled by the endgame, and I largely stopped caring about exploration or loot due to burnout. But the first two thirds are a lot of fun, leveraging the shipwrecked apprentice story to give you that good RPG power fantasy feeling of going from weakling to god.

Should you play Geneforge 1? I like the premise and the setting a lot, so I say yes. But thanks to the recent Avernum rereleases, it is now the oldest and clunkiest Spiderweb RPG in the catalogue, with some frustrating pathfinding and awful inventory management. 99% of players will want to wait for the recently Kickstarted remake to dive into Geneforge—I only played the original because I have a near pathological habit of finally finishing RPGs as soon as a remake is announced. But whatever flavour you pick, if you like good, old, weird RPGs, Geneforge is good, old and has weird to spare.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, the question here is "do/would I recommend this to someone else" even though steam has a moronically strict thumbs up/down system of reviews, I have to say no. I would not recommend geneforge 1 to everyone. It's hard, it's clunky, the mouse lags because of win10's sh*tty direct3D emulation and the game is as unbalanced as they come. This is one of those CRPGS where you find out your have a crappy, borderline unplayable build ONLY AFTER you've played the game for 15+ hours. In contrast the story and setting is absolutely sublime, they are honestly the only reason I would recommend this game at all.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA true hidden gem
Despite being made almost entirely by one man, Geneforge displays the quality level of genre's titans like Fallout 2 and Planescape.

Great atmosphere and writing will introduce you to the highly unique and mysterious world which is sure to provide any true RPG fan with an adventure of a lifetime.
You have an opportunity to take on a role of a new member of a powerful wizard organisation known as Shapers who specialize on creating various mosters, so go forth, young neophyte, achieve your potentian and create an army of fierce creatures to explore the island of Sucia and unravel it's secret.
The only minor minus I can think of is graphics, which look a bit dated and somewhat clunky.

Overall, Geneforge Saga is an example of what a trully passionate team of developers can achieve despite all limitations.\t\t\t\t\t
