\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is kind of a mixed review.

On the one hand, Geneforge is my favorite RPG series ever. I like playing Necromancer type characters, and nothing conveys the experience of having expendable NPC allies more than Geneforge does. The story is good, and the series hasn't aged badly, really. I think even someone new to the series wouldn't mind this although it would probably feel a bit janky.

On the other hand, Geneforge 1 is the weakest of the series.

It's not always clear what your next step is like in the other versions, so basically your strategy is just, go to an unexplored area and kill everything. If the monsters are too strong, go to another area.

Sometimes there are things that get hidden behind walls with the isometric view. Treasure and monsters.

The RNG... there have to be hidden modifiers to attacks or the hit chance you're given is wrong because it's just monitor smashing levels at times and I play a lot of RNG games. Stuff like 6 or 7 consecutive misses at 60+% to hit. I've seen it too often to be chance. I just came here from a ragequit where I had 7 allied monsters who could absolutely not hit anything, round after round with high hit percentages, and I don't remember the last time I ragequit a game.

Allied pathfinding is pretty bad. Your monsters often get in each other's way, or lose an attack because they didn't take anything close to the optimal route to their opponent.

I'm being too hard on this game maybe... it's playable and it has the same good qualities of the other games in the series, but it has bad qualities too. The dev hadn't quite mastered his craft yet. The other 4 games are great.

These 5 games are sold as a set and the price for them is absurdly low, so it's an absolute recommend. I'd even recommend Geneforge 1 at the price they want for it, it just gets a bit frustrating at times. Give it a look. You'll know within 2 hours if the interface and play works for you or not.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, Geneforge is fairly simple to learn, but it will require a small time investment to get the better spells, equipment, etc.

The average gamer isn't going to just waltz through every area and come out unscathed as one wrong turn on many of the maps can leave you either severely weakened or staring at a game over screen that came up very quickly.

If it is your first time going through the game, learn to SAVE OFTEN. If not, you'll be redoing things a lot because the enemies are fairly strong and pack quite the punch when you arrive in new areas.

The story is okay for the time that the game came out. It is all text and you'll have to cycle through all options during important dialogue to learn a lot of what the game wants you to do.

There are certain NPCs that will respond to you differently depending on how you speak to them or what questions you ask of them. It is not uncommon to be speaking to an NPC that is reluctant to speak with you and before you know it, he/she/it is attacking you because you said the wrong thing.

You have three classes of character to choose from (all from a race called "Shapers") and each one legitimately specializes in something different. They each have their own strengths and weaknesses. So playing as a different class each time will pose different challenges. After a short while of playing, these strengths and weaknesses become glaringly obvious. With each level up you achieve, you get some bonus points to increase your base stats. You choose which base stats you want to increase. They're all important but depending on your play style, some are more important than others.

Combat is simple but can be very wonky until you get a good feel for it, particularly how your units move. You're allotted 8 action points per unit per round. You can move, attack, heal, etc. but each action requires X amount of action points. Standard attacks for any class of character or creature require 5 action points. So you're free to move a space equal to three of those eight points and then attack an enemy. Different actions require different totals of action points. If your Shaper is carrying too much, his action points will be reduced based on how over-encumbered you are.

Each map is for the most part very simple to navigate but you're going to be blind to the area until you explore it once and learn the layout (which also uncovers the minimap). What can make the maps challenging is the fact that there are roving enemies and possibly some traps or land mines as well. Geneforge also gives you the privilege of choosing where and when you want to go somewhere as long as you have cleared the map leading to that somewhere that you want to go. Just be cautious and again, SAVE OFTEN because for those of us that like to explore, expect to stumble into some areas that have enemies that are MUCH higher level than you are at certain times.

Once an area has been cleared and the hostiles killed, they DO NOT respawn. So it is safe to move at will (unless there are traps you haven't disabled before...). This is both good and bad. Good because it can be very tedious crossing some maps with hostiles so, it's nice to catch a breather here and there. Bad because if you're a fan of grinding in RPGs like I am... well, you see where I'm going when I say the enemies do not respawn.

For me personally, my favorite part of the game is learning new spells and learning to make newer, stronger creations. It pays to search each area because finding the magic canisters is how you learn new spells and learn to create new creatures (called "Shaping" in this world). I imagine it is possible to finish the game with the basics but higher-powered spells and creations are definitely worth the effort to acquire.

The creatures you are capable of creating include long-distance attackers, melee brawlers, acid shooters that poison others, etc. So there is quite the level of party compilation customization when you get down to it.

The higher your level, the more of your own "essence" you can use to boost your creatures' stats to make them stronger in battle. Your creations can level up with you, provided you can keep them alive and safe. If not, don't worry because you can recover and create more.

Overall, I'd rate this one: 6.5-7/10

Definitely worth at least one playthrough if you're a fan of older RPGs.\t\t\t\t\t
