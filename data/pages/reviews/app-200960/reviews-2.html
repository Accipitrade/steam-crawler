\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn this game, you play the role of a shaper in training. Shapers are powerful entities that can wield magic and create creatures to serve their needs. During your training, you are attacked as you are sailing by Sucia Island, an island that has been barred for multiple centuries for unknown reasons. You manage to swim to the shore of island. As you progress through the game, you are tasked with multiple objectives: 1) find out why the island was barred in the first place 2) determine what is happening on the island right now 3) decide how you want to parley with the creatures known as serviles (creatures that the shapers made to serve them) that inhabit the island and 4) how to get off the island. The game's writing does a very good job of setting up the atmosphere and making you want to play for longer. Furthermore, the game poses several moral and ethical dilemmas as you interact with the current inhabitants of the island. It is ultimately your decision as to how to solve the dilemmas, but the game does a very good job of presenting each one fairly and unbiased. That is, there isn't necessarily one "correct" answer to the issues the game poses.

Overall, the game has some phenomenal writing, and it is very enjoyable to play.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really don't know what to say about this game, as with most Spiderweb games, i'm torn..

The graphics are rubbish, but understandable considering it was made by such a small team. The sound is the same, and i always find myself turning off everything except battle noise.

The gameplay, is unforgiving at first, but you find yourself getting stronger and stronger. I started the game as a shaper, and could only summon one creation at first, but by the end of the game, i was able to summon 7 high level creations with ease, while still able to cast spells. I'm not too sure what else to say.

The length of the game is huge, i started the game with the intention of completing everything, but after the game began to drag on, i gave up and just powered toward the end of the game.

The story is really original with some amazing world building. The only problem with that is the graphics which don't really convey the grandiose nature of the world being explored. It is very open ended, and NPC's in the game react to certain choices you make, there is a lot of different ways this game can go..

Also, my computer is a high end Windows 10 beast, and this game lagged to absolute hell as soon as any text appeared on the screen. Even after searching the web for fixes, it was still unbearable. It's still playable, obviously, but the lag can be teeth clenchingly annoying. The game is almost 20 years old though.. so i guess that's understandable..

Anyway, i'm torn with this one. If you're looking for a fun RPG, with a rewarding difficulty curve, this is the one. If graphics and sound are important, then you probably shouldn't touch this.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI remember playing Geneforge when it came out about a decade ago and decided to revisit it.

I remember finishing it but, I didn't remember it taking SO long. It can be completed in less or more time depending how you play, whether you explore everything or go straight for only the base goals, which choices you make, etc... I tried to explore everything thoroughly and my total play time is 60 hours. That's a pretty long game play! To be honest it's perhaps too long... by the end of it the game begins to feel monotonous and repetitive, boring even as you just go through the motions to finally reach the conclusion.

This a a classic RPG game. You play as a member of the Shaper race (seems like they are human but, maybe just humanoid). You get a choice of picking from 3 classes the "Shaper" who's stats are best suited for shaping creatures. A "Guardian" who is best suited for combat, and an "Agent" who is best suited for casting magic. I don't like that the only female option is the Agent. It would have been better if they allowed you to pick a gender and then a class but, hey, at least there is a female option. No matter which class you choose you will have the opportunity to increase your skills and use any of the fighting techniques (shaping creatures, casting magic, or fighting).

Geneforge for all it's flaws is actually a very flexible game. There are often multiple ways in which you can solve a problem though it's success is often based on your skill level. It also incorporates a lot of moral choices. You choose which (if any) faction to back, you can choose to kill someone or convince them or bribe them, etc... many of the events have multiple choices and outcomes. For example aligning yourself with one faction might make you an enemy of another. That same alliance might make certain map areas easier or harder to cross and might make certain quest options open up or close. Your choices in the game do matter and there are multiple endings to this game. If it were shorter I'd say this lends to replay value but, because of it's length I'm not sure I'd want to replay it again... I mean I did... about 10 years later but, I've got lots of games I don't mind revisiting once a year and this isn't one I'd replay that often.

It's graphics even when it came out were nothing fantastic but, they are functional enough. Geneforge's strength isn't in it's graphics, it's strength is in storytelling. It has a very interesting story but it's slowly revealed bit by bit as you explore the Island and complete quests. There's a good chance you won't even discover every bit of the story before you finish playing but you'll quickly start to get the basics and necessary elements of it. Some mysteries I've never managed to solve the first time around as well as this time (such as the pillars and the shaper tomb - killed everything I could explored everywhere except the tomb still never found the required crescent shaped rock that you need to open it). If you go looking for it though the story elements have a great deal of depth and complexity.

The game's UI is clunky and dated but if you don't mind most classic RPG UIs you'll probably tolerate it just fine. It's most annoying to me when dealing with inventory.

Generforge doesn't have a spectacular soundtrack... in fact, it barely has any soundtrack at all. Other than the title screen there really isn't any music, it's mostly just light ambient sounds of your surroundings. While some people seem to dislike that, personally I loved it. I often turn my computer on mute or turn sound off in games because I don't really like hearing that much game music. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tGeneforge is a great example of an indie labor of love. Created by just one man (with some contracted art work), it's a stellar story: well told, completely original and full of quality role playing opportunities. The world is immersive with believable characters throughout and a grey morality system without obvious good guys and bad guys.

I'll leave a more comprehensive story breakdown to others, but the gist is that you are a Shaper, a group of beings capable of creating life and bending it to your will. This life typically takes the form of Pokemon-esque creatures of war, or peculiarly human Serviles for more intellectual efforts or housework. You find your ship attacked and wash ashore of a barred island, which the Shapers have called off limits for hundreds of years. Here you will encounter the remnants of the Shaper colonies that once populated the island. Multiple groups have formed and thrived to different extents under various ideologies, including the folks that attacked you. The mechanics definitely show their age, as a lot of quality of life enhancements from bigger or more modern RPGs are missing. Combat is also pretty bland, as each creature in your party has a melee move, and some have a ranged move. The turn-based action is solid, but it gets stale fairly quickly.

The artwork is decently attractive, although the interface shows its age. The cursor tends to lag after loading a few save games (death is often an unseen mine away).

Geneforge is a game you play for a solid role playing experience, and an imaginative, deep story. It's often clunky, showing its age and humble origins, but when it is so often on sale for only a dollar or two, it's an absolute steal for ~40 hours of gameplay and immersive storytelling. Plus, I love supporting indie devs that make high quality games. It's a very particular type of game, but this early Spiderweb Software offering really establishes that they know exactly what they are making and love doing it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGeneforge is one of the best RPGs I've ever played, as well as one of the ugliest.

In Geneforge, Jeff Vogel created an original and fun fantasy setting, centered around a society of Shapers, people who use biological engineering to create new species to serve them. They make servants and ships, even levers and mines, out of living creatures. Their whole society is defined by their biological engineering abilities. You play an apprentice Shaper, stranded on a mysterious island that's been forbidden by your society. Exploring this island, meeting the various factions, and learning about the secrets the island holds are the highlights of the game. There are multiple endings to the game, with end slides that change depending on your choices during the game, similar to other RPGs such as the Fallout series.

Besides its unique setting, Geneforge is notable for its hideous user interface. It's like someone took the clunky interface from Ultima 6, a game from 1990, and threw up split-pea soup all over it. It's so ugly that the game starts to feel like outsider art. Come on Jeff, couldn't you be at all influenced by any UI design since 1990? The UI, besides taking up half the screen, is clunky and awkward. The player inventory is just a list on the right side of the screen, which gets completely cumbersome once you have a lot of items. Another annoying thing about the game is that the quick-save and quick-load shortcuts are F10 and F9, respectively, which is just nonsense. Why have them right next to each other?

Despite its shortcomings, Geneforge has a lot of fun and interesting writing and world-building, and some meaningful choices that many big budget RPGs lack. Because of these strengths, I recommend playing it. Thankfully, Spiderweb Software is remaking this game, and will hopefully improve the UI. The setting is still fun and exciting in 2020, and I'm looking forward to seeing more of it.\t\t\t\t\t

I'm not calling the series a paragon of difficulty or anything, but I didn't complete quests as a kid. I went out of my way to avoid talking to anyone, actually. As a kid, I just attacked anything that moved. If it killed me, I would let it live until I ran into it the next time I saw it. Think of it as an ill-informed murder run. Despite that, the game left such an impact on me that I would spend the next 10 years of my life thinking about it. Where did all the people go? What happened to the little monsters I made? Turns out, there was an entire series of games that I would discover much later.

Now, coming back to the game series with the eyes of an adult, this is what happened: Without going into too many spoiler-y details, the first game sets the stage for the conflict that is central to the Geneforge series. While each game can be ended in any number of ways, the sequels always assume that the player chose the most neutral path AND that each sequel has a new protagonist. So, by the third game, I encountered Alwan and Greta. Both are special NPCs that chit-chat with you (if you have them in your party) about their dreams and views on the current conflict. I kept them both around for a while, seeing as it made the game easier for the playstyle I had chosen, but came to agree more strongly with Alwan as the game went on. Abandoning Greta, I stuck with Alwan and completed the game in the most Shaper-aligned way possible. Satisfied, I moved on to the next game. This is where Alwan and the player's emotions can really begin to align. Not only was the Shaper-ending of the third game now only wishful thinking, both Alwan and your new player character struggle against frustration and setbacks as the game works against both of you. The cycle repeats, and I chose the most shaper-aligned ending possible. This time, it was MUCH harder to accomplish. The game very clearly wants you to be neutral or ally with Greta. So, slightly less satisfied, I moved onto the last game.

Warning: This next part has HEAVY spoilers. I can't explain my emotional response without them.

I didn't even know if Alwan would survive the brutal battle of the last game, so I expected him to be dead. I spent the first half of the game annoyed with my only shaper ally at that time. He was unbearable to my now-shaped (hah!) views on the conflict. But, if I wanted to stay true to the values that me and Alwan fought for, I needed to bite my tongue and do what my ally wanted. I felt abused and lied to, treated like a puppet to dance to vaguely-shaper-like ideals that were only in the interest of some greedy bastard. Around the halfway mark, this "ally" of mine mentions Alwan. Without a moment of hesitation, I threw aside the side quests and tasks I had left and literally ran my way through the zones to reach him. I created entire armies of cannon fodder so I could skip combat. I did not want to wait any longer. I finally reach him, and I felt something akin to anger and empathy. Alwan, after all we had been through together, was on permanent life support. Strung up like a puppet with metal wiring and magic so that he could even speak. Whereas I felt like an emotional puppet for the first part of the game, Alwan had become a physical one. While his shaper ideals and views remained untainted, he could never fight with me on the battlefield ever again. He couldn't even leave this room again. It was crushing to see, and the dialogue really drove home how much he's sacrificed, and continuing to sacrifice, for his ideals and his beliefs. Despite the danger it posed to my own character, I immediately pledged to help him. I had social obligations the following day. I cancelled all of them, something I had never done before. I always drew a line between my social life and my gaming life. But this was for Alwan. I spent the next 20 hours plowing through the campaign with the zealous fervor of a crusader. This was no longer about right or wrong. This was getting revenge for Alwan. By the time the credits started to roll, I felt that indescribable feeling of "The Void" that washes over you after you complete a life-altering experience. I just sat back in my chair and stared blankly at the screen, trying to process the emotions. This was something I knew I could never re-capture in another play through. That first high as I experienced the story and characters of a world that was perfect for what I needed it to be at the time. The blind journey with no prior knowledge or experience of the story's conclusion.

You can call me dramatic if you want, but this was my genuine experience. I did not add style or flair to this story. Alwan and his journey genuinely affected me. It taught me how powerful the image of someone who will stop at nothing to achieve what they believe is just and right can be. Geneforge is, without a question in my mind, a complete masterclass of game story and character design. Some may dislike the graphics and game engine, but the story inside is more than worth it. This is my favorite game of all time, and nothing has matched up to it in my heart.

tl;dr : 5 stars. 10/10. Game of the Year. Every Year.
Buy it. Play this game series.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGeneforge isn't much to look at but it's a superb RPG. The setting is an odd blend of fantasy and sci-fi with lots of "Magitek" style technology combined with a lot of fantasy tropes.

You play a 'Shaper', basically an elite sect of magic users that have the power to shape matter and via a combination of genetic engineering and magic, create lifeforms. These lifeforms essentially serve the role of party members. The game allows different playstyles including playing a pure warrior, pure mage or any combination You can also focus entirely on shaping if you prefer to command lots of creations.

The combat is turn based and relatively simple, but also quite serviceable. The later games in the series tended to try to increase combat difficulty by HP and damage bloat on enemies, in the first few games of the series this is absent and difficulty mostly tends to come from difficult encounters rather than inflated enemy stats.

Story wise your character washes up on an seemingly abandoned island filled with destroyed labs and other facilities. As you explore the island to discover the mysteries, you encounter various factions of abandoned creations. The game has a fair amount of choice and consequence and supporting or spurning the various factions can have a fairly significant impact on the story. You also have the ability to completely ignore all the factions and essentially act as a free agent. The choice is yours.

The game is pretty non-linear and, generally speaking, the only thing that will stop you exploring an area will be your character being too low level. The game has no hand holding or level scaling, and on occasion you may have to return to an area later when you are stronger.

The game strongly rewards exploration, with your character often finding powerful items or permanent stat boosts hidden around the world. It follows the pretty traditional RPG curve of being hard at the beginning and your character being godlike towards the end. Arguably no bad thing.

I highly recommend the entire series, especially if you are a fan of old school RPGs.

Note that a remaster of the first game has recently been kickstarted and will be out shortly. The first game has quite a few issues with modern PCs, so it might be worth waiting for the remaster.\t\t\t\t\t
