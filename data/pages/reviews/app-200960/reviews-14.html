\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Geneforge games are some of the best RPGs ever (with the first and the fifth games in the series being highlights, although all of them are good). Massive worlds, lots of old-school stats, tons of different ways to play (the character build system has much more variety than most RPGs, and the ability to create companions to fight for you is a nice touch), many different factions to join, ignore, or fight, and lots of freedom to act in a world where your choices actually affect the way the game plays. Plus, the setting is a refreshing mix of sci-fi and fantasy and, at times, is genuinely strange, as opposed to just the same old boring Tolkeinisms found in most RPGs. If you can get past the admittedly rough graphics and less than beautiful aesthetics these games are awesome.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite being released in 2001 this game lacks the graphical sophistication of titles of that era. Do not let that fool you though. Geneforge's graphical style is a throwback to those glory days of early pre-console gaming; a visual feast of animated pixels. What we have here is an incredibly immersive RPG reminiscent of the days when games kept you playing not because the last achievement is to kill a bajillion goblins but because it's genuinely interesting. The plot is original and offers multiple endings based upon the many decisions you'll have to make while playing and the turn based, AP driven combat system allows you to play in a variety of styles as suiting your character choice, be it a warrior, rogue or summoner. Throw in a well thought out stat system and this feels less like a video game and more like a DnD adventure with bonus graphical interface. With all 5 titles in the saga available for £16.99 (yes, it's a bundle) how can you not be playing this?\t\t\t\t\t
