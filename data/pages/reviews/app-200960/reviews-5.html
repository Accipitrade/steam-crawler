\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I purchased Geneforge, I didn't really know what to expect. I hadn't played too many role-playing games of this type, and I halfway expected it to be a slow, cliched grind to play through. I can happily say that I was completely wrong in my expectations.

Geneforge is built around a very interesting science-fiction/fantasy hybrid of a plot. An order of individuals known collectively as the "Shapers" have the unique ability to create, or shape, life. This ability is a well-guarded secret, and the Shapers are very choosy on who gets to become a member of their order. The player takes the role of a newly inducted Shaper recruit, who has finally been chosen to begin training to become a full-fledged Shaper. Of course, things don't go to plan, and the player is inevitably thrust into a situation that could change the very nature of Shaper society.

The gameplay is very similar to other classic rpgs, but with somewhat different roles to choose from. The Guardian focuses mainly on physical melee and ranged weapons, although they can make use of limited magic. The Agent is an expert at traditional magic usage, although she can certainly hold her own in melee combat. The most interesting role, of course, is the Shaper himself. Alone, he is fairly weak, possessing average magic abilities and next to no close-combat abilities. What sets him apart is his ability to shape. He can create a wide-range of creatures, loyal only to him, that serve as his own personal army. The Shaper need not put himself in harms way when he has an army of loyal creatures ready to lay down their lives for him.

Overall, I'd highly recommend this game to anyone who enjoys classic rpgs and wants to experience a unique story. Honestly, even if you've never played a classic rpg in your life, give this game a go. The game is intuitive enough and the story engrossing enough to entertain pretty much anyone who enjoys a good adventure.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros:
- Interesting mix of sci-fi & magic.
- Well written political intrigue and sufficiently scripted faction ecosystem
- Foreshadowing of the chance to acquire immense power to whet your aspiration to finish the game.
- Combat has variety and can be circumvented by investing in conjuration.
- Map has a good fast-travel system which helps save time

Cons
- A lot of trash mobs which can drag and bore
- A lot of trash items littering the ground
- Vendors/Merchants are sometimes placed in inconvenient locations of hubs which can waste your time
- Occasional time-wasting with redundant walking

Ambivalent
- Hybrid builds are highly unlikely to succeed in early game unless one knows the game well. This can be a plus and encourages prioritization and strategy, and the reward of the Geneforge's balancing power can vindicate prudent player choices. It can also be a source of frustration because of the draggy inundation of trash mobs if 1's stats are not allocated properly.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm going to ultimately say no. And I feel bad saying that, because what the game does well, it does very well. But what it does badly, it does disastrously.

The setting and storytelling are excellent. Really, really excellent. It's a complex story with lots of nuanced interactions between a lot of different factions with their own outlooks and agendas. You fluidly act between them without really locking yourself into a side, and there are plenty of different layers to how the end can play out depending on what you did. The NPCs are scripted well. Dungeons are full of neat gimmicks; automated bio-defenses that can be brute forced, disabled, or even "convinced" to aid you. A couple areas have environmental hazards that are pretty unusual for Spiderweb. The game just generally feels very believably alive in a way a lot of games don't.

The Shaping gimmick to create your own minions is, at its core, brilliant. You effectively create them by spending from your max MP, and can only regain it when the minion dies or you reabsorb it. They level up over time, so they're effectively as disposable as you make them, and you can shoot for quality or quantity as you feel like, leaving as much or little of a casting pool for yourself as you judge. It's a really interesting system and it gels perfectly with the story, aesthetic, and themes of playing God that the game centers around.

Unfortunately, it's an excruciating slog to actually play through to see that story. The interface and combat is horrendously clunky and dated even as far as Spiderweb games go, but honestly that's the least of the problems.

The main issue with the game is that enemy attacks have a massive, massive damage range. Throughout the entire game everyday enemies will be launching attacks that do anywhere from 0 damage (not necessarily a miss -- null damage) to almost twice your max HP for an instant game over. I wish that was an exaggeration. Armor has a marginal effect at best, and the numbers are so explosively wide that you practically need statistical analysis to tell an average reduction is happening at all. And the whole time, your own damage output is generally pretty predictable with much narrower ranges, so you know the dev could've scaled incoming damage properly too and just...didn't. It's a numbers game and you don't have or ever really get all of the numbers that make it particularly reliable.

It seems like the game expects you to leverage your created minions to tank these hits for you, but it doesn't really work. Most creatures' movement range is greater than your sight radius, and unlike later Spiderweb games there is no action penalty for moving around opposing units or attempting to disengage -- so everything just sort of randomly picks a target. Most of the game is out in the open or in wide spaces that are impossible to bottleneck melee enemies, and even in tight spaces permittivity is wonky because movement isn't grid-based and enemies will usually squeeze through. Ranged attackers will just shoot you freely. Even if you just run around a corner out of line of sight and leave your minions in place, enemies will often ignore your minions and pursue you. And in the event your minion does eat the hit, everything you create up to and including explicitly tanky Thaads are very nearly as prone to instant death as you are, forcing you to run back to town to regain the half-to-a-third of the magic bar you had to semi-permanently invest and recreate it.

This ends up turning the whole game, every single time combat initiates for a 30-odd hour game, into a quicksave-scum slog that make old-school classics like Baldur's Gate feel downright generous. With how broken the combat is, I just can't recommend it. I feel like the fluid faction interplay is the only reason Geneforge wasn't better off as a book. Because I'm gonna level with you here -- it would be a really kickass book.\t\t\t\t\t
