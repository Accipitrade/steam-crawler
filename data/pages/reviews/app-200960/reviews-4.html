\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI dont even understand why anyone could like this game. At first it looks good and one is excited to play an old-school rpg. But soon you find out it's just a massive grind. There is nothing to it. It just has a unique setting. But the gameplay is so repetitive I cant finish this game ever. Its not even a good rpg because the most of your stat increase come from in-game power ups and you cannot choose what stats are increased. Its like a giant railroad simulator and of course if you dont see through it, that even your character progression is railroaded to 75 % from these stupid power-ups, then you think its a great rpg. But in fact its more an interactive adventure game and you can only put points into your attributes but not into your abilities which means you can never choose when to learn a spell or to increase a spell because these are abilities you are not allowed to put points in. This part is only increased by strange canisters you find in the game world.

In addition it has the worst turn-based combat system I have ever seen in a game. It all comes down to position your char as far as possible from the enemies and try to fight them one on one. In many cases the combat is like enter a region, kill one enemy, fast travel to a settlement for free heal, go back to the regionp, kill another enemy, rinse repeat. There is nothing else to do. The monsters get more and more annoying over time and they are always just the same you fought before.

And then if your level is too high you wont even get XP at all. So you do quests not in the supposed way then you wont get XP because you brought in the quest when your level is too high. And there is nothing more annoying then to fight through hordes and hordes of boring immobile turrets and you do this for absolutely nothing.

Maybe when it came out people were so starved for old-school rpgs they were glad they could play this boring game but today there is absolutely no reason to play through it anymore.

In my humble opinion the story would be great as a book but playing through it is pure torture for me. I think work is more exciting than playing this.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSpiderweb Software is a company I've been playing games from since the 90s with Exile and Avernum series. I attempted to beat Geneforge some time last year and got about halfway through before I gave up because I wasn't having any fun and wasn't really making any progress. At least thrice I tried my hand at it again, running into frustating traps, powerful foes, and felt completely overmatched by the three routes I knew of to get to the other half of the map.

Turns out that I didn't really need to bust my ass so hard running against a brick wall. After a year absent, I decided to do some minor exploring and backtracking and found two or three other paths I didn't discover by ignoring a few exits from certain towns and zones and adding them to my map. They were zones designed for my character ten levels ago (and I'm at L17 out of 20 or so). I won't say the game became a cakewalk from there because the hardest and most difficult zones were still to come, but just being able to explore new places and finish long-forgotten quests was enough to keep me engaged and powering forward with enough momentum to reach the end (as well as not hoarding every single item I could or trying to save my disposable summons every time).

It's a very good game, and under different circumstances, probably better than the Exile/Avernum series in terms of plot, dialogue, choices, lore, and perhaps combat. Exile is very swords&sorcery traditional with a wide variety of spells and D&D style combat while Geneforge definitely is influenced by the monster hunting strengths of the late 90s/early 00s, but with a much better story than any other one I'd played.

You're a novice Shaper (apprentice spellcaster-adjacent) shipwrecked onto an abandoned island where you begin to encounter slave creatures acting of their own free will without the influence of other Shapers that are long gone. Your main goal is to find a way off the island, but you're pulled into several wars of belief and thought: the Awakened who just want freedom to live how they want; the Obeyers who yearn to be told what to do and follow their masters again; and the Takers who rage against those that abandoned them and want chaos and vengeance. There are people and minds hidden about the world that influence what goes on, plenty of quests and lore, and a pretty good job world building with multiple endings.

If you like old school isometric RPGs, it's quite good and better than I expected.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of my go-to games for old-school gameplay. The graphics, while hand-drawn and pitiable by modern standards, is far simpler and easier to comprehend than Baldur's Gate or Arcanum or others like it. The Story is rather intriguing and invokes a sense of "There is something far bigger than me going on here that I am nowhere near prepared to face. And yet I'm the only one who can do anything about it!"

The choices truly matter even as early as your first encounter! You can choose to obtain power without leveling up super early game or you can choose to take it the slow and steady route at the expense of less abilities and depending on the choice, you... Well no spoilers from me! ;)

The mechanics are easily grasped with the tutorial, load times are minimal, shortcuts are well positioned on regular keyboards, and the combat is both intuitive and strategic without being overwhelming. Presuming you remember to level up! Ha!

But seriously, this is one of my all-time favorites that I still play today. To where I have 3 copies in 3 different mediums!

(And I am only 23. I didn't play when it first came out, or even for a few decades since it's release, but it is a cult classic for me from the first trial I played roughly 7 years ago!

However, rather than ending this [retrospective look at Spiderweb Software] on a downer, I have save what is perhaps my favorite series for last. Geneforge—I’m certain the name is pronounced GENE-FORGE, but the name GEN-NUH-FORGE better rolls off the tongue—has perhaps one of the most unique settings in any RPG due to its high sci-fi/high-fantasy world. This game isn’t Warhammer or other sci-fi worlds that happen to have fantasy elements thrown into them; it’s the opposite situation, a high-fantasy world given ideas from the future.

In this series, the first three games have you play as three versions of the Shapers, a mystical order of wizards that can harness the power of creating life to their will. Shapers can create mindless monsters to fight for them, brain-like databases of information or homunculus men and women willing to serve their masters, Serviles. You play as one of three new Shaper apprentices—a Shaper, a Guardian or an Agent—who is sent out on a rite of passage test to a remote island when you are suddenly ambushed by brigands. You wash up on shore nearly dead from exhaustion on an island deemed Barred where the experiments have been left alone from the outside world. Similar to Jurassic Park, these living experiments have deviated on their own progress, or as Ian Malcom would say, “Life finds a way.”

When you move inland, you realize all the humonculous men have been left to fend for themselves for years without a Shaper. They have even formed factions in dispute with one another, and the arrival of an untrained Shaper like yourself begins to spark these Serviles’ paranoia. Unlike Jeff Vogel’s other titles, there is a surprising depth to these quests with various choices between all these factions on the island. You can meet with these leaders, assassinate them or strike a bargain between one or two leaders; you can treat these non-people however you see fit; and even your own style of gameplay is another choice that can have a drastic effect on the game. Geneforge stands out precisely because of this focused sense of freedom, although there is always a cannon ending. Another way to express how different this game feels from Avernum is if Avernum felt like an Elder Scrolls game made into a Fallout-style engine, then Geneforge feels even more like a proper Fallout title except in a world far more bizarre and compelling of player-driven choices.

Ironically, as a game about making choices, Geneforge is perhaps the most restrictive and the simplest game from Vogel’s lineup. While you can play as a Guardian or an Agent, you aren’t getting the same narrative experience as a lite-Shaper, and even when you do play as the Shaper there is a limited number of spells to use. Your creations also only seem to offer one attack, which can make Pokemon battles look complex by comparison. However, unlike Vogel’s other games, combat isn’t as prominent; the game was also the first to introduce the overworld map screen you would later see from Avadon. This change isn’t bad because the best moments of Geneforge are when dialogue or your decisions play a more important role. My only concern for this series is how long can these mechanics last since this game goes on for another four titles.

Perhaps this reason is why Geneforge 4 and 5 changed gears and offered five additional classes where you can play as the homunculus men. Geneforge 4 doesn’t even let you play as the Shapers; you choose between five Rebel classes and one class for Serviles. There are only two distinct playthroughs as the Serviles are treated differently from the Rebels and the Shapers, but the added classes do provide some welcomed variety after the first three games. However, Geneforge 5 lets you play as all eight classes, Shapers, Servile and Rebels, and as far as offering the most replay value putting that variety at the end sends the series off on a high note from where it first started. Hopefully the remaster will address some of these gameplay concerns, but if they are anything like the Avernum remakes, then maybe those modern touches are all that these games ever needed in the first place.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is definitely a throwback to the golden era of RPG's. It has one of the most unique takes on the genre that I've ever played. The concept of being a lone hero "genetically spawning" your teammates sounded incredibly off-putting, and I was *not* receptive to the idea until an hour or two into the game. This mechanic...actually works surprisingly well, and though it lacks the emotional interactions found in games like the Baldur's Gate series -- i.e. your companions' death is of little consequence -- the combat remains interesting, and it lends itself to some truly creative storytelling.

I said it before about Avernum, and I'll say it about Geneforge, as well as Spiderweb's other titles: Despite the fact that the graphics and other production values are noticeably inferior to contemporary offerings on the market, these things fade into the background after about fifteen minutes. You're given a description of a person or scene and even though the screen will show extremely plain sprites on a plain background, the novel-like gameplay makes your imagination run wild. After a while I truly began to appreciate the fact that these simplistic, MS Paint type sprites were far more intriguing than yet another highly-polished EA turd with gorgeous graphics and absolutely no depth. Give me this type of game any day over the next regurgitated AAA formula; this is the kind of game you tell your friends about years down the road.\t\t\t\t\t

Review: Geneforge 1

TL;DR: A masterpiece by Jeff Vogel, a unique medieval setting with sci-fi elements, open world, open endings and decent character build. I now understand why Geneforge is often rated the best piece of work of Jeff.
Not the first time I write this, but any RPG fan must get this game asap !

Pros:
  • The universe is just great, those gene masters tinkering with life and all the moral choices you'll have to make
  • Character building while basic absolutely does the job and can even involve party management to some extent
  • Choices DO matter, very very different endings with different length and difficulties
  • Big world, lots to explore. I have beaten the game in a little more than 20 hours but that's entirely due to my powergaming nature that enabled me to kill a guy who was the boss (for my choices). One can easily double that duration and add replay value with a different class and different choices

Cons:
  • Graphics are not in their prime, meh...
  • Fights mechanics and probabily of success of different actions could be better explained, it's all kinda obscure
