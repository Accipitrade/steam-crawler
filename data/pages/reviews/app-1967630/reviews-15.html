\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI did not expect much from fresh Early Access release but the core of the game seems already established and a road map is quite obvious. The game already feels like good competitor to Stranded Deep which is finished and released for quite a time. Here are core points for people wondering about place of Survival: Fountain of Youth in vast space of 'open world craft' games:
- Story is not forced or mandatory. In the start, your major concern will be survival and first steps in story helps you to figure it out. After that you will be able to afford to indulge your creativeness and build things as you want.
- Game progress is based on skills/perks and resource/tools/machines. Skills and perks are not simple bonuses to crafting speed and some choices can make your survival a lot easier. But like in a real life, the biggest leaps happen due to exploiting new resources and building new tools. Exploring and hunting is necessary for a lot of critical materials but ability to reveal parts of the map simplifies this process a lot.
- Renewable sources of materials. Respawn times are quite long but still there is no pressure to hoard things and no existential dread of running out of resources.
- Several tiers of tools and equipment. Crafting menu is quite extensive and will expand in the future. Crafting complicated stuff have a lot of requirements but some simplifications are made and overall it is quite manageable.
- A lot of factors to take care of. Sunburn, indigestion, heatstroke, poisoning, flu, fever, injury, light conditions etc. Everything can be solved and even quite easy but it could be a steep learning curve. Some negative reviews are already reflecting that, for example, people having no clue that they are getting injuries due to crafting in the dark.
- Story and lore are there if you need it. It is not just pure sandbox, there is a proper setting and pieces of the puzzle spread across the islands.
Graphics is good enough for such extensive game. Music, on other side, is quite good and really gives vibes of 16th century expedition. But don't expect too much as most of the time you will be listening to nature: birds singing, snakes hissing and another animal growling at you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am a survival game enthusiast always on the lookout for great, first person sims. Tops on my list so far are The Long Dark, Sons of the Forest and Green Hell, in that order. Now having invested over 60 hours in Survival: Fountain of Youth, I can say this game ranks right up there with the best of them. Although it's still a bit rough given its EA status, I have not encountered any bugs that were game breaking or any current play that was limited. Here's a quick breakdown...

Pros:
Challenging survival elements on Normal setting, especially early in the game.
Beautiful graphics and sound.
Interesting story.
Great exploration experience.
Good weather dynamics.
Non-linear gameplay.
Good price in EA for a great game.

Cons:
Inability to move objects from one place to another with a 'right click-hold'. Items must be picked up and selected in inventory.

This game is hard to master even on normal settings. You will 'faint' and lose your items many, many times. It is a frustrating challenge, even for experienced survival gamers. I liken it to Interloper mode in The Long Dark where mapping is crucial to know where you are in relation to resources and the region. But unlike Interloper, you are able to find caches with weapons, tools and valuable resources. A nice mix!

All in all, it's a great game filled with realistic survival elements and challenges. There is much to explore, even in its current state and much to keep you busy until the full release. This is NOT an easy game in its default mode. So for all the reviewers complaining about how far certain resources are or how long it takes to acquire certain resources to build needed machines or tools, maybe find another genre to play. Because these drawbacks are what survival is all about and are what make this game so good!

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is the pinnacle of the survival genre for me at this point. I have enjoyed many survival games in the past, but none have gripped me so hard with their gameplay before.

The map is unique, filled with secrets, but not to the point that you are annoyed by their quantity.

The story is simple to follow, yet beautiful and surprising, so you are often left wondering what you will uncover while preparing for the next journey ahead.

The crafting system is vast, but I never felt overwhelmed. New items were always only introduced once they seemed within reach.

I guess what I am trying to say is this game has struck a perfect balance in many ways for me personally. I was always eager to try out new crafting recipes, prepare for the next adventure, or stock up on much needed items. Things always seemed possible, and I was never too bothered by anything. I always wanted to play more. For me, many games today just start being a headache after a certain point, too much to manage, too much to explore, and too much story, so I rarely finish an experience. This game felt like a book that I could not wait to turn the page in, and learn more. I finished what is in store for now and I can not wait to get more from the devs.

Improvements I wish could be made:
- Make turtle shells spawn from every turtle, I have killed like 7, and even though I can see it right there on it, I can never harvest it. Maybe you could make it so that if you unalive the turtle in a specific way ( ie. hitting it only in the head) the shell is always there, otherwise it gets damaged and is unusable.
- I have never found horseweed, and I swam around huge rocks for 2 hours straight looking for it. Overall I think the marine biome needs a little bit more work, but I trust that you are on it.
- Allow me to slow time while aiming, at least if you are intending to keep animal movement so fast and clunky. Especially against larger predators in caves, it never made sense to use ranged weapons, and I would just resort to melee and heals, which felt like a shame. Maybe you can add a focus meter and make it be used up with (v) as well as this. Also because spamming (v) is an exploit at night and in caves.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally good.

It's one of these games that actually attempts at being a survival experience and not an adventure game with a hunger bar slapped onto it. My first semi-blind playthrough took 33 hrs, altho I like to take my time, explore and prepare, so take that as you will. The game is, compared to other offerings in the genre, very polished for an early access release. UI is also really nice. That said, the crafting UI is very, very game-y and if you're a fan of how Green Hell or The Forest handle their crafting UIs, you very likely won't like this one.

In most survival games I've played, there's a point where you can grow your own food, have excess of every resource and generally become unkillable. This isn't the case in this game (not right now at least). Natural resources respawn after +20 in-game days, there are various status effects you have to keep in mind or take care of (weather, diseases, etc.), you have to sleep once in a while to not fall over from exhaustion. It's always a risk if you go out exploring without proper prep. Exploration is fun and rewarding. The plot is there for you to unravel at your own pace, there's no HURRYUP'pers at any point. Graphics are respectable. not AAA quality by any means, but the game does look nice.
As far as performance goes, it's meh. Not great, not terrible. GPU is almost never fully utilized (RTX 3070). There's no upscaling (DLSS/FSR), no borderless windowed mode (it can forced with Borderless Gaming or alternatives). Also no FOV slider (at the time of writing this review) so check out the demo first (if it's still up) or some gameplays on YT to make sure you're fine with it.

What I don't like, in no particular order:
- Going down slopes makes you do tiny little jumps. These jumps count as being mid air, so you can't jump by pressing your jump key. This makes nailing jumps trickier than it should be. Also the sound of constantly landing on the ground is annoying.
- Fall damage is very "aggressive", so to speak. Falling from small heights deals little bits of damage that add up over time. I'd prefer if the lowest required height to take fall damage was increased along with the damage the player receives.
- House building system is rather painful to use. You have to craft an item that's essentially a "building block", craft a hammer, place "ghosts" of what you want to build using the hammer and then manually go through the so popular "crafting popup" for every single piece. It's somewhere between Icarus/old Conan building system and Valheim/Rust, where the latter is superior. That said, once built, you can freely move most things around without deconstructing them, which is really, really nice.
- There's way too many crafting stations. And most of them have upgrades, one or two levels. Upgrades which either unlock crafting recipes that this station should have access to from the beginning or something that's available later in the game. There's an alcohol distiller that's sole purpose is to make one item. Sure, it makes sense it does just that, but it's a video game. Why can't the alchemy table do this?
- Certain actions take way too much time. I think I know why it's the way it is, but as the same time I wish it didn't take me 45 minutes to pick 3 oranges from a small tree.
- Storing large amount of heavy items is a PITA. Storage containers have infinite slots (I think), but are limited by weight, so if you wanna store a lot of rocks or ores, be ready to build multiple boxes.
- Refining ores doesn't follow the established "confirm the action and stare at passing time" crafting system. You put the ores in, light the fire and have to babysit the crafting station to manually remove the crafted item, put raw resources in and press "CRAFT" again. It's tedious and not fun. Same applies to making charcoal.
- Other minor balance oddities like a barrel holding 5 more units of water than a palm sized bamboo stick.\t\t\t\t\t
