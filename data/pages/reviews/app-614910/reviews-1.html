\r\n\t\t\t\t\t#monstercakes is a basic mobile app-like ripoff of 2048, the additive game where you put two (in this case 3) matching symbols together and they add up and form a new tile. It's been done a few times, mostly as mobile apps, so this ripoff of the same game from Russian shovelware pushers already has no value for gamers. But the notable thing here is that while this is a trash game, it has high player counts for a few reasons. Firstly, trading cards, secondly, it's an achievement spam game, and finally the Russians worked out they can propel game popularity through the Steam item marketplace, so there's that ecosystem going on... none of which involves actually seriously playing the game, because it's trash!. Seriously, what value does this have for gamers, when the only interest is in the underlying Steam community features, and not the game itself? Ugh. Why is this good for gamers?

From a technical perspective, the game doesn't meet basic minimum requirements that most PC gamers expect as standard.

While there are options to change the resolution for the game, all this does is scale up the simplistic 2D art assets used to make the game, which makes little or no difference to the graphics quality. Without any other substantial graphics tweaks, it's not possible for gamers to improve the lacklustre 2D visuals.

The game features simple, cartoony 2D graphics, of the type you normally expect to see in low effort mobile apps. 3D graphics programming does require a degree of skill and competence and unfortunately not all developers have the budget or talent to deliver this, despite 3D graphics cards hitting the mainstream in the 1990's. Considering this is being evaluated as a PC game, having the graphics phoned in like this isn't going to result in a high quality, visually impressive game that PC gamers are used to seeing.

The controls can't be customised because the game has such a dumbed down, simplified interface that it's just point and click stuff like a browser/Flash game. The fact that the interface is this dumbed down might be seen as a problem in itself, however... this is a fairly shallow experience if you're the kind of gamer that likes to play games with deep, rich control schemes and interaction. You'll get none of that here.

The very poor quality of this game puts it squarely on-par with ancient 1990's Flash/Java games, and given it's 2023 (and this was released not so far back in 2017), gamers and the industry expect and deserve better than this kind of low effort shovelware.

These technical defects push this game below acceptable standards for any modern PC game.

One of the driving elements of this shovelware game is "achievement spam", that is to say the game features a ridiculously large number of trivial/automatically granted achievements, 118 of them, in fact. It's a damning indictment for this shovelware that people only bought it for easy achievements, and not because it has any serious merit as a real game.

#monstercakes didn't appeal much to the people who own a copy of the game, either. It has achievements, and they show us a very clear picture that the game didn't really capture any interest from gamers. You are given all the achievements for the game just by starting it, but less than 25 percent of players bothered to get that far before uninstalling the game. Hardly a success story, gamers just weren't all that interested in the game.

Reviewing SteamDB to check how popular this game was with players reveals a surprise... there's a very healthy spike in player counts for the game. But this only appears around the same time that trading cards were applied to the game... so this is just card idlers getting their cards and moving on. A closer look at the numbers shows the game just has a couple of players every week running up the game and idling it for cards and the secondary item marketplace. This is supported by the extremely low achievement rate... less than 1 in 4 people who own this game actually played it.

#monstercakes is relatively cheap at $1 USD, but it's not worth it. Given the defects and quality issues with the game, coupled with the unrealistic price, this is impossible to recommend.

#Monstercakes is a 2017 game from a Russian indie developer which combines a 3-match element with the puzzle slider game called 2048. In the ‘2048’ game which was designed by a 19yr old Italian named Gabriele Cirulli way back in 2014, the objective is to simply slide tiles with the same numeric number into each other doubling their value. You can slide up, down, left and right. Depending which way, you slid the board, all tiles will slide the same way and only those with the same number will combine. After each turn a random tile will appear on the board with the numeric value of 2 or 4. The objective is to create a tile with the score of 2048 and you win.

So, the numeric values in 2048 is 2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048.

#Monstercakes is similar in this way, it also has a progressive combine cake recipe. For instance, 3 white cakes will combine and turn into a purple cake, 3 purple cakes will combine and turn into a green cake and so on. The highest cake is brown and when you manage to combine 3 brown cakes it will explode a 9-block radius and also give you one of 4 powerups available. Allowing you to drag the given power up into the grid and explode all cakes in the direction the arrows are pointing.

While the powerups start off and may look random, they actually aren’t. You can only have 4 power ups in total and if you spend one and then manage to match 3 brown cakes, you will be rewarded with the same power-up you just spent. Each power-up is decorated with various directions where it will explode on the board, you cannot have more than the 1 of each powerup. So, the trick to winning this game and never losing is to use one of your powerups just before you match 3 brown monster cakes together. You will then rid the board of nearly all cakes and you will never become overwhelmed. This is how the top player on the leaderboard gets the score he does.

Its also crucial to know by heart the progressive colour combination values. It goes like this…

White, Purple, Green, Aqua, Red, Yellow, Brown (Brown explodes a 9-block grid)

Since there isn’t really much to the game once you know this, it then brings us to the second reason why most people buy this game. Achievements and stickers. There are 118 achievements to this game that will unlock within the first few minutes. Achievement hunters will be happy to know they ALL unlock. On top of this, the achievements contain all numerical values and alphabet letters. This allows players to type out their name or any message they wish to place onto their Steam profiles in the Achievement showcase section. Click on my steam profile if you want to see it firsthand.

Likewise, the game also drops 3 random sticker packs a day which are in current circulation. (Note: you must log in and out of a game from menu every 30 minutes though. You can’t just idle). In each pack you will be rewarded with 1 sticker. When you get dupes, you can recycle those stickers for fragments. 1 recycled sticker = 1 fragment. When you have 5 fragments you can trade them in for 1 token. When you get 10 tokens you can trade it in for 1 pack which could be rare, that is to say, a pack which is no longer in circulation.

So, putting this all simply. Every now and then, the developer will release a sticker pack for a certain time period. Each day you play you will be rewarded 3 stickers from packs in active circulation. To obtain old stickers no longer in circulation you must either use the token exchange system or buy them at market. Spinners can also only be obtained by token exchange or bought in market. These are animated spinner stickers that will spin when you equip one and hold your mouse button down on them. They won’t work in your steam profile though, but again all the other stickers can be added to your item showcase section in your Steam profile making it that little bit fancier.

You can also create your own stickers which must be a transparent 256x256 png file. After you upload them, they get placed in a review queue section where players can vote on which stickers, they want next. The developer then loads these into a pack and integrates them into the game. The players that create the stickers accepted will then get a fraction of the revenue from direct purchases.

This is where things get crazy. In the community market there is currently a Christmas stocking sticker for $183.37. Its not animated, there’s nothing special about it. It’s a picture of a Christmas stocking nothing more than that. Unfortunately, players everywhere are all too willing to prey on the curious, the vulnerable, or those with an obsessive compulsion.
