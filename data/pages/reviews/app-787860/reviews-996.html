\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, I'm biased a bit because I feel like Giants' business model focuses on them making money at the expense of the consumer rather than it being more of a mutually beneficial exercise. The game engine improvements are nice but often don't warrant having to purchase an entirely new game, new (or the same) DLC, and wait for modders to port their mods over to the new game.

SCS and their ETS2/ATS products have a much better, more fan-friendly model that's all about upgrading the game you invested in ages ago and paying for new content via DLC. With the various equipment licenses, mapping options and the like, Giants could be rolling in plenty of money without having to start over every time.

That all said, the latest edition of the game is "good". They didn't go backwards for the most part and added some much needed improvements to the engine. The visuals? Honestly, I don't see the difference (I've got a RTX 2080 and had a 1080 on FS17). Looks fine, bushes and trees still don't look any better so I don't see what the fuss was about. Ground still looks like same textures, etc.

However, there are some great changes:

- Instead of the static animal sites on the map, you now place them with two sizes available for larger animals like cows and pigs.

- You can adjust terrain finally! Smooth out those weird mapping decisions made by Giants.

- Instead of buying fields you now buy parcels that include 1 or more fields and other structures/sites.

- There's a new contracts system which takes the old "jobs" system from FS17 and makes it more free form. You aren't currying favor to get fields at lower cost. You're just doing jobs like moving merchandise from one site to another and doing the usual field maintenance tasks as before. The rewards are not necessarily outstanding but they can be useful during the early game or when you have downtime and need the funds.

Meanwhile, there are things they completely missed despite the obvious logical step conclusion one would normally make:

- You can't remove junk/structures/etc. from plots you buy except for the base "home" plot you get when you first start. So there's this big chunk of land that has a fence and some broken down cars on it. Fun...but why? I can adjust terrain and sell starting structures but I can't pay a fee to clear some junk on my land? I can't buy a bucket for my wheel loader and shovel that crap off my land?

- The America map included in the base game is both insulting and frustrating at the same time: the place is run down like they picked the worst, most depressed part of the Rust belt and said, "Welcome to America". Meanwhile, it's more like canyon country than farm country. Small fields dotted around a plateau with a few big fields with windy roads barely able to accommodate a tractor, let alone a harvester.

- The train returns but in worse shape than before. At least in FS17 you could just leave the train wherever and be done with it. Now, the train automatically travels around the track whether you like it or not. You can still get into it and control it but if you aren't running it, it's trying its best to mow your equipment down as you get stuck because of the physics engine on the tracks. Yay.

- Horses are an interesting addition but they're overly simplistic and a big time sink. You can't use helpers in any way and the only way horses are valuable is if you run them regularly (ideally every day to maximize value increase). It's fun for a bit then it gets tiresome. You can only tab into a horse if you recently rode it. Otherwise, you have to head to the paddock and hop on again. And instead of basic movement controls similar to your character, there's this acceleration system that puts the horse on auto at a particular cadence. It's awkward and takes away from the fun. It's not the end of the world or anything but I feel like the implementation is lackluster.

- Equipment and mods are like every FS iteration: you start with a decent if limited selection and modders fill the stable out. Giants has released some mods with old favorites and items you're used to but since they're mods and aren't paid for, it begs the question of why? Why not roll them into the base game? And the mods in-game (which tend to be safer than hitting up a modding site and hoping the one download button out of 20 on the page is the right one) are taking forever because the vetting process is slow, from what I've read.

- And of course some minor quibbles are still there and still not addressed for whatever reason. For instance, my pet peeve is the fact that tractors shut off when you leave them. Hold on, you might say, there's an option to leave them on. Au contrare...it doesn't work right. If you go far enough away or tab through enough vehicles, they still turn off in that mode. You might say, per the words of the company, that it's a technological limitation given updates to active objects, etc. But then FS17 had a wonderful mod that turned them on and kept that state just fine. It hasn't come to FS19 yet (and may never, who knows?) but it's missed. Many a log has fallen from a logger because of an errant tab (you know, because Steam's overlay key combination counts as a tab in-game).

All in all (and this is the tl;dr part), it's a fine edition of the game. You get features you can use and the Europe map makes up for the lackluster inspiration of the American map (I miss that Montana map now from FS17...felt like home). The animal feature and terrain tools are big steps that are welcome but the horses and map design hamper what could be a great version of the game. Depending on your ideal price point, you aren't missing too much by sticking with FS17 if you have it and waiting for a sale on FS19. But if you really want those new features, you won't go too wrong by getting FS19 at this point. Plenty of mods have made it over and more keep coming.
