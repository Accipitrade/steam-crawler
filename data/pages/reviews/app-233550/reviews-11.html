\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnother World (or Out of This World here in the USA) was something of an oddity in it's day and might be even more so now.
An artistic project and labor of love, it doesn't seem to quite fit in any category. It looks like a Prince of Persia-style platform game set in another dimension, and in many ways it is. However there are lots of odd puzzle bits and set pieces that don't seem to build up to anything. That's not meant as a bad thing. We're just very used to having games that reuse certain enemies/puzzles/situations with slightly more difficult twists as you progress. Another World bucks that trend. There are certain enemies you will see exactly one time, in one place, in one level...and then never again. I'm unsure how many unique death animations there are but the fact that there are multiple certainly makes it all feel a little more real in an odd sense. It's a small thing but having your character "die" feels different then "losing a credit".

It should also be mentioned that Another World wants very badly to be cinematic and "an experience" instead of merely a game. Opinions will vary on that sort of idea but it's certainly aged better then many other games that tried this. For being a 2-D game with no dialog (that I can recall) and a distinctive art style, it tells a solid clear story. It's not entirely original but it does exactly what it sets out to do and is fairly enjoyable to play through. That's far more then I can say for many of the FMV "games" from the dreaded MultiMedia Era.
It's also subtle in a way that "interactive movies" just never were. The graphics are rotoscoped polygons(instead of sprites), there are no menus/gauges, and I mentioned there was no dialog, yet I never had trouble reading the vibe/emotions of a scene. It's a subtle thing and hard to put into words but if Another World is aiming to be a movie it's shooting for foreign arthouse scifi, instead of summer blockbuster.
The game is short, but demands close to perfection from you. A perfect run could easily be completed in under an hour but instead you'll spend quite a bit of time seeing those unique death animations I mentioned. In a way that's a little bit of how the game fails to break out of it's cinematic aspirations on two fronts. First, the replaying of a short sequence multiple times after each death breaks the flow. Secondly, it often feels as if you're just sort of directing an on-rails platformer. Not literally on rails but there is very little in the way of choice in most situations. It's not terrible or anything but video of anyone playing the game would look virtually the same for any of the action/puzzle sequences. You solve a puzzle one way, in a certain order, or die. You make the exact jump at the exact point or die. It's less about finding a solution to a problem and more about finding the "approved" solution.
Lastly, credit where credit is due. I didn't call it a labor of love as a cliche phrase. With the exception of the music I believe, this game IS Éric Chahi. He wrote it, animated it,programmed it, etc. He even painted the box art. It's an inspiration to independent artists and developers everywhere that he poured so much of himself into a game that's still unique today, and also highly regarded enough it warrants a 20th Anniversary Edition.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tExtremely short (less than 1 hour), if you know the puzzle parts already... but if you do, you wouldn't be reading this review, heh.

For everyone else, this was Half Life, before Half Life existed. A highly cinematic action/puzzle/adventure 2D platformer/shooter. As far as I know this is the first game to ever do scripted sequences this particular way. Think something like the sniper mission in CoD4, except you're not spoonfed by your companion as to what you're supposed to do. That's where the puzzle element comes in.

If this is your first time and you're doing this without a walkthrough, expect to spend a LOT of time in the caves. It will only become apparent to you as to what you should do in the caves after you got through half the city and it will keep resetting you in the caves on death until you do that one thing you're suposed to do. Now this is quite amazing, because for a scripted action game it rather excourages a lot of exploration so that you get hints for the puzzles.

If you can get this game super cheap - like less than 5 eur - and you're interested in some gaming history, absolutely give it a try. Fleeing high powered laser shots as they keep blasting steel doors apart behind you gets the adrenaline pumping... or just watch a quick playthrough on youtube. It makes for a quite good movie.\t\t\t\t\t
