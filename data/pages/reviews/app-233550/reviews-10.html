\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOh nostalgia, why must you let me down?

How hard is it to give a facelift to a game while staying true to the original? I'm not sure which version of "Out of this World" (now called "Another World", cause reasons) they decided to base this anniversary edition on, but it sucks. I 1st played this game on the SNES and in my opinion, that remains the best version. It was funny to see a cartridge have load times. I loved the base that the music had and liked the way the music would have variations. I liked the base on the sound effects. le sigh...

THIS version of "Another World" sorely lacks in some areas while improving in others...
There are much sharper backgrounds now and some puzzle/enemy mechanics play the way they were perhaps originally intended. For example, the leeches on the cavern roof actually drop down on the first stage. There is an adjustable difficulty on the game, but I did not use that.

Why do I have only 11 minutes on this game? Cause it was enough time for me to feel betrayed. 2 action segments in. Both had complete silence from the soundtrack. What were originally panic inducing moments, moments that said "hey, you're REALLY in danger right now, you better do a thing..." are now quiet with occasional sound effects that sound like they were in a hurry to play. The gun which was originally a very impressive weapon is now a small thing that tweets when you fire it. Some of the motion/animation has been replaced with basic polygonal modeling. This game was groundbreaking back in the day because the animation had an intense hand drawn feel and some of it was rotoscoped. Now... it feels cheap. I'm glad I bought this on sale, cause it is not worth 10$.

Now, pardon me, but I'm going to hook up my old SNES (thankfully still works), take out my copy of "Out of this World" and enjoy a classic.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is no doubt that Another World is considered a milestone in narrative and cinematic presentation in video games. ric Chahi's grounbreaking masterpiece is still influencing countless creators till this day, expanding upon the ideas and mechanincs, refining what we today call "cinematic platformer". Although credits where credits are due, the originator traces back to the very first Prince of Persia.
What makes Another World different however, is the more cinematic approach, without any kind of visible user interface on-screen and a somehwat more linear progression with a variety of set pieces aimed to tell a more action-packed hollywood-style sci-fi story rather than the more game-y and complex level design, platforming and puzzle solving of the aforementioned Prince. But Another World is not without its puzzles either, using your weapon and wits (and precision and speed) to outcome each cinematic event.

But does a grounbreaking - for 1991 standards - game still hold up today? In some ways yes, the game is as enjoyable and interesting as it wasback then, but not without some issues that I wish this anniverary edition would tweak.
First of all you notice that the game packs some gorgeous redrawn graphics and higher resolution models bringing the imaginative alien world truly to life - but for nostalgia seekers you can always revert to the original simplistic amiga graphics with a press of a button.
Sound is also offered in original and remastered version but unfortunatelywithout the option to hear the magnificent music soundtrack in game. The reason is that Chahi's original and intended vision was a game without the additional music that was added in later ports but it would benice if we could have that option as well. Strangely enough , the music is offered as a bonus in the game's extras - along with a few nice assets, artworks, early designs and an interesting 20-min documentary about the creation of the game. I find it odd that the bonus content can't be accessed by the main menu in-game but instead though the launcher/installation folder. This is a wonderful little package to celebrate the legacy of the game.

One criticism can be made against the control scheme of the game, which hasn't aged all that well. Perhaps due to the fact that it's a relic from the Amiga days, where you had only your joystick and one button to rely upon, many actions like shooting and running are bound to the same button, making some sections really awkward to navigate, while pressing up results in jumping. (However there is still another separate jump button and I question why they couldn't refine the controls to give you an option for sepeerate attack and run button prompts)

Lastly, another complaint is the quality of thsi port. Constant crashing is prevalent resulting in some frustrating situations - some times the checkpoints are not so generous - but at least there are plentiful - and the safest way to avoid thsi serious problem is to locate your executable file and change the compatibility to "windows 95" (at least that seems to fix the problem for me and others). For a game claiming to be an anniversary edition designed for modern computers, requiring you to revert to win95 compatibility is a bit embarrassing.
If not for this fix and the checkpoints, I might have resulted in giving this a thumbs down because of the shoddy port regardless of the quality of the game itself.

As for the quality of the game itself, it does require some patience , trial & error and some parts might seems a bit rough and archaic, but the game holds surprisingly well even today despite some shortcomings that show its age. It's not particularly long, especially if you know what to do (and perhaps speedrunners will find some excitement attempting a perfect run - and in different difficulties with the new "Hard mode" actually being the original "normal difficulty" of 1991), but for the rest, a regular playthough might perhaps culminate into 2 - 2&half hours.

Just like a movie.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGingers...in...space!

PROS: This game is a classic, and the feel was carried over. The feeling of one of the older Prince of Persia games is still held in the game, giving every action weight. The atmosphere is heavy, full of dread, and makes you feel set in the world around you. The lack of dialogue keeps the game moving at a quick pace, and with all games of this sort, it's short. That's not the insult it seems on first view. Games like this need to be short and crisp to keep the impact. Too long, and the game can potentially start to drag.

CONS: The visuals are stylized, but they won't mesh very well with everyone. The game has a lack of direction in some parts, but it isn't too bad. It's not moon logic, but you may find yourself getting stuck in very few areas that have only small indicators on how to move on, if any at all. There are also a few trial and error spots that all but guarantee a death on your first pass to show you how the puzzle works, so that can be a tad frustrating.

ACHIEVEMENTS: Simple and easy, most will be gotten through gameplay. I actually had to go back for another playthrough just to unlock the one to die a number of times, so that should give you an idea of the game's overall difficulty.

FINAL: This game is a classic, and feels like one. That last caveat will be the make or break point for anyone looking at this game. If you enjoy classic style 'realistic' platforming with a decent challenge and a silent story, I'd highly recommend this game. Anything else, and it may not be to your liking. Still, it's only a few hours to complete the game, so I'd still recommend at least giving it a look.\t\t\t\t\t
