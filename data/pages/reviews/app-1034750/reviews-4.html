\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Flower Collectors is a game that's unafraid. It presents a story that is proudly anti-fascist and only possible through the representation of many different kinds of people (well, animals in this case). It casts you as a protagonist (Jorge) that is pretty repulsive in some ways and doesn't goad you to celebrate that. It is a game that revolves around non-violent game play while presenting a story often about violence. The Flower Collectors takes a lot of big swings, and for me most of them land.

The Flower Collectors takes place on one street corner. One balcony really. The primary gameplay involves taking pictures of different events on a street corner and helping a journalist, Melinda, uncover a murder. This may sound simple,but this gameplay works for the run time (about 3-4 hours) and the situations you observe keep it interesting. There is also quite a bit of dialog and some story choices you make that help give the story some stakes. What I appreciate about this gameplay loop is that you don't just move from one set piece to another, but get to be an actual actor in the story. I am not sure if you can fail any sequence, but the game does put time limits on certain sections which adds just enough tension to keep it from feeling too relaxed. I thought this was a smart system for a game and I think The Flower Collectors did a good job with it.

The Flower Collector suffers a bit in polish though. Many of the animations are stilted, which can be a bit distracting in the middle of a scene or dialog. But, this is made up for by the voice acting. It's quite good. Specifically, Jorge has a range that I was really impressed by. Jorge was able to ride the line between being a sympathetic character while also a jerk well and in a way that did not feel too forced. I'm not sure I would have bought the character otherwise.

I am overall impressed with this game and recommend anyone who wants to play more games that are unashamed of taking a political stance to pick it up. It goes for it in a way that many games don't and for the most part succeeds.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis really immersive game can be thought as a mix between Hitchcock's classic "Rear Window" and Firewatch game. The game has a nice, interesting noir story, very well narrated and voice-acted. It can also be conceived as a theater piece since all ten episodes take place in your apartment (and balcony). Although there is little to no real puzzles, The Flower Collectors is very enjoyable since the story advances by means of your voyeaurish investigations (in a few situations, you will also guide characters down in the street âradio in hand- by directing them in order to keep them safe from patrols).
It all begins with a murder down in the plaza below, and you, riding your wheelchair and armed with binoculars/camera, have to resolve it in the following days. There are characters that will help you on this quest, and your apartment will also be the scenery where people and situations take place. The game is very well crafted since the isolation inside your home is not a limit, but a very well squeezed resource.
If you liked Firewatch, this is a short game that very much resembles its unique perfume.
Note: all characters are anthropomorphic animals ânot humans. There is really no need or explanation of that, and I could not find any proper usefulness in this choice. Anyway, it is something forgettable as soon as one is hooked with the story and events.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt a time where democracies all over the world seem to be sliding back into authoritarian regimes, when dictators like Orban in Hungary have amassed power to promote skewed traditional values at the expense of others that don't fit within their idealized visions of the nation, narratives like The Flower Collectors remind us of the dangers authoritarian strongmen pose, and how precious democracy really is.

Set in Barcelona, Spain during the tail end (no pun intended) of Franco's regime, you play an ex-cop named Jorge whose daily monotony gets overturned when he witnesses a murder in the town plaza one stormy night. Together with Melinda, a journalist for a leftist publication, they seek to uncover the mystery behind this murder and find the deep ties that strangle everyone in this small town. From the cabaret that serves as a reprieve for artists and poets seen as degenerates against the regime, to the church that enforces strict values for the sake of maintaining power within this system, everyone is involved and connected somehow through six degrees of separation. All this ties with the general elections coming up, and the future of Spanish democracy with it.

The historical backdrop reeks into the everyday lives of these characters; after all, life itself is political as Melinda notes, and it seeps into the most minute details of the town's denizens and their lives. The game does well in discussing how police authority can be used and abused in authoritarian environments, as well questions whether those often deemed as 'degenerate' against a government's traditional values really deserve to be labeled as such in the first place.

While the narrative and world building is engaging, areas in terms of gameplay need to be more polished; as Jorge is disabled and bound to a wheelchair, you conduct most of your investigations and searches seated from the balcony, with Melinda serving as your feet on the ground. You relay to her instructions through your transmitters, while watching from above through binoculars and camera lenses. At the end of the day, you take the clues you amassed and pin them up on a board, searching for connections and answers in the process. As most of your tasks often leave you to watch the action taking place, you're often left feeling like an observer most of the time. This may have some narrative use, as Jorge's arc is one that transforms him from passive observation to taking on a more active role and responsibility, but it does leave one to wonder what details they're missing that could help expand upon the situation of each character and how they tie to the greater dangers of the world as a whole. Sometimes, the instructions on the left-hand corner of the screen are too vague, and I've spent quite an amount of time searching in frustration for clues in a heavily detailed environment with little to no direction, unsure of what I should be looking for in the first place.

The animation can appear jarring and odd at times, lacking a polish as characters sometimes slide across the ground; which is a shame, because overall the art style does contain some charm to it. The world outside Jorge's balcony is no doubt alive, and the details do make one feel they are in some small Spanish town, but areas in the rigging could be improved upon to avoid yanking the player out from the immersive experience of the world.

Overall, The Flower Collectors is a charming game with a lot to talk about. While areas in the gameplay and animation could be improved upon, its message feels relevant and timely.\t\t\t\t\t
