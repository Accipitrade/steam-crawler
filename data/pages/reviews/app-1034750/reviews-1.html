\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe premise of the game is nothing new in movies but it seemed like an interesting idea for a game. Unfortunately it ended up being closer to a point and click. The story is simple and straight forward. Given the subject matter, it leaves a lot to be desired. This game is meant to be serious and address serious issues, I was having an incredibly hard time taking it seriously solely because of the animations. The animations, out of everything, are what left the worst impression. The animations come across as lazy and unpolished, frequently not matching the situation that’s currently playing out before your eyes. (Ex: the police “punch” a character but the animation is of them stomping. The character getting “punched” just lays down on the ground like Apu spills his tendies)

I would argue that animations are just as important as story and worth the time and money to make them look clean, polished, and at the very least convincing. Clearly, all the animations were taken from Mixamo because I recognize a bunch of them. There were no in-betweens so characters would snap, slide, blend into their next animation. The fingers frequently don’t animate correctly. (Ex: hands go into an “ok” position and they bend back like hot dogs) The devs did not spend enough time on the character movements as the characters are often sliding around during walk and run animations. All of this just really took me out of the experience. It’s hard to connect with what’s happening when you’ve got characters that are over the top animating like the Sims. (Ex: Main big bad villain waving at the bouncer with one leg up like a schoolgirl)

Unfortunately the story and the game play are just ok and are not enough to make me look past the, honestly, comedic animations. I feel like the making of this game was a bit rushed. With more time and dedication to all aspects of development (particularly animation), this could have been a better game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI would say this game is worth a play. It's story is really what carries it completely as it's so very passionate about the source material. The game is presented to you through the eyes of a man in a wheelchair looking off his balcony, he is powerless to interact with the world around him. He also is someone that starts almost entirely opposed to the message the game is telling, he is a grumpy annoyed old cop who wants to be left alone and judge people from up above. Even still, with enough evidence and observation he begins to understand and see the world around him. He doesn't fully get everything by the end and has his reservations, but I personally like that part as it shows that maybe for us to spark societal or political change we need to get some people we don't really like onto our side, something I think our current society struggles with understanding as we often like to take the nuclear polarizing option. That is what I liked about the game/story, I would go as far as to say this is the kind of material I'd like to see used to educate children in school. Now what isn't so great is the visual presentation. The character portraits of the townsfolk are pretty ugly, very polygonal but not quite achieving the low-poly look of something like "For the King", the waiter or rabbit character being the worst offenders. The animations and pathfinding are pretty bad, they look like The Sims moving around in the early games. The gameplay is pretty uninspired, just click on the interact points. There are a couple of puzzle-like parts matching evidence but they aren't really puzzles as you have no time limit, no limited tries, and the game tells you if you got any part wrong instantly. Nevertheless, they should be included as-is because they do make sure the player understands the events of the day. It's was good to play, not mind blowing but still very interesting and with many moving parts.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn the shadow of Facist General Franco’s death, the nation of Spain began an uneasy transition into democracy. It is the mid 70s and Spain faces its first free election in 40 years. It is during this period that our protagonist, Jorge, lives above a sleepy town square. Jorge is a wheelchair-using, former police officer who spends most days watching and sketching the world around him.

‘The Flower Collectors’ is a narrative heavy detective story with a ‘whodunnit?’ theme. You spend the majority of your time observing the residents around you and trying to piece together what happened, when a man is shot dead in the square below your apartment. It is brought to you by Mipuni Games, the same studio that released the well received ‘The Lion’s Song’ in 2016.

Graphically, The Flower Collectors is fine. The colour palette used is evocative of the setting, the warm oranges dominate and really give you a sense of the Spanish locale. The use of a smaller environment is a nice idea, but it is often let down by the lifelessness of it. NPCs within the square feel static and inanimate, with no real ‘awareness’ of what is happening around them. This is compounded by poor animations that feel almost ‘stock’ and are very exaggerated, bringing to mind the cartoonish animations of The Sims or even Lionhead’s ‘The Movies’.

The game’s characters are anthropomorphic cats, dogs, foxes etc. which suits the cartoon style the game has gone for, but much like the scenery around them, they are fairly basic models; with no emotion conveyed. This isn’t helped by the dialogue system in place, whilst the voice acting stands out as well acted, the game’s delivery of it is lacking. The omission of auto scroll dialogue makes sentences feel disjointed from one another and serves only to break immersion. The switch between English and Spanish pronunciations is strange too; an option to experience this in the native tongue would have been welcome.

These issues with characters really undermine the game’s greatest asset, its story. Whilst this is no game changing tale, and at times almost excruitantly generic, it is mostly a well delivered and interesting affair. The game handles exposition well enough, with the usual trope of investigating items and objects. These fill in tidbits of information and the characters you meet/observe are likable enough; although sometimes it feels like the emotional connection to them was born from how you expect them to react to situations rather than how they are actually reacting within the game itself. Sadly the themes of oppression and history of the era are largely an unexplored backdrop to the game’s events and whilst they help set up the narrative, it feels like a missed opportunity to learn more about this important part of Spain’s modern history.

The main villain of this piece is not a well crafted antagonist. but the game mechanics themselves. Often the slow and boring sequences eliminate any pacing or tension the story has built. Game play sequences amount to little more than point cursor at X, for an arbitrary amount of time. You are tasked with investigating the world’s characters by the means of sketches, and later photography. These mechanics are woefully thin, and could have been expanded upon to give you a feeling of being more involved. These are brought together at the end of chapters on a noticeboard, where you have to piece together your findings. These sequences serve as a good way to recap events and keep you on track with the story. The chapters themselves are handled poorly however. Often ending with a ‘oh I’m tired’ sequence, followed by a fade to black. This feels lazy and frustrating; especially when it happens around a story revelation that has you motivated to learn more.

This is a theme that runs through the game unfortunately. How the story is presented is often at odds with how you want to play. You may notice something in the environment that is interesting, but there is no reward for doing so. A broken window springs to mind, which stands out and could have been interesting, considering the mystery aspect of the game, but it isn’t even given a line of dialogue and is never explored later on. You often find yourself having to guide a character into a building, avoiding security. There are no choices or player freedom as to how to do this and the glaringly obvious distraction technique is not used until later on when the story decides it should be used; it feels restrictive. Timed sequences also feel arbitrary with little feedback as to how failure (almost impossible) might be handled, with poorly used music cues used to inject a little slice of contrived tension.The lack of incidental happenings in the environment and lifeless characters, make these sequences dull and forgettable; with little to no challenge. For a game based around observing people, observing them simply isn’t very fun.

The story progresses and wraps up in a satisfying manner, it is pleasing to see how things turn out for characters and there is scope to have further lines of dialogue depending on information/evidence gathered throughout the game. Annoyingly some of the story beats are helped along by frankly ridiculous realisations from characters, however. A character’s identification is clearly featured on a newspaper a fair while before the character notices, and the game’s later macguffin is handled very poorly are two stand outs. It is frustrating to realise things far sooner than the characters do, with no reasonable explanation as to why. One of these realisations is the main character’s past history, a huge part of the story itself, with absolutely no explanation as to why he wouldn’t have remembered it sooner or even need to remember it at all. Maybe if the game’s POV character had been explored in greater detail, perhaps to explain this strange omission from his memory, you could look past this; but it’s not. Sadly Jorge amounts to little more than a nihilistic ex-cop cliché.

The Flower Collectors is an interesting enough detective adventure game that makes frustrating design choices. A curious little world, let down by poor mechanics, an often stiff and lifeless world filled with immersion breaking audio and visual design. There is a good game to be found here, but it requires a lot of patience to navigate the simplistic and dull gameplay.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Flower Collectors is a breath of fresh air in the Detective/Puzzle video game genre with a lot of drawbacks.

The game relies a lot on how enthralling the story is, it kinda succeeds a it. The story is interesting but one of the first things I noticed is that character arcs are not well executed, everything seems a little bit rushed and sometimes contradictory, but that doesn't necessarily make the story worst. You can definitely nitpick some things like the constant and sometimes exhausting “progressive” talk throughout the game but the story is very political so it is expected. I didn't enjoy that some things are left unexplained by the end of the game, it doesn't make sense to make a sequel out of the story so I don't know the purpose of leaving details out.

Gameplay could be better, I played on keyboard and mouse and it was rough. Moving was a little bit awkward and slow. Selecting dialogue was also kinda rough but not a major issue. The game does support controller play and I think that may be the way to go. Animations are good for an Indie game, the art style is very interesting and overall great. Voice acting is not very good, it feels awkward at its best and empty at its worst, but it is passable for an Indie game. Some of the animations are kinda janky but not a huge deal, the main thing is that sometimes you have to wait for animations to complete to continue the story and that can be good or bad. The game doesn't require you to do something every single second of gameplay, it makes you observe a lot and that's the point of it. I would describe the whole gameplay as a big cutscene with some actual gameplay in between. Puzzles and problem solving are not challenging but are definitely entertaining.

Now let's address the two huge elephants in the room, the inspiration from “Rear Window” and the fact that the characters are anthropomorphic animals. The inspiration is clear, a man bound to a wheelchair who likes to watch the neighborhood tries to solve a mystery from afar. Do the devs credit Hitchcock? From what I can tell, no. The characters being anthropomorphic animals is something kinda shocking at first but you get over it quickly. Does it take away from the actual story? Kinda, it doesn't make the game worse but the main theme of the game is a very heavy and real subject, it makes it seem less serious. If you believe this game is “furry trash” just because of the anthropomorphic animals, grow up it is a clear stylization decision and nothing else.

Achievement hunting is not very good, you can't 100% this game on your first gameplay. You have to go back and make different decisions to get some achievements and that is definitely boring. The game has little to no replay value and lacks extra content in general.

Overall I would not recommend this game to the casual player even though I really enjoyed it. I think it takes someone to really like story heavy games and be open to some unpolished aspects to enjoy this game. If the game is on sale and you are willing to look over some details and like story driven games, go for it.\t\t\t\t\t
