\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe flower collectors is first person investigation that takes place in Barcelona in 1977 two years after death of Franco and in the weeks coming up to the first elections that signalled Spains' transition into democracy.

Your character is Jorge a former member of the infamous Policia Armada who was retired to a wheelchair following a savage revenge beating. The game takes place mostly in Jorges apartment, on his balcony overlooking the central plaza he lives on. Whiling away his days drinking, stoned on painkillers and spying on his neighbours, he witnesses a murder and decides to piece together the evidence and solve the crime. He's aided in this by a rebel freelance journalist, Melinda, who was to meet the victim for a story she was about to break.

The game wears it's influences on it's sleeve, most notably the Hitchcock movie "Rear Window". Much like the movie, the entire game is from the point of view of a character confined to a wheelchair, observing everyone around him. The murder even takes place during a thunderstorm, just like in the movie. After the murder, upon seeing the polices' lack of protocol and urgency surrounding the crime, Jorge becomes convinced there is something up. Enter Mel, who reveals herself as a journalist who had scheduled the meet with the victim for information on atrocities committed by the Policia Armada and the Guardia Civil.

Over the course of the game you will piece together the stories of the people you have been spying on, solve the murder and help Jorge reconcile himself with his past.

For the most part the game looks fine. The slightly cell-shaded look to the graphics works for the scenery. The issue with the graphics comes to the character models. They're designed well enough, it's just that none of them have proper eyes. They all have this eerie blank stare like you've wound up on the set of the Children of the Corn or some other horror movie. It just destroys a lot of the relatability of a character when you're constantly looking at them thinking "Did they mean to use searing comets for their eyes or was it a mistake?"

The model movements aren't properly done either. They all move with a real artificial style and really make it seem like they are completely separate from the ground or any other objects they're interacting with. The movements don't always sync up with what they're saying either in the dialogue scenes. Some times they're giving off nonchalant body movements when giving a really serious or important line, other times I was looking at a character go full Calculon while describing a mundane activity. This is a particular bugbear as there areso many dialogue scenes that it results in the lack of proper mo-cap becoming a really glaring issue. The lip-sync is terrible too also. There's a chance that's it's synced to the original Spanish and the English voice-over is just a dub but there's no option to play with Spanish Voice-overs so I've no idea. Blasphemous had a similar problem in that, while it was developed by a Spanish studio staffed almost entirely by Spanish speakers, it was released with only English Audio to try to give it as wide an appeal as possible. In fact, it only recently, as of this videos release, got a Spanish Audio release years after the game launch.

The dialogue is decent. It progresses the story but it's not going to blow you away. The best I could say about it, is that it's functional. Most of the characters just outright say what they feel, like they've all been to the same support group or something. I suppose this is a result of the limitation of the graphics. Without the ability to convey anything through facial expressions, you're relying on the script and the voice actor to do the emotional and tonal heavy lifting. Unfortunately, it falls downs on both counts. The voice actors efforts are completely undermined by some really poor editing that leaves huge gaps in-between each line. It really takes the immersion from a scene involving two people in a room when it's abundantly clear that the voice actors aren't.

The use of animals as characters mirrors the similar use of them in the comic series MAUS which uses the cutesy animals as a sort of fluffy counterpoint to the Nazi atrocities it depicts. The flower collectors uses them as a counterpoint to the horrors of Francos Spain. However, unlike MAUS, flower collectors never really goes into any real detail. For all the story you're told, the police were a nasty group of people just beat up and imprisoned a load of artists, poets and gays. A few died and that's what fuels the story but it goes nowhere near the reality that, at one point, around 250 people a day were being shot dead by the police forces in Barcelona alone. In Madrid the number was closer to 400.

I suppose my main issue with this is that the developers could've gone into some real depth here about a topic that isn't very well known. Spain under Franco was basically a War-Crime factory for over thirty years and almost no-one outside of Spain knows a huge amount about it. Even less when you venture outside Europe. So there was a huge vein of historical material that could've been mined to give the story some real bite and traction. Instead the mass deaths are pared down to a handful and the White Terror isn't even mentioned. They even cop out on Jorge by having him state he can't remember what he got up to during his time in the Flower collectors, in an act of ultimate fence-sitting. It would've given the scenes so much more impetus if he'd actually been a witness or even responsible for some deaths. We're given a hand waivey "I was drunk and I can't remember" excuse. I found this odd as it almost seemed as if the writers didn't want to discuss the very events that underpinned the game. This running away from the source material undermined the story and the characters in a big way. With such an interesting angle, I can't see why the devs didn't completely go all in on it.

As for the story itself, it's passible. The series of events that lead from the murder to the resolution never get too far fetched and the mini-stories of all the minor characters are quite good, in particular the one of Carmen the singer. I was confused by the dream sequences being dropped so suddenly. We get one almost every chapter end and they seem to be building to a crest regarding how Jorge came to be in his wheelchair but then we never get another one. Then we're just told bluntly how he got injured. It seemed like such wasted opportunity.

The gameplay is good. Given the nature of the main character, the style of investigation is entirely observation based and it's done quite well. The mechanic is repeated throughout the game so, if it has you bored in the first twenty minutes, I'd quit the game and get the refund because it doesn't get any better as the game goes on. My main issue with the gameplay is that it's quite repetitive in that most of what you're doing is just scanning for an item or person, finding them and clicking.

In conclusion, The flower collectors is an interesting little game (2.5 hours) with a decent story and some OK artwork. It's a game with some good points all offset by some poor design and/or execution. It has good artwork but its' simplicity has all the characters look like they've been hypnotised. The voice-acting is good but is offset by the terrible mo-cap. The dialogue and story work well but there's so much more that could've been put in to really enhance it and give some impact but it falls short in the scant playtime. This is a bit ironic as more game time would've really highlighted the repetitive nature of the gameplay even more and ruined the game.

I'd like to recommend Flower collectors as a game with a unique play angle and a decent storyline but I can't for full price. There's just not enough here, both in content and effort, to justify it. But on sale, I definitely recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is one of many noir detective games featuring anthropomorphic animals in my Steam library, which is not a sentence I ever thought I'd type. If that's your extremely niche favorite genre, then you'll love The Flower Collectors.

A Rear Window-inspired voyeur mystery set in 1970s Barcelona, this short and sweet little video game will take you through the conflict and emotions of a retired, now-disabled cop who is forced to look at his own history through a new lens after witnessing a murder from his balcony. With the aid of a cunning left-wing journalist, you may be the only hope for uncovering the truth.

The Good
  • The story and characters are compelling, and the historical/political angle is relevant and interesting to today.
  • The investigation style is unique; it's entirely observation-based, and you direct your journalist friend to perform actions down on the ground.
  • The voice acting is really stellar, and the dialogue is pretty darn good.
  • The mood is spot-on for a noir mystery.
  • You compile notes, photos, and sketches on a pin board. The mechanic was simple, but I loved it!

The Not-So-Good
  • Playtime is very short. Probably about 3 hours (as long as you don't accidentally leave it paused and running like I did). I would never recommend paying $20 for this game, no matter how much I liked it. Get it on sale if you can.
  • Animations are clunky. Navigation and UI are fine, but watching the characters move and react is not unlike watching Sims throwing a fit. It's especially evident when you're interacting up close.
  • Waiiiiitiiiinnggg. I appreciate the realism, but sometimes you're just waiting for your journalist friend to walk from place to place. Given that the whole game is spent observing, it's really not fun to just... wait. And watch.

All things considered, I still really liked this mini-adventure game. I'd love to see this concept even more fleshed out in the future; it felt a little more like a short story than a full novel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCalling The Flower Collectors a detective mystery game is like calling cheesecake a health food; the components that make it means you’re correct but mostly on a technicality. This isn’t to say the game fails on its premise, but it feels more like a visual novel played out at a distance (pun partially intended). Sharp writing, good voice acting and an intriguing story kept me hooked to complete the game in one sitting, but I walked away from it feeling less like a sleuth and more of an inquisitive spectator.

Since this is a story-driven game, discussing its plot in detail would lead to spoilers, so I’ll only touch on themes and what’s known in the marketing. With the game taking place in Barcelona in 1977, how much is reflected of our real world is beyond my historical/social knowledge, but it certainly addresses subjects that are very real and still encountered in our modern time. Playing as Jorge, a wheelchair-bound ex-cop, a murder occurs in the plaza outside his home. Through morbid curiosity, and with the local police not making progress, he falls in cahoots with a journalist named Melinda, teaming up to lead their own investigation. While player control is with Jorge, being a wheelchair user means he can’t wander about to question people or examine scenes; Melinda acts on his behalf this way. Either through binoculars or a camera, Jorge can scope the surrounding neighbourhood and indicate what Melinda should investigate or who she should talk to via receiver radios. It’s seldom for Jorge to speak to others directly (excluding Melinda), so after directing Melinda, there’s downtime as she chats up a person or examines a scene. You’re not left twiddling idle thumbs for very long, usually long enough to give the area another scan or think on possibilities for the case while she does her thing.

Thanks to solid writing, information that’s reported back is succinct, to the point and lacks useless fluff (red herrings excluded) that also gets recorded in Jorge’s journal for later reflection. After significant events or the end of that chapter, registered info, clues and testimony must be placed on a timeline to uncover motives, occurrences and eventually, solve the murder. Unsurprisingly things aren’t that simple story-wise as the investigation unfolds, but unfortunately it is that simple gameplay-wise. It boils down to plunking puzzle pieces into place then moving right along to the next chapter. As the player, you aren’t investigating per se; Jorge and Melinda are doing most if not all of the vocalization and thinking - You just slap their findings on a corkboard and call it a day. There didn’t appear to be any fail states for placing the wrong clues to the wrong place either, just a quick ‘no, that’s not right’ and have you try again.

This would’ve gotten in my way of enjoying the game if not for the script, concept and actors pulling me in and keeping my interest. Where choices really become your own is in the limited order versus chaos dilemma the game poses. Considering the year, location and political climate the game communicates, I can’t help but feel your moral alignment with these choices are merely black and white. Order is intrinsically evil as it favours corruption and brushing things under the rug to retain balance, while chaos isn’t pretty, but it means letting the truth be known and taking the ‘system’ down a peg*. The lack of finger-wagging (there’s no saving you from Melinda’s incensed wrath at times, though) by the game makes it a story to experience and not having any titanic weight of ‘the big moral lesson’ hanging above your head. For the heavy subjects the game addresses, it’s all played seriously but with sensitivity to avoid portraying the villains as cartoonish in their evil intent, or portraying the locals as cardboard cutouts whose fates are easily disposable. This treatment also extends to Jorge’s disability and using a wheelchair, a concept that’s not lofted to push an agenda or try to garner empty sympathy; it’s a part of him as a character/person, the game works with and around it. Admittedly, this concept was what garnered my interest in the game in the first place. As someone with disabilities of my own, it’s not often to see such characters portrayed without being treated as a punchline or used for the aforementioned empty sympathy.

Despite its genre, The Flower Collectors favours solid, full colours instead of hard noir comic stylings. Ignoring the awful occurrences nearby, the game is warm to the eyes, which avoids strain when scoping around for clues. While it’s only one location and with the limited sight of it, Jorge’s neighbourhood is a nice place to look at. The warmth of the visuals does mean that when there’s an action to trigger, it may not immediately be apparent since there’s no evident highlights or markers, the HUD itself being very limited. I only had this occur twice in my three and a half hour playthrough, and anything time-sensitive generally has a generous window to work with.

While the gameplay is limited to looking around, putting clues on a board to get a yes or no response and a handful of dialogue options, The Flower Collectors does just enough with its story, interactions and concept to keep it from being flimsy and dull. There’s some replayability with side plots that can be missed, though they don’t seem critical to the game’s completion, more of wrapping up all the optional loose ends. You can find better if you want a mystery game that asks more of you as a player, but on the other hand, if you want an interactive mystery to solve and not have the stress of various webs of intrigue to sift through, The Flower Collectors gets a definite recommendation from me.

*My own moral alignment may be influencing this view, so I can’t call this a criticism in fairness.\t\t\t\t\t

The Flower Collectors: Rear Window with Wildlife!

The Flower Collectors is a detective noir, murder-mystery in which the player takes on the role of a wheelchair-bound, ex-Policía Jorge as he tries to solve a crime from his balcony with the help of a young journalist. Set in Barcelona in 1977, the game’s narrative is intertwined with the complexities of a post-Francoist Spain just before its first free election in four decades.

Overall, this game was an enjoyable experience with a familiar but intriguing narrative and fascinating social setting. However, the low overall production quality, particularly in terms of the aesthetics, along with the short playtime and the limitations imposed by certain development choices (which in turn, feel like cheap crutches) make this game hard to recommend at full price despite the positive elements.
