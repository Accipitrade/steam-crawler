\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCinematic but in a good way. It's really great at building tension and making you feel like a space is more than an arena for gameplay. The story is pretty simple, but it's kind of incredible how much you end up reading into the little 16bit faces of these characters by the end of it. There's also a really good romance subplot that feels earned entirely through unexpected mechanical shifts. It's also not afraid to use cutscenes, which is kind of rare for an indie game. Like proper, cutting to pieces of animation that have been edited in sequence cutscenes that are just dripping with character.

The puzzles are good and the level design is well suited to teaching you how to use all your little abilities, but the early weapons never really felt that useful outside of the level where you get them, and the combat is never particularly challenging. I died three times (twice to the final boss and once because I got stunlocked in a pool of electrical water that I didn't see rushing into a room), and I was never that close to dying anywhere else in the game.

Check it out though, the vibes are immaculate and you get special combat abilities by building a collection of various hats which like, c'mon, pretty great.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR = Lots of games are done in pixel art style. This game succeeds in being pixel art.

It's tempting to be particularly critical when assessing pixel art games, because the gaming market is so full of them. Olija manages to distinguish itself from the pack, and I noticed two things in particular that helped it do so.

#1 It's incredibly well layered
For me a common frustration of pixel platformers is that I find them difficult to become immersed in. When I'm following a visual narrative I want the creator to take me somewhere and the lack of dimensions in pixel art often stops me from feeling this way.

In Olija, cut-scenes, menus, levels, everything is so consistently detailed and heavily layered. There are always multiple layers in front of and behind the main character e.g. to show weather and background world events (e.g. layers of fog, or critters running in front of the "camera"). These extra visual layers really help to add texture and depth to the world.

#2 Each "screen" exists as part of a whole
As in most pixel art games, Olija separates each screen presented to the player into "scenes" - with the player moving to the edge of one scene before the view will jump to the next. It can be difficult to remain focussed on the narrative when games do this, because each view is treated (and presented) in isolation.

Olija overcomes this issue by keeping multiple scenes active around the player. For example when Faraday throws his harpoon and embeds it in a creature, he can then "zip" straight to the creature's position. Often, progressing through levels requires you to cast the harpoon across multiple screens to hit a particular creature so that you jump to its position and move forwards. This is a neat trick which forces the player to consider each scene as part of a whole.\t\t\t\t\t
