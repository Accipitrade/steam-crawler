A concise, well crafted journey through Japanese folklore

Olija is perhaps the epitome of short but sweet. I was really surprised by the tight gameplay and especially how well combat was handled, the weapons were varied and the animations made my fortune seeking Lord Faraday look awesome (there's also hats!).

The game initially seemed quite open ended but instead is quite sensibly crafted into a series of fun levels that the game subtly steers you through and while the difficulty never really poses a challenge there are some fun mechanic focused bosses and ponderous puzzles.

I really enjoyed how the story was told in Olija, the atmosphere was created through a series of scripted actions and the narrative was presented in a minimalistic but effective way, where dialogue was used sparsely and seemed better for it. The pixel art is basic but far from ugly and it is a testament to the games design how characters and relationships are formed through unspoken gestures despite the initial perceived constraint's in the games art style.

Overall, this is a great indie experience I would urge anyone to play. Although my time was short in Olija, I found myself lost in it from the outset.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDecently fun action platformer. Controls mostly tight, except the harpoon throwing is a little wonky since it only accepts 8 directions. Throwing and shooting diagonally can be a pain. There were also some weird bugs when I played, sometimes at the start of an area certain sounds wouldn't play, like the background noise or the harpoon thrusts, and one time the game crashed during a loading screen (didn't lose any progress though). So minor stuff, not a big deal.

Music was pretty cool.

Not nearly worth 15 dollars full price, though. There's not enough gameplay (my run lasted 6 hours, and there are a decent amount of cutscenes), and the story is serviceable, not fantastic. Not an unforgettable gaming experience by any measure. Get it when it's on sale or something, and actually worth it to buy.\t\t\t\t\t
