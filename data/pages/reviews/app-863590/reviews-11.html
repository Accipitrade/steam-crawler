\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game really surprised me. Grabbed my attention more than many of the big budget games I've been playing lately.

In a nutshell its a 2d top-down space shooter with "twin-stick" like mechanics. There is a surprisingly engaging story, which starts out with your ship being transported through a spatial rift, along with a friendly space station which acts as your base of operations. from there you have to figure out where you are - investigating the galaxy and the forces behind the events at the start of the game.

Probably the key standout feature of the game is the shipbuilding aspect. its a hex-grid where you place different modules - guns, armor, engines, etc. Not the first time I've seen something like this done but its done well here and makes the player motivated to progress in the game to build a bigger more powerful ship. I could go on about the positive stuff but the bottom line is - if you like 2d space games you'll like this one, just buy it.

A few things I wish were improved, or maybe will be for the next game:

-Fleet mechanics. One of the problems these type of games always suffer from is that you start out zipping around in a small agile craft, dodging fire and attacking when you can. But because you end up with a big, powerful, but slow ship, you end up playing the game like a tank just sitting there absorbing fire. Its fine, but my point is - some kind of fleet mechanics where you could have other ships follow you and take commands maybe would have balanced this out some and given more variety to the combat gameplay. Then the player could be made less like a big health-pool tank, and the gameplay could retain some of the more agile maneuvering thats present at the start. This might be too difficult to implement, I guess.

-More worldbuilding / flavor text. I wish there was more dialogue and maybe even an index where you could read more information about the world. I can understand when this is restrained when there is a ton of voice acting involved, but with none of that there isn't really a limit to what can be put in the game. I kept thinking about this because the writing in the game is pretty good - I just wanted more of it.

-Color Palette Weirdness. There is something going on with the color palette or color range in this game. I'm not sure what the right words to use are. But in a room with a medium level of light, the game becomes very hard to see. The player ship just disappears into the black space background, and other elements in the game do the same thing except for the HUD. I compared using several different monitors with different panels (IPS / VA), and its just something about the art design. I hope this is resolved in the next game.

-friendly fire. This is nitpicky but its too easy to accidentally commit friendly fire. Especially with weapons like the missiles. At least code the friendly AIs to stay away from a firefight so I don't hit them.

This minor stuff aside I really enjoyed starcom nexus. Right after I finished it I looked up the next game and put it on my wishlist.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRemember when space games were about the wonder, the discovery, the oppressive vastness of the universe, instead of being spreadsheet simulators with severe Aspergers? You probably don't, because for some reason the only game that really did that was Star Control 2/Ur-Quan Masters, and that was made 30 years ago.

A lot of people in the reviews reference Ur-Quan Masters, and for good reason. This game almost managed to out-ur-quan the Ur-Quan. Almost. That's a huge achievement already, though. If, like me, you've been searching fruitlessly to scratch that itch, this game does it - and that's all I'll say about it. From now on, I'm going to assume you haven't played Ur-Quan.

So how is this different from any other 2D indie space game?
- it's about exploring a mostly untamed galaxy. You won't find a space station orbiting every star, you won't find life flourishing on every planet; many planets are just boring rocks. So when you find something, it's that much more meaningful.
- the galaxy is also unknown. In order to be able to even know where the stars are, you have to get within reasonable range to detect the star - that means that there's going to be a lot of stumbling upon new things when going into the unknown. Makes traversal that much more exciting.
- free movement. You don't just travel wormhole to wormhole - you can just go through deep space instead, it takes longer, but it's absolutely doable. And many star systems can be only reached this way.
- the research and the technobabble. In order to buy upgrades, you need Research Points - and those can only be found by, well, finding scientifically interesting things. And every RP "event" is unique and has it's own flavour text. And there's a LOT of it. Even after 20 hours you'll STILL be finding new things.
- on that topic, there are planetary anomalies/events. There are a few that are of the same 'type', most of them are completely unique encounters - and some are pretty typical, but some are bizarre or even morally challenging. The writing and creativity are god-tier.

In other words, this game is mostly about exploration and the story. And it's incredibly good.

Another thing worth mentioning on the side is that this game expects you to think - it's not a "go to this quest marker" kind of game, space coords are there to give you a sense of direction, but ultimately you have to think for yourself. I'm not going to lie, I got stuck a few times and had to look things up, but looking back on it now, all of those things were because of some kind of thinking error on my side. In other words, my fault.

The story is exciting and keeps you guessing until the very end; everything is properly foreshadowed and still manages to throw you off. The game gives you an option to write your own Captain's Log, which you can put as little map markers, and hoo boy, I think some 10 hours of my gameplay was writing logs. But maybe that's because I'm weird.

The game feels a little shallow towards the very end, and that's probably my only complaint. Luckily, the big story reveals make up for it. Overall, this is a huge recommendation from me. This game is a testament to the level of quality we can expect from a great solodev. Imagine if all the money that goes into making all the AAA garbage got redistributed to people like Kevin, the dev. Cheers, Kevin, for making this game - I've already wishlisted the sequel. As for everyone else - if you like space, sci-fi, technobabble or just a good exploration game, this is among the best ones out there. Enjoy!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you like exploration and space games this is a great choice. It focuses more on exploration and history than pure combat or interactions with other races.

While this game is not a simulator, the game history flows with more realism and more hard science than the usual. Instead of a small sector packed with planets and stars you have a big universe with A LOT of empty space and a great deal of stars and planets to explore. This does not make exploring boring because most of the time you are traveling through linked portals and when not you usually have an objective in the map.

You can upgrade your ship which is basic but there are no other rpg's mechanics which is good because ultimately you do not need to kill everything in your path and makes the game more dynamic through the end.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarcom: Nexus is obviously a love letter to classic PC sci-fi adventure games like Starflight and Star Control, and I'd say in that respect is succeeds in being evocative of those forerunners in how it crafts and presents its own cosmic tale and mysteries.

Overall, I enjoyed the game enough to finish it through to its conclusion (at least what there is of one), but it is rough around the edges with persistent and annoying bugs, especially in the mission log. If you're not nostalgic for these kinds of space adventure games, I'd say give this game a pass because it's probably going to be more frustrating than fun for you.

By the time I hit the 12 hour mark I had pretty much exhausted everything that I could really do on my own, and at that point started using walkthroughs to find things that I had missed (or try to get any kind of guidance on active missions that had provided little to no context for what you are supposed to do). It's frustrating that in some cases the game is good about highlighting locations that are important points of interest on the map, then in others they simply don't and you have to just wander around and try to figure out what the game intends for you to do. When I was younger (and pre-Internet) I think I would have had more patience for those kinds of things, but as I've gotten older my tolerance for wandering aimlessly looking for that one anomaly I might have missed like the proverbial needle in a haystack just isn't fun.

I found the world building in this game sufficient but not exceptional. Many of the alien factions felt like cardboard cutouts that were propped up to serve a single purpose but they just didn't really gel into a broader, deeper whole. I was left really wishing there was a more defined and consistent "world" in play, where it was obvious where certain aliens were operating, and an overlay on the galaxy map showing different spheres of influence so you could tell where anything was. Hunting down a ship from one faction that you have to talk to at the end game was extremely tedious, for example.

Exploration in the game is fairly basic, with you scanning planets/objects and then effectively scanning again to survey if there is an anomaly present. Then you are presented with either a single outcome/result, or offered a few different choices. Having read the developer retrospective, I can understand why they chose to simplify this instead of having the more involved planetary landings of Starflight, Star Control, Alien Legacy, or Mass Effect. It's simple and it works, but it does lose a bit of the sense of wonder having the galaxy be so "small" and condensed. But at the same time I can concede that forcing players to deploy a lander and drive around looking for minerals or ruins on huge planets is not something most modern players are going to want to do.

The combat in the game was fine, and a nice diversion in between exploration. I felt there was often too much combat, and it was way too easy to accidentally shoot "friendly" aliens, but it was fine.

If you want a fun little space adventure game that mixes arcade combat with exploration, Starcom Nexus will probably be able to scratch that itch. I'm interested to see what the developer does with their next title, because this developer does show a lot of promise and I'm hoping the sequel will be an evolutionary advancement over this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Star Trek: Voyager" meets "Choose Your Own Adventure" with puzzle solving.

The trailer is misleading because most of the early game involves exploring and scanning (mostly dead) planets and choosing outcomes, like an RPG ( rather than an ARPG ). There are a handful of other races, in a massive galaxy. Difficulty is scalable ( from Easy to Ultimate ). Combat is similar to old school Star Control games from the 1990's. This is not a space trading game like "Elite".

The early to mid game is grindy because you need to farm a lot of resources, in order to upgrade your ship. The distance between some of the stars is such that you could plot a course and leave to make a cup of tea! I'm surprised that more people don't quit the game at that point.

Command staff NPCs and alien NPCs say the same thing over and over, which gets tiring because it gives a "dead feel" or "not fleshed out" expression to the world. It's strange because the main quest encounters were professionally written and encompass various topics from advanced physics, biology and computing. I really liked the ending. I watch and read a lot of sci-fi and I thought that the story was original.

The last 10 hours were arduous, because the quest tracking system became atrocious with a, "Now what do I do?" endgame. Command staff hints, in the form of dialogue, would have been a welcome addition. The game started wearing thin and I had to rely on online searches. The unity engine freezes temporarily at the endgame. Unity engine can't handle high FPS gaming.

It took me 51 hours to complete because I was playing on Ultimate difficulty. I recommend this at half-price.
