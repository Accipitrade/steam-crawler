\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhew, that was quite a ride!

The game feels a bit like Space Rangers, with that general space opera theme and text quests.
It absorbed me and didn't want to let me go by slowly feeding new content, story, and discoveries.

The game is good. It is well written, so curiosity always drives you to a new system to see what kind of anomaly you find next. There is quite a lot of content, so if you decide to ignore question markers and just go exploring the galaxy on your own you will not be disappointed.

Only issues I could find with the game are very basic trading system and balancing issues. Battles became way too easy after I've invested research I've got from optional quests into Plasma weapons.

To conclude: I've spent 19 hours playing this game and I enjoyed every minute of it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn short, I would sincerely call Starcom: Nexus a legitimate spiritual successor to Star Control. Like Star Control, Starcom is part of a nameless genre of action-RPG 2d spaceflight games, where you're alone against a strange and hostile universe with an overarching mystery to unravel one planet out of hundreds at a time. The combat is relatively simple, but solid and with a few surprises here and there. The shipbuilding is excellent, in spite of some seemingly arbitrary constraints; I found myself spending hours trying to squeeze the parts I needed into the shape I wanted. No mechanical aspect of Starcom is perfect, but neither is there any one part I could call bad, and the true sense of exploration and good writing marks Starcom as worth far more than the sum of its parts.

While a good number of quests boil down to fetch quests, you won't realize until well after beating the game due to the puzzles and navigation required. YES, PUZZLES. Some things get marked on your galaxy map immediately. Other things? You find an anomaly that shows a star cluster. You, the player, have to actually use your brain and mentally map the anomaly onto your star chart and follow a riddle unless you want to spend a good chunk of time brute forcing the solution. At another point, you'll find a couple huge stargates in orbit around dead planets. They're misaligned, and not only do you have to turn them on but figure out where to point them so you'll get launched close enough to their target to paint it on your radar. Even areas without proper quests are peppered with short, interesting text segments (many of which have one or more options you can take) that slowly unravel the story of where you are, and why.

The developer is a mostly one man team, with some freelance asset work IIRC. I'm not mentioning this in the hopes that you'll be kinder in your opinions, rather that I was shocked that one man put together such a well-realized product and in only ~1 year in Early Access. He listens to his players, and is already laying the groundwork for a sequel/spinoff/whatever.

In conclusion, I can't rightfully say that Starcom is my favorite space combat game, but I can definitely say it's my favorite space adventure in recent memory. If you absolutely have to have complex combat encounters with reams of variables to track, look elsewhere. If you want to explore and occasionally have to actually think, enjoy the also nameless genre of text adventures crossed over with real time exploration, and/or want to play a game that feels like what they should've been doing with the Star Trek licence, buy this and don't look back.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a fun exploration game with a mix of arcade shooter in it. I loved finding all the interesting bits and side quests in it,
and the story kept me intrigued till the end.
The ship builder was pretty fun too, though it did turn out to be a bit of a hassle at times to track down the specific resource I needed for parts early on.
Fighting hard enemy swarms was pretty rewarding though.

As for the negatives:
Starcom doesn't hold hands much at all. It's usually a good thing in games, but somethings seemed to have to be done a little arbitrarily in certain orders to advance other side quests.
I also, admittedly was playing under the impression that I needed to follow the wormholes to play for a while, which had me thrown through a loop when I realized... you can indeed just, truck your way through space.

Either way, I reccomend it if you like exploration, story, and arcade-like topdown shooters. Well worth the price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe more I played, and the more I considered that what I was experiencing was the product of a single person, the more impressed I became. While there are some pacing issues, and the game is a little shallow in the one area that most likely attracts the most players - the ship-building - in the overall big picture Starcom is very well put together. The combat and ship design is simple but fun, the stories are well crafted and engaging (and at times surprising), and the game itself is visually simple but done in such a way that it's utilitarian nature adds rather than detracts to the game as a whole.

I'd recommend Starcom to anyone interested in a quick but fun game that one guy dreamed up and made real - a real accomplishment in this day and age.\t\t\t\t\t

Gameplay:
nice and intuitive interface, there's an option for console remoter or keyboard and mouse (fur us PC elitists (: ).
There's a tech tree, you can get to all of it if you grind, but even at it is you unlock most of it.

Graphics:
if you're here for the graphics, you're in the wrong genre. That said, there are some magnificent stars (Like a pulsar) and planets, and things explode in a vary satisfying way.

Audio:
everything important is conveyed with writing. I didn't notice the soundtrack, so it can't be bad.
