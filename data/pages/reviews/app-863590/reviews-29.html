\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReally enjoyed the game. Main plot is interesting, and left me with (good) questions, as well there are a number of side quests and events that happen along your journey. Lots of sci-fi themes and ideas are explored. All the aliens you meet are fairly unique, but I wish you could learn more about them.

Gameplay wise it reminded me of StarControl 2. You're given a ship that starts off weak, but it's modular - so as you gather materials, and unlock the tech tree your ship can improve in the ways you want (only 2 real things to balance in your design: travel or combat). Eventually you do reach a pinnacle in technology where the choice matters less than what you want your ship to look like. That is where the game is different; you build your ship with parts or modules. The only negative was that some alien ships looked like they were done in a similar way, but the build looked less blocky like mine and more designed (smooth edges).

Exploration is one of the focal points of the game, travelling between planets and systems. The starmap has a tagging option so you can mark points of interest.

There is some trade, and diplomacy in the game. Never got the hang of the trade system, but it helped in more than a few pinches for certain materials.

One thing I wish was in the game was a better ship transponder system - it was so hard to identify most ships until they were almost or in visual range, and this led to a few unnecessary encounters. The IFF is basic: blue = good, red = bad.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game play is essentially being a science ship in Stellaris. You travel around wormholes, you scan planets, you click some option following the text you read when you get an anomaly, and then you do it again. Do that enough time and you then fly home to upgrade the ship with new techs/modules. Rinse and Repeat.

In this game play loop, there are only 3 basic goals: upgrade your ship, learn more about the story, and solve puzzles. Upgrading the ship is addictive but pretty straight forward; only a few modules care about their location on the ship you design. The story is fairly generic sci-fi with all the usual tropes about AI, the origins of myths, a progenitor race, and information getting slowly revealed to create a big "twist"; I was mildly curious, but never invested or excited. The puzzles are good but range from the trivial (use object "X" at location "Y", highlighted on the map), to obnoxiously hard work (get Mathematica out and plot 4 circles of varying radius to see where they intersect). Some of the puzzles are fun and intersect with the game lore well, but these are disappointingly rare.

There are other aspects of the game like combat, diplomacy, trade, a morale system, etc... But these are either solved by clicking directly on what you want, or are minor enough to be completely ignored. The game is also a little unstable. UI bugs are rather common, and at one point my home base disappeared and I had to edit the save to make the game playable.


tl;dr The game isn't bad and it did keep my interest, but neither the story nor the game play are involved enough to make it fun. Sadly in a week I'm sure I'll have forgotten all about it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI might be dating myself when I say that the very first PC game I bought was StarCon2. It wasn't the first PC game I played, but I had the pleasure of sitting down to this game during a seasonal shut down.

I don't think I saw sunlight for four days. Now, like then, I have put off writing a review for this title because I just wanted nothing more than to play it.

Space exploration games are rare, and doing them right is even rarer.

Starcom Nexus brought me back to the feeling I had with those early days of PC gaming. As usual, you start off with a tiny ship and go out into a galactic playground and have run ins with other space faring races. Careful! You might find getting off on the wrong foot with a race is a one way trip. Fortunately, your 'practice' adversary is just right.... you can handle them as you learn to control your modest ship, and you can quickly upgrade your little ship into an apocalyptic nightmare for those early foes. For me, at just the right juncture, I met up with someone who was a little tougher, and I had to be very careful around them. Once I figured out where to farm and how to use my new toys, I was able to take care of myself as I explored.

I've read some complaints that some players found they were stalled in the game. It happens in this genre. My experience worked out quite well, that I was not able to advance for a little while, so I farmed a bit until I was getting better at beating the alien XXXX out of the aliens, then I was given more clues to proceed.

The game is gorgeous, and the writing is quite good, sometimes motivating me to slow down and read the event dialogue boxes more carefully than I generally would. Why? The writers know to keep it to the point while presenting some ideas I have never seen before in the genre. No TL;DR here for me.

I don't know why we don't have more games of this genre. Now, in particular, I feel like we are out of good ideas for games. Maybe we could coax more developers to develop games of this sort while we wait for the next genre to develop.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA really fun space exploration game with good writing and tons of secrets to ferret out. Totally worth the full price and a steal on sale.


Edit: Having played, and loved, the game a second time, before finding other's frustrations with it here, I will add some thoughts.

There are so many research points to gain from many different sources, that it blows my mind hearing people say they had to cheat to gain more. Maybe they just kept dumping all their points into weapons, skipping the very QoL techs they then complained about not having. Maybe they never chased the comets or probes, shot at some space rocks, or just explored anything but the planets on their map. I really have no idea, but getting stuck without tech points is not really possible.

This game is SLOW, to give the player the feeling that space is VAST. It's a design choice that serves this game very well, but does not cater to instant gratification.

Does this invalidate player's criticism? No. They didn't enjoy the game, and that is fair, but it did make them leave uninformed reviews imo.\t\t\t\t\t
