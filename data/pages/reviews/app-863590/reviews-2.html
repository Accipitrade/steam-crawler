\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd like to take a moment to praise a game that brought me a lot of joy, and I sincerely hope you'll give it a try, as well. An indie studio turning in such a splendid offering is something special.

First, subjectively best: The writing.

The writing is simply excellent. Elements of Star Control, Star Trek, and all manner of sci-fi get some lovely respect here. Characters and especially culture are standouts, a delightful spread of communication, technology, design... the Phage were legitimately creepy. The art was excellent, but the dialogue was chilling and appropriate. The plot was creative, and while I'd love to engage in a discussion of quantum elasticity with the studio, that's not a critique, but praise. As a game designer at CGL, every time I see writing get this kind of love it just fills my heart.

I want to emphasize here the joy in every species I encountered. Really, really fun, and gorgeously depicted in art, dialogue, behavior, and tech.

Technology is similarly given a lot of attention, and the detail and creativity of anomalies and circumstances are wonderful. Even with a few paragraphs of description for survey teams I got invested in the moral implications of actions more than pure profit, and was rewarded in some unexpected cases with delightful finds (and often research or artifacts).

Gameplay-wise, there's a kind of hybrid of the aforementioned Star Control as well as Star Valor. Top-down UI, modular ship building that expands with research and exploration unlocks makes for easy and somewhat addictive gameplay, as rewarding on a tactical as moment-to-moment reflex level without being so fast as to be frustrating. I recall the design philosophy behind Lo-Fi Studios' Kenshi in that they never wated a player to feel like a problem or death couldn't be avoided. I never felt trapped into a situation, and could find ways to subvert, avoid, or overcome any obstacle.

Critiques: As nothing loved can go without some measure of good-faith examination, the game's map and quest board don't quite keep notes as well as I could like. In a game so dense, I can forgive it, but you may wish you kept notes or profited from a better memory than mine. Alternatively, the Steam and reddit Communities have lots of spoiler-friendly guides to questions I was able to find within a minute or less with a Google search. In that sense, the community has filled in what few gaps I could find.

The ending was EXCEPTIONALLY satisfying for me, though I'm positive results may vary subjectively. That said, I would adore the opportunity to continue exploring with this crew and story, and, should an ending you reach permit such a thing, an expansion or further sequel would be more than welcome with more of the same expanded upon.

With a sequel already out, I've put in the purchase within minutes of finishing, and as soon as this review is complete, the installation will be finished and I will begin Starcom: Unknown Space.

I want to finish by extending my absolute congratulations to the studio, as well as my envy at having made something so absolutely FUN, engrossing, and enriching. \r\n\t\t\t\t\t This game is... awfully meh. I was only barely able to force myself to complete the game and I see no replay value to be had. It starts out ok, but toward the end you're just left wonder what you're supposed to do next and you have to go online for help figuring out what to do. Gotta find 3 ships to turn on? Well good luck with that unless you go read a post telling you exactly where they are cause there's no waypoints or markers or hints telling you where they are. Sure you might have passed them by 10 or 20 hours ago in your playtime, but yeah who remembers a random ship you can't do anything to?

Ultimately at full price (22$ ish) this game isn't worth it. It might be worth it to you on sale for 5$ or less if you're really really bored.\t\t\t\t\t
