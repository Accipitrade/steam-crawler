\r\n\t\t\t\t\tShort Version:
Outstanding game, worth every penny if you like isometric space exploration. My advice to anyone wanting to give this a shot – buy it and play for an hour. Most of the core mechanics of the game stay the same (my play through took almost 30 hours) and everything gets bigger and better. If you like it keep going, if not refund. The only thing you will not have a chance to fully delve into is the story, which is one of the strongest points of the game but more on that later.

Pros:
1. Story and overall writing is superb. A true science fantasy mystery to keep you engaged.
2. Visuals are nice, it’s a very smooth looking game with a nice and somewhat unique art style.
3. The engine (Unity?) runs the game very well. Even with dozens of ship, drones, plasma projectiles and missiles on the screen the performance is smooth.
4. Decent length and very little repetition of game events. A lot of love went into crafting this.
5. Music and sound effects are solid.
6. Quest chains are well laid out and provide for a lot of freedom to just do your own-thing. You are never punished for exploring a system too early, assuming you can outrun or outfight the natives.
7. Massive world to explore. Map feels endless with a lot of the space being empty which suits the setting just fine.

Cons:
1. The combat is simplistic and gets repetitive. While there are several very different weapon systems to use, after the umpteenth time you have to clear a system of stubborn low tech bug alien ships, this becomes a chore. The same is true for every enemy in the game, fun at first, gets repetitive later.
2. Traveling is a big, BIG, portion of the game. Either in system flight, exploring a distant star several sectors away or jumping though one of the two types of the fast travel options, you will spend easily half your playtime commuting.


Longer Version:
In Starcom you play the role of a “Star Trek-esq” starship captain that was propelled to - lets just say – somewhere else. Luckily for the hero, his ship and crew are not alone, a friendly space station was also teleported. Your initial goal is to explore this strange galaxy and find a way to return home.

At its’ core this is a space exploration science fantasy game with simple and elegant mechanics. Most of your time will be spent exploring star systems and scanning planets for anomalies. Said anomalies playout as a small choose-your-own-adventure type minigame with an often a very pretty image for that added visual. These tell small stories, sometimes connected to the mail plot, often tangentially related and at times just stand alone events. Exceptionally well written, as is just about everything in this game. As short as the stories are, they do an excellent job at world building. At the conclusion of each anomaly exploration you are usually rewarded for your efforts with research points, resources, or negative (almost always avoidable) consequences. With few exceptions, once a planet or other object, has been duly surveyed, it is marked as such on the world map and there is no further interaction possible with it.

In most systems you will encounter alien space-craft. several of the species will start off friendly or neutral and might stay that way depending on how you treat them. The others exist to provide the combat elements. There are 4 main enemy types in the game: fighter craft, frigate size and capital ships and finally tough-as-nails space stations. Each of these will belong to one of 10 races populating the galaxy with varying weapons and defense systems. For example, during a certain quest chain your ship will be forced to fight Avian-creature ships with plasma weapons (likely the same basic weapon type as what you would be using at that stage) but with access to a short term warp / blink drive, resulting in their ships jumping all around you firing from every angle. Chaotic and a lot of fun to fight. Another example is a robotic enemy – when first encountered in my game, they made me turn tail, run back to the homebase station and invest heavily into weapons research. Finally, there is a brilliant side effect to something that happens near the endgame, a certain hostile bug race gets a real nice powerup and you only find out about it by flying through one of their systems and discovering that you can no longer just wipe the floor with them without any effort. It took me a moment to realize why that happened and put a big smile on my face as it made perfect narrative sense. While it can be a little tiresome, combat brings its own rewards as enemy ships drop valuable resources as their modules get destroyed.

Speaking of the home base “Kite” station – you will return to it multiple times throughout the game to unlock new technologies, get quests, upgrade your ship and replenish lost crew. There are 2 officers onboard that will help you in your mission and a science jerkoff that really should open his ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ shipment of ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ archeological ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ urns. Ship upgrades are divided into two parts: research and construction. Research uses the research points you discover while exploring, and makes guns, engines, tractor beams, etc just plain better in some straight forward way. Construction is a missed opportunity. In essence it allows to build your ship the way you want – more engines will make it fly faster, more guns will linearly increase your dakka output, more reactors will make the more engines and the more guns not run out of power after the first salvo with the auto speed boost still turned on. The problem is that the construction is very restrictive both in terms of system placement and overall ship size. Having researched the largest ship hull possible, I unintentionally ended up with some odd cross between a hammerhead shark and a Terran battle-cruiser. Neat. Neat but slow, so let’s add more engines, except I can’t because the build screen ran out of space. Again, what’s there is fun but seems under developed.

Travel between star systems is accomplished via either preset “jump gates” connecting 2 static systems, by activating wormholes that can link to any other previously discovered wormhole, or just plain old flying. The map is designed in an intelligent way and provides an easy wormhole at the end of many jump gate sequence strings neatly eliminating the need for a long return journey.

While there are several ways to travel, the distances themselves are very long. A lot of the game is watching the ship fly in a straight line while making the odd tiny course correction. This is the weakest part of the game, further into the playthrough it becomes an imposition. All this exploring is necessary to experience the many interesting events found in surveys and to progress quest lines. The majority of which are well structured but some feel a little rushed. Two examples come to mind: there is a certain red “virus” that’s built up to be a major threat but gets resolved almost effortlessly. A race that was narratively built up to be integral to the story did not feel important at all once actually discovered in an annoying south eastern corner of an even more annoying nebula. Research shields before going and keep in mind that shield generators stack.

The above is the core mechanic loop of the game, explore star systems, survey planets, fight, get resources and research points from all three, return home, rebuild and upgrade. The core mechanic but not the core attraction, that honor belongs to the writing. While it has been mentioned in passing several times above, the writing in the game is wonderful. From humorous, to sad, to serious it delivers the very atmosphere that makes this game memorable. It reads like a love letter to classic science fiction and tells an original story with really interesting twists. In conclusion this might just be one of the best hidden gems on steam. I really hope that the dev polishes off some of the elements for the sequel.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarcom Nexus is the answer to the question - what would happen if an 80's arcade space shooter was given a plot? In this case, it is a good answer - the monotony of a space shooter is broken up by a detailed, grand story line that was quite entertaining, even if it did require redoubling of the willing suspension of disbelief. The touted exploration element of the game is really more of a hunt for plot points as the story is slowly assembled. But the ability to construct your own ship down to the modular level and upgrade its technology was a surprisingly fun feature that fit well in the story (and was required to complete certain missions). Overall, there is a little something for everybody here, and this game deserves a try, especially when on sale.

That being said, I have a reservation that is not limited to this game. Here it is - are games ever going to evolve past the concept of the player's avatar being a device upon which the universe created by the developer suicides itself? As I kited enemies and farmed their ship corpses, I wondered if their strategy was to think I will just eventually feel guilty about killing their entire species and let them land a hit.

I have never enjoyed the endless slaughter of respawning pixels, whether they be monsters, robots, ships, npcs, or some combination thereof. I will eventually become so bored it will cause physical pain. Starcom Nexus crossed into this territory a couple of times when trying to shoo away hordes of respawning alien ships so I could finish surveying the last planet of one system or another, only to find out the planet had nothing to offer. The idea of being given the ability to befriend aliens will not give you an out from endless combat, as some are hardwired enemies. And even the friendly ones have a habit of lazily drifting between you and your enemy during a space battle and then turning on whichever ship accidentally hits them first in the crossfire.

The day that some progress will be made is when developers give their spawn a desire to live. It's OK for AI enemies to run away or avoid conflict, and it is OK for players to let them.\t\t\t\t\t
