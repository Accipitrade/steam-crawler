\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood game. This game is unique in that I fully recommend it, and will note down the only issues I've had with it below. If none of those hit hard enough, there's a high chance you'll have an enjoyable experience.

Weapon Controls - Your starting lasers will remain your primary fire weapon, even when new weapons seem like they should replace that. Meaning you will be sharing a secondary fire key, pressing a button to swap what weapon you're using at the time, for everything that isn't your basic weapon. It makes using more than two weapons at a time feel relatively bad, but not unbearably so.

Poor Late Game - Early game is okay, but Mid game is where this shines, as you traverse the galaxy and meet new aliens. Solving the overall 'mystery' late game feels weak compared to all the side content, to the point where I eventually looked up guides due to the slog that resulted from all the backtracking. (Said backtracking be literal backtracking; warping from point C to A. No new enemies in the way; just traversing space you've already seen. A lot.) Combined with multiple moments of a quest marking a new spot on your map, only for you to arrive there and have nothing to do due to missing something elsewhere... really brings down the late game experience.

Captains Log - Fine in concept, bad when combined with the above point. You will need to consult this thing to solve some late game objectives, and they can be very... obtuse. Every single log entry that provides an X/Y coordinate without an option to auto mark it on your map feels bad, and it happens often.\t\t\t\t\t

Starcom Nexus is probably my favorite among the genre of 2D top-down space exploration games. It features a sizeable universe to explore, lots of secrets to uncover, and light RPG elements that add a feeling of progression to the campaign.

The gameplay is highly focused on exploration. You can travel through nexus points which are super-wormholes that allow you to instantly travel to any other Nexus point whose coordinates you are aware of, wormholes that connect two systems to each other, or just fly through interstellar space... which takes a long time unless you've bought all the speed upgrades for your ship. This travel system is the game's greatest strengths, as it allowed the developer to hide some star systems in hard to find places, far away from wormhole-starlanes.

Unlike some other games within the genre, Starcom Nexus does not have a procedurally generated universe. Every solar system was placed by hand, and most of the things you can discover on the planets are hand-made too (with the only exception being generic random resource caches). That means there's a lot of unique content to discover, and sometimes very unique ways to find that content. In one case, you have to nudge an accelerator into the right direction so it can shoot you towards a distant star system... if you fail to nudge it into the right correction, it will just shoot you into empty space. There are a lot of little secrets like that, and exploring the universe is pure joy thanks to it.

Combat is pretty fun, too, but not the game's biggest focus. You can install different weapons on your ship - initially there's only plasma guns, but you can discover alien technology to unlock missiles, lasers and an ultra-beam. Enemy ships can have individual modules shot off (always aim for their strongest weapon in order to disable it!), but your own ship's modules merely get damaged and can be repaired later. It's a fun system and adds some strategy to the space battles.

The game's RPG elements consist of "Research Points" which you earn for making discoveries on planetary expeditions, examining stellar phenomena, and dealing with alien races. Those points are used to unlock new technologies for your ship. The tech tree is designed similarly to those of space 4X games like Distant Worlds, but it feels very much like an RPG's skill tree and "Research Points" are this game's equivalent of experience. You can also collect resources from the wrecks of enemy ships and use them to construct new modules for your ship, like weapons, engines, energy generators etc.

The story is pretty decent, and you piece it together bit by bit from discoveries you make during your exploration trips. I loved the way it was slowly unveiled as I made more and more discoveries, so the delivery is perfect. But I didn't like the ending at all... it felt kinda stupid, especially since you only get two choices but can't join the "bad guys" even though it would make sense for you to do so.

Overall, it is a really enjoyable game, especially if you love exploring and figuring out secrets on your own, with little handholding. Only the story's conclusion is a bit of a letdown.\t\t\t\t\t

My major criticism is that I wish there was more!

...Though to be serious I do wish there was a proper NG+ option, that certain puzzles had Slightly less obtuse solutions, and that relationships with aliens were a bit more nuanced rather than one or two quests associated with them to get on their good side.

Still though, great game for people who wanna dip their toes into 2d space exploration games but don't want an overly complex experience (minus a few particularly headscratching brain teasers)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA game heavily inspired by Starflight. You're tossed into a wide open universe and set upon the course of discovering a way back home.

The great pleasure in this game (and Starflight) is in exploring outward into the unknown, finding all sorts of interesting things on various planets, and gradually unraveling a well done story. I've had an itch for something like Starflight for about 30 years, and while I'd like to say this game has scratched that itch - instead it's just left me hungry for more. This is a great and desperately under-served genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played the entire Star Control series back in the day, especially SC2. I loved those games, and keep going back to them even today. When I first saw Starcom: Nexus, I was VERY interested, hoping that we would finally get another game that would bring back the excellent gameplay and adventure of Star Control.

After putting more than 27 hours into this game, I have finally completed it, and I'm ready to give my opinions.

This game is fantastic. No question about it!

The Good:
-Ship construction! StarCom: Nexus is far superior to Star Control in this area. You have a ton of freedom to trick out your ship any way you like, in order to fit your play style. Want a fast missile boat to keep your distance from enemies while pelting them with a hail of guided missiles? You've got it. Or do you prefer heavy armor and tons of short-range plasma cannons to shred your enemies at close range? How about turning your ship into a speeding bullet through space, able to explore anywhere you want with impunity? My final ship was a massive dreadnought bristling with missiles, point defense cannons, energy reactors, and as many engines as I could build.

-Exploration: In many ways, Starcom: Nexus also beats Star Control with satisfying exploration. In nearly every star system you go to, you will find a number of planets that contain surface anomalies, artifacts floating in space, massive derelict space ships, or strange, unexplained phenomenon. There are even things you can find out in deep space: extra solar planets, abandoned technology, debris fields from ancient battles, and more.

-Combat: Executed very well in Starcom: Nexus. You will find yourself getting into tangles with multiple ships at once, some times facing down an entire fleet of enemies by yourself. Battle tactics become very important as you keep an eye on your energy levels, hull strength, and (once you unlock the tech) shield strength. You must carefully decide when to engage and when to back off from your enemies to repair and recharge. There were times I found myself in the middle of several enemies with completely depleted energy reserves, no power to fire my weapons, limping away as fast as my barely-powered engines could go, while my foes shredded their way through my hull plating. Very exciting.

-Secrets and Quests: While there are a number of 'fetch'-type quests in this game, I found that most missions required some degree of intuition and deduction in order to complete them. There were several missions that truly delighted me with the amount of cleverness required to solve their mysteries.

The So-So:
-While there is a variety of different alien races you will encounter in this game, I did not find them to be all that memorable or compelling. Star Control 2 certainly did an amazing job in this area, with each alien race having a distinct music track, personality, and motivations.

-Scientific Research: This was very exciting in the early game--with the right amount of research points, it was possible to unlock scientific breakthroughs that fundamentally changed how I played the game. However, from mid- to late-game, research became much more of "make this aspect of my ship a bit stronger". There were still some research paths that didn't unlock until later in the game, which kept my interest, but I found that I was ultimately a bit disappointed by the research tree.

-Game Ending: At the final climax of the game, I was left saying, "Is that it? Oh... okay."

Bottom Line:
-Do I recommend this game? YES, Absolutely yes! It was a ton of fun to play, especially for an old Star Control veteran like myself. Is this game on-par with Star Control? Sadly, no. While it excels in some areas, other areas are pretty lacking. While I have gone back to play through Star Control 2 at least 3 times, I don't feel much of a desire to go back and replay Starcom: Nexus.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very enjoyable open-world, story-based exploration and action game which kept me playing to the end. It does require a moderate amount of reading of story pop-ups when exploring, etc so it might not be for everyone, but each bit of text is usually quite short.

+ Customizing and upgrading your ship through the tech tree is fun.
+ Plenty to explore (viewing the map while navigating can help a lot - space is big!)
+ Very nice graphics (especially the planets, nebulae, and lighting effects).
+ Atmospheric music.

There can be quite a bit to keep track of with the story, but the log, history search, and map notes (right click to add one) are very helpful, so no need to worry about missing some key info; the map usually has enough indicators to point you in the right direction too.\t\t\t\t\t
