\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarcom: Nexus is a spiritual successor™ to Star Control 2. Though the mechanics and progression are significantly different, the premise is similar; a bad thing happens, and you fly around in your ship, exploring the galaxy, pissing off various aliens, checking out various planets in each system for resources, artifacts to research, or clues to progress the story and solve the Mystery of the Thing and Why It Happened.

Combat is different in that there's no separate combat mode; enemy ships appear, randomly in certain systems, and usually attack in groups as opposed to 1v1 combat. You also don't have a fleet, but your ship is quite powerful, especially on the default normal difficulty; I played on hard and had a few challenging fights but most were fairly easy, though the difficulty can be set much higher (or lower).

Killing enemies yields various resources (which can also be obtained from some planets, as well as respawning asteroid fields and large wreckages) which can be used to build modules to customize your ship. You can recycle those modules at no loss to test out various builds, so the only limiting factors are your current total resources owned and invested into your ship, and your research.

Research is the main limiting factor, as there are a finite number of places to get it. You can it from exploring planets, destroying enemies (generally up to 3 times per class of enemy that can drop them, and only when there is a specific technology that can be unlocked; for instance, destroying sentinels will unlock laser technology, so you can get three chunks of research points from them to unlock lasers, then no more). There is a lot of it to be found--catching alien probes, flying near comets or certain unique anomalies, progressing the story, handling certain encounters in certain ways will all yield more of it. I'm not sure if it is possible to get enough to completely fill out the research tree or not, but I was able to get everything plus maxing out two of the four secondary weapon trees, and there were a significant number of potential resource gains that I missed.

There is a potential downside with research in that you absolutely need it to progress at certain points by researching story progression projects, and so it's theoretically possible to handicap yourself by not having enough points left to do that. It's unlikely for this to happen before the very end, though, as unless you've thoroughly explored and done everything, there should always be several sources left.

My only gripes are

1. Moving between sectors, especially early on, where you don't already have nexus or warp paths to them unlocked takes a very long time. I was inclined to use cheat engine + speedhack just to speed up travel time. Though I do this in a lot of single player games as well, and there is technology later on to speed up deep space travel and make large warp hops without the use of gates.

2. The "good" ending felt like it could have used an extra bit of story/explanation before going to the credits.

Other than that, it was a lot of fun and should give a good 35-50 hours of playtime. It may have slight replayability if you want to focus on a different secondary weapon tech tree or handle certain aliens or scenarios differently, though the locations of the systems and planets are fixed (and need to be, given the number of quests/puzzles that rely on triangulation, directions, and locations uncovered.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOK just finished it at this moment and had to write a review about it.

First of all this is a one-man-game; Kevin the dDev did a fantastic job, is pretty active all around the web answering questions and ironing out bugs. I played it just now, as I told ya, so most of the problems I saw on the web when looking up something were ironed out already so dont believe what you read, this is a 0 bug game at the moment.

Second, the FEEL. If you are looking to scratch that itch left from Freelancer, played Rebel Galaxy and loved it ypu will love this as well. Never thought that a top-down game would fill the void of Freelancer but yeah, it did. And it did it nicely.

Building your ship up is fun, trying to balance design vs utility was a challenge for me. I have to have a beutiful ship, call me vain. It took me more than half the game to settle on a design route and after that I just went crazy. I probably spent hours trying to iron out tthe design of my ship. It's addicting even if its block by block its super fun.

OK more gameplay, the galaxy is wide, you fly, byyourself or via warp gates to different systems, scan planets, şf you find an anamoly you investigate it which is done via text based decisions, which was great for me (I'm an old dude sho grew up with D&D and text based PC games).

It is repetitive but the game is not that long, around 40 hours I guess (my game was open on pause more then I wanted, ugh stupid real life getting in the way), so it doesnt bore you.

The Combat is fun, using plasma weapons to point and shoot as your main and a selection of or combo of secondaries like missles, lasers, drones and a fricking DEATH RAY!!!! Yeah the shootings good movement is a bit floaty but you get used to it quickly. In short combat is serious fun.

Exploration as I briefly said is system hoping, qoing after mini quests, solving puzzles, finding things which u cant understand at first then when connected with something else makes you go AHHHH so that was it. Enjoyable.

OK The dreaded subject, the CONS. Yes there are cons but none game breaking. The map might be a bit more user friendly like when u zoom it sooms to where the pointer is, the secondary weapons selctor could be bound to 1-2-3-4 and always show which is slected etc etc. Quality of life improvements thats all.

Replayability. Hmm thats a hard question. Not immediately like some other games but I probably will play it atleast once more, have 5 missing achievements for them mostly. But once more would suffice I guess.

Without draging this any longer my heartfelt many thanx to Wx3 Labs aka Kevin. What ever he publishes I will be an early supporter from this point forward.

You the rest of tthe people who are reading this. The game is cheap for what it offers, dont stand there just go buy and enjoy it already\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn some dark corner of the internet I once found a simple flash game. It was a poor copy of one of my favorite indie games. Starcom: Nexus feels a lot like that. It's a collection of features that's been done better by other games with a thin layer of generic science fiction to tie it all together.

Starcom's setup is simple: Your ship and a space station are transported to some distant region, and you have to scavenge, fight enemies, and explore to solve the situation. The station lets you research new tech and build better ships with a simple hex-based system. This isn't the worst setup for a game, but there's just not that much meat on its bones.

It's pretty simple. You explore, fight, and build your ship. Exploration and combat is a shadow of what a game like Starsector does. The events you find on planets are boring versions of Strange Adventures in Infinite Space and FTL. And the ship building pales in comparison to something like Cosmoteer (which is FREE) or Reassembly. And if you absolutely crave space exploration with a story to push you forward, there's always Escape Velocity: Nova, or its love letter Endless Space (which is FREE). Even its premise was lifted from an older space shooter. If Starcom was even half as good as these games, but with their features combined, I'd consider it worthwhile. But it's not. Combat is boring pew-pew nonsense with a grand total of four weapon types. The aliens barely have an identity of their own. Exploration boils down to gathering either Science for research, or resources. You'll use those resources for ship building or, much less exciting, to unlock progress. These hidden costs are extremely annoying, and they don't add anything to the gameplay other than being hard roadblocks. God forbid you actually give me a mission or something to get the same thing done. The shipbuilding is a massive missed opportunity. You don't lose resources for trying new designs, so it's perfect for making new builds for various tasks, but the simplicity of the system blocks this. When I tried building a ship for a combat mission the game even informed me that the survey module is mandatory. But I still have to pay for it.

In terms of visuals, it's as boring as its mechanics. The ships all have the same blocky appearance because they're all built out of the same blocks. They're drab and dark, easily melding into the background of space. This counts for your own ship as well, and it can be hard to see what's going on. Visual design of aliens and allies is dull, as is their writing. You start with four named allies, and I couldn't tell you a single interesting fact about any of them. I ran into a friendly lizard alien captain, but apparently he's the captain of every lizard ship I encounter.

At present I'm completely uninvested in this game, and I regret my purchase. I have gone over the refund time, so now I'm stuck with it. Money down the drain, as far as I'm concerned, but I'll probably try and see if I can still find some worth in it. I will update this review if I somehow end up changing my opinion. But at this point, if I could still refund it, I would. Even at sale it feels like a ripoff.

Here's a list in roughly the same price range that you would be better off playing instead:
-Starsector ($15)
-Weird Worlds: Return to Infinite Space (€3,99)
-Infinite Space III (€8,19)
-Escape Velocity: Nova (Abandonware)
-Endless Space (Free)
-Cosmoteer (Free, but being updated for paid release)
-FTL (€9,99)
-Reassembly (€12,49)

Yes, these are all cheaper. Three of them are free. FREE.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis review is directed at the developer, moreso than the game. For players, it is a fairly solid experience for the first 2/3s of the game, or roughly 25 hours, and so I highly recommend you play it for that part. However, I cannot in good conscience recommend the game, as the developer has made some very bad choices and has no intention of rectifying them. I've felt this way about the game and their choices since launch in Dec 2019, but I felt I'd wait a year, see if they would address the problems on their own and try out the game then. I've done so, and all of the issues, I had at launch are still present in the last 1/3 of the game, and therefore I see no alternative but to write up this lengthy, negative review.

But if you are a new player, go play the game... you'll enjoy it up to the part that I'm going to be discussing in detail in this review. The graphics, sounds, most of the gameplay and UI is 10/10, so you'd be doing yourself a disservice by not experiencing the first 2/3s of the game. The last 1/3... well, read on...

ALSO: I SHOULDN'T HAVE TO SAY THIS BUT, SPOILERS AHEAD.

=====

So how to begin...

The ending of the game felt rushed out the door, and leaves the same kind of "bad taste" in your mouth, like Mass Effect 3's ending (before EA released the major patch). But it is unlikely this one will ever get an update to fix it; I waited a year and nothing happened, and all my issues with the last 1/3 that I had in Nov 2019 (a month before release) are still in the game.

All of your choices in the game are irrelevant, to the ending story of this game. Whenever you make enemies, there isn't ever consequences for that; just wait a few days and they are buddy-buddy. Some remain enemies forever; some never become your allies even when you help them; some are blood enemies until completing a quest and now love you long time, no joke. There isn't even moral consequences for making severely bad decisions or violating Starcom directives resulting in court martial offenses, from your superiors. Its like Star Trek's Prime Directive, but no one is punished when they repeatedly violate it. And, uh, I repeatedly violated Starcom directives and never suffered any consequences... in fact I'd say I was rewarded and actively encouraged to violate them, and punished when I didn't. Not exactly how that should work.

The last 1/3s problems are:
- An invasion, with no actually invasion going on. I rarely find enemy invader ships, when I travel about.
- Trying to get the communicator parts, when they almost never drop from ships and you rarely find the ships. Then the 500 data research cost, when you have probably expended almost all sources of data at this point. Usually I have to cheat to get the communicator parts (had to this run, as I found only 2 out of 3 parts).
- The requirement of the Warp Hopper and Warp Shock research. These are basically useless ingame, due to poor design decisions, but are suddenly required at the end. If you had little data to spend on the communicator, then you now have to spend another 500 on the Warp Hopper stuff. Sometimes, this will prevent you from completing the game as you cannot find anymore research data.
- The fact that you relay information to the alien races and none of them, really help you at all. Hell, the only race that seems to have benefited was the Chitik; now even their smallest ships are uber-powerful scary... even more powerful than the invader ships, honestly.

I don't really care to go deeper into stuff, as it depresses me, as the game is so good, before this point, that it disheartens me to think about it. So, I'm moving on to the other issues of post-launch.

1] There are serious bugs still present, that have gone unfixed since launch.
- There are missing planets and anomalies, making it impossible to get all achievements or all quest completion done in a single run. For example, there are two "labyrinth" anomalies. Each game, you'll either get one or the other, and more rarely neither or both. In my current run, I never could find the multi-colored planet, the underwater labyrinth, or the plague planet.
- Some research does not work. Like Probe Capture.
- Anomalies aren't always where they are supposed to be (like the Saurid base being a full sector and a half away from its suggested coords) or the prompt doesn't always activate to get stories from the fish people (you are supposed to get a prompt to find the golden octahedron, but I never did).
- There is random placement of some anomalies... probably why some never get generated, but for example, I found Havok Flak research in the same system as New Tartarus... so roughly 7/10ths into the game, when I had insufficient data to research it and honestly needed it more around the 4/10ths portion of the game, and now its useless to me.
- There is a bug with both the Zoar Station and the Citadel, wherein, if you buy certain products in a specific order, certain purchases will dissappear, preventing you from ever getting them. This was a problem then and is a problem now.

2] There is major story plotholes that have not gotten explained, despite repeated requests to do so. These are:
- Exactly who were the Progent, and why did they feel it necessary to activate the Sentinels to attack themselves?
- If Eos was locked up, why order the Sentinels to kill all humans? The Sentinels are the ones with the access codes to Eos prison, so...
- It is said that we are in the past, roughly 1 billion years after the big bang, or 12 billion years before our normal timeline, at the beginning of the game. Except that the logical paradoxes don't line up. There is clear evidence that the races in the region were created through lab experimentation and then seeded on planets to eventually evolve into the races we encounter. But that kind of evolution takes billions of years, so why do they exist 1 billion years after the big bang, but don't exist 13 billion years later. It just doesn't make sense.
- We are supposed to be in the milky way. Except that it doesn't look the milky way, at all. Even accounting for the fact we are only 1 billion years after the big bang, there just isn't enough stars or planets to actually make it look like we are in the milky way.
- There are some locations in the game, tied to achievements, that lack any clue on their location. You have to cheat and use guides to find them.

3] Promised post launch support that disappeared.
- The developer did a poll in oct/nov 2019, of features that players really wanted for post-release, the two biggest being procedurally generated map and story improvements. A year later, after release, and there is still no eta on this stuff.
- The ship building had so much potential for expansion, but we never got any more work done on it. Even having decorative parts to help us make shapes would have been nice, and technically they do exist ingame. If you edit your save file, you can manually place decorative parts on your ship.
- The end of the game was promised to get addressed and improved, and it hasn't. It plays out almost exactly like it did in nov 2019.
- The developer has said that a lot of what he promised for post launch wasn't possible because it was too hard to do in the game project. Well, if that is the case, the next game better not be built in Unity, because if it is, I'm calling bull.
- The developer has basically disappeared from the forums. He'll occasionally pop up, but mostly is gone now. No reports on the next game, and he used to do weekly reports... a week report, every week since the first days of EA, and these stopped roughly around Feb 2020.
- The developer has no intention of fixing reported bugs or even telling us what is going on with his next project.

=====

tl;dr = Most of the game is good, and you should play it once. There is no replayability, and the developer has abandoned the title otherwise, so don't expect any updates or improvements, ever again.\t\t\t\t\t
