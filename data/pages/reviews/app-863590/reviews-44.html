\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is an unexpected gem for me. It has a strong combination of exploration, puzzles, and top-down combat that scratches an itch for a game that lets you act the part of an explorer in an unknown region of space in the best sort of homage to stuff like Star Trek, Star Control II, Mass Effect and the like.

Combat's fun, all ship-based, but you can send away teams down to planets to explore (and get themselves in trouble) while you advise from the bridge (because the Starcom organization is savvy enough to NOT let the captain lead the away teams). There's research to upgrade your ship's equipment and unlock new toys, and a pretty good ship builder in the shipyard section, where you can turn your little runabout into a Dreadnought with sufficient resources (evil grin). The only criticism I have is that the ending is a bit abrupt, but overall Starcom Nexus was a hell of a ride.

Also note: There's no problem that can't be solved with more plasma turrets. The Federation could learn from Starcom. (Unless you're on a nightmare difficulty level or something - at which point there's no problem the Axial Cannon can't solve!)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is fantastic. The explosions, mystery, and exploration dragged me into this world and consumed my weekend, and I don't regret it. That said, the end of this game left me a little wanting.

Pros:
- Graphics and art style are exceptional. I'm reminded recent space-themed 4X titles such as Master of Orion and Endless Space. The explosions are phenomenal as well. Most ships are composed of multiple segments, and the way they break apart and detonate one by one is beautiful.

- Combat is excellent. I believe there are seven different weapons systems (not counting ramming and hop shocking), so I was given quite a few ways to destroy my enemies. Additionally, shields work differently than in most games. They only consume power while off and gradually lose strength while active. This creates a dynamic that I haven't seen before in sci-fi, so I loved it's addition to this game.

- Ship building is great. There is a large variety of modules you can affix to your ship. Since you can experiment freely with the resources you have, it's fun to find the right balance between style and fun.

- Exploration is both scary and rewarding enough to keep me tense and on my toes. I don't know about other people, but I found some of the celestial phenomena to be a little creepy- particularly the giant stars.

Cons/Suggestions:

- There were a few bugs. They never ruined the game, but when I noticed them, they put a small damper on my mood. The biggest one I remember was bloom from explosions coming from offscreen even though the ship exploded right next to me. I can also remember loading in without shields. If I autosaved next to an enemy, I'll start taking fire because the game doesn't record shield strength in its save files.
Also, there are quite a few typos scattered throughout the game. It's possible these were intentional, but I haven't seen anything to indicate so.

- The game isn't quite as seamless as I would like. Firstly, the game doesn't load super smoothly. I can tell when I'm passing over a boundary because my frames drop for a split second, occasionally, my shields will lose a fair bit of their strength as well.
Secondly, using the galactic map is a bit jarring sometimes. With the push of a button, I can go from viewing my ship up close to viewing the entire galaxy in about a second. I would vastly prefer something like Supreme Commander's strategic zoom function. In that game, players can seamlessly zoom in far enough to see scratches on their units' paint and out far enough to view an 81x81km map. Also, your screen automatically zooms in where your cursor is pointing. I'd vastly prefer a system like this coupled with fog of war to the current one.

- I'd prefer a different ship builder. Starcom currently uses a hexagonal grid for all ships except stations (which partially use the honeycomb) and unique objects (derelicts, ancient transports, etc). Each hex has its own name and function (lasers, drone hangars, engines, hoppers, etc).
While this system serves its purpose, I find it to be quite limiting compared to a system I saw long ago in a mobile game. I would prefer to lay down the hull of a ship using a grid and attach weapons and other parts to said hull. With angled and curved pieces, I believe this system would make for a far more compelling and flexible ship builder.

- Exploration can sometimes be a bit slow. I found myself pressing Alt-Tab quite a bit. In fact, I'd wager that nearly a quarter of the time I spent in this game was browsing YouTube while I waited for my ship to arrive at some distant star system. I always felt rewarded for getting there, but, by the time I finished the game, this problem was becoming a little frustrating.

- System Names. Navigation is a pain in the ass sometimes. Just introduce a number system or something.

- I understand the developer doesn't have the resources to implement the following sweeping changes, but I felt like this game could have been so much more. I would love more 4x elements. As the sole representative of Starcom, it makes sense that you would take a more diplomatic role in addition to traipsing about the galaxy shooting bad guys. With an entire fleet of custom ships and some more in-depth character interaction (for instance, Mass Effect), this game could be genre defining. Look, all I want to do is RP Captain Picard and say, "We'll bang, okay?"

Over all: 8/10
It's worth the full price, and you should buy it now.\t\t\t\t\t
