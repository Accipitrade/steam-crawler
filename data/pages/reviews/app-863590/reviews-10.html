\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have some concerns with the design on this one, but overall a positive experience. There are places where I feel like there was clear room to expand but given that the developer is brand new and probably working with a budget of nothing, giving a thumps up and looking forward to see what their next project looks like.

Starcom: Nexus is a top-down space adventure where you take command of a ship to fly around and then, when all hell breaks loose on your first day, try to figure out what happened and how to restore some sanity. Controls are your classic Asteroids setup with rotate/thrust, with weapons (mostly) turreted and used with the cursor. The major two pillars of the game are the exploration and the story.

First up, there's a very large number of star systems, and there's no real predictability to what can be found where. Each new star you visit is a mystery. This can be both pleasing, when you find an important item you need or a new point of interest, and incredibly tedious when you find nothing but an impact crater and some metal.

The map is organized at three tiers of navigability, being gates (instant between any discovered gate), portals (only connect two points with some brief maneuvers between portals), and then just pointing your ship in a direction and flying through empty space. This last bit can take minutes between hops, even with maximum upgrades into engines and flight speed. You will often need to fly the slow way to get to new places that you can see on long range sensors but no portal lines exist. Sadly, sometimes Starcom turns into "Turn on autopilot and look at something else for a while" because it accurately captures the sheer tedium of flying through nothing. Further: The map does not give you an easy way to see which systems you've explored, or which parts of space have been "swept" by your sensors, which makes it really frustrating sometimes to find things if hints are cryptic or you missed one. Exploration is both a strength and a curse here.

On the flip side, the story is good and has some unexpected twists. Everything is conveyed by short text conversations and planetary surveys but the way it's laid out to be progressively discovered is well done. At the start of the game you have no idea which clues will wind up being the important ones, and as you will eventually discover there are very good reasons for why things are so mysterious. There are several alien factions to discover with different ways of speaking and different ship designs, and several people back at your home base who will feed you important hooks at key moments. The ship's log is an essential tool here, giving you a database of conversations, planet surveys, objectives, which can all be searched. Sadly you cannot click on map coordinates in the log to just open the map there.

My greatest frustration with the story is that the ultimate objective is a bit cryptic. There was a point where I knew broadly what I was supposed to do but there was no real hint on the specifics. This ties back into the map not being very helpful with showing you what you've explored and what you haven't. So it's overall good but I feel like the story did not get a full end-to-end pass for feedback from people who didn't already know where to find things.

There's plenty of combat in the game, but the combat is basically just an obstacle to you landing or exploring things that need landing and exploring. Your ship is made up of modular parts that you can change any time you're at your home base, starting off with a very small pitiful ship (it's your first command) and then gets progressively bigger and beweaponed over time. You collect research data from encounters with aliens or observing cool spacial events, and you can spend that research on upgrading your ship's components and performance, while paying for new parts with scavenged materials.

Sadly you can only fly your Human ship, and there is not a huge mechanical depth to mess around with due to the design rules. For example I spent a lot of time with missile racks, and there is no way to carry more missiles or increase how fast you build them except for adding more racks... which has unfortunate impacts on the shape and size of your ship because missile racks are forced to always face forward and must have nothing else in front of them. As suggested earlier the primary focus of the game is story and exploration so the combat and ship building *works* but (perhaps appropriately) it feels a bit secondary. I only ran into one situation where the combat was too difficult and it was easily overcome by spending some time trading for more materials and finding more research.

In terms of audio, there is no voiced dialog for anybody, including your crew which leads to things feeling a bit soulless. The music is low-key but fine. I would have liked more, especially racially-focused themes when you fly through territory belonging to specific aliens or talk to them. The sound effects for combat are especially meaty, and listening to enemy ships explode in a rolling thudding explosion as their components disintegrate is quite satisfying.

Graphically, being a top down game in space there's not a huge amount to look it. It feels like most of the technology went into the combat (deaths of large ships are quite lavish). There are a lot of different images for exploring planets but you just get a small static picture there is no actual realtime exploration. Nothing here is bad, but on the whole it feels like it would have strongly benefited from having a dedicated artist or two doing more nebulae, asteroid rings, alien cities and shipyards, bigger and more detailed (interactive?) exploration screens etc

In terms of engineering, the only outright problems I saw was a couple of text tags that lacked proper descriptions, and tooltips related to controls not updating to respect custom binds. Saving/loading etc all worked fine. The ship log might benefit from some optimization because it was taking noticeably longer to open at the end of the game than the start, but it's pretty understandable considering how much stuff goes in there.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStarcom Nexus feels like a love letter to older games like Star Control. How I slept on it so long, I'll never know. It's an incredibly chill "explore and do things in space" game. It is extremely rewarding for players that enjoy exploration with lots of events in space and on planets with fun little writing snippets that pay homage to sci-fi throughout the ages.

Ship customization and combat are a blast. Combat takes place on a 2D plane and controls feel a bit like the old Asteroids game. You can arm your ship as you see fit (I loved missiles and lasers) and tune it to your hearts content. I do wish there were more options (and there are a lot!) but you can build a ship to match your playstyle very easily.

I binged and beat the game in a few days (around 20 hours) and was definitely left wanting more. I'll echo what some folks say and indicate the game does just "end". It's just slightly better than "Rocks fall, everyone dies" but not by much. Otherwise the package is a ton of fun and I can't recommend it more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEver since playing Star Control 2 as a kid, I've had a soft spot deep in my heart for games about exploring space, unravelling deep mysteries while meeting all kinds of strange aliens along the way.

Lately, Steam has been filled with a wide variety of space exploration/combat games, to the point where it's hard to even pick one -- yet most of them are dull at best. None of them bring back the magic, the overflowing creativity of a game like SC2.

Except Starcom Nexus, which to put it bluntly, is really freaking good. This is not "good" as in "good for an indie game", this is good as in one of the coolest space exploration games ever made.

Despite its flaws, it does so many things so well and is filled with so much creativity and charm. The plot is really well put-together and almost reminds me of the Outer Wilds in terms of having to piece together little fragments of information to slowly figure out what the heck is going on. And the "anomalies" on the various planets you explore are filled with all kinds of interesting stories that will keep your imagination fed well. Don't rush through these -- savor every word! Some of them are quite good and skimming them will miss that impactful moment.

The core gameplay systems are solid enough as well that it doesn't feel like you have to slog through them just to experience the story, either.

I would recommend playing this game on a higher difficulty; the default is just too easy. But you can tweak it at any point during play until it feels right, which is very convenient.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinished the game for the 2nd or 3rd time with the last run on easy just to capture the final achievements I was missing. It was long enough from my last game where I didn't remember everything so kept it a little fresh. There isn't a lot of replay as the map and tech tree stay fundamentally the same. Most of the variation will be in how you configure your ship and a slight bit of RNG when you explore planets.

There's a lot of time flying your ship around the map and hitting "x" on planets to scan them, and "x" again (on PC) to explore in more detail if something is found. There are a lot of planets to scan with the main reward being RP (research points) or some of the various resources. Destroying ships can yield a small amount of resources and some of the ship technologies require you to recover parts from enemies before you can research them.

Designing ships wasn't quite what I expected and it's a little bit like building it with legos, where the legos are different modules (reactor, engine, shield, armor, turret, etc.) you start attaching to the mandatory command module. The modules have to be researched and they all generally have a lot of upgrades.

But the game is well balanced with exploration, trading, research, combat, shipbuilding, and story all playing an important role. Especially on the first run - but if you run through again it will be much faster as the story is linear and again, the map stays the same and the races are in the same places.

Worth spending your time on at least once, and if you're like me you can spend a bit more time getting the achievements. Note the last two I got were dependent on some RNG and meticulous record keeping.

Promotions for Stars and Bars is dependent on morale and you may not get all the positive events needed for the 2nd promotion in a run through. Comet Chaser is frustrating as you need to chase down a random tech before you can detect them, and it's easy to miss the message even after you have the detector. It doesn't log detection into the journal so you can't go back and search if you missed the pop-up. I added notes to every system I entered (you can annotate the map) so I knew where I had paid attention during my explorations because I believe the systems they appear are randomized each game.

Anyway - this got longer winded than I planned. Definitely enjoyable and recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t
