\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBefore you make an assumption based on the big red thumb on this review, take note that I did enjoy this game. I know that sounds contradictory, just hear me out for a moment.

The idea behind the game is great, I'm a strong believer in the idea that having simulation games that mirror real jobs is a great way to help people decide what they may want to do in life- without wasting their resources finding out the hard way they aren't fit for the job. Of course no game will ever give you a perfect representation of a job or career considering their role is to entertain first and foremost, but it is better than nothing. It's also a fun way to peak behind the scenes into systems that we normally can't see.

This game does all of that well, and is pretty fun. Some of the situations are pretty serious, and quite a few of them are pretty routine. You can even play the game based on your home town- provided it's not incredibly small. The description is a little inaccurate there. You cannot play in any city, just most . Trying to load up a map for smaller, lesser known locations results in waiting 3 hours on a loading screen that doesn't actually plan on loading.

Which leads me into why I can't recommend this game. I could spend all day talking about my favorite features, spend forever arguing about how the repetitive nature of the calls and emergency RNG are actually good things, but at the end of the day it doesn't matter- because the game is poorly optimized, and doesn't run well enough to deliver on it's promises. The map download function spends hours doing what google maps can manage in seconds, the game itself lags during the most critical moments, and loading screens are just pitifully slow.

Rambling aside, it's a pretty decent game, it's just a little lacking on the technical side. If you think you want to try it out, I'd recommend instead checking out one of the newer games by this developer, or getting this one on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
