\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIs this going to be the best game you have ever played? No. Is this going to be a fun game to pass the time? Yes! For me I found this game to be intriguing and enjoyable. The gameplay is simple and repetitive, but compelling the first few times you respond to a call and see how to resolve an ongoing situation. You manage the emergency responses in various cities, by sending medicals crews, police, and the fire department to deal with earthquakes, robberies, fires, deaths, and many other terrible calamities that can and will ravage your city.

I like to play this game to calm be down, especially when I have a bad time with other games.
I care about getting everything right in games, and that I control emergency services makes it more meaningful to me. Still, I do wish I learned about the fallout of my actions like in a newspaper clipping and saw how it affected the city, the public's perceptions of emergency services, and whether or not I will be fired for poor performance. Perhaps add in a break room to see how other responders view each other and myself, the dispatcher, and I would certainly enjoy it more.

I certainly enjoy playing this game and would recommend it to other people to gain a respect for the men and women that do their part to help keep society functioning. I would not mind seeing a pandemic DLC added for this game that reflects the coronavirus' effect on a city. You could have PPE gear, personnel that could choose not to wear PPE, encounter people who could become agitated or violent when meeting services that do or do not use masks, face shields, and the like.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile simple in gameplay and somewhat bland on art style, 911 Operator is a great simulator for emergencies. Instead of playing as the usual mayhem induced protagonist, you play as an operator trying to mobilize and respond to threats, prank calls, hostage situations and more.
As I said before, the game has a pretty bland art style, or at least, the models for the people seem to be traced ones, giving it a really stilted vibe. But the map and icons are more of a minimalist rendition, from the firetrucks and helicopter symbols, to the locations that signify the response team required for the job.
The gameplay is repetitive, but I find the duty's loop to be engaging, specially after having a f**k ton of teams and vehicles, making the higher difficulties and accelerated clock all the more fun for the kind of person who enjoys having to manage a lot of stuff at the same time.
The achievements are also quite fun to chase after, and most can be unlocked by just playing and experimenting with the calls.

TL;DR
Buy if you enjoy managing a bunch of stuff at the same time and having the protagonist being a goody-two-shoes non-homicidal dude.\t\t\t\t\t

Short version: Good strategy game but some serious UI and design shortcomings.

911 Operator is an interesting riff on the strategy genre; rather than deploying a military force against attackers and holding strongpoints, you're responsible for handling the emergency response efforts of an entire city. In many ways, this makes it more difficult than your average military title because you're forever up against limited resources (both financial and assets) and the occasional onslaught of calls that requires you to really test your juggling abilities. On the face of it, this makes for one great game, but there are sadly some serious blunders in its design and execution that keep it from being all it could've been.

The Good
This game tests your ability to keep a lot of balls in the air, directing appropriate resources to deal with issues, and recognizing what needs attention and what doesn't. When things get really hectic, you're constantly shifting assets around to stay on top of one crisis after another; when a disaster or mass casualty accident occurs, multiply it by ten. It's thrilling.

This isn't just emergency whack-a-mole, though; you are a 911 operator, so you will field calls. Choose responses to get the information you need to respond appropriately - or not. Not every call actually merits a response, and you need to make those decisions, too. It creates a nice balance between rote "match the assets" gameplay and more in-depth engagement.

On top of all of that, you choose who you hire, what units you field, who staffs those units, and how they're equipped. You can optimize units for tactical, medical, SAR, fire, and any number of roles, or build all-rounders that can handle more, but not as efficiently. Your budget isn't bottomless, so you're often going into a shift with less than what you hoped for, but maybe enough to get by. Do well and you'll earn more so you can improve your services. In that, this game is a fusion of strategy, tactics, and financial management.

Where It Goes Wrong
Unfortunately, there two major problems with this game (and one minor one) that keep it from being a walk-away winner.

While you decide how to deploy your forces at the start of the day, the game wasn't programmed to care. After dealing with an incident, units don't go back to their assigned bases or patrol areas; they go wherever they want. In real emergency services, units can't just go on a vacation after dealing with a crisis - they return to their deployment points so they can be counted on to be ready for the next deployment. Not here. So while your strategy is carefully designed, it comes apart pretty quickly because the game is absolutely rubbish at how it handles post-deployment.

That's compounded by the UI, which routinely covers up both units and crisis markers, making finding or interacting with them difficult. Unit icons don't stack when parked, they just cover each other up. Combined with the "go wherever" attitude, it means you're forever losing units and ending up with a mass of medical units but only seeing one ambulance. In large cities, you have to stop frequently just to put your forces back to readiness, which is a major blunder by the developers.

Finally, for a game that centers on emergency calls, the choice to record so few calls is questionable. Calls repeat regularly, exactly the same as before. It creates tedium because not enough effort went into creating content, which is disappointing.

The Bottom Line
Certainly the best of its field, 911 Operator has much to recommend it, but is held back by some serious faults. Definitely worth the time so long as you can deal with its interface and design frustrations.\t\t\t\t\t
