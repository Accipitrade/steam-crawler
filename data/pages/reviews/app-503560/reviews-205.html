\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere are no mechanics for entering caller information, which is something I was expecting to do. Addresses are set as a waypoint on your map when you request the location via a dialogue option. The waypoint will be colored to correspond with the service that is needed (blue=police, red=fire, or white=EMT). I would much rather have it be up to the player to determine which service is needed instead of it being labeled for you, I have not come across an option to change this.

Situations arise on the map that aren't actually called in, but instead, show up as a notification. I'm indifferent about this because it does provide a challenge when it comes to prioritizing, however quite a few of these situations I would have liked to have taken the call for. Most of these notifications are medical related (heart attacks, broken bones, etc) which I see as missed opportunities to help teach the player, but I'm still getting both repeat and unique calls after playing for about 4 hours.

After your shift ends, you will be shown a "scorecard" which give you a positive or negative score for each situation depending on how well you handled it. You'll also receive money which will be used to upgrade staff, crew equipment, and vehicles; a feature I wasn't initially excited about, but warmed up to later on as a sort of replacement for the lack of progression. You can also get an idea of how well you're doing in real-time via your reputation stat on the top left corner. If you make a bad judgment call, your reputation will take a hit and vice versa.

I found this to be a great entry for those who have a hard time getting into the RTS/management genre, especially if you're interested in the idea of managing emergency response, because prioritizing calls is a lot of fun.

Plus, it's always amusing to have police request heavy back-up after being shot at the address of the local bakery in your normally peaceful hometown.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe gameplay is extremely limited and simplistic. An icon pops up on the city map telling you which vehicles are required to resolve the incident. You left-click on the nearest matching vehicle, right-click on the icon to send it there... and that's basically the entire game.

Calls add a little depth, but mostly boil down to selecting a few obvious dialogue options to find out which vehicles are required for the job. They are, however, very well-written and backed by good voice acting, so they do add to the game a bit. The only problem is that there are so few of them that they start repeating during your third eight-minute-shift.

The game is also very glitchy and unpolished. During just 90 minutes of playtime, I helped two men give childbirth and watched a fire truck drive straight through a lake, only to become permanently frozen on the other side. And despite working just fine yesterday, today the game won't even start anymore.

I probably wouldn't be so harsh on the game if it wasn't for its price. If you take the day one DLC into account, you're paying nearly $20 for a game that struggles to differentiate itself from the Clicker genre and shows you everything it has to offer within 30 minutes. Even with the current discount, it still feels overpriced.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've wanted this game for a while, and finally picked it up in the Summer Sale. I'm glad I did.

At its heart, it's just a RTS. I normally dislike this style of game, but they made one that I enjoy, so kudos to the deseigners! You can buy units of different sorts (police, fire, ambulance), and hire crew for each. What sets it apart is that you can use your actual city's map to play on. It's pretty cool to control these units on your home turf. However, that's part of the problem for me. I live in a border city (Windsor, ON). The map makes Detroit part of my city, which takes a lot of the realism out of the game for me. A minor complaint, but enough of one. Another issue is that I know my city well enough to know that the locations they give on the map are way off. Like, miles off. They could do a lot better work on that. My final complaints are abot the repetition, both of the calls, and the areas of the city. It might be different calls coming from the same location on the map, but location is ever the same. The designers could go with spreading things around the map. I understand that new calls can be dowloaded from the Workshop, so I'll give that a go.
I'd say sure, buy this game, but maybe get it during a sale. Only hardcore RTS enthusiasts would be happy paying the full price, methinks.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is surprisingly fun and has a great balance of difficulty.

You're given about 4 police vehicles, 3 medical response vehicles, and 3 fire response vehicles for average, (at least at the beginning of the game) and can build your units. Obviously, in a real city, 4 police cars on patrol at once would never be enough. However, in combination with how often emergencies come up, it is a great balance of difficulty and not blowing your head off trying to maintain too much at once.

The calls ARE somewhat repetitive, however there is also an element of randomization to them. For example, you might get a call about a parked car that somebody is suspicious about. You can then ask the caller about where the car is parked and why they might be concerned about the car. The first time I took this call, the car was not in a place where I should be concerned, and I ignored it, and I was correct to do so. The second time I took this call, the caller said it was somewhere where cars should not be parked, "for safety reasons." I missed this tiny detail and was punished for it. I thought he should have just called city services and gotten it towed, but I was supposed to check for a potential bomb threat. I'm assuming that with all the calls there are some elements of randomization, and you need to adjust to each of these details. Some people calling about a fire can resolve it themselves if the fire is small enough, whereas other times you simply need to send firemen. Furthermore, on the campaign, you will have certain unique calls. On only the second campaign map, I had a call which was incredibly interesting and surprising.

The calls are semi-repetitive, and while the gameplay is the same, it's enough to keep you entertained for at least a while. Being able to play on any city map is also a great feature, however it is somewhat limited. If you choose your city, it will likely choose the most populated or most interesting for gameplay reasons section of your city. If the section is too large, it becomes difficult. So for example, my area of the city is somewhat boring and gridlike, and wouldn't be difficult to play on because you could simply position your teams in the corners and send whoever is closest and they could make it there quickly because of the gridlike nature of the map. Instead, it's actually the downtown area of my city, which has more interesting diagonal streets and looks more busy and is more difficult to play on and position your teams on.

Overall, I think the game is polished very neatly, but could use a little more. There is almost no keyboard control, and when there is, mouse is faster. The gameplay can be difficult, and it gets tense when you have difficulties responding to everything, but there is also the satisfaction of seeing the end screen at the end of each day and see a perfect day. The preplanning and management is good, but early on it can't solve issues that you might have due to some RGN (e.g. investing in a new ambulance because you had issues responding to medical requests and getting a lot of fires the next day).

However, it is a fun game, especially if you can get it on sale. It also has workshop support where you can find some additional content. I have not personally tried it and there is always the potential that the workshop content (like calls) could be not-quite-right (not responding the way a 911 operator actually would).\t\t\t\t\t
