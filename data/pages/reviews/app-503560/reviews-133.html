\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a nice little casual game. If you, somehow are interested in emergency services and would like to put yourself in an operator's chair, buy the game - and support the devs.

4 hours in and I'm already receiving the same emergencies/calls over and over again, I wouldn't recommend playing it often and for extended periods of time. There is workshop support and you can add new emergencies, so I guess it is possible to shake it up a bit.

What I don't really see is the need to improve the units or increase their numbers. After playing a certain city for ~14 duties, I can get 100% efficiency without calls, and 98% with calls. (in easy mode, though)
So I guess increasing difficulty would have a serious replayability improvement on the game, extending expedted playtime to well over 20 hours.

The voiceovers are good for the calls, I recommend turning them on after you can deal with the autospawning emergencies.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t911 Operator succeeds more as thought experiment about this constant dilemma for emergency service workers than it does a game. As a game, it’s the same looped five minute experience, with varying degrees of pressure to it. You sit in front of a map of the city you’re playing in, choosing which emergencies to assign which vehicles to, interspersed with phone calls in which you need to elicit appropriate information – severity, location, possible escalations, if it’s a total idiot – in order to make your judgement call.
Some situations require multiple vehicles, some situations escalate even after you’ve arrived – your police need backup because someone starts shooting, or someone’s injured because you couldn’t get to them in less than a heartbeat – and some situations have to be abandoned because something more terrible has occurred elsewhere. Mostly, though, you’re just right-clicking to send a police car, ambulance or fire engine to a point on the map, and hoping nothing else goes wrong while you wait for them to get there. It’s compelling in small doses, but only a few hours in I’d taken its point to heart and struggled to see what else I might take from it. It’s definitely effective at creating tension, which I admire, but that tension grows increasingly one-note. Great game highly recommend!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t911 operator is a very interesting and fun game, though it can be repetetive at times

------PROS------
Very fun management
Good upgrade system
Calls are fully voiced
voice acting is good
50 different calls
Stress inducing, but in a good way
Sometimes emotional
Offers a lot of choice
You can give real first aid advice

------CONS------
Although features keep it interesting at all times, the core gameplay can be repetetive
There is not a lot of content, but the content that is there is good
Only fun for 2-3 sessions a day before you get bored

------SUMMARY------
A generally good game that is very fun and stressful, although not very realistic it is very tense effectively simulating what its like to be a 911 operator. Personally I would give this game an 8.2/10 If you like simulation/management games than this is for you. NOTE:
The base game is good but the special resources DLC is almost essential to fully enjoy the game, but I do recommend the other dlc to, if you have the money.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t 5/10: Mediocre

A briefly fascinating yet flawed simulation game that gives some insight as to what it is like to be working at the end of an emergency services phoneline. A few allowances have been made to make it workable as a game, such as having a massively larger proportion of incoming calls warrant some form a response, and this is very welcome. However, this game still lacks progression and variety. It is what it is, and it is that from minute 1 of the game. I enjoyed it and am glad I picked it up, but it wasn’t a game I felt the need to go back to very much once I got the hang of it.
