Pros
* One doesn't need a super powerful rig to play the game! A plus for people like me who use a laptop.

* Love answering those emergency calls! It really is quite the on-the-edge-of-your-seat kind of experience.

* Scarily realistic road mapping of cities like Plano! A big win, in my own honest opinion.

* The ambiance is truly amazing! Props to the voice acting team, and the sound team!

Con(s)
* If your team needs the important supplies, from guns to first-aid kits, at the get-go, then why the hell must a 911 operator be given $15,000.00? It makes no sense! Even with stuff priced in the thousands! $50,000.00, at least, should be what one should start with.

When you look past the insanity of the game, it is, overall, a hellova good game! That is why I rate it 8/10!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t911 Operator has a great premise. The idea of being both the receiver of dramatic calls and the dispatcher of the resources to lend aid to those dramatic situations is very enticing. But how do you make a game of it that goes beyond just sending police when the police are needed, or sending ambulances when ambulances are needed, etc?

Jutsu games has opted to turn the career of a 911 operator into that of a manager of the first response resources of the police, firefighters, and hospitals. So while for much of the game you are in fact the guy who picks up the phone when the emergency line rings, you are beholden to an economy, staff, and inventory.

So we have a game with the dreaded task of balancing a tactical game with a management game. Fortunately, the tactical game (the actual answering of calls and dispatching units) is fun and novel. Unfortunately, the management game is unapproachably difficult. And not in a compelling way.

But first, the good. 911 Operator shines when you begin your shift. You're looking at a map of the city you are sworn to help protect. Incidents pop up across the city in the form of little bubbles. They'll range from car accidents, speeding, accidental amputations, robberies, epilepsy, house fires, to any dramatic (and often ridiculous) scenario that takes place in a city on any given day. Calls will come in. These are voiceover sequences with branching dialogue where you try to determine the needs required by the situation. Sometimes you'll hear of a break-in and you'll need to send police right away. Sometimes a child calls you and asks for math homework. You really do have some freedom in how you conduct your self in these calls.

With that variety and attention to detail, the city you're protecting feels alive. It's compelling. You finish your duty and your proud of your units and the work they did to keep your city safe.

Then the problems start when you get to the management screen. You are of course graded on how well you did that day. Did you miss any calls? Were you too late to any scene? If yes to either of these questions, buckle up because you're about to bleed funding. And when you lose funding, you're unable to buy new units. And you better believe that when you start your next shift, things are going to be more difficult because in 911 Operator every town is on a one way downward spiral to crime-hell.

If you had funding, you could buy bullet-proof vests, first aid kits, safety suits for your firemen, a helicopter, new staff, and a million other helpful tools that could help you in your quest to protect and serve. But alas, you were too late responding to that woman who had a bat in her house because there were two shoot-outs happening and you felt those needed more attention. How dare you.

And its 911 Operator's weirdly punishing criteria mixed with its inability to balance the game that really brings it down. Balance becomes an issue because of one of 911's most novel features. You can play this game in any city in the world. When you choose "Free Play," you can type in a city and download the map and presto you are now the first response kingpin of wherever you like.

At first this feature is a blast. I played a game set in my hometown and it adds so much flavor to each encounter. There's drunk and disorderly calls coming from that Chinese buffet. That's fun.

But the problem is, cities come in all shapes and sizes and 911 Operator has no idea how to scale its difficulty to fit the terrain of those cities. Now obviously, it'll be more difficult to work first response in New York than it is in Tonopah. There's so much city to cover in large metropolises. That difficulty spike is expected.

What's not expected is that it's as difficult to play in New York as it is in a small town if that small town has a damn river running through it and not that many bridges. The incidents will occur in any old place and the choke point will cause huge delays as your vehicles bounce back and forth across the river trying to take care of incidents and then drop injured citizens at hospitals or criminals at police stations. I played a small town on easy difficulty with a river running through it and the game became wildly difficult by duty #3. Success was never meant to be.

Ultimately, I'm not sure any amount of DLC's released could overcome this issue. I do have all the DLC's and while they add some variety to the calls, incidents, and units, that core difficulty imbalance prevents me from getting much more than a half dozen shifts into any one map.

Bottom line: Some real fun to be had in the novelty of playing the part of a 911 operator, but the fundamental balance and design flaws render the game too frustrating to stick with.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR : Good game.

This is my first review on Steam and I'm gonna make it short, but effective: I love this game. I haven't spent 1000 hours on it, but I'm sure that I would if I had the time.
The gameplay is very simple, yet there's a very distinct complexity to the game. You have to strategize, prepare your units and evaluate the appropriate response to every type of crime. On higher difficulties, you can't save everybody, so you need to go case per case.
Perhaps hardcore strategists that enjoy fairly complicated gameplay with lots of information to take in will find this game too simple, but I find it refreshing to have a game that asks for this level of thought without feeling like you'll need 1 hours to take decisions.

Moreover there's a lot of mods to allow players to have a more varied experience with the phone calls and scenarios.

Hell : You can download maps from all over the world and play IN YOUR OWN TOWN ! How awesome is that !
