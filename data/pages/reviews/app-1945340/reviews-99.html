\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll start with the positives: Good music. Tormentors are a good addition to the sandbox.

Now for the negatives:

I can't say much about Lightfall without echoing other reviews; a tedious campaign with weak characters, shoddy dialogue, and a nonsensical waste-of-time story with no meaningful repercussions that was shoehorned into the broader narrative at the last minute in order to extract another year of profits from Destiny's playerbase. Strand (like Stasis before it) is currently in an absolutely broken overpowered state, enticing players to buy the expansion or play group content at a comparative disadvantage until the subclass inevitably gets "fixed" once Lightfall sales reach critical mass. The predatory monetization continues with the very idea of the "annual pass," effectively a $100 surprise egg built on FOMO psychology (that's $30 more than a regular full priced game, by the way). Either gamble $100 for unknown content from the upcoming year right now, or miss out on limited time content and pay more when buying the content once it actually releases and you know what you are purchasing. All while the mobile-game-style cash shop receives hundreds of dollars worth of overpriced exclusive items on a regular basis.

Destiny's core strength is its basic moment to moment gameplay and mechanics. As a D1 vanilla player, those mechanics have never felt flimsier than in this expansion. All but a few primary weapons hit like a gust of wind- requiring full or multiple magazines to finish off the weakest of opponents in mid-range activities. After recent changes, overshields take more time to whittle down and are more common than ever. Champions exacerbate the problem by being shoehorned into most activities, locking players into a select few loadouts based on mere compatibility rather than enjoyment. Meanwhile, bugs are an assumption taken for granted, and more frustrating than ever. Random "physics" deaths are still a common issue as they have been since launch. The new seasonal activity will instantly kill you for walking to the next objective half a second too early. If an ally uses a finisher while you're near an enemy, you will travel to the moon at approximately the speed of light. Players in Gambit and Crucible fail to load in properly, leaving them invisible for the duration of matches. Error codes will throw activity progress in the trash without even attempting to reconnect you, and then reprimand you for "quitting" once you return to the game. In the game's present state, bugs are- ironically- like a swarm of flies. One is tolerable, but several are a nuisance- and in its current state, no play session is safe from a plethora of minor annoyances made insufferable by sheer variety and frequency. But despite years of player complaints about these (and other) issues, bugs are expensive to fix and don't affect the bottom line unless they break the game altogether. Bungie will never fix a problem unless they can sell the solution.

Core activities wallow in stagnation- one new strike, no new Crucible maps, no new Gambit maps, no vendor loot refresh. The game's best content- its raids and dungeons- are still untouchable to a majority of the playerbase due to the lack of an LFG system. The gameplay loop of these core activities is built around increasing power level- a long begrudged system of making an arbitrary number go up by heavily grinding activities you would otherwise avoid in order to play the content you may actually want, and being sent back to the beginning every three months in order to keep the treadmill spinning. These are extremely common complaints, but potential solutions are neglected because- like the game's bugs- they are expensive to fix, and offer nothing but unprofitable player satisfaction in return. Again: Bungie will not fix a problem unless they can sell the solution.

Neomuna is undercooked. Contrary to the dynamic neon-soaked metropolis promised in trailers, it has the same hollow, depressing feeling as an abandoned mall. From the redundant facades of sterile buildings to the plastic streets below, littered with Cabal assets copy/pasted from Vanilla D1. In short: outside of the novel premise, it's the same as every other patrol space in the game that you've been ignoring for the past six years, except for a poorly tuned light level handicap and some activities that you will stop playing upon acquiring the exclusive items.

Because of the game's (highly profitable) "speed before quality" approach, the cycle of mediocrity will continue until something substantial happens to the company's bottom line. Lightfall isn't it. The game reached peak player numbers upon the DLC's release, and likely record profits in turn. Whales with more dollars than cents will continue buying every lump of coal in the cash shop, feeding the anti-player economy and gradually pricing veterans out of enjoying the ever increasing amount of paywalled content. Lightfall, hopefully, represents a turning point in the playerbase where all pretense is abandoned of viewing Bungie as anything except a for-profit company seeking to wring its players of every possible dollar while serving the minimum possible product that people will buy. A tall order made easier after the sunk-cost fallacy has already lured millions deep into their tangling web of dark habits. All that players like me can do is exactly what I'm doing right now: recommend that no one buys or plays this DLC or the game as a whole.

Lightfall is an egregiously overpriced failure and disappointment on almost every level. The upcoming raid will be unable to change my overall impression of the expansion, even if it is the best in the franchise to date.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI strongly disagree with the negative reviews around this expansion.

- Destiny is a living game. Giving us a story to interact with over the course of the expansion makes a lot more sense then front-loading the entire story like the previous expansion. I don't want to have the lead up to the grand finale dumped on me only to wait a full year to find out what the hell is happening with The Final Shape. S22 and S23 continuing the campaign makes sense. It's an improvement over the seasonal model of old.

- Neomuna is an incredible open world. It's dense, it is full of activity, it has more to explore and do than previous open worlds. It's visually some of their best work in Destiny 2's patrol zones, if not their best. I personally think only Dreaming City is comparable in depth and environmental beauty. Europa was pretty neat, i'll give them that. Throne World was ugly and lacked content.

- The transition to post campaign content was done far better than in previous expansions. They made the transition a bit seamless when it comes to going for exotics and strand, and there isn't actually a bad grind for strand. It's actually super reasonable.

- Last but not last, the campaign may have had writing and pacing issues, but the gameplay and mission design was great. It was not all bad too, while this had the worst final boss of all campaigns I do feel, the final stand with Caitl and our allies felt ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ sick. We actually had allies. The relationship between Caitl and her father felt personal, but she owned it. She was greater. There was so much power in the moments the two had, I think it was the highlight of the campaign. Caitl is ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ awesome. Best girl.

Lightfall is setting the stage for a good year, and unless they really drop the ball with post campaign support, i'm going to say this is a better expansion than Shadowkeep, Beyond Light and Witch Queen. I'm actually interested in the game and where the story will go. I can't say that for Witch Queen and Shadowkeep, and Beyond Light's story barely had any interest and left me ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ hanging.\t\t\t\t\t
