Gameplay: 7/10
Graphics & Sound: 8/10
Narration: 7/10

✔️ Creative and interesting game mechanics, compressing a colony management game into a roguelike experience.
✔️ Cute and cozy graphics & sound design.

❌ The game is just too difficult in my opinion. Its general mood doesn't prepare you at all for how difficult it is to actually complete a run. Not only you have to juggle with tons of resources to build structures, feed your characters, and advance in the game - but the game also punishes you with devastating events for having extra resources. I get the point of only taking what you need to keep going, but the overall complexity of the game is already pretty daunting even without that. At this point it's an actual game design flaw in my opinion.
❌ In addition to the crazy difficulty of this game, the randomness can completely mess up your progression, sometimes giving you areas without enough food to advance, or without enough resources to fulfil your travel prerequisites. So not only sometimes you will lose because of poor decisions in an unforgiving game, but also sometimes you will just be destroyed by the game itself without any chance to compensate for the bad luck.

Sadly I cannot recommend the game, despite finding it generally interesting and cute, but its challenge level is just too dissonant with its general presentation, and after 7 hours I still haven't completed a single run of the easiest game mode, so I'd rather prevent other people from having a similar experience... I might keep trying to play it, and maybe if I realise at some point that I was approaching it from the wrong angle from the beginning, I could change my review, but that seems unlikely.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is bizarre. I held off writing a review for a long time, because this is one of those games that I would give a sideways thumb or a neutral review if I could. It has a lot of aspects I like, but also a considerable amount I don't, but in the end I gave it a recommendation because I think it's a great game for a specific subset of people -- the game has plenty of polish, and some of the aspects that I would take the most points away for are also some of the ones with the greatest overall subjectivity.

As Far As The Eye does provide a roguelike experience, but the challenges are centered around route planning and gathering cycle optimization. A lot of the game involves repetitively having your followers complete the same set of actions over and over again with the goal of attempting to acquire specific combinations of resources needed to move onto the next area. Unfortunately, as you get into the longer journeys, you can't see the entire map (and the resource types and quantities needed to complete your journey) from the beginning. Since the combinations of resources are random and not every type of area has the same resources (or a guarantee that there's a meaningful amount of the types it does have), this means that you may end up in a section of the map where the only paths available to you don't provide even a chance of successfully completing your journey. With that said, many reasonable assumptions can be made about the likely resources available and resources acquired, making that situation unlikely if you know what you're doing. It could be compared to similar situations in roguelikes such as FTL in which you end up at the end of the game in an unwinnable fight -- it's possible that your run simply had no possible path to victory, but more likely a more experienced player would have been able to identify play and pathing errors that could have given you a better chance if corrected. Since there isn't a direct opposition or any type of combat in AFATE, though, losing to the situation of "none of the resources you need are available" could end up feeling impersonal and frustrating.

Aside from weirdness on the direct gameplay loop, AFATE also has a progression I'm not particularly fond of -- unlocking penalties. As you play the game, you unlock different traits that are rolled on each character. Both negative and positive traits are unlocked, and while overall you gain more than you lose, it's possible that early on a player might actually put themselves in a worse position by accidentally unlocking some very poor traits while failing to unlock any particularly good ones.

Finally, I just don't like the game's color palette very much. It does stand out from the crowd, but I find that it grates on me as I actually play the game. I can definitely see this as being a positive for some people though, which is why I save it for last -- it's a big deal for me, but anyone that the color palette or aesthetic doesn't appeal to can already see that from the screenshots or trailer; they don't need me to point it out.

Anyway, hope this helps someone in coming to a bit of a better understanding about the nature of the game.\t\t\t\t\t
