\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is something new and fresh in the survival-builder genre. You are playing as a small tribe of individuals and gather ressources on a map until you have enough to get to the next map (think: Sectors like in Faster Than Light). Each individual gathers experience points and can specialize, so you will have your Stone cutter, your druid, your gatherer who is also a cook etc.The game is turn based, a map has about 50 - 200 turns. Since you don't have forever on the map, you really have to optimize what your individuals do, where you put your buildings etc.The artstyle, writing and presentation is wonderful. Very cute, with just the right amount of mystical-fantasy stuff that is neither cliché nor "too out there".If you are like me frustrated by the small amount of good survival builder games, I recommend to try this one, since it really goes a different route.On the negative side, the UI could be clearer at some points and I miss some Quality of Life stuff (like keyboard shortcuts), but it doesn't hamper the fun.At first I gave a negative review due to unfair RNG, but the developer reacted immediatly to the criticism and I would expect some more finetuning there, so no worries about that,All in all, a great game.\t\t\t\t\t

Overall: B

PROS:
As Far As The Eye does what all great pieces of art does: it combines elements from other, familiar works and arranges them in a way that feels unique and satisfying. The two biggest influences seem to be FTL (just like most indie games these days) and Civilization, but there's also elements of European board games thrown in there. I find the setting and light storytelling to be quite interesting and calming, which is juxtaposed with a gameplay loop where you doing everything just to survive both the next turn and the 100+ turns after that.

There are a lot of choices to be made, and you will find yourself in many dilemmas over high risk/high reward vs low risk/low reward. Sometimes there isn't a clear option to take, and it's up to you to decide what tradeoffs and risks you're willing to take. It is extremely rewarding when you leave a "halt" (maps you progress through) with large surpluses and a healthy tribe.

How you play one halt can have drastic effects on all of your future halts, but there's enough random events thrown in to keep you in check in case you happened upon one of those magic RNG seeds where everything just works out (anyone who's played a roguelike has experienced one of those runs). Basically, you're never safe and should always keep an eye out for surprises if things are going _too_ well.

CONS:
  • The tutorials are quite intrusive and annoying. There are a few times in the "campaign" (truly just a fancy way of saying "tutorial mode") where I was forced to make choices that were obviously suboptimal even as a new player learning the game. I really wish there were more options for the tutorial than just "Basic" and "Disabled" (though the fact that it's labeled "Basic" gives me hope that they plan on adding an "Advanced" option)
  • There are definitely some opportunities for UX improvements. I find it very tedious clicking through the job specializations and having to remember the names of the units who are good at whatever I'm trying to do as well as their unique traits, etc. Having the unit selection change when you toggle through your tribe in the specialization screen would be easier for me. There are icons that summarize this information without having to open the specialization screen, which is helpful.
  • The hexes can appear a bit cluttered at times, and it's sometimes annoying trying to actually click on the unit rather than the hex itself or a building on it. Pro tip: it's usually easier to click on a unit's balloons or their icon rather than trying to click directly on them
  • Translation issues. It's never bad enough to cause any confusion, but it would've probably been a good idea to have a native English speaker do some proofreading. I could've overlooked this in early access, but I expect better from a released game.

Conclusion:
From the amount of cons listed above, it might sound like I didn't like this game. Quite the opposite. I pulled the trigger on purchasing it after less than five minutes of watching some gameplay on YouTube. I've been itching for a game like this for a long time, but most strategy games that have come out in the past decade have completely disappointed me. As far as first impressions go, I completely lost track of time in my first sitting and didn't quit until I realized it was past 1AM! It's definitely not a perfect game, but what it gets right it absolutely nails.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game was incredibly brutal until I discovered the Council building and the Knowledge resource, and then it became an incredibly fair and deep city-building/roguelike/4x game.

The biggest problem with the game is there isn't a good reference for how all the mechanics work. I think it will be better once an online wiki is available.

My tips for a winning initial game:

Opening:
1. Get 40 wood and build a sawmill. Assign one of your workers to be a gatherer and gather wood. Later, when you don't need wood, switch this worker to be a food gatherer and build a fruit hut for 80 (you can do this earlier if you get a 4th worker). You should have enough rations for this, and the carpenter/fruit gatherer jobs share the first 3 levels.
2. Get 120 wood and build a dispensary. Assign one of your workers to be a druid and harvest herbs. IMPORTANT: Druids generate a ton of Knowledge after they hit level 2, so just keep leveling the druid
3. Have your third worker be a dedicated builder/explorer. Explore the map and do all of the following:
* Find a source of stone, build a quarry for 100 wood, and gather stone until you have 120 stone
* Find a source of ore, build a minefor 100 wood, and gather ore until you have 120 ore
* Find one packbeast and recruit him
5. Now, you can build the Council auxiliary building. It has to be built on a plain next to your starting location, so make sure you leave some space! Build it and click on it and open the tech tree that you never knew existed.
6. Look at the bottom left. See that technology? It saves 6 rations/turn for your WHOLE TRIBE. You want to beeline to this thing as soon as you can. You need to upgrade your Coucil twice, and it requires some combination of Wool/Wood/Stone/Ore (I don't remember exactly). Then, this perk costs 900 Knowledge. Rush this ASAP.
7. The tree on the right hand side is key to managing Vagaries. You can skip ANY vagary by paying 350/400/500 knowledge (increases as the game goes on). You can also get earlier and earlier warnings by upgrading this tree, so you can be selective (e.g., you are on a map with no jungles, and the next vagary places disease in jungles)
8. Also prioritize a market and upgrade it to Tier 3. You need 5 different resources to do this (Knowledge/wood/ore/stone/wool). Don't be afraid to rotate workers around. The market is pretty much useless if you don't upgrade it, but it can really save your bacon if you specialize in one/two resources.
9. Mobile buildings are overrated early-on. You will have plenty of wood; have your woodcutter keep leveling up and SPEND your resources
10. This leads me to...DONT STOCKPILE early on. If you have harvested enough ore/stone for a tech upgrade, get it and move on.
11. Always get the first upgrade on the fruit hut (-1 harvest time), even on permanent buildings. Trust me, it's amazing, and will level up your gatherer even faster (gatherers can cost no food per turn when fully upgraded, depending on your build).
12. Select skills that increase knowledge-per-harvest whenever you can (e.g., if you aren't having food problems, have your fruit gatherer take the +Knowledge per turn instead of fruit per turn)
13. In the council building, there are relatively cheap tiers of knowledge that reveal the path costs for both one- and two- paths later. Use these to plan your routes! Not all resources are available on every map!


Anyhow, all the people who are upset about the randomness of Vagaries and needing too little food probably didn't rush Tier 3 Council. And yes, the devs should have made it more obvious how critical this building is for early success. But don't let that turn you off from this game; it's an incredible resource management game, with a relaxing atmosphere and beautiful art. It is challenging without being crazy 4X (think Stellaris) level deep. If you think you'd like it, ignore the negative reviews here, give my advice a shot, and enjoy

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't often write reviews, but I really like this game conceptually and would like to advocate for an improvement to the vagaries system. There are several Vagaries that makes the game feel more frustrating than fun. Not because they are random or difficult, but because you can receive a mix of them within a few turn span that will end your game. That is, there is a chance that your game can just end.

For example, I can be 2/3 through a level with 40 turns remaining and receive two vagaries that reduce the turns on the level by 20. You can also receive vagaries that increase the resource requirements to leave the stage beyond the resource availability on the stage.

There are ways to mitigate these issues by trading for resources, etc., but often times the game strikes a careful balance between resource consumption and production and you can be put in a position that is mathematically impossible to recover from. I think a simple change that prevents receiving a few of the vagaries more than once per stage could go a long way.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great concept with excellent narrative and art design that needs serious balancing. Although the challenge of a rogue-like is dealing with adversity, managing limited resources, and accepting the cruelties of fate, the starting conditions and available player choices feel too restrictive to form actual strategy.

Player choice is often illusory. You may have to decide between two paths, but one may require a resource that you cannot acquire with your starting tribe, or lead to a region you can't effectively exploit. Game units feel fragile, dysfunctional, and neurotic, depending on the random traits you are dealt. Many random disasters simply can't be avoided, mitigated, or insured against. If you're 10 turns from the goal on a well-designed resource engine, you could still pull an event that simply adds 20% to your target number, putting it out of reach.

The fail state of challenging is frustrating. In the future, I would like to play more scripted campaign or challenge modes, see some of the unit traits balanced, and see more choices on the main map to increase the sense of player agency.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wish I could put neutral on this, because I think this game has a lot of potential. At the moment though, some of the gameplay elements are frustrating and outright unfun.

Firstly, there are some negative traits so overwhelmingly bad that if you find them in the early game, you restart, if you find them later on, said characters are likely to find themselves in 'an unfortunate accident'.
Clumsy: Lose 5 hp every harvest cycle. Unless this character is standing still, that practically means that it slowly dies over the course of about 50-100 in-game turns. Which isn't a lot, given that there's about 160-200 turns in a single halt, of which there's approx 5 of in total. Healing this one character becomes so much of a hassle that it literally takes a dedicated druid to keep them alive. That's insanely inefficient.
Eternal beginner: Not as damming as the other one, but a lot of the later skills are insanely useful and necessary relatively early on in the game, so I'll usually restart if I see this in my first three characters but I'll live with it if a later character has this trait.

Quick term definition: Semi-murder; This doesn't quite kill your character, but one other bad event, and they'll likely be dead.

Secondly, there are some vageries (Random negative events) that are a bother:
Mosquitoes: Some squares basically semi-murders your characters now, and that means you'll have to micro-manage each and every character to make sure that each and every character traverses only the paths that don't have bugs on them. This takes a long time and can quickly be undone if one of the workers change their pathing due to a resource drying up.
Nap: This just cancels what everyone was doing. So that you have to recall and redo each and every command you'd been planning up until then. Just why?
Ovework: "everything that works semi-dies", aight then, I guess I have to manually select every character and ensure they don't move the turn before this happens. Starting to see a pattern with the negative vageries?

Some folks complained about shake and the other building damaging things, but they weren't a problem for me. Having a dedicated builder usually meant I could pop up stuff wherever in a hurry.

Finally, the one thing that annoyed me more than anything else: Accidentially spending vital resources and then losing because I no longer have that vital resource to be able to travel with. I'd love if there was a way to spend these resources prior to moving around so that I don't press a button and accidentially doom hours of gameplay. This accounts for about 50-75% of my losses.

All in all, the gameplay has a good foundation as it stand, but it's just too frustrating to be able to fully recommend it as of yet.\t\t\t\t\t

Drought, Wildfire, Thunder, Fire, Fire in your buildings, half the maps resources being burnt down, lose 20 turns towards the flood meter and be given only a single day left to gather up the 200+ wood you need to get out, whereas if that didn't happen you'd of survived and actually beaten the game.

And the CUSTOM GAME where you can TURN OFF EVENTS.

And/or pull a video of somebody winning, just know that the difference between me and them is the fact that I don't want to wait around for that perfect game. Games are meant to be played, enjoyed, and you're meant to get BETTER at them. Not flounder around until the game lets you win.
