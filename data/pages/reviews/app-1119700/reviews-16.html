\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeautiful graphics, nice and relaxing soundtrack and somewhat of an interesting game play loop. Sadly it just isn't that fun. The entire game revolves around RNG, and even the best thought out approaches will lead to your failure.

This definitely is not one of the "relax and chill" city/colony builders that you could find on Steam. If you are looking for that, steer clear of this. If you are looking for a game where you mess up 1 turn and then lose, this is the game for you.

The campaign was enjoyable for the most part, sometimes you mess up a few turns and lose the entire level and have to redo it but I guess that is what they were going for. Also, there are no save slots/save games. If you lose in the campaign, you start from the beginning of the chapter.

The character progression is interesting, the more your Pupil does in a certain skill the better they become. As they become better, unlock perks and become even better - rinse and repeat.

But there really isn't too much depth to the game. Build a base, survive the RNG then pack up and leave. Then do it all again at a new level with new RNG.

Your pupils eat a crazy amount of food, and you will run out very quickly unless you always have a dedicated food harvester/baker/etc.

So that leaves you with an average 2 more pupils to do everything else, except you're gonna need a builder to build everything... then a miner to mine and so forth. 90% of the game is just feeling as if you don't have enough pupils to do what you really need to.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love roguelike/x hybrids. In this case, the x is actually 3x (explore, expand, exploit). Behind the calm and peaceful surface appearance lurks a very hard, tightly balanced game, with a small but robust tech tree that provides multiple paths to victory, letting you adapt your strategy to the random world you're given.

The game gives the tense feeling of constantly being one mistake away from losing, while being lenient enough to allow a winrate close to 100%. This is not something seen often in roguelikes, but here it is appropriate, given that each run takes several hours to complete. As a result, playing this game will put you in a mood that is a nice mixture of relaxed alertness. The simple but very well made graphics enhance the depth of this experience.

There are some minor balance problems in the game: these mostly take the form of flavorful but underpowered mechanics, which you can mostly ignore. Luckily, this doesn't affect the core game mechanics which are clever and subtle, allowing for different styles of play and making you face a variety of challenges on your path to victory. Overall, the developers really succeeded in breaking new ground and making something unique, interesting, and beautiful.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've only played a small amount of this game thus far but I can tell it's going to become a favorite of mine for a long time to come.

The art direction is superb: color palettes and balance, animation, character design, environments, etc. Just all to die for. And, along with that, the sound design and music is also top notch. It's a pleasure to experience.

Also, the lore of the world is so, hmn, gentle? Not sure how else to describe it. Like, the danger the Pupils face is imminent and defeats can and do happen when the water reaches the caravan or starvation comes upon them or any number of other things. But the heart of their history, their culture, and the way they view and interact with the world has a softness to it that's so calm and pleasant.

I only have a few complaints so far. Namely, in the Campaign stages, around and after the stage where farming is introduced, I feel like there needs to be some tweaks made to make it smoother (especially for those playing that are newer to the genre). There's a tightness to the timing that can be off-putting. There's an efficient order of operations that needs to happen, to optimize the potential outcomes and, to a veteran of the genre, it's clear what needs to happen, in what order, to best avoid stagnation that can lead to starvation or other failures. But I don't think that that efficiency will be as obvious as it needs to be, for people coming to the game as people newer to the genre.

Like, the Campaign is the tutorial with story laid down over it. It's a beefier tutorial than you might expect for something not titled explicitly as "tutorial". And that hybridization of story mode and tutorial may be muddying things a bit. If it's meant to be a story-driven tutorial, then it definitely needs tweaks to make it gentler for people learning the game. If it's meant to be a Campaign with story, but beyond tutorial, then that steeper jump in difficulty/management makes more sense. But it seems to be aiming to be the former more than the latter, what with how actions are halted with tooltips and the like that walk you through how the game is played. So there's a bit of jank there that probably needs to be addressed a bit better.

Anyway. That's really my only complaint with the game thus far. Everything else has been a great experience and I'm very glad I stumbled upon this when looking for games similar to others I have but wasn't in the mood to play. And even with the issue I feel are present in the Campaign mode currently, I definitely recommend picking this title up. It's truly great.\t\t\t\t\t

EDIT After months of waiting:
I'm changing my review from positive to negative because developer fails to iron out the issues I outlined before. They seemed to have abandoned this game and no longer bother to improve this game. Without additional fix, this is an unfinished game and does not deserve my recommendation.

The bottom line: If Developers can iron out issues with game balance, this game is worthwhile for any rogue-like and/or city management fans out there.

I don't want to repeat other review's comment about aesthetic aspect of the game. They are great but UI has quite a few issues. There are many other quality of life changes the game can use either, but the biggest problem really falls on game balance. However if devs can listen to community feedbacks and fix these issues in the next few patches, this game will be in a very good spot. I'm even looking forward to the DLC already.

A few tips for those who are struggling:
1. Like another comment said, rush Council as soon as possible. You can reduce your ration cost by 6 per turn by purchasing the One For All Council option (bottom left), it will significantly help you.
2. Consider dedicate a Pupil to a Harvest Hut and upgrade it. The upgrade reduce harvest cycle by a turn, which is roughly a 33% increase in efficiency. This can help a lot to alleviate early game food pressure.
3. Try roll up a Pupil with Agile trait if you are really struggling with the game. -1 Turn to any harvest activity is simply too powerful.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is nice and challenging. Haven't yet finished any expert scenarios yet, only all of the basic ones with all the tribes and of course the "campaign" (actually, it is just a tutorial).

The tutorial does not explain all of the mechanics, but you will learn most of them. The rest have to be explored by paying attention to the interface (click on all buttons and read all tooltips on them, buildings especially), the council stuff and caravan upgrades especially, which is fine, and reading random tips on the global map, which is a weird place to learn things that can be important, but whatever.

Despite what some of the reviews say, in my opinion, the GUI is good and easy to use. There are enough tooltips that explain what everything does, selecting what you want is most of the time easy and to highlight resources you can leftclick on them in your storage (top left). But there doesn't seem to be a way to highlight all of them and quickly finding sacred sights and remains is hard since those can not be highlighted. But these are mostly minor problems.

Again, despite what many reviews say, the council building (and its upgrades) have not been necessary for me to finish most of the basic scenarios. Many vagaries (random bad events) can be safely ignored or their effects circumvented. There is a way to play by highly relying on the council building, but it is not the only one.

The harmony score that can be seen at the end of a scenario seems to reflect how much resources you have gathered and wasted (symbolizing taking what is not necessary for survival and completion of the journey) and probably increases the frequency of vagaries. So if you are mostly working directly for the objective (which you should anyway, since the game is about optimization) you will not have to worry much about the harmony score and face less bad random stuff. Probably.

Random generation could be improved, since some things just don't make sense (auras that protect ore in an area when there is none or vagaries that affect jungles that may not be on the map at all).

All in all, game is nice, clean, cozy (despite the challenging nature), allows for multiple strategies to achieve objectives, presents challenges that require adaptation (depending on the difficulty of the scenario) to win and has random generation for maps, scenarios and other things which provide replayability. A fresh idea and good execution.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall this is an adorable roguelite centered on resource & time management and character specialization. The general concept of the game being that you have X turns on the map until you lose and need Y materials to continue traveling. So you send your shapeshifter animals to various resource node to gather/harvest/mine, construct buildings, try to level your people up in professions, and save up enough resources to go to another map. Rinse and repeat until you reach the last spot on the map. This is broken up by major and minor vagaries... which are RNG bad stuff like "after 5 turns, anyone working as a cook will get ill" or "lighting strikes three spots" which sets fires & harms buildings. The major issues with the game come from randomized map resources and not being able to plan far enough ahead sometimes.... which might make you feel like you were set up for failure from the start. You may think a spot will be good to travel to but then find out none of the resources you need are there or there's only one forest but you need the wood to cook so you don't starve. And so on. But its a roguelite.... so you lose, maybe unlock some more traits, and try again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs far as the eye is a calm and relaxing resource managemant game at its core - its bright colours, tranquil soundtrack, and mostly whimsical graphical style. Its actual mechanics contradict those statements however, as it actually is a very hectic, optimization based game where your every waking moment is one knife's edge away from certain death.

The game incorporates a karma system to force you to play as it wishes you, stand against that mechanic and you'll find yourself facing disasters after disaster until your run is absolutely ruined - that's also where it's at its weakest, however.

Probably the main problem is design choices clashing hard with gameplay: The relaxing resource management doesn't sit well with hectic risk mitigation, especially if that risk is losing a 4-8 hours long run - as it is, As Far as the Eye is hard to recommend.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter I played through the campaign (which was basically an extended tutorial) I found this game to be a joyous and very positive experience. I was really looking forward to playing the main game. Imagine my massive disappointment then when the actual sandbox game experience proved to be anything but joyous. It was horrible.

The game has serious balance and "fun" issues. It's all down to a mechanic called the "Vagaries". Completely absent from the campaign, these are random negative effects that occur if your "Harmony" (a stat that is hidden from you!!) on a given map gets too low. So while the game strongly encourages you extract as much resource and experience for your workers as possible from each map you halt at, it punishes you very hard for trying to do so.

Vagaries come thick and fast after a while, with the next one arriving while you are still coming to terms with the last. Literally only two or three turns apart! This is not fun at all, and ruins the game by putting a massive hidden clock on everything you do (over and above the actual visible clock of the incoming flood). These random effects are never fun to deal with and are simply frustrating and annoying at best, or run-ending at worst. Very bad game design here.

The game does allow custom setups for your runs, but as far as the Vagaries go, the choice is either on or off. Turn them off and the game becomes pointlessly easy, but turn them on and it becomes frustratingly unplayable. There is no happy medium where you can simply turn them down a bit. The game desperately needs that.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInteresting game. But once you figure out what to do it becomes easy to win any game. Though there are a lot of aspects to the game that can ruin your run even if you thought it was in your grasp. Like missing some vagaries etc. Knowing that you can remove some vagaries might help. But you gotta explore the game and know what tools are at your disposal to make it more favorable for you.
SPOILER ALERT. TIPS to help you win. If you happen to lose all the time, try figuring out how to support your pupils need. 1 cook, one fish/meat trapper - this should be enough to feed 5-7 pupils. Also try to reach lvl 3 gatherer with all pupils. That helps reduce food consumption. So the rest of the pupils can gather resources required to reach next halt. Also don't instantly go to next halt if you have already met the requirements. Check what is needed for the next halt and try to gather some (or all resources if possible). That way you have more options to go around if new halt doesn't have enough resources to go to the next one.\t\t\t\t\t
