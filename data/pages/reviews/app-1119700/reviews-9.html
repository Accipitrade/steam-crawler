\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am very glad I picked this up. It's unlike anything else I've ever played before, drawing elements from a variety of genres with resource-gathering and city-building components without being especially beholden to them. But I think it is a game that really requires you to be in the right mindset to play it, or to have somebody keeping you company while you play, if only because answering their questions will help you more fully process what you're doing.

Above all else, As Far As The Eye emphasizes flexibility. You have limited numbers of workers, so even if some of them are specialized, you'll often have to switch their roles. You can place a fixed structure, but invest some extra resources and you'll have one you can reposition when the nearby tiles dry up. Stockpiling certain resources can be wise, but often it's best to grab what you need when you need it, and use what you have as soon as you have it. I like these aspects of the game a lot! It's easy to have the sunk cost fallacy hit hard in city-building games; A FATE seems specifically designed to counter that.

There are also decidedly unforgiving aspects to the game. Having to avoid interrupting workers lest you make them drop (and thereby destroy) their resources is just rough. The UI doesn't do a great job communicating... well, most things, to be honest (it's unpolished and a little overwhelming to look at, with lots of tiny tiny type). And it's especially easy to miss when second-tier workers, waiting for materials to process into goods, are standing around doing nothing, because there's no good visual cue for this.

And yet, when I reeeeeally slowed down, carefully studied the map and my workers, and played the game in good company, it really started to sing. This is a game that's putting serious thought into how its mechanics ask you to relate to the game world, and if you pick up its train of thought, it really is something special. I just wish it did a better job inviting you aboard.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very nice, enjoyable game with relaxing music and cute art style.
The gameplay is simple but offers a lot of options for upgrades and specialisation and the rng makes every run unique with different resource requirements and obstacles encountered (including pupil traits) which keeps it interesting.

I do see a decent number of people complaining that the game is frustrating and too rng-dependent. While it is true that a lot of things depend on rng and a few unlucky conditions can sometimes ruin a very promising game, most of those can be mitigated/prepared for if you manage your resources properly (which the whole game is about in the end).
I have encountered a few instances where I couldn't get past a certain point in the game and got a little frustrated but usually some practice and/or change of strategy is enough to overcome the obstacles, especially if you have a little luck to go with it.

All in all, it's a very enjoyable game if you like time and resource management and have a little patience to look at what you have and what you need, and plan ahead. It is not overly complex but provides a decent challenge, especially in longer runs.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a beautifully designed game that is surprisingly difficult. You manage a handful of pawns with the goal to help them survive the coming disaster, and as you'd expect survival requires adaptability as well as preparation for the unknown. If you're not into strategy or civilization type resource management games, this one probably isn't for you. There is a lot of random qualities to the game, but once you learn what the random events are you can usually avoid or mitigate most of the negatives of them. That being said, the game is pretty unforgiving if you make a lot of mistakes or don't prepare properly. There is also the occasional nearly impossible scenario, which can be frustrating but I've also learned a lot from those runs.

Overall, I love the game. Completing a run successfully is very rewarding, and the game flexes different strategic muscles than I'm used to.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a fun enough game so far, however if you are an impatient sort of person maybe not the game for you. I wish there was a "maybe" recommendation because whilst I've hit the checkmark in the "yes" box I shall now proceed to complain about the game.

I've failed to get through the second portion one of the tutorial levels a few times now, and there is no saving so each time I fail I have to go through the first part before I can try the second part again. This seems like a flawed design.

However, what irritates me most about this game is the English. It is either poorly written or poorly translated from another language. I do have a sense that it is probably poorly translated based on the sorts of grammatical errors as well as inconsistencies between English and American spellings - pick one or the other and stick with it. It is a great shame to be let down on the writing so badly when the world building relies so heavily on said writing. It is tiring to figure out what they meant to say. There has clearly been a lot of love and care put into this game, and so why they wouldn't simply have a native English speaker proof-read all of the dialogue and instructions before release is beyond me.\t\t\t\t\t
