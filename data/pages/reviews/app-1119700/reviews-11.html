\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was surprised at how much I like this game. It reminds me of kind of a mix between Rimworld, Age of Mythology, and Civ. It takes some time to get used to the mechanics, but once you do, the game is very enjoyable.

You start with a small tribe and you get to decide what they'll work on to guide their career advancement. Each campaign is a journey to the eye broken down into small halts, or maps. I really like that you can unlock techs with knowledge, keep your pupil's skills, and build mobile buildings to give yourself a leg up in the later game halts.

The major problem I had on my first 2 campaigns is that I would get to the last mission before the eye and deplete all of the resources before I could get to the eye and either my Pupil's would starve, or the flood would come. I found that the longer campaign feels like you have more control as you have more missions to accumulate techs, buildings, and skills. I also found it helps to look at the map and highlight the future paths. I try to start gathering the resources needed for the next round before it even starts. Also the size of each Halt matters. The larger the Halt the more resources there are. I try to avoid small maps at all costs.

This game is honestly very enjoyable and extremely cute to see your pupil's transform into different animals when they become different professions. I really like the mystical feel of the game and the ability to improve and build up making future halts easier.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter many hours of enjoyable gameplay, "roguelike turn-based resource-management game" is definitely an accurate description. However, there's a lot to break down there. In the abstract, you control workers — called Pupils — to exploit resources while progressing through sections — called halts — to ultimately reach a finish line, called The Eye. Each halt requires a variable amount of resources to be gathered within a time frame. The challenge lies in optimizing decisions while random events — called vagaries — can throw a wrench in your plans. Planning ahead is key, which takes gaining familiarity with the many available options.

As expected in a roguelike, elements of randomness and uncertainty are present. These primarily come in the form of required resources to move past a halt, random vagaries and the halt's layout. Gathering a required resource is trivial earlier in a run, but your decisions at the start can greatly affect your ability to handle other challenges closer to the end.

A strategy will always be built — at least partially — around survival because Pupils require food upkeep. It's great you got that iron ore (or whatever resource) you needed, but it's not so great when you realize everyone is going to starve. Unfortunately, a run that's about to fail will likely be unavoidable unless you have a lot of things already in place to recover. This often led me to prematurely end a run. There isn't a "push your luck" factor because there is minimal permanent progression. Aside from the tutorial campaign, there are eight scenarios to unlock with minor variations. Completing a scenario also unlocks traits for Pupils.

In a single run, progression comes in the form of upgrades and skill trees. Pupils can work any role, but the more they do in a given field, the more proficient they become. For example, a gatherer can become a woodcutter who will gather wood more efficiently than a Pupil that has no experience in gathering. Specializing Pupils is a very satisfying part of the strategy and synergy.

A complaint I've seen in other reviews is that a resource will appear to be inadequately available on a halt. I have learned about ways to mitigate this the more I've played. The council building has upgrades that allow you to block negative effects from vagaries and plan further ahead for each halt. This information could greatly alter which path to take and which resources to focus on. There is also the market building which allows for trading one resource for another at a loss.

Overall, I found the learning curve and difficulty to be reasonable for anyone familiar strategy and resource management. The game does fall short as there is no way to plan ahead certain building upgrades without already knowing what they are and their cost. For example, if you build a mine, you will know what its possible upgrades and associated costs are only after it's built. As far as I can tell, there is no in-game method to open a UI to look ahead at a building's possible upgrades (i.e. Civilopedia in Civ). It is slightly frustrating needing to remember info — that's readily available in many other games — in a game where planning ahead is really important.

The UI can also be a little frustrating at times. Giving Pupils orders as it cycles through them can often lead to accidentally assigning an already-assigned Pupil. Additionally, it's hard to know a Pupil's specialty at a glance without opening a separate information panel or hovering over icons. These images are arbitrary and will only be recognized after lots of runs. Even after my playtime, I am not always sure which icon is which at first glance. Resources can also be hard to spot on the map. You can always check via tooltip by hovering over a hex, but I found myself overlooking resources, such as stone on a mountain. As far as I know, there is no way to simplify the map view so that resources are easier to spot (i.e. strategic view in Civ).

The art style is very pleasing, and it's one of the factors that immediately drew me in to learn more about the game. Like with the resources, it visually can be hard to separate certain things at glance. The music is very relaxing. Even though it's a game about survival, the soundtrack is very calming and fits nicely with the game's aesthetics.

Strategy-game fans who decide to pick up As Far As The Eye are likely to be thoroughly entertained.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game premise is beautiful and I love the intro cinematic. It seems like a rewarding challenge! I wanted to love this game. I took my time with it, I didn't give up like some reviewers did after losing on the first tutorial.
I came to prove all the negative reviews wrong.

I was wrong.

The problem with this game is you lose in your first few turns, but you won't actually feel that result until you play 50-100 more turns, possibly even going through Halts (which are NOT checkpoints). By the time you do feel the ramifications, there's NO chance to recover due to the nature of the turn limit and you must just lose/quit. This makes you feel like you are wasting your time.

The game is challenging, yes, and planning is required. But after you lose several times in a row without feeling a tangible reason, you start to feel like every move is not enough. You can feel efficient for 30 turns squeezing every bit out of your turns...and suddenly you are starving somehow and everyone will die before you can even react to that. So you adjust and stock a lot of food next play...but now you have none of the actual resources you need to progress, so you adjust again. 10 turns later you are starving again. Some call this "challenging" but really it's more like a huge chore.

Alas, even after you finally get the "right solution" to the "puzzle" you don't feel like you solved anything meaningful, just that you got the game to "work" so you might finally begin to play a game that actually isn't there.

How about you actually GIVE him some useless or niche skill, but something that goes with what you told me in the dialogue, not just "he forgot how to do X now from reading something on the wall".\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd rate this like a 7/10 maybe 6/10 but I gave it extra credit for the beautiful art style and interesting concept.

The sound. It's just not immersive. I know that it sounds weird to say; But other games really put quite a bit of energy into sound and storytelling. This game was a total miss. Lots of text lots of story; and it's hard to follow along with; and the characters seem a bit lifeless and detached from the narrative. It's a bummer cause just a small update could fix this pretty quickly. But as it stands. I felt really not immersed even though the world would seemingly be very complete judging this book by the cover.

Secondly. It looks sorta like a Civ game or a builder game - I'd say that it's not. Its a puzzle game that looks like a builder game. It moves pretty quickly and obviously that's the point. If you like kinda quick thinking race to the end kinda style games then this is for you. But for me I like to take it slow and max things out and build stuff and explore and expand. This game didn't really scratch that itch for me.

All in all. I think I paid something like 25 dollars for this game. :/ I played it 5 hours. and lost interest. I'd say it's an okay value for the money. Maybe something in the future i'll pick up again. But i'll say it again. This is a puzzle game. If you like puzzles, you'll love this, it's challenging and sometimes frustratingly challenging. Every stage is slightly different and comes with different art styles as you travel from map to map. It's very cute, very well designed and very interesting in concept. It's actually a game that has a lot of potential.

The story is interesting in concept. But it's short. It feels a bit faceless, especially with no voices or main characters. I sorta didn't fall in love with my little worker sprites the way I do with other games... so. It just lacks personality in the places that count. And unfortunately for me... that's where this game took its greatest hit.
