\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is cute, really cute. The art design is adorable, Your worker units in this game, called Pupils, have such great character to them, which is only bolstered by the sweet written moments in the campaign. I want every one of their polymorph forms as a plushie. As for the writing, there isn't a lot but what is there paints the deep lore of this game. Gameplay is tight, keeping things engaging although able to be pretty forgiving in the earlier difficulties.

This plays like a 4X game with roguelite elements. Namely that there's no going back on your decisions in a Journey (the in-game name for these "runs") and that death of your Pupils is permanent. Every Journey is a series of individual levels or stages called "Halts" wherein you have to collect the necessary resources to progress to the next stage. These resource requirements tend to be somewhat random, but not impossible. Often this game demands a great deal of optimization and resource management to make it through these challenges.

It's as if you were required to play the first 20 turns of Civ or Humankind or Endless Legend, optimizing just those first 20 turns before you start the match all over again on a different map (which lets be honest is how some of those games go). Resources, particular buildings, and Pupils are carried through to the next Halt, enabling large payoffs from good optimization early in a Journey. Personally. I really like this design and I think it's an interesting take on both the rougelite and 4X genre.

There are a couple small quality of life changes I'd appreciate, namely in regards to having some sort of shortcut to automatically select the best Pupil for a particular task rather than having to tab through each one to check. Also at least for me, the ASMR whispering voices make me anxious (as does all ASMR in general, so this isn't an issue with the quality of the ASMR in this game). I wish there was an option to disable only those sounds although turning down "Sound Effects" helps. This leaves me feeling that I'm still missing some of the rest of the atmosphere though so I often just suffer through the anxiety.

There's often an urge with game journalists and reviewers to call good indie games "indie gems" and I feel as though that term gets used way to often for it to have any meaning. But I'd genuinely consider this game a gem. It's a relatively small game, but leaves me with an incredible impression.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great little resource gathering and management game with adorable style. It is also deceptively difficult. Don't load this up, listening to the calm music and whispered ASMR gibberish of the creatures, thinking you're going to have a relaxing time. You're often going to be painstakingly plotting each turn and fighting to survive.

High Point: The wonderful design and smooth experience. I love the little world they created in this game. It's colorful and vibrant and really feels alive. The randomness of each time you load up a new game (between the map layouts, character traits, resource requirements, pathing, etc) keeps things from getting overly procedural.

Low Point: I would honestly say that the food requirements for the "pupils" you lead are...perhaps a bit too demanding. There are so many potential things available in the game to focus on, but I felt for a lot of my runs, I spent so much time trying to keep my little peeps fed that I couldn't enjoy expanding them out into all the varied classes and traits.

I literally just finished getting the 100% for achievements (which are annoyingly not detailed), so I think I'm about to hang this up for good. Over time, you do figure out the tricks and it gets more intuitive. Just be prepared for a steep learning curve. For anyone just starting out and struggling, I'll just say: Don't sleep on pepkins. Harvest Hut + Fruit Gatherer will get you to the end most runs. And the Council building will be your best friend, don't put off building one. ;P Good luck, and see you at the Eye!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this game, the art style is beautiful and I like the zen-ness of it, but the unwinnable scenarios are pretty frustrating. I am really good at these kinds of games too. I played through the tutorials, easy enough. I read a bunch of stuff about people saying how you just need to prepare properly and what not, and that's fine, so I did. I got through all the Vargies that lowered how many turns I had and wrecked all my buildings. Felt pretty confident. My final Halt (The areas you stop and play in) had 10, freaking, 10 Aura's on it. Most of them interlinked with one another. For those of you who don't know an Aura is something you have to send a Druid to calm down so that your other Pupils can harvest resources in that area. The only issue is I have one druid, and by the time I calmed down two Auras in an area and started working on a third aura in the area, the first would kick back up and prevent anything from being harvested. That was just for one small spot of the map, not even mentioning the other seven. There was literally nothing I could do. No amount of preparation of resources could get through this wall. You would need several druids to clear the map, and the game only allows you 3 pupils at start, and two that you can get through other caravans. Even if I had all five pupils from that scenario, I would have had to have them all constantly calming the aura's while having one harvest food, simply so we didn't starve, let alone gather the resources to move on from the flood.

Just sort of bad RNG. I feel like if the game had like, a limit to how many auras can spawn on a map or something, and how close to one another, this unbeatable wall would have been unavoidable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tif your favorite part of resource management / builder games is starting a new game, then this is perfect! it's like a cuddly cross between Frostpunk and Niche, but with added layers of meta-progression inspired by the roguelike genre. it's definitely an acquired taste for sure, like scotch or anchovies. if you're broken like me though, then this is a real gem!

some notes on usability:
--navigating around the menus can be cumbersome at times, and most failure states don't have an alert--e.g. when a worker is stuck waiting on resources, or you're running dangerously low on food.
--coming from games like Cities Skylines and Oxygen Not Included, I found AFATE's lack of graphs distressing. besides being a lot of fun (i know, i'm broken), the data viz in those games helped me explore the complex systems and learn how they work.
--the game is overly reliant on diegetic tells. i love the attention to detail, but without adequate overt messaging to back it up, it took me nearly 4 hours to notice that, for example, the balloons attached to the characters change color to indicate their current status.
--while beautiful, the game is not super legible. i often struggled to see where my workers were and which workers were which, or tell the difference between jungle and forest. (again, some data viz would be great here--really miss the color-coded overlays available in most games like this that let you see the status of different units, resources, spaces, etc at a glance!)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA very tough resource-management rogue-like, which does not correlate with the cutesy visuals and zen lore. I concur with others that it's FTL meets Civ, but doesn't quite hit the mark.

I love the idea of a civilization game where your "city" must keep moving, gathering the resources as it goes, else be sweeped up in a tsunami.

There are a plethora of resources (pepkins, wood, stone, ore, wool, fish, fine fish, spices), and each worker gains experience in one of the 19 professions depending on what they're collecting or doing. So you're urged to specialise your workers to maximise their resource collection. However, starting with only 3 workers, you frequently need to get your workers to specialise in 2 or 3 professions. And the only way to get more workers is to find them in random events.

So the key gameplay is prioritising the resources you NEED. But beware, if you take too much resources (empty a tile, or leave some at a previous node), you anger mother nature, who will cause catastrophic events to occur more frequently.

The game could have done with more polish, such as making more defined graphics to more easily see tiles, clarifying a lot of the hover-information, and making a clearer turn synopsis text box.

In a "games are art" way, this game can be construed as a commentary on climate change, where it shows just how hard we would have to work to be "one with nature" to reduce carbon emissions.

This game did not provide the "just one more turn" kind of gameplay I was after. I guess I'll keep an eye out for a Mortal Engines mod for Civ 6.\t\t\t\t\t
