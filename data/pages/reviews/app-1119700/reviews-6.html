\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wouldn't recommend this game to anyone that doesn't enjoy micromanagement, hyper-efficiency, and sheer-random luck in your game draw. This game is like a mesh of Stellaris (without the automation for resources), FTL (random survival journey to get to the end of the line), and Binding of Isaac (random starts, paths, traits, and misfortunes). The only downfall is it doesn't have the one thing that makes those games a reward to play: planning for the downfall you know is coming.

The basic game is starting a path with 3 Pupils that have random positive and negative traits that can help or hinder your progress. That's fine. What the real kicker is the resource management. Naturally, one knows that not every resource is going to be available all the time, and you're given things like building a Market or Council to help with resources or with avoiding some of the harsher misfortunes (or Vagaries) that will hit your tribe.

And they WILL hit.

You up your chances for misfortunes or negative effects when you deplete too many resources (or "more than you need" according to the game), but you need those resources to either build your buildings EACH time you make a stop at a new location (because Mobile buildings are expensive, but worth it if you put the time into it), or because you need to sell/trade those resources when you're at a stop that doesn't have things like stone or ore, or in one instance, trees for wood (I immediately reset that game, wasn't even worth trying when it's the FIRST stop and you NEED wood to build anything).

After that, the things like auras and ritual sites or "remains" and some such, hit you more often with negative effects than helpful ones. Things like: you start a game and its requirement is 2 Pupils, but you searched a ruin without first analyzing it, so you didn't know it was going to take 1 Pupil from you. Welp. You can suffer through that, but I don't, and reset. Ruins and Auras are more trouble than fun to interact with, as unless you have an Expert Druid (which you won't have until 3-4 stops in, IF you dedicated 1 Pupil to soothing auras and harvesting medicine extremely early on, which, why would you, that's expensive), and that Expert takes 2 turns to sooth it, UNLESS you remembered to take the trait to permanently sooth auras late in their tree.

Granted, the game does have some enjoyment, but again, that's mostly when you get lucky, don't have 6 misfortunes 10 turns each apart, and remembered to focus your Pupils to their dedicated trades (and not accidentally mix them up because there is NO automation for resource gathering) and remember to check on them so you don't forget to assign them jobs when they run out of resources (because pressing the spacebar to finish a turn doesn't trigger the same warning box as clicking the turn button does to tell you that a Pupil isn't working).

Finally, I think my last dislike of the game is the fact that, for some reason, after assigning Pupils build requests or movement requests, or any request to do something, sometimes they just... won't? They'll stop halfway through their action and sit, pondering why life is so hard, and unless you check on them because you're accustomed to the spacebar tactic, then... well. Good luck?

The graphics are pretty, the sound effects and music are interesting and do help with setting up an atmosphere whlie you're playing. Ultimately, the frustration of trying to get to the end, only to have a misfortune tell you "well, all your mobile buildings take 80 damage" and there's not enough resources in your storage OR on the tiles you're on to fix it -- kills it for me.

By all means, give the game a chance. I bought it while it was on sale, and I would recommend you do the same.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t[24h at the time of writing] As Far As The Eye is an interesting take on the resource optimization genre mixed with Civilization hex optimization and logistics. Challenging and rewarding while learning the game fulfills my personal desires for a video game.

Everything is scarce and I managed to fail the easiest journey several times before getting my first victory. However, every run I learnt something interesting and often could only blame myself for the mistakes. Many of the reviews harp on the game being unfair - I do not share this opinion. Like in many deeper resource management games the point of failure is NOT the point of error. Neither should losing count as negative, but as a learning experience. If one attributes a lost game to the last event that tipped you over the edge you fail to improve on what lead you to such a teetering position in the first place. Long term planning and a balance of greed while knowing when its time to run is the key.

Each of the offerred tribes come with their unique challenges and I found myself adjusting my strategy heavily from previous run to win with the harder difficulties.

If you enjoy resource management / rogue like / city builders the game is well worth the 9.99$ that I paid on sale. Thumbs up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSaw the reviews; bought it anyway. Glad I did, but also understand why this game doesn't have universal appeal to some kinds of players.

TL;DR up front: If you like Reus, Faster Than Light, and Monopoly, you'll probably like this game. If you like to reload when you get a really bad dice roll, or keep reloading to reopen the same chest until you get a legendary weapon, or keep playing until your characters become godly and overpowered, you probably will not.

There's no saving in the campaign at all (the campaign is sort of like... five short difficult puzzles based around late game mechanics, you can clear the whole thing in an hour if you know what you're doing, otherwise it takes about 3-5 hours). The campaign does not have "save and exit", and you're going to die more than once in the campaign. Also, the campaign is not very fun. Be prepared for these realities. The "Campaign" does teach some later level techniques, but the regular game also has guidance on how to move and generally what to do. If the campaign gets frustrating, skip it entirely and come back to it later. From the reviews it's clear that a lot of people never made it to the actual game and just got frustrated with the campaign.

In the regular game you must save when you exit, it saves automatically every turn, and there's only 1 save file. The game is made to be played and finished. This makes it play much like Faster Than Light or Slay the Spire; in 2 hours you either made it to the end of the round or died, so savescumming would be silly, yet the other most popular complaint people seem to have is the lack of multiple save files and ability to reload a couple turns back. You're not meant to play until your group becomes overpowered, and everything doesn't have to be perfect... you're just trying to get to the eye before the world floods, while leaving enough behind for those that come after you.

Like Reus, the game is beautiful. It feels like you're lost in a Studio Ghibli film. Like Reus, there's no epic saga where you play the same squad for months and months and get super buff, there's no building a massive base with 10 million of everything, in fact there's even a game mechanic to punish you for taking more than you need from the land. Every "cycle" (aka every game over) has the potential to unlock some new content, but you will always be walking that narrow path of "don't run out of food", "don't take too much food", and "get moving to the next map before the flood comes". Zero "late game domination" occurs.

Like Faster than Light, the game is a *little* bit forgiving, but not very, and there's some randomization that can work against you. If you screw up badly enough over the course of 4-5 turns, or get a really rocky start, or pick too hard a path too early on, or decide to open a risk/reward chest and get a bad enough punishment, you'll probably find yourself dying unless you're really good at pulling rabbits (and food/medicine) out of your hat. Sometimes things just don't work out the way you planned. You can screw up and forget to give a unit orders, or send them somewhere to explore and find nothing, and still be totally fine, but if you run completely out of food because you weren't paying attention, you may not be able to get things back on track quickly enough before everyone starves.

Like Monopoly (assuming you play 4+ players), you're probably going to lose more times than you're going to win, especially early on, and sometimes it will just feel like you only lost because luck was against you and you kept landing on tax squares and couldn't get all the railroads like you hoped. Better players will usually do better, and you'll reach a point where "bad luck" can be almost entirely avoided with good decisions and caution, but you'll need to get better at knowing what's coming up ahead and when to be cautious and what potential risks may be encountered, which involves losing and learning. And even if you win, no matter how rich you might have been, next time you play you will start with nothing. If you expect to win every time you sit down to play, even as a beginner, you're probably going to get frustrated. If you're OK losing and setting up the board and playing again, the game is a delight.

People who find these games frustrating will probably find As Far As The Eye frustrating.

People who like these games will probably like As Far As The Eye.

I, for one, love this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat do I like
It has gorgeous graphics and a nice gameplay loop. I like the fact that you have to think ahead and plan out your route and plan your resources. But that's about it...

What do I not like?
The game feels unbalanced. I Could not even finish the campaign without failing many times. I mean gathering food is so slow that if you get 1 more pupil, you are already lacking in food. Even when I had 1 gatherer and 1 baker and a wood gatherer (needed for the bakery) I barely had enough food to keep up the 3 pupils. That was in the campaign.... where you almost have no debuffs. I couldn't even gather all the other resources I needed without the pupils starving to death. Not untill I got the 4th pupil, an expert baker in the campaign, could i finish the first halt. But boy was that the worst decision I could make, cause in the next halt I couldn't get enough food to even maintain the 4 pupils I had. So yea, one could argue that I suck in the game. Or that the game is just unforgiving if I can't even keep 3 pupils alive even if I have put them all on food gathering/creation duty.

Also there are a few questionable choices regarding difficulty... Like all pupils above the amount of 3 need a camp or else they won't do anything. And if you come across a halt without a rhino thing, you can't build a camp. This means you have 4 pupils that need food, but 1 pupil that doesn't do anything without a camp. Feels a bit like a forced difficulty. If you need to survive, who cares if you have a camp... You will get one if you contribute, not when everyone is dead because there is no resource to create a camp for you. In the next runs I made sure not to have more than 3 pupils.

After this campaign mission, I felt like trying a custom game. When I started noticing the complaints of some people in the other negative reviews, I decided to refund the game and not even go beyond the treshold of no return for refunds. I feel that the learning curve for this game is just way too steep and you need lots of hours and luck to win some runs. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tOvertuned. Badly.

The core gameplay loop is fun, but gains are small and the Vagaries (bad things that happen randomly) are both entirely too frequent and often border on crippling. Additionally, the "balanced" starting Halt is often anything but, and frequently spawns with only one or two tiles with a given resource available at all, which makes the Market more necessary which in turn makes the Vagaries that damage buildings even more punishing. I'm enjoying the game, but it's obviously tuned around someone who has 100+ hours and extensive experience to be able to complete a journey to the Eye on the default game mode *sometimes*, which is obscene.

Adding to the frustration, the game frequently changes the focused Pupil without reason or warning, and often a Pupil will either ignore a command or accept it and simply not follow through on the next turn. This also applies to the Caravan pre-deployment on a Halt, sometimes the Caravan will just... not move on a Turn. Given the incredibly tight scheduling required to succeed with any consistency, it would be generous not to call these bugs game-breaking.

To add insult to injury, completing a Journey To The Eye unlocks new traits, good, bad, and "neutral" (many of the Neutral traits are either nearly useless or more bad than good, absolutely none are generally positive) and as a result, later journeys to the Eye are even less likely to succeed. After two successful Journeys I've found myself rerolling more starts than not due to the punishing bad Traits or simply getting extremely unfavorable Jobs with my opening Pupils.

I paid $10 for the game, and I feel like that was fair, but if I had paid full price I'd be livid, especially with the game getting progressively harder and less fun as time goes on. The game is absolutely gorgeous, even on lower settings, but I cannot in good faith recommend that anyone buy it because after a few enjoyable experiences it becomes a repetitive exercise in failure. Again, the core mechanics are good and custom games are still enjoyable, but the developers have a lot of tuning to do before I feel like I'm doing anything but wasting my time managing Pupils into unavoidable death spirals.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm not really one for writing reviews but I saw this game has mixed reviews recently and thought I should help improve that metric.

The tutorial is not a fully scripted walkthrough, and the game does allow you to fail. Failure is an important part of this game. One the surface, it's a cute 3-X game (there aren't really enemies to exterminate, so I can't justify calling it a 4-X game) as described in the game's own description.

But beneath that surface is an incredibly challenging resource management game that rewards thinking several moves ahead and exploring the options the game offers but doesn't explicitly tell you to explore. It will be frustrating, there will be a lot of failure, and sometimes you'll hit dead ends mid way through a run that felt really solid. If you want to be able to win every rogue-like run you start, this is not the game for you. If you enjoy working out strategies over multiple iterations, you may enjoy this game.

In fairness, I think my biggest pet peeve with the game is the trait system. When you start each run your characters are given X number of random traits. Some of the negative traits are truly debilitating to the point where I will immediately abandon a run and start a new one if a character receives any of a few particularly nasty ones. The idea is to be able to adapt and accommodate those negative traits, but some of them present what amount to insurmountable challenges, especially at the start of a run. Thankfully the game doesn't really punish you for just restarting, and you can play custom games where you can remove negative traits altogether if they become too annoying.

Beyond the gameplay, I personally found the theme and conceit of the game to be really great. The "campaign" (tutorial) provides just enough background on this little world you're playing in to allow you some attachment to the success of your characters. The lore of this game, while limited, is actually really neat, and fits quite seamlessly with the actual gameplay mechanics. I'm also a big fan of the fact that excess resources gathered and left behind count against your final score.

Also, for anyone who enjoys this game and is reading this for some reason, check out Slipstream, another rogue-like resource management game I've really enjoyed.\t\t\t\t\t

However, after completing all the short starts, at the core I just don't feel like its fun.
My "x trait" unlocked steam achievments show under 10% of the playerbase have them, so around 90% of people probably never finished a single run, which would take 2-3 hours at most.
Since planning ahead is the key to sucess, when you lose, its often hard to figure out what could you have done differently. So after figuring it out everything gets better? Well no.

Runs snowball however, if you manage a good start. There isn't that much to do at the last stops, unless you barely scraped by until then, but if you have a bad start you probably die at the first or second stop anyway.
So getting to the end feels unsatisfying I guess? You get a Score, and some traits. Overall new runs feel like "ok now do the same thing again but this time it will be even more tedious".
Maybe you will be in that portion of the 10% who's having fun, but unfortunately I can't recommend it.
