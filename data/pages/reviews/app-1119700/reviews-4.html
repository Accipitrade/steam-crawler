\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good concept, but poorly executed from a gameplay perspective.

The map is very intensely cluttered, to the point that it's difficult to select your units or parse what is going on. If you zoom out to see the layout of the map, the details of each square are hazy. If you zoom in to see the details, you can only see one tile in each direction. There's no comfortable middle ground, it's just "Too far out to see what's going on" or "Too cluttered and myopic to see what's going on."

I might be able to get past all the (incredibly frustrating) gameplay hangups that all the bad reviews seem to mention with some practice, but I frankly won't be putting in the time because the UI is so cluttered that figuring out what is happening is a visual chore. I should be frustrated by the game and it's difficulty, not my inability to see where my units are.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wanted to like this game so bad. The basic premise of tight resource management and city planning in a rogue like setting really appealed to me. I really enjoyed the process of planning out each halt (map) versus the entire journey. What really stopped me was the fact that so many of the game's mechanics failed or simply served no purpose. Some examples:

1.) There are random events that can be discovered. The benefits are often wildly trivial and the consequences often result in a complete run loss. Complete run loss happens often, and you often have very little control over it.
2.) ANY loss of the life of a pupil is game over. When your time is limited and skills have to be cultivated over the course of your entire run, you usually get your full group of pupils and stick to them until the end...otherwise you may simply lose because you lack enough specialized skills. Couple that with miserable traits like "lose hp every turn" and some runs are a non-starter.
3.) Healing is terrible. In a game where it's often tight to get from halt to halt, and in a game where hoarding resources is punished...the fact that it requires upwards of 12 turns to make ONE healing potion that restores a portion of a pupil's hp is downright terrible design. It's better to avoid illness altogether or tank the hp loss and hope your pupils don't die (unless the world's most lucky trader comes by).
4.) There's an entire knowledge point system that can go into a few categories of "tech"...but only one of them (the main knowledge tree) seemed useful to me. I toyed with the others for testing, and it came at the cost of my odds of winning.

In short, if you want to try a game that's a bit frustrating and fallen short of what it could have been...go ahead and give it a try. Otherwise, I'd recommend looking elsewhere.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI loved this game, and could easily play it for hours despite the fact I'm honestly terrible at it. LOL There is just so much good stuff here with the story, sound, and graphics!

But I don't recommend it for one key reason:

There are no "save points".

Each play-through involves clearing several different levels ("halts") before finally reaching the end. It doesn't matter how many halts you have already cleared, if you lose or die on a particular level ALL of your progress is lost. You cannot simply redo a single halt.

Games like Civilization or Age of Wonders: Planetfall let you obsessively create save files for every possible outcome, and it would be super awesome if this game let you do the same. The fact that it doesn't is hugely disappointing.

And on top of that, stats do not track if you lose.

With how easy it is to fail at this game, you'd think you'd at least get some rewards or perks via interesting stats to follow. But sadly that is not the case.

If you lose, it's like nothing every happened. You don't get any rewards for what you did complete. You don't even get to look at the stats for all the times you died or at what stage you died. There is a lot of data that could be compiled just so you don't feel like you've wasted hours of your life when you lose.

Even the data that is tracked when you do complete a successful game is lacking, IMHO. I'd be really curious to see how many times I gathered certain resources, what resources were the most popular, how many total Ruffalos I saved, what professions I use the most etc.

tl;dr - The game appears to be designed with competitive gameplay in mind. (I believe there was a monthly contest for high scores, not sure if it's still something they do?) And could use some serious Quality of Life improvements for your average/casual player that just wants to explore and enjoy themselves. Until that happens, I don't feel comfortable recommending the game.


(Also - just tossing this out there, but if you are considering getting this game on the Switch DO NOT DO IT. I originally played the Switch port and it's essentially unplayable. Honestly, it's the worst port I think I've ever played. But I liked the concept and the art enough I decided to give it a shot on Steam while it was on sale.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAt first glance, this game looks like it could have been made for me. It's a rogue-ish game with procedurally generated "halts" (hex maps) that you travel between FTL-style to reach a final halt. But instead of slaying every foe on your way, you explore, gather, and build. It's such a beautiful little game that should, by all rights, be a relaxing experience, but the mechanics just don't line up with the feel it gives off at all. In a game that's thematically about rebirth and cycles, you keep failing for arbitrary reasons that you had no way to prepare for. I'm fine with learning by dying, but if the death doesn't bring any new knowledge...

Please bear in mind that I am not one of those people who complain about randomness in every punishing game out there. I loved Darkest Dungeon, which is very punishing, but in DD and many other games like it that I have defended against the "fake difficulty" mobs, the randomness can be accounted and prepared for. The negative effects can be mitigated by hedging your bets, withdrawing when things are rough, etc.

In As Far As The Eye, you can't do anything except hope you don't get certain vagaries (random negative events that happen every now and again) that are nearly guaranteed to ruin you. The "rodents" vagary has single-handed ended three runs in a row for me now. Rats keep eating whatever resource I happen to need the most at the moment, leaving my pupils dying for hungry or straight out blocking me from moving on. I gave up when not only did they eat all the meat I had hunted, but directly after another vagary stopped my trappers from working.

So despite some good ideas and a beautiful theme, I can't recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

Most games increases the difficulty of your experience by throw in a bit of random elements but still provides ways to avoid them. That's a very important key concept since that would challenge players' analytical skills and strategy/management mindset.

When RNG factor is greater than management, then you'll know it aint a great game after all. If you enjoy RNG to this extend, might as well just go gambling which you'd have a better prediction on the result but the statistics are also better than this. Overall, i'll give it 4/10 in terms of "strategy games"\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to love this game. I loved it during the first 5-10 hours I played, completing 2 runs.

The art is awesome, the concept of pupils being able to change apearence when they change jobs, and level up those trades to unlock new forms is inovative, as is the permanent and portable building system (but ultimately both are shallow).

Then two things happens :

(1) it gets repetitive and tedious, as new tribes unlocks basically does not change the gameplay much. It pushes you towards a different strategy, while offering little to no new gameplay features. Every run is : get a food production stream rolling in the 1st/2nd map, then when you are confident with it, focus on caravan/council upgrades and harmony generating schemes. Since portable buildings take up space in the caravan, you will always only get portable building for your main supply chain, which is food (gathering fish, game or wheat + cooking, and maybe a 3rd for spice), reinforcing the repetitiveness of it. Maps are randomly generated, but it does not matter as the gameplay itself isn't really impacted by map layout. You spot ressources available, deploy, exploit as much as you can, then move. Once you are past the 1st/2nd map, your production streams should be well oiled enough that nothing short of an extreme combination of major bad lucks (or player errors) could make you lose.

(2) Every game you finish unlocks new pupil traits based on what you did, and roughly half those traits are NEGATIVE AND CANNOT BE DISABLED, meaning your pupils will have chances to have those negative traits at random. The set of negative traits you start the game with are quite manageable, but some of the unlocks are nasty stuff. So, instead of that classic take on roguelikes where, the more you play, the better your team/character/ship/whatever gets at achieving your goal, in As far as the eye, the more you play, the more new ways of getting your run screwed by bad traits you unlock. You can spend a currency to change those traits for a new set of random traits, so this just becomes a currency drain which adds nothing to the fun.

There is a good base, and alot of potential, but the game cruelly lacks depth. Give us a currency after each run to spend between runs to increase the probability to get a set of chosen traits and/or decrease the probability to get a set of unwanted ones. Allow us to use that currency to chose some of our starter pupils traits, give them some pre-leveled skills. Let us unlock "tribe perks" that needs to be chosen before the run and that allow us to customize our gameplay.

There could also be more scripted interactions, like multi-halt side-quests where you need to bring X ressources to someone on the next halt, or habor a pupil until you reach his destination X halts later. Anything to give a personnality and a sense of uniqueness to the randomly generated maps.

There are so many option to add depth to a roguelike like As far as the eye, it feels like a waste not to have any of them.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI feel compelled to leave a public review of this game because of all the misleading and disparaging comments.

This game is unique. It is hard. It will throw you off. You make a plan, and it falls apart. Failure is to be expected. You will not unlock everything each run. You need to focus and plan 2-3 nodes out. You need to learn the effects of catastrophes. You need to learn how to optimize build orders and adjust based on the size/type of node you are on.

Despite the cutesy art style, this is a complex strategy game. Respect it as such.

Yes, there are some bugs. The UI is not perfect. And the performance can fall out at times. But all of those things are to be overlooked for anyone that is looking for a new school game that is on par with greats like FTL and Into The Breach.

If the flavor and style of this game even remotely appeals to you, get it.\t\t\t\t\t
