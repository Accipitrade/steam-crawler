\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHere's how good DOOM is: DOOM is so good, I exceeded the character limit on Steam Reviews gushing about this game. So here's my attempt at an abridged version.

DOOM is a near-perfect blend of everything that makes both classic and modern single-player FPS games good. DOOM is everything that every FPS game should strive to be. DOOM is my current contender for Game Of The Year.

First things first: this is a video game designed for newer hardware. It's an extremely well optimized game, mind you, but if you're like me and running hardware from four years ago, you'll be forced to run the game on the lowest graphics settings. This won't be a HUGE loss, since the game's visual design and art direction are juuuuuuuuuust barely bold and varied enough to carry the game by itself, but it's still something worth considering.

Right, now that the disclaimer's out of the way, we can start talking about the game itself.

DOOM throws all of the Modern Military Shooter bullcrap out the window in exchange for good old fashioned RIPPING AND TEARING. You regain health by pumping the forces of hell full of lead and then ripping a demon's arm off and using it to whack it's head clean out of the park, all in the span of half a second. You're constantly running, jumping, climbing, hunting for secret collectibles, and firing off your entire ammo reserve without stopping to reload. It's an extremely fast game with satisfying gunplay and a slew of unique enemies that each bring wildly different challenges to the table.

The game's single player campaign has a story, but aside from the three-or-so instances where the supporting cast forces you to sit through a good few minutes of exposition, you're free to completely ignore it. The Doomslayer (yes, that's what the legions of Hell call him) himself certainly does, and he does so with a sense of bravado and sheer hatred that somehow makes him more personable and fun to play as than most other AAA video game protagonists. This is in spite of the fact that he has no voice actor and is 100% silent at all times.

The campaign mode also features an impressive number of collectable upgrades that have actual, meaningful impact on the gameplay. Weapon Mods give most weapons two unique alternate firing methods, such as allowing your shotgun to fire grenades. Killing as many demons as possible and exploring secret areas will grant you upgrade tokens that can be spent to upgrade these weapons further. Protip: get the Ultimate Cooling perk for the Deployable Turret as soon as possible; it'll shred absolutely everything to ribbons.

The campaign's expansive levels also hide away secret entrances to Classic DOOM maps, as well as Rune Challenges, which grant an equippable (and MASSIVE) buff to the player when completed. If you find yourself in possession of Runes such as Armored Assault and Rich Get Richer, you will quickly become a force of pure destruction worthy of a title as awesomely edgy as "Doomslayer".

You won't encounter any bossess until at least halfway through the campaign mode, oddly enough, but this is made up for by the sheer spectacle of a fully decked-out Doomslayer facing off against the most epic incarnation of the Cyberdemon yet seen. The Cyberdemon is not the only classic DOOM boss monster to return, and there's also an original boss encounter that also invokes that same sense of spectacle and climax. The boss fights of DOOM are an absolute thrill to play.

My only real beef with the campaign mode is that the last few Mars levels of the game start pulling the whole "locked in a room with waves of demons" stunt for much longer than is necesarry.

The game's other highlight is called SnapMap, and it's DOOM's attempt at bottling the lighting created by the Classic DOOM modding community. The results are mixed, and while the in-game level editor will never truly suffice as a substitute for full-blown modding support, it's still quite the interesting toy.

SnapMap levels are designed by dropping preconstructed rooms into a 2.5D blueprint. Yes, I said preconstructed rooms. There is basically no way to create truly original geometry in SnapMap. Considering that we live in an era where games like Trackmania and LittleBigPlanet are best sellers and the Hammer editor is freely available to everyone, the fact that you're restricted to 100 versions of hallways, curves, and arenas is nothing short of baffling.

Thankfully, SnapMap's logic gates are infinitely more flexible. SnapMap's logic gates are visualized as a series of icons chained together with arrows which tell you which icon is affecting which other icon. SnapMap even comes with a complete set of tutorials that will teach you basically everything you need to know in order to make these icons do whatever you please. Creating a map where the goal is to collect as many Imp skulls as possible while avoiding an invincible Hell Knight is a relatively simple task. I've probably spent just as much time and had just as much fun tinkering with SnapMap logic gates as I have blasting through the main campaign.

The final part of this review will be dedicated to the game's Multiplayer component. DOOM's multiplayer was farmed out to Certain Affinity, the guys behind the Multiplayer for Halo: Master Chief Collection and Call Of Duty: Ghosts. And it shows. It really, really shows.

DOOM's multiplayer component gains almost all of the bullcrap that the campaign shed, thereby losing basically everything that made the campaign great. You're permanently saddled with two-weapon loadouts, your ground movement speed is nerfed, your aerial mobility is flat-out not there, and the Glory Kills are replaced with lengthier third-person-camera animations.

Yes. The camera in multiplayer switches to third-person whenever you perform a Glory Kill. This is the exact kind of gameplay mechanic that seems harmless on paper but ends up screwing you over once you're in the thick of the battle. This same level of stilted stop-and-go, as well as a crippling lack of self-identity, oozes throughout the entire multiplayer experience, and even the ability to temporarily transform into an aggro-drawing super-demon can't fix that.

I already summarized my thoughts on DOOM as a whole at the beginning of this review, and I'll do it again to cap this review off: The sections of DOOM developed in-house serve as excellent examples of what every FPS game should strive to be. Everybody who likes fun video games that are fun while also being fun should play it ASAP... though older computers might have to lower the internal resolution scale to keep a solid framerate.\t\t\t\t\t
