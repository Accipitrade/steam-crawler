\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOverall a good game with a good campaign, but has a not so great multiplayer and an ok level editor.

Singleplayer: This game follows the "gameplay first" principle a lot more than most AAA games. Great campaign, though
the start is somewhat slow. Id tech 6 (the engine) has been upgraded a lot since it's previous iteration. The game is somewhat graphically demanding and requires quite a lot of resources, but within reason.

Multiplayer: Standard cookie cutter FPS multiplayer that you have probably already seen in CoD or Halo, just slightly faster and with OP demon power ups. Probably not worth your time if you want an arena shooter expierence.

Snapmap: The game's "level editor" is ok but not great for serious modding/level creation. It has a great visual scripting system (at least compared to other games) but customization is very limited. What ruins it is the limit of demons spawned at once wich is capped at 12 and the overall limit is around 50.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFar better than it had any right to be, considering it's fairly tortured and long winded development process, Doom is a fantastic call-back to old 90's style FPS's but with a slick, modern appearance. It's very well optomised and runs brilliantly even on mid-range machines with the level design and art style really standing out at every turn. It even sounds great, the guns and monster noises having a real visceral thrill to them - and don't even get me started on the amazing Industrial Metal soundtrack.

It takes a handful of modern concepts such as the obligatory light RPG mechanics and blends them together with classic 'don't stop moving' FPS gameplay. The levels are vast and intricate and whilst they're not so open as to present multiple routes, it's still a mostly linear game, there are many nooks and crannies within which to find various secrets (the best of which are repurposed chunks of maps from the original Doom!). It's quite long too with each level taking around an hour or two to progress through depending on a) how good you are and b) how thorough you're being and with 13 stages to get through it'll take a while before you've seen everything.

The story is pretty basic although the game seems to delight in this a little with your protagonist willfully ignoring the various instructions he's given in the name of smashing the crap out of everything instead. It does follow that gaming trope of having information logs scattered about the levels to provide a bit more context should you wish it but this isn't exactly Deus Ex - you're not going to be playing Doom for it's story, you're going to be playing it to kill hundreds and hundreds of demons in a wide variety of interestingly brutal ways.

There is a multiplayer mode although it is one of Dooms weaker points. It has received some improvements since launch, and the DLC is entirely focused upon this and has added a bit more depth, but curiously where the single player is a wonderful melding of old and new the multiplayer takes the focus more on the new. It dumps the single player gun mechanics in favour of Battlefield-style gun loadouts presented across a variety of tight arena maps. It's not terrible, it's just not all that interesting, covering as it does the standard MP modes although it does have one variation where a single player can become one of the demons and rip and tear their way through everyone else until they get blown to bits which is quite fun.

Finally the game does also have a built in level editor which, whilst not completely open, is much easier to use for the layman than a lot of editing programmes that come with games. Clearly geared more towards the console versions those who are familiar with this sort of thing will likely find the PC version annoyingly restrictive but that aside it does have a decent range of tilesets, customisation options, triggers and so forth. Several new sets have been added to this since launch and there are menus that present the communities favourites for you to try out yourself.

Overall Doom was a brilliant surprise and a rare gem in a year of AAA games that have largely dissapointed. The recently added challenge mode, where you can race through the games levels killing as fast as you can for points to vie for a place on the leaderboard, only adds to the longevity of a game that had plenty of replay value to start with. Aside from a slightly underwhelming multiplayer mode I simply cannot recommend Doom enough - it's easily my favourite game to come out of 2016.\t\t\t\t\t
