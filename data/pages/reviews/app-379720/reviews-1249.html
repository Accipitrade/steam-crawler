Revenge of the Tech Demo Shooter

I’ve long resisted picking up the 2016 DOOM reboot, but the $5 USD discount offered by Steam during the 2020 Summer Sale finally convinced me, since DOOM also happened to be on sale for about that price. I’ve reviewed a number of other games that I would consider to be proper "retro throwback boomer shooters," so now I shall review this game - and in the process, perhaps I can explain why I’ve been so averse to the DOOM reboot(s) thus far.

Graphics
Visually, the DOOM reboot looks... good? I affix a question mark to that sentence because we shouldn’t really expect it to look bad, being a relatively recently-released AAA title from id Software, published by Bethesda. Models are relatively high-quality, textures are decent, reflections are nice, and so on - theoretically, DOOM checks all the boxes here. But at the same time, the visual design of the DOOM reboot sometimes hinders its gameplay, as evidenced by the game’s necessary usage of extremely high-contrast red, blue, and green item pickups, and (literal) highlighting of enemies that can be killed with melee for a health boost, elements which decidedly clash with the rest of the visual design. Moreover, the level design is sometimes unclear, with invisible walls in locations that appear to be platformable, and paths forward not always apparent. Some may decry the graphical simplicity of games like DUSK, AMID EVIL, or Ion Fury, but the DOOM reboot goes to show that detailed graphics aren’t everything for a fast-paced shooter - sometimes less baroque environments can seriously aid game design across the board.

Audio
The audio design in DOOM is decent, but I’ll play the contrarian and say it’s not quite as exceptional as some have claimed. Enemy cues are audible if not as distinct as in previous titles, weapons are competent, and voice acting is fine - but said weapons lack a certain punch (both in the audio department and elsewhere, as described further below), and all sound effects come across a bit muddled, as if heard through a helmet, which, immersive or not, is arguably to the game's detriment. The soundtrack has received accolades, and I found it to be good, though it lacked the satisfying distinctness of DUSK's non-reactive metal tunes as a result of its reactive design. Thus, rather than driving the action, the DOOM reboot's music is instead driven by the action, almost too much so.

Performance
This is one area where I have to give the DOOM reboot serious credit. This game performs beautifully considering how graphically detailed it is, and at 1440p on my current system I can manage anywhere from 120 to 200 FPS depending on the environment with everything maxed out. The adaptive v-sync option conveniently caps the framerate to my monitor’s refresh rate, and the graphics options are extensive, complete with your choice of OpenGL or Vulkan renderers. The idTech 6 Engine is an impressive piece of kit, and this game really shows off how well-optimized it is. I did experience several inexplicable crashes, but beyond that, performance was impressive.

Gameplay
DOOM might be among the first - and certainly the biggest-budget - in a subgenre of what I call "retro throwback FPS games," though it is more of a reboot or reimagining than a throwback. Where DUSK seeks to recapture and modernize the mechanics of old shooters, DOOM instead seeks to capture the feeling of those shooters, opting for new mechanics in the process. Thus, DOOM is a game about balancing your ammunition and weapon usage, strategically using mechanics to regain health, and dodging enemy attacks. It's unfortunate, though, that weapons feel underpowered across the board, and that glory kills will tank the player's momentum, offering invincibility frames instead while an animation plays out. The design sensibilities of the campaign are covered more below, but the core gameplay loop feels decidedly “good but not great,” still falling somewhat short of the spirit found in titles released more recently.

Singleplayer
The singleplayer level design of DOOM feels like a cross between classic Doom, Quake, and more recent indie titles like ULTRAKILL. Some areas are more open and expansive, reminiscent of classic FPS level design, while others are small arenas the player is compelled to clear of any enemies before proceeding. Still, the campaign feels inspired by the feeling of Doom, but inspired by the substance of Quake, with a greater degree of verticality and powerups borrowed directly from the Quake series. Unfortunately, I was often frustrated by the DOOM campaign, between the Rune Challenges necessary to unlock staple features such as air strafing, occasionally abstruse level design with key items hiding in the background, and aforementioned invisible barriers causing the occasional death by falling into the void. The campaign eventually gets going after acquiring enough weapon upgrades, but the weapons ought to be good unto themselves, and it should not take six hours for the gameplay loop to become enjoyable. By the time it does, the game itself starts to feel repetitive, as the final chapters feel almost like a victory lap for a now-overpowered player.

Multiplayer
Doom’s multiplayer component is almost par for the course: a melange of generic player-versus-player game modes that all fail to capitalize on the game’s strengths, instead turning it into a team deathmatch-based arena shooter that seems intent on treading a bland middle ground between old-school Quake and new-school multiplayer AAA shooters. Make no mistake, the multiplayer component of the Doom reboot is no Quake III Arena, and even if it is instead more in the vein of Quake Champions, the fact remains that that title left much to be desired, too. The one saving grace of Doom’s multiplayer component is its support for SnapMap, a feature covered in more detail below.

Community Content
The other titles I picked up during the Summer Sale were a mixed bag as far as support for community content goes, with Black Mesa leaving me very impressed and Dishonored and Prey less so. The DOOM reboot unfortunately fits into that latter category, doubly disappointing given the legacy of the Doom series, with WADs still being made for the 1990s Doom titles to this day. The DOOM reboot pays lip service to community content support, but practically speaking, that’s all there is - lip service. The "SnapMap" system, for all its abilities, and for the longevity it grants to multiplayer with both PvP and PvE support, comes off as a hollow imitation of the moddability of Doom (1993) and Doom II, and the Nexus and ModDB pages for the DOOM reboot lay virtually empty. A lot of my gripes with this game could have been fixed with proper mod support, so this is a disappointing note to end on.

Verdict
Despite its accolades, the 2016 DOOM reboot comes off as a game that’s still a little confused about what it wants to be. The faux support for community content and milquetoast multiplayer components of the game smack of annual- or biennial-release AAA first-person shooters, while the singleplayer campaign endeavors to capture the spirit of classic FPS games, if not the mechanical substance. My writing in this review has been rather ambivalent, so I will make one thing clear: the singleplayer component of DOOM (2016) is fairly good, and the reboot itself is a fairly good game. Still, we are now in a veritable revival of fast-paced, old-school shooter games, and many of DOOM’s lower-budget indie counterparts are able to soar to even greater heights of game design. So it’s worth a play, but I would not pay for DOOM (2016) what I paid for DUSK, or for AMID EVIL, or for Ion Fury.

I would recommend DOOM, but would suggest waiting for a sale.\t\t\t\t\t
