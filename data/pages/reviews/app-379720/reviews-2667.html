\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBelieve the hype.

Calling Doom an FPS these days isn't descriptive enough, not only because Doom basically pioneered the genre but so many wildly different first person games exist that further delineation is required. Doom is an FPS in the same style as Serious Sam, Painkiller, or Shadow Warrior. While there aren't as many enemies as those games, you will be given a large arsenal of weapons to mow down a large number of enemies in an arena type setting.

There's no health regen, no chest high walls for cover, no iron sights; just your sprint button, reflexes, scattered health pick ups, and your trusty double barrelled shotgun. What distinguishes Doom from its contemporaries is the 'stagger' mechanic. Do enough damage to a demon, and they will 'stagger'. Meleeing them in this state will perform an execution that not only looks awesome, but causes them to barf up extra health. If this sounds like it would get tedious, it doesn't. The animations are very quick, and each monster has a variable number of executions depending on where you 'melee' them from. This also means your best defence is offence. Attacking enemies so they can be executed gives you a health bonus, rewarding your aggression. The gameplay becomes a delicate dance of constantly putting out damage while still dodging projectiles. You can't cheese your way out of the encounters.

When you're not finding hordes of demons, the rest of the game is navigating the map toward an objective. This is often straight forward, but each level is littered with secrets that both reward exploration and enhance replayability. These secrets don't just give health, armor, or weapons, but permanent and customizable buffs. There are secrets that increase your max health, armor, or ammo, or ones that provide passive bonuses, or ones that provide weapon secondary-fires (mods). No collectibles are there just for fun, they all improve your character. That said, the game is fully completable even if you miss most of these, so you aren't beholden to tracking all these down.
Secrets are cleverly but not obtusely hidden. You won't be clicking on faceless walls that show secret doors. Most are just hidden above rafters or through out of the way tunnels. The mini-map greatly helps here and makes finding all the secrets it's own satisfying side game.

The story is your usual Weyland-Utani, Avatar, Evil-Corp trope. It doesn't especially matter and a compelling story would probably be a detriment to that game's aesthetic and very tongue-in-cheek gameplay. This is not a survival horror type game in any sense of the word, but you will be treated to many scenes of splendid gore and splattering.

Doom takes everything that was awful about Doom 3 and makes it better. Graphically it's good, but not stunning. I guess it's hard to be blown away by the terrain of Mars or a space installation, but scenic pastures and mountains would feel very out of place here. My computer is 4 years old and this is the first game I've started to notice some FPS drops, so be warned.

It's still a fantastic game, and a great step forward for the stagnant genre.\t\t\t\t\t
