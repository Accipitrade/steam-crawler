This is how you make a fast paced shooter and tell a story at the same time. I admire ID for being able to convey quite a bit of the Doom Marine's character in his few scripted actions while still acknowledging that the last thing the player wants to do is sit through exposition.

A few times I did think it would be nice to have a bit more enemy variety to stave off the creeping repetitiveness I experienced (in the end of my second playthrough). Even if they used the heavy hitters more often or in greater combinations instead of hords of imps and knights, or a few more bosses would be welcome.

A sprint button was my only other wish as the movement seemd a little sluggish at times (exploration).

Aside from a few glitches and bugs, this will be one of my favorites for a good while.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPreface: I am reviewing the campaign ONLY. While I feel the multiplayer is good in its own right, if you are buying this game, it is for the campaign.

Combat/Controls: 10/10

Modern FPS are effectively hit and run simulators. You pop out of cover, trade some damage, and drop back under cover where your health heals over time. DOOM breaks from this one-dimensional strategy and honors the roots of the genre, giving us fast-paced, streamlined action during each demon encounter. Health and ammo are recovered via pickups placed strategically around maps and dropped by killed enemies. You are forced to engage in action to recover lost health instead of hiding behind cover. Glory kills, meleeing dazed enemies to death in the most gory ways imaginable, are the only guaranteed way for enemies to drop health and will have you diving into the fray of battle even on the brink of death. Staying in motion is vital as demons are capable of dealing heavy damage from range on a stationary target.

The combat is not mindless; you will learn the habits of certain demons and use them to your advantage. All enemies have unique traits that creates a battlefield the developers refer to as "combat chess." The imps are the common pawns, the pinkies the rooks that charge in straight lines, the summoners the bishops that dart across the map in an instant, the hell knights are knights that strategically corner you and jump in for big damage. Your job is to analyze the positions of all of these demons in real time and run the correct way, use the correct weapons, and be as accurate as possible to survive to the next combat zone. Certain guns handle certain enemies better, and weak spots are present in all demons if you are accurate enough to hit them. If you are anything like me, you will soon find other FPS combat boring in comparison to the pace-driven, absolutely thrilling combat mechanics in DOOM.

Controls are just as polished as the combat. You are always in full sprint; one less button to click! Guns are on an easily accessed wheel, as well as hotkeys, to allow for quick switching mid-combat. Platforming is smooth and does not feel like a chore. Climbing rocks and walls to evade enemies is quick and seemlessly integrated into the fast pace of battle. This section is where DOOM shines. No one does in-your-face, hardcore combat better than DOOM.

Story: 7/10

If combat and controls are where DOOM shines, story is the where it twinkles half-heartedly. To summarize, an evil corporation implements demonic energy from Hell as a source of renewable energy for mankind. However, a demon worshipper turns traitor and lets loose the demons on Mars. Your job is to close the portals to Hell and eradicate the demons. While it sounds silly, the implementation is a lot of fun. You wake in a demon-infested base on Mars and push your way through horde after horde of demons, the story pushed to the side in favor of the meat of the game (that being tearing through Hell's creations in the most entertaining way possible). The result is minimal cutscenes, and an experience that feels like you are meant to play the game, not watch a movie. Codex entries are hidden throughout the campaign which expand on the lore of DOOM in a subtle fashion, tucked nicely in the menu area for you to access at your leisure. A man I refer to as Dr. Scary Voice interjects intermittently during your quest to explain the significance of a location or object.

While the story itself is nothing special, I am happy to award a 7/10 for the seemless implementation in a game that prides itself on pace and combat, as well as expanding on the lore of the series which had been thoroughly neglected in the past.

Visuals/Optimization: 10/10

DOOM is a beautiful game. I won't dwell on this too long. Everything is polished with high-quality textures and atmospheric level designs. The most impressive aspect of this is how well the game is able to run on budget systems. Even on Ultra quality, the game runs far better than similar AAA games. iD are also the first to implement Vulkan API into a AAA game, allowing for systems that run on high core count CPUs or AMD graphics cards to pull even more performance. iD's game engine, iD Tech 6, is often hailed as the best game engine in the business, so go in expecting a playable and beautiful experience.

Audio: 10/10

DOOM come away with yet another perfect score here. The DOOM soundtrack is a spectacular take on modern metal, mixing the electric guitar of old with synths of new, and will have you headbanging even as the gates of Hell pour open. The soundtracks react to player actions, especially glory kills, so it ramps up and down with the thrill of battle. The GDC talk "DOOM: Behind the Music", available to watch on YouTube, goes into depth about how Doom composer Mick Gordon created the soundtrack, and it is a really amazing video to check out if you're interested.

The sound effects are similarly well crafted. iD worked with Mick Gordon to harmonize the sound effects of items and weapons with the tone of the music. Everything audio feels incredibly polished. It is probably no surprise then that DOOM won Best Music/Sound Design at The Game Awards 2016.

Overall: 9.5/10

DOOM is a reboot done right. The combat is reminescent of its roots with just enough new to feel like a modern game with its own personality. Perhaps the worst part of DOOM is wondering how iD can surpass this with the sequel that is all but confirmed by the cliffhanger ending and general success of the game.\t\t\t\t\t
