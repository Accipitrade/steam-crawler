\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen Nu-Doom was on the cusp of release, there was a significant number of those skeptical about the game's direction. The many complaints were some shoddy pieces of level design, the glorified cutscenes referred to as glory kills, the fact that Doomguy wasn't immensely faster than the demons, so on and so forth. To put it bluntly, it was 4chan grade nitpicking by purists who weren't going to play the game anyways. Having a more open mind and having faith in id Software despite their, at the time, recent shortcomings, I figured I'd give this game a shot. I found it was a good return to form, but is plagued by issues that can more or less ruin the game for some.

I'll get started on its presentation. It's influenced by heavy metal imagery with its heavy soundtrack, extensive amounts of blood and gore, and fast paced gameplay. id takes a more traditional route for story in an FPS by not relying on cutscenes so often. Compared to every other big FPS in that timeframe, even Wolfenstein: TNO, there was a notable lack of plodding plot so the player could go straight into enjoying the actual game within seconds of starting a new campaign. It's a shining example of how action games should be handled.

Going further into the gameplay, the action is fast and intense, moreso than the original Doom. In the original Doom, with the always run option on, you're almost never going to be tied down by enemies. The enemies are extremely slow compared to Doomguy, who can run an estimated 60 MPH and the enemies briskly walking towards your general direction. In Nu-Doom, you're still fast, but the enemies can catch up with you pretty quick unless it's a mancubus or a cacodemon. The game encourages you to run, jump, and dodge your way around enemies as opposed to taking cover and tactically picking off each enemy one by one. With a wide assortment of weapons to choose from, you can either barrage the enemies with a wide assortment of arms or run through with your weapon of choice. If you get an enemy close to death, you can choose to glory kill them which puts you in a 1-4 seconds long cutscene where you can't take any damage and can rip the enemy apart with your bare hands. Alternatively, you can shoot the enemy since the reward's something meager like ten health. To put things in perspective, the least amount of damage I've taken from an enemy was about 25 or 30.

Outside of combat, you can explore the gameworld and find secrets which are hinted towards by secret passageways, damaged doors, or attractive items teasing you from the other side of a window. The game encourages you to look through every nook and cranny to make the Doom Slayer as powerful as he can possibly get. The levels are designed in such a way to accomodate unique combat sections with multiple layers, condensed exploration areas where you're likely to come back to a part of the level after you find out how to unlock that big door, and areas away from the beaten path that offer goodies to make your game life easier. Before I continue, I'd like to give my props to CZG from the Quake mapping community who now works for Machine Games for helping design this game. I'm pretty sure the level design would have been mediocre without his input.

The game's length is pretty substantial. I'm sure you could blast through this game in about eight to ten hours if you play on the regular difficulty and don't bother with secrets, but then you're not enjoying the game to its fullest. Each level is unique visually and architecturally, each providing a different feel from one another. Over time you get more weapons, more enemies to fight, and greater challenges to overcome. Getting to the end, I felt pretty satisfied with the game, despite some issues I feel got close to ruining my perspective of the game. I will get to those later.

The graphics this game has are pretty damn cool. Cold, isolated mars levels and tech heavy military bases. Foreign hellscapes with fire and brimstone and dilapitated labs overrun by demons. Combined with the big, bad demon designs id Software put in there, the game is definitely an aethstetic treat. However, I feel the game, even at the time, is only above average when it comes to the technical side of its graphics. It has everything a modern game has, but nothing too special aside from good texture work that seems to take the best from standard textures and megatextures from id Tech 5. It looks as good as your average AAA title, but it performs a hell of a lot better. Running this on Ultra settings at 1440p with my GTX 1080, my FPS rarely drops below my monitor's refresh rate of 144hz. Using Vulkan, I get 140-170 on average, with a consistent 200 in areas with few effects, enemies, and low geometry, but it goes down to the 130's in intense battle arenas with lots of crazy geometry and effects. On my previous build with the R9 290, which is the AMD equivalent of a GTX 970, I got an average of 110 fps on Ultra settings at 1080p using Vulkan. For the game looking as good as it does, it runs extremely well, possibly one of the best optimized games I've ever played.

There are some other aspects to the game which will tie in with my criticisms. First of all, snapmap is dumb and they should have just released regular modding tools. Not many people used Snapmap and as a result there isn't much going for custom levels or campaigns. There's some gimmicky levels and a few good ones here and there, but there's nothing that can match up to the campaign solely because the resources aren't available to the community. The multiplayer is crappy Halo, don't bother with it.

At leas the main part of the game is still good, right? In my opinion, yes, but to an extent. As I've stated before, this game is plagued by some issues that I want to make known. The gameplay is flawed. Glory kills can get yourself killed if there's an enemy nearby and ready to attack you, and the reward isn't that great considering the risks. The only time I find glory kills useful is to avoid wasting bullets or to make myself invulnerable if there's a big attack coming that I can't dodge. I find myself having to break my flow in map progression to be sure I pick up every item before I go somewhere new because this game has a bad habit of blocking off parts of the map completely if you go somewhere. This has happened way too many times for my liking. Although the game lets you retry levels, it doesn't change the fact that if you reach the end and find that you missed some items, there's a chance you're forced to restart the entire level if you want to get that item, which can suck if there's a lot of progress you made since the beginning of the level because it clears EVERYTHING you've done since that point. I stopped my second playthrough because of how many times it's happened, but I would like to come back and finish it once I get over my frustrations. (CZG, why u do dis? /s) Certain weapon progressions don't make sense to me. Some require tokens which you get by killing enemies, while nicer add ons can only be achieved by doing some arbitrary task using that weapon. One is to kill multiple enemies with a single super shotgun shot. One can abuse this challenge by reloading a checkpoint and killing a specific group of enemies over and over agian until you get it. In fact, save scumming checkpoints is the easiest way to complete these challenges early. I wish the game made this unlockable with tokens instead. I also don't like the rune challenges, it's extra padding in the game that breaks the flow of the campaign. The bossfights are garbage, easily the worst parts of the main campaign.

Despite all these flaws, this game was a purchasse I don't regret. It plays great, it runs amazingly, it's super fun to explore the game world, and the music is top shelf. I found this a good purchase at 60 dollars even though I didn't reach the hour to dollar ratio I usually go by for recommendations, but since it's 20 bucks now, it's a must buy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA satisfying and brutal journey to hell and back. Doom delivers a relentless core gameplay loop that is polished, simple, and addictive. The push-forward combat system which is supported by the use of micro-QTE demon annihilating sequences in order to regain health is fantastic. This gameplay mechanic embodies the feeling of aggressively slaughtering hordes of demonic forces with no remorse and is ideal for the Doom Slayer protagonist.

This mechanism is supported by a variety of vastly different weapons, smooth level design, a plethora of upgrades, and a gameplay focused storyline. The manner in which Doom presents its nightmarish landscape and characters is spot on. Neither of these ever cross into the territory of being overly edgy but rather capture a hellish fantasy future precisely.

The production polish in the weapons, world design, and enemies that all perfectly intertwine with the core push-forward combat system make this a great first-person shooter.\t\t\t\t\t
