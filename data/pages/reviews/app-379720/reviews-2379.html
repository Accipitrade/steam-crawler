\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBy the time I got around to playing this, I'd already heard a lot of the hype. I kept my expectations under control, however, and calmly waited for it to install. After it stamped its obnoxiously large footprint on my drive (70 GB), I fired it up, praying my machine could run it since it's a bit behind hardware-wise. I cut my teeth on the first DOOM games on a 486, but the many shooters I've played since then had left their impression. It showed as I cowered behind cover, trying to survive onslaughts of erratically moving and flanking demons. My conservative playstyle worked, after a fashion, for a while, but eventually the enemies grew too aggressive for such tactics. After reloading a save for the third time, I threw caution to the wind, waited for a gap in the fire, then rushed out and became a whirlwind of death.

...until some mechanical press thing flattened me.

By then, I had figured it out though. DOOM isn't your typical cover shooter. It's about action and non-stop gameplay. Lately, so many games are so loaded down with cut-scenes and the like that it can reach upward of 20-25 minutes of dull exposition before you get to the next part...the tutorial and practice enemy or something. Some games you can play for an hour before you even get to the actual game (i.e. first level). Not in DOOM though. Story is imparted to the player via holographic projections and audio messages. Also, it's not a game of "follow the map marker". Exploration is fun and encouraged via easter eggs and minor collectibles. Just watch this and tell me it doesn't make you want to find more: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=SZGjA0O-PsI

Also, despite my system being dated, the game ran smoothly with no FPS loss. Solid evidence for the optimization it seems to have received. Highly recommend this one if you're a fan of the DOOM series or just shooters in general.\t\t\t\t\t
