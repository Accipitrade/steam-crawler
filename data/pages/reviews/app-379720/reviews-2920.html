\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm a child of the original Doom era.

I grew up with Doom and Doom 2 and Heretic. I remember when the first generation of gamer parents were freaking out about the blood and gore. I remember marveling at the "true 3D" when Quake came out, and "colored lights" for Quake 2, and "wow, a good story!" when Half-life graced the store shelves, and "multiplayer only? really? wait, multiplayer only is actually fun!" when Unreal Tournament and Quake 3 showed up.

As the FPS genre has continued to innovate, though, it innovated away being enjoyable to me. I loved the Dooms and the Quakes and the Unreals, killing demons and Strogg and strange alien beasts and things that are very clearly "other" and "evil" and "fake." But then came Counterstrike, and Call of Duty, etc. I feel like shooters lost their fantastical edge to capture realism, a mentality which became more and more pronounced as the years dragged on, and this direction just wasn't very fun.

And then Doom 2016 arrived on the scene. The developers basically said, "You know all the stuff that's become staple in the FPS genre the last decade? Forget it. It wasn't fun. Let's go back to the basics instead." And, boy, the basics are fun. The game was like an instant time warp.

There's no reloading weapons.

There's no realism.

The premise is simple: there's a zillion demons trying to eat your face. Kill them all or die trying. Here, have a bunch of guns.

The play is blisteringly fast-paced. 2016 Doom Guy runs about as fast as 1993 Doom Guy. The basic idea for each map is to find different color access keys to progress. The music takes a page from the original Doom's book as well - lots of guitar riffs and pulsing beats to get your heart pumping. Much like the original Doom, all the maps have tons of nooks and crannies to explore if finding secrets is your jive, but you don't lose anything essential by ignoring these. The level of gore, allowing for modern graphics improvements, is about the same. All the perennial favorite weapons make reappearances, like the chainsaw, the super shotgun, and the BFG. The chainsaw and BFG in particular are immensely satisfying to use, perhaps even more so than their original incarnations.

There are a lot of new things (to this franchise, anyway, not in general), too. There are weapon mods, armor mods, and runes to add a few light ARPG elements, as well as jumping, double jumping, and the ever-satisfying glory kill, but these all serve to enhance the fun and chaos, not take away from it. There's a story there if you care to pay attention to it, but you don't miss out by ignoring it. I still have no idea what was going on, but I know Olivia is bad and demons are bad and I need to kill the bad things. That's about it.

I finished playing the single player campaign this weekend. I was so into it, the only times I took breaks were when my fingers were hurting from too much mouse clicking and keyboard smashing, or when I'd gotten so revved up on adrenaline I needed a breather. I have yet to try multiplayer, from from a single-player perspective, Doom is the most enjoyable first person shooter I've played since ... probably UT2k4.

If you like the original crop of FPS that emerged in the 90s, get this game right away. You won't regret it. The single player campaign alone makes it well worth the price.\t\t\t\t\t

Doom is a reboot of the beloved id franchise that we all know and love. It has seen some good iterations and poor iterations. This is by far the best. The best part of this game is that you can either choose to get into the story (spelled out in data logs) or just skip to the best part: the combat. The combat in this game is so fluid, so responsive, that it completely overshadows any story the game might of had. You won't care about anything else, just going from glory kill to glory kill, ripping the limbs off of cacodemons and showering in the visceral gorefest.

The music in this game is excellent and perfectly suited. It is some hard rock/metal that works perfectly for the tone DOOM sets throughout the 10-15 hours you will be spending in the campaign. I am not a fan of metal or hard rock for the most part, but the composer really did a great job and turned me into a fan.

The graphics are beautiful and for a while it's just different shades of red on a landscape (then I realized I'm on mars. duh). However the locales quickly change and taking a trip into hell is quite satisfying. I did not spend any time playing the multiplayer and the snapmaps so I can't comment on that but the campaign more than justifies the asking price. If you are looking for an FPS that does not have regenerating health, allows you to feel like a god, and has some of the most satisfying combat in recent memory than you owe it to yourself to pick up this masterpiece.\t\t\t\t\t
