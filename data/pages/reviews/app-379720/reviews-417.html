\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDoom 2016 vs 90s era Doom Trilogy

1) Doom 2016 weapons are better
-The pistol, though less satisfying than the other weapons (as it should be), is no longer completely useless
-Melee attacks, also pretty useless in the original, are now a critical part of the game
-Power-ups like quad damage or Haste add some novelty to the combat.
-The berserker power-up is hilariously overpowered, allowing you to kill any enemy with one punch. It makes no sense, but it's badass and a lot of fun.
-The 2016 BFG is better than the original. It's very cathartic to go into a mob of zombies and take them all out in one shot. The animation looks amazing too.
-Weapons are now customizable, to good effect

2) Doom 2016 monsters are better overall, although this has a downside (see point 6).
-All the monsters are smarter and more interesting, and have melee attacks if you get too close.
-Monsters are more mobile, and no longer get stuck.
-I especially liked the new Mancubus designs, although their increased mobility seems unrealistic.
-Cacodemons and pinky demons got a huge buff, and are way more interesting than their counterparts from the original.
-Revenants don't pose anywhere near the threat they did in the original. Their missiles barely leave a mark on you and don't follow you around. It's not a big deal, but the game could have used a "glass cannon" enemy like the original Revenant, one that could be taken down fairly easily, but if they get you, you're toast.
-Would have liked to have seen more new enemies, but no biggie: the new monster mechanics add a level of freshness not present in the original. You get the best of both worlds: nostalgic enemy types that play very differently from their 90s era forebears.

3) 2016 maps are about as good as the original Doom. Whereas the original had 30-32 maps, Doom 2016 has only 13, but they're much larger than those in the original and more detailed as well. That being said, one could argue that the simpler, more abstract designs of the original Doom made for a more enjoyable experience, with tripwires and crazy elevators all over the place.

4) Doom 2016 has amazing graphics. Doom is my first experience on a proper gaming computer, and I was just blown away by how good it looks. Between battles, I would sometimes stop to just take in the scenery, and marvel at the technology that allows artists to create such realistic textures-- for instance, metal with chipped paint, or areas where the paint has worn away due to heavy use. If there's a pool of blood on the floor (as there often is), it seeps into the grooves between the linoleum tiles. I began to gain an awareness and appreciation for all the different ways light can reflect off a surface. And this is all before the development of ray tracing, Lumen, etc. It astounds me how good 3D graphics have gotten in my lifetime, and Lumen/UE5 seems to be a huge leap forward from anything we've seen before. I can't wait to see what the future holds.

5) Doom 2016 has a fantastic soundtrack by Mick Gordon, mostly electronic metal that sounds absolutely badass in the heat of combat. The music is mostly original, but a few of the tracks call back to Doom's original soundtrack, which was also really good. One YouTuber made the pithy comment: "Sweet! This amazing album came with a free game."

Reading the Wikipedia article about Doom 2016, I saw that Bethesda originally didn't want to have a metal soundtrack because it was too cliche. That's got to be the dumbest thing I've ever heard. Seriously, what else would fit? When you're bludgeoning rocket launcher-wielding skeletons to death with your bare hands, hippie folk music just ain't gonna cut it.

6) This is controversial, but I think 90s era Doom is better in terms of combat/gameplay. The reboot doesn't quite capture the Doom magic, because several key elements are missing:

a) Back in the 90s, monsters roamed freely around the map. When you called an elevator, there might be a monster on it. Maybe not. You had to be ready for anything. In Doom 2016, for the most part, if you fight enemies, it's usually you because you willfully triggered the battle, and you generally get to scout out the area in advance. It lacks all suspense because you're generally not allowed to leave a given room until all the monsters are dead. You could do a more-or-less pacifist run of 1993 Doom, whereas in 2016 Doom, it's actually pretty hard *not* to kill every enemy.

Whereas monsters could catch you off-guard in the original, Doom 2016's combat feel very scripted. Nowhere is this more apparent than the boss battle sections, which feel out-of-place. The 90s-era Cyberdemon was terrifying because he was wandering around the map like any other enemy, making thunderous noises with his mighty hooves. He could be in any room where he'd fit, just waiting to one-shot you with his rocket launcher. It's a very different feeling when, in the 2016 edition, you must fight the Cyberdemon at the agreed-upon time and place.

b) The original Doom games often had lots of monsters attacking you at once from a great distance. The 2016 version avoids this: the monsters are almost always accessible to you, and you never have to deal with more than like 12 at a time. When you kill one, another one teleports in. Consequently, you won't get mobbed by 50 Imps at once like in the original Doom.

This, and point #1 above, must be due to hardware limitations. They make you kill all the enemies before unlocking the door because if they don't, your CPU will explode. The original monsters were simple 2D sprites and were pretty dumb. Doom 2016's enemies are 3D models, and way more intelligent and versatile than they used to be, which requires more computer resources. I love the way Imps will lie in wait charging up a fireball, then dash around the corner to hurl it at you. That's awesome! I just wish there could be more of them: 50 stupid Imps are more viscerally satisfying to murder than 4 smart ones.

c) The 90s era sound design was better because it was simpler and there was less extraneous information. All the monsters made distinct noises and made them constantly, so you could tell approximately, but not certainly, where everyone was. It added a sense of dread not present in the remake; you would hear a door opening far in the distance, and the snortling of approaching Imps, and feel a certain adrenaline rush. You knew they were coming, but when? In the 2016 edition, only the pinkie demons and zombies make useful noises. The other enemies make subtler sounds that tend to get buried amid all the other ambient noise.

Worst of all, the player character is remarkably quiet when injured or killed. I wanted more screams of agony, like in the original. Actually, the demons ought to scream more when injured too.

~~

All this would make it seem like I didn't enjoy the game. On the contrary, despite these definite flaws, Doom 2016 is still fun to play in its own right. Although the combat starts to feel very samey after a while, Bethesda did all it could to compensate for the major limitation of only having a few monsters in play at once. And if you prefer to play games like the original Doom-- frankly, I do-- nothing's stopping you from playing any of the sixty jillion Doom WADs that fans have made over the years. The modding community is still active all these years later, and some of the levels they've created look absolutely amazing.

Ultimately, I do recommend Doom 2016, but I doubt it will have the staying power of the original, and it's pretty sad that a big-budget AAA game doesn't quite compare to a much slimmer game from 30 years ago that remains relevant despite its own limitations.\t\t\t\t\t
