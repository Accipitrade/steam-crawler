If you have any fondness for the Doom games of the past or enjoy shooter games AT ALL, this is a must play.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI recently finished mopping up some single player achievements, so I'm going to review this before I put it aside for a while.

The nineties Doom games were my first PC action games on my first PC compatible system, and will always have a special place in my heart for that. In theory, I'm the target audience for this, especially given the various nostalgia nods scattered about. I have mixed feelings about this game in practice. I enjoyed it enough to finish it, but at 70 hours I'm already done.

DOOM tries to recreate the feeling of the original games while also chasing the RPG trend in modern FPS games. The progression elements are well-designed, but the game could have easily done without most of them. Your advancement depends on completing various challenges or finding secrets, and missing too many upgrades or investing them badly can make the endgame a struggle. It's possible to go back for everything you missed from Mission Select, which is nice for completionists, but doing that just to catch up would make a first playthrough even more an ordeal. I consider myself pretty experienced at solo FPS games and died a lot on Ultra-Violence, so consider a lower difficulty if you want more casual play. I do appreciate that the higher difficulty is unapologetically brutal, and it made those victories feel well-earned in the end.

The game tries to expand on the excuse plot from the originals, but we just get a bunch of mumbo-jumbo about how the Doom Slayer is the chosen one, and funny voiceovers about the Union Aerospace Corporation trying to make their energy extraction from Hell sound like a good idea. Borderlands 2 this ain't, and the attempts at humor and lore mostly fall flat.

The nimble movement system includes double jumps and vaulting ledges, and it takes nearly constant dodging to stay alive on higher difficulties. The maps require a lot of vertical navigation, so you'll need to master the movement system to explore them fully. The Automap has returned, which helps with finding objectives and secrets, and can be upgraded to show all collectibles on the map. There's a quest compass of sorts, but like most such systems, following the pointer in a straight line often gets you nowhere, because you need to take the long way around a chasm or obstacle, and the Automap is helpful there too. It's easy to overlook things like a door or hatch that can be pried open, an air vent to jump into, or a hand scanner for that severed hand you found elsewhere, so look around carefully before looking up a walkthrough.

You can Glory Kill enemies with melee when they're at low health, performing a deeply satisfying finishing move on them, and making them drop bonus health. The chainsaw can instantly kill an enemy and make them drop a pile of ammo, at the cost of scarce chainsaw fuel. The BFG works like the one from the Quake games, launching a tiny green sun that fries everything it passes with bolts of plasma, but ammo is rare and it carries only three shots. Timed powerups appear in some arena battles, granting Quad Damage, Haste, Invulnerability, or a Berzerk rage that allows instant Glory Kills of healthy enemies. Frag Grenade, decoy Hologram, and Siphon Grenade upgrades can be found; they have unlimited ammo but a long cooldown.

Finding a Rune Stone hidden in certain midgame levels enters a bonus mini-game level where you can earn a Rune, which gives you additional abilities. You can have up to three Runes equipped at once, making a wider variety of builds possible.

There are Doomguy action figures to collect which unlock character and weapon models in the gallery. Each level also has a secret area opened with a lever, that unlocks a Classic Doom map from the menu. I didn't find that mode as much fun as I thought it would be, fighting the same enemies in a world of pixelated walls just made me want to boot up the old Doom.

I haven't bothered with the multiplayer, since there's no co-op mode, and a deathmatch mode with no anti-cheat is kind of a sad joke these days. The classic Doom franchise practically created co-op and deathmatch as we know them today, so this is especially disappointing. A number of annoying glitches also occur in solo play, from weapon animations getting stuck to certain achievements not unlocking properly; I ended up replaying the campaign to get one of them to unlock.

The graphics engine renders the cold corporate space stations and demonic creatures alike in gory detail. It looks a bit too much like every other modern sci-fi shooter in places, but to be fair, this franchise made those tropes a thing to begin with. The sound design is pretty solid, and the metal music soundtrack does a good job conveying atmosphere. Some tracks are recognizably inspired by the classic Doom soundtrack, and new versions of some iconic sound effects bring back memories.

Overall, I had some fun with this and feel like it's a good enough purchase on sale. The world of gaming has changed a lot since the Doom days, and it's never the same going home again, but it was a nice nostalgic visit to the old neighborhood.\t\t\t\t\t
