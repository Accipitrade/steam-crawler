\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIts been a while since I played an action game like this. The last game I can compare it to was Duke Nukem forever, but this is definitely far more gritty and gruesome. When I was younger, I played a lot of the original Doom and Doom 2 at my uncles house (he had newer Pentium 2) and was hooked. Always looked forward to playing Doom whenever I was there.

This new game reminds of a lot of those older Doom. Its much faster though, and took me about 17 hours to get through. I did not rush it and enjoyed the game for what it was. Action packed, gory and violent with plenty of weapons and ammo. Enemy variety was great, level design was great. I played on default (hurt me plenty) and though it was just right to enjoy the game without being too hardcore. The addition of melee kills puts a new aspect on finishing enemies to gain health and powerups a new tactic.

The game runs beautifully. Even on my older Amd 8370fx and rx580, it is smooth as butter. I played on 1080p and locked it on 60fps. Well optimized on vulcan. I wish all games were this smooth.

I recommend. Got it on the summer sale, and its worth the price.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDoom for shooters is what Slay The Spire has been for card games in my eyes. Wow, what a breath of fresh air. I enjoyed Doom a lot these past few weeks (the campaign to no surprise, but the multiplayer to great surprise)! Although the first half of the game was much better than the second, it’s still worth playing at least once, and I think if you play it once you’ll probably try to play it again. I know I will someday.

I guess it should not be a surprise that Doom got a reincarnation in 2016, which was probably the beginning of the peak of the current reboot/remaster/revival movement we’re going through in gaming. After all, Doom is one of the most iconic shooters ever, and is the origin of the “deathmatch”. That’s massive. Although a number of games in the series have come out in the years following the legendary original, I think this reboot is really what the series needed, and the success of the new Doom Eternal is evidence of that.

I feel like I keep saying this in my reviews, maybe it’s a sign that I need to play games with a stronger narrative element to diversify my gaming, but while Doom has a story, I feel like it’s hardly necessary. It’s a simple story about demons from Hell getting loose on Mars and you, Doomguy, having to beat the everliving ♥♥♥♥ of them and close all the connections remaining between Hell and Mars. Honestly, I didn’t pay attention to it at all; I just destroyed demons nonstop, so I really have nothing to say about the story.

What I really want to talk about in this review is the gunplay & level design. The gunplay in Doom is incredible. The game starts out slow with only a pistol and shotgun as a part of your arsenal, but the gun wheel quickly expands, and so do the alternate firing capacities of your weapons. What you end up with is a sweet blend of close and long range as well as slow and quick firing guns to shred apart your enemies. Every gun feels like it fills a distinct purpose, but even if you don’t play that way and use whichever gun you feel like as you spam the weapon scroll wheel, the speed at which you can switch between your firearms and never have to reload is just a thing of beauty.

Whenever a gun leaves an enemy just shy of death because you ran out of ammo you can go in for a beautifully animated chainsaw kill, or when you’re low on health go in for the glory kills. Either way it creates this awesome mixup of long to medium range encounters as well as close range where the danger escalates. Glory and ammo/health pickups could sometimes come at a price!

The level design in Doom is what allows the great gunplay to shine, too. At least, up until a point. The early part of the game is basically divided into two sections. There are long narrow pathways where lots of platforming is required and the movement is swift and linear, and these become even better when you finally unlock double jump (an ability I think gets given to you far too late), and vast and tall sections where you battle large swathes of demons with powerups, armor, and health scattered throughout. The ebb and flow of destruction and quick but smooth movement is really well crafted, and it gives you enough time to go whew after finishing a big battle, but doesn’t let you get that second breath in before you’re surrounded again.

This great design doesn’t last for all of the game however, and actually by the 2nd time you enter Hell it slowly becomes a series of quite restricted and poorly lit rooms one after another where you fight what feels like the same hoard of zombies again and again (think: the last 1/3 of Dante’s Inferno if you ever played that). Luckily, this downgrade in gameplay only covers about the last ¼ of the overall story. It is a bummer though, as when you’re riding the massive high of a game as amazing as Doom, it can feel like a sharp downward turn of the quality of the experience.

Overall though, the gameplay is great. The levels are amazingly lit (to think you play this game without a flashlight at all and everything looks amazing is nothing short of incredible), the demons all look and act incredible, and the gunplay is top notch.

And, in my mind, the hidden gem of Doom is its sound design. We all know the soundtracks are incredible, one of very few games nowadays with a really meaty metal but industrial vibe, and where it really fits, but the brilliance in this game here is everything accompanying the soundtrack. Every demon sounds amazing, every weapon sounds distinct, the jumping and landing sound effects are perfect. It’s all just so satisfying. I can only think of a couple games in the past 5 years that I’ve played in which I just love every sound that was in the game.

On top of all of these positives, Doom actually has a really good multiplayer. It really fills that Unreal Tournament/Quake itch. Even more, I hear the new Doom doesn’t have multiplayer in the way that this one does. That’s a weird choice for me. However, the nice thing is that it somewhat ensures that even though we have a new Doom game, this Doom still attracts players for it having a kind of multiplayer new Doom does not. In other words, it adds extra value to buying this Doom even though we already have a newer, bigger, shinier Doom on the market.

Overall, I think you cannot really go wrong with Doom. I’m not a big shooter guy, in the past half-decade I’ve been into Depth, Siege, and few others for far fewer hours, but Doom is an amazing singleplayer FPS experience. That difference of being a singleplayer shooter, just like Slay The Spire being a singleplayer card game, ended up being exactly what I was looking for. Hopefully Doom can be an inspiration for more great singplayer shooters (looking at you, Ravenfield(?)), just like how we’ve seen Slay The Spire seem to be an integral part in the now successfully implemented designs of One Step From Eden and Monster Train. Doom’s packaged with a nice multiplayer, excellent visuals, a killer soundtrack along with incredible sound design more generally, and just feels like an important piece of gaming history. I recommend Doom for really anybody except those who can’t handle gore, or whose religion would forbid them from playing, haha.

9.0/10

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Save your money and buy DOOM Eternal instead.

At first glance, DOOM 2016 (or DOOM 4, or the DOOM remake, or whatever you want to call it) seems like a dream come true for lovers of the franchise. With plenty of frenetic demon-slaughtering action.

And then you look past the nostalgic filter and realize, oh wait, this sucks.

The combat gets very same-y after a while with half the weapons being flashy at best but utterly useless past their challenges or introductory levels. I spent most of the game using just the combat shotgun for small demons and the rocket launcher for the big ones. The chainsaw is ludicrously impractical, and glory kills got very boring, very fast, since you only get 'diversity' in them if you actually aim the kill at a certain bodypart on the demon, rather than just letting the animation be randomized - nevermind the fact that there are limited animations for several demons which just feels cheap (looking at you, Mancubus).

I thought the addition of an actual story and plot was interesting but it left me with more questions than answers - apparently the 'meat' of the plot is in DOOM Eternal instead, go figure. Probably should have saved my $8 and put it towards that one instead. There was plenty implied but not enough explained - why's Earth *got* an energy crisis so bad this cyborg thinks tunneling into Hell is a good idea, for one thing? What kind of stuff was Olivia Pierce offered by the demons if she basically fed her employees into Hell like a meatgrinder? A cure for her condition? Or something more? And *why* is Samuel just apparently okay with most of this? Nevermind *everything* with those templar knight guys.

The maps have good intentions, but they're so very...dull. The same industrial hallways or the same demonic catacombs, spattered in blood and runes, funneling you into arena fights with demon hordes. Exploring them feels pointless outside of completion/collectibility and I got lost on more than one occasion even with the expanded map because there's just so little diversity. The multiplayer had a lot more diversity.

And speaking of. Truth be told I could have just uninstalled, been slightly disappointed, but not been bothered enough to write out a whole review. But the Multiplayer. Is something ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ else.

With no matchmaking (enjoy being paired up with 2k+ hour veteran players when you're a level 1 scrub), NO ANTICHEAT, and most everything locked behind a progression system - I could understand the cosmetics being locked, but the various demons, runes, and weapons being locked behind level progression? Nevermind the fact that 99% of all said items are in fact useless - the rocket launcher and plasma gun are the only ones worth carrying, as are the Harvester demon and the Magnet rune. Trying to 'get good' is like running headfirst into a wall and hoping it eventually breaks before your skull does, and about as enjoyable.

The only way I got the achievements was, once I got the ones that actually took skill, I started AFK'ing in matches to leech EXP to unlock the armor piece ones. And you may be indeed be asking, why I would do this when this isn't exactly making things better?

Because there's no report system or votekick or any method to try and control how your matches go. Why bother trying if you know you're not going to be able to affect your outcome, because some top ten player grabbed four more of his top ten buddies for a little pubstomping? Or because some cheater decided to just go around insta-gibbing you for s#its and giggles?

No matchmaking I could understand, game's old, small population or whatever, but I'm honestly still trying to wrap my head around the fact that there's no anticheat whatsoever. Like good lord, how little do you have to care about your gamemode not to put in that at the bare MINIMUM????

There are so, so, SO many things in the Multiplayer that could have been put in the Singleplayer campaign to make it better. Gorgeous and more diverse maps (like Outbreak, Molten, Orbital, and Boneyard), various demons and weapons - hell, you could have AT LEAST made those things available in the Arcade mode, which just feels like the regular campaign but more boring. Who wouldn't want to play a Prowler hunting down other demons? Or dealing with one stalking you through the darkened halls of the UAC? Playing Infernal Run while being chased by a horde of pinkies?

"So you just want better Singleplayer, isn't that what SnapMap is for-" AND YOU'D BE RIGHT IF SNAPMAP MODE HAD ANY OF THESE FEATURES, TOO! The limitations in that mode make creating any sort of interesting mini-campaign or private multiplayer map absolutely pointless. I've never seen a map editor that's so limited. Only two weapons? Only twelve enemies at a time? The game itself can handle rendering a hell of a lot more than that, why on *earth* did you put in those kinds of limitations?

In short, there's a lot of potential in DOOM 2016 that just completely and utterly falls flat. Whether because Bethesda or idSoftware or whoever didn't care enough, I don't know.

But I'm thankful I only spent $8 instead of a full $60 like some people.

(You guys should have remade the Heretic/HeXen games instead.)\t\t\t\t\t
