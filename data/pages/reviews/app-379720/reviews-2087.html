\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl;dr: Once you get into the rhythm, there's very little challenge to be had - even on ultra violence - until you encounter a boss monster that can one-shot you if you're not careful. Also, I miss the little details such as Doomguy's face (I don't take to the Doom Slayer name) and holding the pistol in his *left* hand.

Full version:-

I picked up my copy of Doom 2016 Edition ( ;) ) when it was on sale, because back in the day I was unsure if I wanted to play a modern take on the old school run 'n' gun genre - let alone the seminal original Doom. Doom 3 left a sour taste in everyone's mouth, with it feeling more like Quake 1 in some aspects, trying to be a psychological horror game, and be all action. When Doom 4 was released to rave reviews by practically everyone, I remained unconvinced and the game fell off my radar.

Cue more recent times and I'm pleasantly surprised. Doomguy has a personality that befits the universe he is in, there isn't any stupid (nor indeed insipid) reloading mechanic and the weapons feel as though they should. The levels are well designed enough that its clear which direction to go (although the compass can be helpful), the automap is genuinely useful and easy to navigate, and of course the necessary secrets and sometimes alternative shortcuts can be found.

This, however, is where the game starts to unravel. The secrets and what type of secret they are will appear on the automap, and there is an upgrade that reveals everything on the map if you so choose (I elected not to). I kind of enjoyed not knowing at all and having to look out for cues, but I suppose in this day and age a misaligned texture would get multiple posts of "OMG BUG!!!111one!11". *rolls eyes*

There's also the feeling that no matter what, you will get 100% kills because - save for unlocking the optional classic maps - you will go to every area in any given level. In the original games, there could - and would be - entire areas that you didn't even know existed and you'd be left wondering why you only got 70% kills or something along those lines. This ties in with the Gore Nest mechanic where you end up in a part of a level that is locked down until you get rid of all the demons (comedically referred to as "demonic presence returned to safe levels" once completed - which does amuse! XD ) but it does interrupt the flow. The original games had you just hunting for the exit, or the keycards (present here) to work your way to the exit...killing all the monsters wasn't the main objective, it was instead a secondary objective that you as the player put upon yourself. Like completing an entire episode/game with just the pistol as your solitary weapon.

In effect, its like the Predator rooms in the Batman games except instead of methodically removing each threat with increasing levels of difficulty on the game's part and creativity on yours, its the same repeated and quickly tiresome roadblock when I just wanted to speed through as per the old games. It kind of felt that Id/Bethesda wanted to impose their own playstyle as opposed to letting the original playstyle room to breathe and entirely take over as it should have done.

Outside of the Gore Nest locked down rooms, there's good fun to be had, amusing moments, and the nostalgic touches are welcome. Although playing the classic maps only highlights how the modern enemies are not a direct replacement for the old ones, which actually dampens the nostalgia on one hand and gives it a fresh take that may or may not be welcome. Your mileage may vary on this. Likewise the Quake power-ups don't feel right here (Quad Damage? Really? I'd have liked to have seen Doomguy pick it up, look at it, and then throw it away!).

On sale though? You can't go wrong and, at the very least, you'll quite a few hours of of solid entertainment and a good grasp of what made the originals great, even with pesky modern elements trying to sneak in. Rating: 4/5 BOOM-click-clicks!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is much more similar to Shadow Warrior 2 than the great Doom games that came before it.

You cannot save your game when you want, the checkpoint system is awful and will waste hours of your time.

Demons flash and become stunned when they are low on health and you are expected to melee them. The maps are not open and are restrictive in movement and you will have little room to maneuver compared to previous titles.

There is a character progression system with advancement points found on the map just like in shadow warrior.

Your character will start out with half of the potential health and armor, meaning everything
will two shot you before upgrades.

It is not a bad game but Doom fans may be disappointed and find that this game is more similar to Shadow Warrior 2 than the previous Doom games they loved. As a Doom fan I would not recommend.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game RIPS AND TEARS! I was skeptical at first but decided to give it a try and I'm so glad I did. The campaign is wonderful with just enough story to keep you interested, but centered solely on making you feel like a badass who destroys the minions of hell. It features fast paced combat and very few cut scenes. The game is meant to be played on Ultra-Violence, but I feel it truly shines on Nightmare. The one con is the multiplayer. It's rather lackluster and the emotes take away from the overall feel of the game, making it more akin to fortnight without the overdone battle royale. If you're looking for an excellent single player FPS that really redefines the genre, this is the one for you. Key words here are Push Forward Combat. Slay on!\t\t\t\t\t
