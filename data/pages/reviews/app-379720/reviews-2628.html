\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBest shooter in recent memory. Has the chaotic Doom feel and tons of little in-game extras to make you smile in between demonic genocide runs. The visuals are stunning, and overall the game is just FUN to play. There are plenty of difficulty settings for everyone, and the multiplayer mode holds it's own as well. I spent most of my time with this game on the campaign, which was very Doom-like, plus a little extra. Lots of hidden features, in-game-minigames and throwbacks to the original series.

The only problem I had with this is that the arena-based combat format was predictable and a bit repetitive, and the game is super-linear (which works well for this format). Once you realize that you're going from one circular arena to the other, and the only thing which changes is the scenery and the number/level of monsters spawned, the game can feel a bit repetitive, although each arena does present a unique challenge, so you can't just use the same tactics from arena to arena and expect to survive. Additionally, there isn't a lot of randomization in the game, monster spawns are almost always pre-determined and don't change much between replays. This is good for speed-runners and OCD Steve Wiebe type completionists, bad for providing a feeling of a unique experience between playthroughs.

Overall I highly recommend this game, it's just FUN to play!\t\t\t\t\t

THE DOOMSLAYER

Despite the fact that I was barely one year old when id Software released Doom in 1993, the game has always been part of growing up. From playing Doom and its older brother Doom II on floppies, the quirky Super NES version, Zandronum mods, all the way to the oddly modern Brutal Doom, the greatest child of the Johns Romero and Carmack is, without a doubt, the most important game of my life. It's an adventure that holds up amazingly well to this day, a testament to the id team's almost miraculous sense of game design, codifying the rules of a genre that had only just been born.

To say I was not at all excited for id's return to the series 23 years later would be an understatement. None of the original team was present, and the company hadn't been relevant for years, with their last release - Rage - having a fairly muted reception. The extremely rough beta of the generic multiplayer, with its modern progression system and focus on unlocks, did absolutely nothing to cool my distrust of what I was sure would be a weak cash-in on a classic name.

I somehow ended up buying it on launch day - a decision that was two-thirds alcohol and one-third Steam having my credit card information saved - and somewhere between being handed a gun within the first five seconds, a beautifully self-aware rejection of pushy FPS storytelling, and the colossal industrial metal soundtrack driving every single combat arena, I was won over.

And I haven't lost a single bit of interest in the year since. If anything, decisions that may have seemed uninspired or pandering when I first played appear outright brilliant now. So much so that I would go so far as to say that DOOM is a perfect distillation of the essence of action video games.

Sure, DOOM is a one-trick pony. In the same way that Mad Max: Fury Road is a car chase with a movie built around it, DOOM is a series of hyperviolent arena bloodbaths that funnel you immediately from one to the next. Any pretense of story or artificial complexity is merely window dressing. While that first seemed a bit lazy from a developement perspective, it serves to do one very important thing: remove all obstacles between the player, and what the player really wants to do - ♥♥♥♥ ♥♥♥♥ up.

Not content to settle for just taking the training wheels off, DOOM does everything to encourage you to play as quickly and recklessly as possible. In a stroke of design brillians and simplicity, DOOM has no enemies with hitscan weapons. None. There is no conceiveable reason why a player should take cover, and why would there be? Any Doomslayer worth his salt should be in the fray, knee deep in the dead. All aspects of the gameplay encourage this, from the much higher than average base speed of the player, to the fact that the easiest way to regenerate health is to perform a Glory Kill.

Those Glory Kills, far from being the flashy bit of pointlessness that so many games tend to leave them as, are absolutely essential. They cause slain enemies to drop scarce health pickups, or ammo when shredded with the chainsaw. Since there's rarely going to be a time where you aren't shooting or getting shot at, without Glory Kills you are at a serious handicap. This serves to get the player moving, and are a reward in and of themselves, deliciously brutal and smoothly transistion to and from the shooting without a hitch.

Even the more modern systems of weapons upgrades and mods fit the game naturally. They integrate perfectly with the side focus on map exploration, as more secrets award you with more points to gain the ridiculous number of gun upgrades available. Far from a distraction, they are the closest link to the original Doom and its replayability. When you know the secrets, every replay becomes something plannable - get this secret first, follow this weapon upgrade path, beat the game faster. Speedrunners should adore this game. Not to mention the fact that it's almost essential for survival in the tough-as-nails Nightmare difficulty.

Aesthetic shoutouts go to the goofy 90's death metal artstyle and the absolutely pitch perfect soundtrack by Mick Gordon. If the millions of listens on his Spotify account are anything to go by, DOOM is going to be a classic solely for its BGM. The ambient sections are reminiscent of some of the best Metroid Prime material (the highest compliment I can give), but the battle tracks are where it really shines. One part Nine Inch Nails, two parts Godflesh, and a few spinkles of Slipknot, DOOM is absolutely as metal as it needs to be, without descending into the cheese that you might have worried about.

I could gush about his game for pages, honestly, and I hope id has a sequel up their sleeve, because we really need another round of the pure, unadulterated gameplay that DOOM offers. All killer, no filler, no microtransaction ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥, just you, your boomstick, and masses of hellspawn. Rip and tear.

(Oh, and the multiplayer is actually pretty kickass now too.)
