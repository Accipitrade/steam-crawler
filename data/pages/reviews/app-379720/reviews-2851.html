\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell, well, well, Bethesda. It appears you have my attention.

In the interest of fairness, I should say that I had minimal expectations from DOOM. I wasn't terribly thrilled with Wolfenstein: TNO, and although the steady trickle of trailers for this looked objectively great, it just seemed like it was going to be another middle-of-the-road, big-budget shooter with lofty aspirations but limited delivery on its promises. I was fully expecting a middling, three- to four-hour long campaign that was scarcely more than a tutorial for the multiplayer, and, as someone who seldom indulges in online gaming as a result of anything besides pure inertia, I was completely prepared to overlook this game.

And then, one morning, finding myself in uncharacteristic excess of free time, I said "F%@$ it," downloaded it, and fired it up.

And holy moley did those feels from 1993 come bubbling up as soon as I curb-stomped my first zombie.

To say that DOOM is a good game would be like saying "steak is meat." Duh, everyone knows it's good by now. The issue is, very few reviews have been able to adequately clear up the important question of WHY this game is such a time-sink. Naturally, it's very polished technically, with airtight controls, a skill-based progression system, interesting level design, and enough content to keep you occupied for weeks if not months (assuming, of course, you are one of those completionists who absolutely, positively has to get every last needle out of that haystack.) Those are the game's positives, sure, but this level of polish is expected from, if not necessarily delivered by, every Triple-A title these days. No, what sets DOOM apart from the crowd is the small stuff.

A while ago, I reviewed Wolfenstein: TNO. I expressed some favorable thoughts about the gameplay, glossing over the obligatory sewer level (the less is said about that, the better.) The crux of my argument was that, in spite of being an extremely good shooter, W:TNO struggled to find its footing in one very important area: pacing. It told its story well, but frequently did so at the expense of the speed with which it delivered its narrative. The game often required you to go on extended side missions to find random crap strewn around a base, talk to people, and explore the world which the characters inhabited. Now, while I applaud the effort that went into the building of the world, I objected to doing so at the expense of the actual game. Certainly, you had the option to skip these segments, but as gamers we are often hard-wired to search every last nook and cranny, to find that missing scrap of paper with the three or four words that would give you a tasty morsel about the way the world operates. Is that a bad thing? No, but it's seldom fun when you have to slam the brakes on the Nazi-killing and go on a fetch quest for a side character.

Well, DOOM basically says "you know what? F@&^ all that, let's murder some demons." It leaves all of its story in, but it's communicated to the player with such finesse and care that you barely even notice it's there. You walk away from the game feeling thoroughly satisfied, but not mentally drained. You feel like you were an active part of this world, rather than just a collection of pixels interacting with other, demon-shaped pixels via the judicious application of gun-shaped pixels. The meat of DOOM's narrative lurks in the background, in the hidden nooks and dim hallways off the beaten path that only a committed gamer would explore, but the jist of the story is on display, written in bold, red letters dripping with the blood of your slain enemies.

Consider the Doomslayer, aka the protagonist, aka the Doom Guy. He's an entirely silent protagonist, but there is so much personality to his silence that we hardly notice or care. From the first moment of the game, as he literally tears apart an enemy, we get the distinct impression he is not to be messed with. His personality isn't in the way he talks, but in the way he moves and acts: from the casual way in which he removes a corpse's arm to unlock a fingerprint pad on a door; to the satisfying crunch that accompanies every Glory Kill he perpetrates; to the casual fury with which he pries a shotgun from a the cold, dead hands of an enemy. He is anger personified. He is not the terrified knight trapped in the world of Lordran, wary of every shadow. He IS the shadow that the demons are afraid of, and good God does that approach make you feel powerful for the game's entire duration!

The less is said about multiplayer, the better: if you're spending your time with the game's various MP modes, then you know why you play this game. I would argue that they are underrepresented and lack substance, especially when compared other, more involved MP titles (to say nothing of the core game's single player component), but they can be fun in small doses. The SnapMap module, similarly, does an adequate job of giving the creative types among us considerable replayability, but as I am not one of said creative types, I putzed around with it just enough to determine that I'd rather be playing the campaign instead, again and again. So, your mileage may vary on those modes, and that mileage is largely dependent on your desire to commit yourself to them over the campaign.

One last, albeit extremely important, thing about DOOM: it's incredibly generous with its system specs. This may not seem like a big selling point, but in an age of crappy console ports, DOOM stands out as a game that can run very well on a mid-range system (read: mine) while feeling enjoyable and looking amazing. By contrast, those with powerhouse rigs will absolutely be rewarded with some of the most incredible visuals of 2016, combined with extremely even 60fps rates and relatively quick load times. I don't know if this is something games typically leverage, but in my book this has scored DOOM some serious brownie points (as someone who grew up with Origin's 90s games, which you couldn't even boot up without massive upgrades to your computer's hardware, I consider this a tremendous asset to DOOM's overall design).

So yeah, Bethesda. Keep it up. Do more of this, and you might just make me a less jaded human -- at least where games are concerned!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am so ambivalent about this game.

First, I'll state the obvious: everyone involved in making this game should be very proud of themselves. The the game looks fantastic, conjuring up hellscapes that are somehow varied and thematic. The combat flows flawlessly, both in terms of control and pacing, so every encounter is thrilling. I usually ignore sound as much as possible, but I'm constantly struck by the haunting ambience and the contextual music that is SO HEAVY AND SO AWESOME that it tricks me into thinking that I enjoy industrial metal and that I enjoy straightforward shooters. It's true to its classic predecessors without being slavish. Perhaps most importantly, there is no crafting element.

So I say without reservation or hyperbole that this is one of the best games of its type, if not the best. Bravissimo, Bethesda.

That said: I'm not sure that I'm cut out for this type of game anymore. Because the type is a very good shooty action game with tons of backtracking and wayfinding and getting lost and looking for keys and doing first person jumping puzzles a lot even though there's a stingy checkpoint save system (WHAT'S WITH THE CHECKPOINTS, Y'ALL?!). It's challenging, yet, but it's also infuriating to meander along and figure out how to get to the checkpoints through the broken catwalks and seas of lava or whatever. It feels... not dated, because again, this thing is vital and important, but old-school. Some people like old-school; that's how I like my RPGs, f'rinstance. But I can't get behind an old-school shooter, at least not in the required doses.

This is a game that begs you to play in short bursts, but the backtracking and the checkpoint saves (G-DD-MN CHECKPOINT SAVES) make it difficult to do so. You never know what you're losing when you quit the game out of frustration, or if you'll ever find your way back to what you're supposed to be doing. I'd leave it on my hard drive, but even in the age of nearly infinite storage, this game asks a lot; it's 80 GB, BY FAR the largest game I've ever installed. I had plenty of space, and now I have none.

It's a beautiful monster, but one I'm not sure I have the room (literal and metaphorical) for it in my life. Still, if you're more patient than I am and have even a passing interest in the shooter genre, you can't go wrong with DOOM.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great game that is a lot of fun.

Personally, I dont like the multiplayer so the longevity is limited but the campaign is good. This is not FEAR. Dont expect creepy. Expect a lot of blasting of hellspawn with some familiar seeming weapons, if you're old like me.

One of the stand out aspects of the game is the graphical quality, particularly the lighting and effects. The balance between light and dark, shadows is also particularly good. Texture are quite impressive in the main. The metals look metallic and there has been some imagination behind the design. The architecture and level design is very good.

What made this stand out for me is that the PC port is bery well done. I can find nothing about the port to complain about. Given the company behind it, I was going to skip it entirely as I thought it would just be a nother terrible console ported game. Glad I was encouraged to get it.

This is a highly polished game with a fun campaign that leads you on. It isnt particularly long but there are aspects of it that will see you get a solid 30-35 hours out of it without stringing it out for the sake of it.

The audio balancing is a bit off for me. Could have been enhanced by creepier effects. They are there but not brought to the fore for some reason.

It has some nice quasi character development to keep you interested though in reality its a first person shooter. This adds to the feeling that the choices you make matters and gives you some unlocks to work for along the way rather than just charging through the levels without it becoming overly tedious.

However this is a really well ported, well designed game that looks brilliant and plays very well indeed.\t\t\t\t\t
