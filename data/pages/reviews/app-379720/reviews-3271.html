\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, I'm reviewing strictly based on the single player campaign, because I got enough of a feel of the multiplayer during the beta not to feel tempted to try it again.

If you want an FPS that is actually an FPS, that doesn't hide behind a story, huge progression trees or miles of generic levels, look no further. Id knows how to take a simple task, aim and shoot, and make it an exercise in euphoria. Every kill FEELS AMAZING. Every arena is a playground to take advantage of the incredibly fluid movement system. Glory kills fuel your tempo and keep you in the thick of things. Upgrades are focused on complimenting your playstyle. 'Feel like a badass' is an understatement. As a fan of solid FPS titles, I got what I wanted and more, and I am SO happy to have such a vastly different opinions on single player than I did from multiplayer.

Outside the Rip and Tear, the exploration and secrets are just enough to be entertaining, and from my experience fairly optional, as it mainly just gets you upgrades (and in some cases guns) just a little bit earlier. There's also a small upgrade path that gives you much more 'help' finding things, in case you are don't enjoy scouring the level completely unaided AND you can replay levels to hunt down those you missed. The only boost type I would highly recommend keeping an eye out for are the Argent Cells; they give permanent stat boosts (health | armor | ammo), which is super useful for the more insane arena areas and especially the bosses.

Speaking of which, the only real weakness that stuck out to me in the game were the bosses. They weren't 'bad', as they focused on the quintessential boss elements; learning patterns to conserve health, figuring out how efficiently deal damage, and how to trigger the 'stagger' phases, which re-up your health and/or ammo. But, they didn't take nearly as much advantage of the movement system or weapon types as the arenas areas did. They were all set in flat, circular 'coliseum pit' areas, and revolved around strafing and peppering the boss with your more efficient weapon mods. (Oh, and a personal peeve that really only affects a handful of areas; MAKE STAIRCASE LEDGES CLIMBABLE).

I think my opinion is fairly clear at this point; DOOM single player is a masterpiece, a worthy successor to its genre-defining roots, and absolutely worth the price, even if you never touch the the multiplayer. I have every intention of replaying this campaign many, many times, aided by the many difficuly levels and level select. The only recent game that I've played that comes close to this title is also rebooted Shadow Warrior, and I can only hope that these reset the par for what an FPS should be.\t\t\t\t\t

The multiplayer experience is lacking quite a bit, in my opinion. There is no co-op experience built in and the PvP has a ton of hackers which completely ruins any fun to be had. Personally, I'm not the type of person to buy a game and then spend hours of my time to build a multiplayer experience before I can actually play with my friends.
Overall, this game is great if you are looking for a single player experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's DOOM, just like the good old days. If you want an action packed thrill ride with ridiculously large and powerful weaponry, the supernatural, and plenty of throwbacks to the original, this game will fulfill your desires. DOOM brings back the run-and-gun gameplay that popularized the FPS genre and adds a few modern features.

First and foremost, DOOM is reminiscent of old school, run-and-gun FPS games. It has health and armor pickups (no health/shield regeneration), you can carry more than two weapons (ten in this case not including grenades), and realism is left weeping on the porch. Rather than have a run button, DOOM boasts a walk button because your default movement is running at a full sprint; I found this rather humorous and never really used it.

The modern additions to the tried-and-true gameplay mostly present themselves in upgrade systems. Weapons and armor can be upgraded and special, supernatural items called runes can be acquired and equiped to further alter your abilities. Armor upgrades can alter the gameplay, but not by much; they typically make adjustments to abilities you already have. Likewise, runes alter your existing abilities. Weapon modifications, however, add alt-fire options and genuinely alter the gameplay.

Aesthetic design of the UAC installation on Mars and certain other environments hit all the right notes. Expect an excess of gore as the game goes on and a few chilling scenes. DOOM is definitely not for the faint of heart.

Now, for the cons. The game has a repetitive cycle that may wear down some players: Enter a room, get locked in the room, demons teleport in, kill them all, the room unlocks, and you move to the next room. In between rooms, you can expect exploration and secrets. While it does get repetitive, the variety of weapons, environments, and enemy combinations helps keep it somewhat fresh each time.

Overall, I love it. It's a breath of fresh air. DOOM's back, baby!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've hated AAA games for a while at this point; they've conditioned me to expect nothing good or fun from a reboot or modernization of an old game or the schlock that gets peddled and shilled all over the internet (granted that DOOM also got a lot of advertising in the same vein as well). DOOM (4) is the first game in a long time to give me hope, especially from an idthesda game, that it's possible for the old school to get a fresh coat of paint and still look good.

The game is raw and non-stop combat with large sprawling levels with lots of vertical and horizontal movement and plenty of demons to blast. Visuals have only been getting better since the Build Engine, and as much as I've hated the modern idtech engine games (MEGATEXTURES DURRRR), idthesda has managed to actually make them work. There are lots of little gameplay details that keep the game moving (weapon wheel bound to one button, fast "glory kills" that give back health a la 40k Space Marine, etc). It's one of the best games I've played in a long time and I don't regret paying release price. Their version of the Reflex map-maker and Halo 3's FORGE is great as well; doesn't have the customization of a Build Engine .wad but the gameplay is still there and at the end of the day, that's what really matters.

Multiplayer blows, though. That's just Halo with a reskin. Play it for a bit if you want, but you're not gonna have a whole lot of long-term fun.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRECOMMENDED!

On top of graphics quality, it's easy to achieve + maintain. I use a single factory-clocked GTX 960, and manage practically Ultra on 1080p (Working on 4k). I had to turn 2 things down 1 notch to achieve my 60 fps, but other than that, Ultra on 1080p.

Gameplay is another aspect that is really well done. It may seem boring watching other people play and uneventful except for the occasional demon hoardes you run into, but trust me, it flows.

Storyline is done pretty similarly to the Ultimate DOOM's, but slightly better explained and characters (besides yourself) have names.

Last thing to brag about is how much tribute it pays to the older DOOM series. It follows the storyline, has the throwback secret rooms , and uses almost the exact same guns.

The only thing there is to complain about here is multiplayer. Take COD and HALO, they make a baby, that kid becomes acquaintances with DOOM, and then is brothers with Quake. In a nutshell, not DOOM. I absolutely hate the multiplayer. Could have been done so much better. But besides that, WONDERFUL game, GREAT JOB ID! LOVE IT!\t\t\t\t\t
