This is a truncated review because of Steam’s upper character limit for reviews. For the full critique, please watch the video.

Doom 2016 is a game of duality. It’s a modern AAA shooter that harkens back to the classics that defined the genre. It adapts the gameplay of the originals to suit a modern audience, but it also seems unimpressed by a lot of additions that were made to the genre over the past couple of decades. You see this divide between the old and the new everywhere in Doom 2016 and this contrast between what id embraced from more recent shooters and what they chose to keep from the original Doom is both its biggest achievement and its greatest downfall.

Let me start by saying that when Doom 2016 fires on all cylinders, when you’re in the middle of fighting the legions of hell to a kickass soundtrack, it’s a bloody riot. The original Doom was already a power trip, but it was obviously limited by the technology of the era it was made in and some of the gameplay aspects are very much of its time. Doom 2016 is basically a kept promise that the games industry made 30 years ago. They told us to imagine how cool this stuff would be if it were rendered in a believable, lifelike style. And Doom 2016 delivers on that promise like no other game I’ve ever played. It’s visceral, exhilarating, fast, frenetic and fun. Even the pre-baked finishing moves are implement in a way that adds to the high-octane gameplay without detracting from the rhythm of the fight, as it usually happens in most other games.

However, you might be forgiven for thinking that Doom 2016 is nothing more than a recreation of what happens when you stick your hand in a blender and hit the purée switch. But remember how I said how Doom 2016 is a game of duality at the very beginning of this review and how it’s adherence to traits from the original as well as some modern additions were also part of where Doom falters? Well, let’s discuss these points some more.

The original Doom didn’t just consist of mowing down hundreds of Demons. In order to progress through its labyrinthine levels, you had to find colored keycards to unlock specific doors. And Doom 2016 once again re-uses this aspect of level progression. And I have to say that I was never a big fan of game design that worked this way. It always just seems like a cheap timewaster to me to have some locked off door somewhere that you have to find a key for, when you could just as easily design the level in a way that would have you battle your way through it in one direction without backtracking.

Speaking of backtracking through empty levels, here’s another thing Doom 2016 recreates from the originals: Secrets. There are a bunch of secrets to find in almost every level, ranging from collectible figurines to straight up weapon and suit upgrades. And it’s not like those secrets are truly secret. They’re highlighted on your map, taunting you with their presence in order to waste time you could be using for killing enemies in order to run around these empty levels after you’ve killed everybody in order to fill up that pointless upgrade tree.

And while I mention the challenges and how they grant access to upgrades, most levels also give you direct, specific challenges which detracts from the free-flow combat that the game excels at. I’m playing Doom, for crying out loud. I don’t want to be methodical and pick and choose weapons and combat styles in order to tick off a checklist so I can unlock more upgrades. If you truly have to have an upgrade system for weapons in a game called Doom, why not just unlock these upgrades periodically once you complete levels? It’s like the game wants to constantly tease you away from the excellent combat to do something else.

Another thing the game constantly wants you to do instead of ripping and tearing through enemies is reading. Oh there’s so much reading to be done in the codex that you could be forgiven for thinking that this was a Mass Effect game. And yes, I read all the codex entries, which were all flat,boring, matter of fact attempts at making the Doom universe seem grounded in real science, even though it’s chockful of nonsensical technobabble. There’s also tons of stuff in there that chronicles the doom slayer’s previous adventures and that he’s some legendary badass among the legions of hell who vehemently fought against his return when the UAC sent expeditions to hell. It’s so goofy, especially when contrasted with the serious tone of the writing. No amount of vocal effects on the narrator who retells the doom slayer’s achievements makes this stuff sound epic.

I don’t know why id did this. They knew that players didn’t care about why you’re here, because the reason you’re here is obvious: You’re the doom slayer. Here be some demons, kill they ass. That’s enough justification for anybody playing Doom. So why is it then that we get paragraphs upon paragraphs of flavorless flavor text and constant intermissions where we’re being forced to sit through conversations that tell us why this futuristic corporation decided it was a good idea to tap into literal Hell for energy?

And while I’m already criticizing the game, one thing that I’d like to mention is that I’m also not the biggest fan of how combat encounters play out. Most of the time, enemies just spawn in in waves. I guess this is another way of giving players hordes of enemies to deal with without frying processors and GPUs by having dozens of active enemies on screen at any given time: Just stagger them out and spawn them in once the previously loaded enemies have been killed. This might be an efficient way of managing the flow of combat and the number of enemies the player has to deal with, but it also means that you have absolutely no way of knowing how long the fight will last and what tactics you should employ. This is most apparent whenever a combat arena contains some kind of powerup, like the berserk mode, quad damage or haste. Because picking those up during the start of the fight is usually pointless, since fights tend to get more difficult over time. But you can’t know if those four revenants are supposed to be the climax of that fight or only a prelude to two Barons of Hell that the game spawns in right at the end.

The way the combat encounters play out over the course of the campaign becomes fairly predictable as well. If you’re about to enter an arena, you can bet your ass that the first couple of enemies will be some Hell Knights and Revenants, then usually a Summoner, then come the Cacodemons and Mancubi and in the later levels, the Pinkes and Spectres, while all of the waves get interspersed by a number of cannon fodder enemies. And to top the later fights off, the game usually spawns two Barons of Hell. Pretty much all of the bigger arena battles in the second half of the game follow a pattern like this.

Now I know how this might look. I’ve spent the biggest part of this review criticizing Doom 2016, but I still like it. I want to reiterate, when you’re in the midst of battle making demon go boom, that’s when the game absolutely shines and that alone is worth recommending. And I don’t need to waste half a review telling you that that stuff is absolutely awesome. It’s all the rest that surrounds it, the unnecessary side-tracking through keycards and secrets, the bland upgrade systems, the lifeless narrative and the perfunctory flavor text that unfortunately detract from what Doom 2016 does best and that’s what needs mentioning. Doom 2016 told players to rip and tear, so let us ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ rip and tear. I know, asking for less ambition in a game is rare but let’s be frank here, this is Doom. You only have to get one thing right and that’s pointing and shooting an ever-increasing arsenal of awesome feeling guns. You don’t need to reinvent the wheel and you don’t need to add blinking lights and other nonsense to the wheel. Just rip and tear is enough.\t\t\t\t\t
