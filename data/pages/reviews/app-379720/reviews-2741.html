\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBethesda and Id have done the impossible - rebooted Doom again and made it amazing. It's true to the spirit of the original Doom and fits the tone of the series exactly. The soundtrack is very metal, the plot is full of camp, and the visuals are bloody, shiny, and delightful. There's no reloading, no cover button, and (importantly) no unwelcome secondary DRM.

That having been said, the latest Doom does change a lot of the classic Doom's gameplay, story, and design... and it's genuinely impressive how much of it is for the better, especially given that half of these changes sound terrible in theory. Let's start with the first-person parkour, which is easily the worst-sounding new feature that's somehow excellent in practice. Every level and nearly every firefight encourages platforming where it's not required outright, and you haven't got legs to look down at, or a gaint Mario 64 shadow circle, or even much time to be looking where you're going in the first place with all the enemies popping in hither and yon. The baffling thing is that it's actually very intuitive and does help keep the pace of the game sped up and the levels interesting. While I'm at it, it's got a very simplified version of a Bethesda RPG system built into the core gameplay. You're always picking up new upgrade points, hidden items, and otherwise levelling up... and it's shocking just how much better it makes the game, especially after Fallout 4. Most guns compete for ammo with other guns, like the two shotguns, the two rapidfire projectile rifles, and the two energy rifles. You'll also blow a cooldown for all of your grenades simultaneously when you use one of them, but they're otherwise infinite. Somehow, not only does this not ruin the game, but it actually gives you the only motivation you'll ever be presented with to swap your favorite weapon(s) for the other ones. And unlike Doom 3's attempt at a more serious plot, Doom(4)'s storyline (without spoiling too much) is essentially about the Doom Marine helping his boss (a large, grumpy robot) stop the UAC's lead researcher (an evil, cyborg grandma) from opening a portal to Hell on Mars. The odd thing (in any other context, at least) is that it's actually much better than the alternative.

It's not flawless, however. The audio sample rate tops off at 48khz for some reason, and anything above that can cause distorted sound. The in-game vsync does a very halfhearted job and (Nvidia) hardware vsync is only slightly better. The ammo carrying limits for most guns are painfully small, even when upgraded to the maximum extent possible, and any gun that runs out of ammo will autoswitch you to your default shotgun, whether you want it to or not. During the late levels of the game I realized how much more fun I'd be having if there were simply more ammo to collect and the chainsaw was completely gone. Instead it's balanced around the chainsaw's passive "ammo pinata" capabilities, which are supposed to keep the game flowing by making enemies spawn a full allotment of every ammo on death. That would be fine if it weren't for the tiny maximum fuel count the chainsaw has and the fact that it's impossible to know without prior experience whether there'll be any fuel in the next room, or even in the rest of the level you're in, and where. It also suffers from Bethesda level design, making long, confusing, crudely looping treks inevitable, although mercifully more rare than is usual for a Bethesda game.

All things considered, Doom(4) is a masterpiece. It's entirely true to its roots, yet is still one of the best proper FPS games in modern times. It's got many design elements that, in any other game, would be severe flaws, but improve the game and evolve the series considerably. I spent a good deal of the downtime induced by card key mazes conjecturing about which of Doom(4)'s assets would be repurposed verbatim in the next Elder Scrolls game(s), as often happens with Bethesda's games... but for a change, it actually didn't bother me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe 4th installment of the DOOM series is quite the enjoyable experience. With gameplay never getting old, a variety of weapons and deathblows, leaving one with the feeling of a great satisfaction after every gorey battle, and a rewarding system for exploring the environments of a hell-ridden space station in mars and, well, Hell itself.

I thoroughly enjoyed DOOM. Although I haven't played the other installments, I saw this one and just had to play it. Slaughtering armies of all kinds of challenging demons in the most brutal way possible sounds like a great time, and it's programmed well. Plus, the frequent challenges each stage gives the player, encouraging discovering more of the environment, and even triggering a takedown I'd yet to see. Combat is very nice and fluid, and the weapons are interesting and all are fairly useful throughout the entire game.

The story is explained through exploration, via finding data logs describing the environments, demon types, weapons, powerups, characters, and audio logs. But exploration offers so much more to the player. Upgrades to all weaponery, chips from super soldiers to increase a weapons stats, little collectibles, and so much more. I really like this because it encourages the player to search the environment in order to become stronger. Secrets are a fantastic implement on enjoying the land layed out and rewarding players for taking the time to explore, and DOOM succeeds perfectly at this.

It's a darn shame the multiplayer isn't played as much. Although I had a fairly short time with it, and had fun nonetheless, most of the time spent was on finding other players. It's obvious people don't play it too much, for it not being sold as a multiplayer game originally. And Steam decided to run a maintenence check in the middle of Infected, cutting my already short time even shorter.

DOOM does a very good job of giving the player a great bloody experience, with many challenges and varieties of ways to take every situation, and getting stronger after every fight.\t\t\t\t\t
