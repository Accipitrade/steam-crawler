\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is solid, but I certainly don't feel like it's the second coming of christ, even looking back at the year it released. An enjoyable experience for people who would like an action platformer with an arena shooter mixed in.

Doom 2016 was crowned as king of the old school shooter renaissance period, and I think most of it is simply the name attached to it, and devs behind it. While the movement and no weapon limit is certainly old school, it is nowhere as faithful as Rise of the Triad was, even Shadow Warrior reboot is a bit more faithful in its structure. Doom 2016 is actually a departure from the classic formula, by making it more of a platformer than it ever was before.
The main gameplay is utilizing your fast paced movement, jumping, double jumping and clambering, to move around the map efficiently, you reach new places to gather more resources, get away from monsters, and look for obscure vent entrances, unlikely climbable objects, or hidden usable items to explore and gather more abilities and unlockables. Abilities that allow for more freedom of movement mid-air, or faster clamber animation. The damage over time hazards on the ground are usually minimal, and sometimes you gotta watch out for death pits, insta kill laser walls, or just death from falling, which is either lethal or completely harmless.
Combat has similarities to the classic doom, selection of weapons, all accessible at a press of a button, lots of strafing, quick movement, and a lot of classic enemies. Even here we have the added dimension of platforming, where clambering over catwalks by double jumping lets you find cover both vertically and horizontally, looking around for ammo now happens during fights more so, than just retreating to a room full of ammo like in og Doom. Some of the resources like health, armor and ammo are now also acquirable through finishers, if you stagger an enemy it will light up, and depending on the color, you will know when you're in range to finish an enemy off, making you stand in place for a bit, but with some goodies as a reward. At first the animations are ungodly slow, and I must say that an upgrade to speed them up is a must, especially if you plan on using that mechanic often for challenges etc., I tried to avoid it unless in a pinch.
The regular enemy encounters are usually pretty easy, where the combat gets difficult is arenas with waves, there you can learn a layout and jump around efficiently, to make yourself as hard to hit as possible, while peppering the demons spawning around. Lastly are the bosses, which have all sorts of projectiles and aoe attacks, that you can dodge using your platforming options, like doublejumping over them, clambering onto newly made pillars, and at one point even ducking underneath.
A new feature to the series is permanent upgrades, be it to your base stats, minor buffs, choice of 3 ability buffs (most regarding finishers) and new addons for your weapons' alt fires. Adds a really good amount of variety in the gameplay, but engaging with a combat focus progression, will naturally push you further into platforming exploration, which has significantly less upgrades in comparison.

Music and sounds are really nice and bassy, some of the tracks sound good enough to listen to outside of the game. Once again, I don't see it as second coming of christ, and while metal influenced osts were uncommon around the time, I found music in DS3 to be much better, and Mick's work on Prey felt more interesting to me. Still, the gun and enemy sounds is definitely where its at, and while I think they overdid the bass at places, it was overall enjoyable to listen to.

Visually it's impressive, I like the art direction and re-imagined enemies from first 3 games, and the levels, especially inside hell. The animations and vfx are very flashy, I clap for the triple A publisher company, for as always pumping ungodly amounts of money into graphical fidelity, driving the ever stagnating gameplay innovations, thanks to people buying games for their looks. At least Doom is a very unconventional game at the same time, or rather, an unconventional mix, of 2 pretty well known genres.

Story wise, I like how minimal it is, but I don't like how it stops you dead in your tracks, the killing machine has to stay locked inside a small room, while holograms flash and spout exposition. A very inelegant way of delivery, in a game that begs to be as hands off as possible, but unskippable cutscenes and walking sections are a plague not easily extinguished, unfortunately partially because of a HL2, which I really liked.
At least it's over the top enough in the end, where I don't feel to bitter about being strapped into a clockwork orange chair for a few minutes sometimes.

The game goes for very cheap nowadays, I don't see a fault in playing it, and while I still prefer Shadow Warrior reboot (the first game), for my 2016 era shooting needs, I think it's a fun game to go through and have a bit of a nostalgia wave, flow over you.\t\t\t\t\t
