\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor new players, Doom offers an interesting break from the usual first-person shooter style you might be familiar with (Call of Duty, Halo 5, etc.) and instead puts a focus on in-your-face, bloody combat. You'll be close with your enemies in this game, rather than picking them off from a longer distance. And it's this close-quarters focus that really brings out the game's intensity. It's constant, fast-paced action all the way through, with only occasional breaks from the carnage.

For classic Doom veterans, this game is not too far from the norm of a Doom game. It's fast-paced, visceral and brutal. You can feel like a true badass again, destroying anything in your path, but you're never shy of a challenge. The demons will mess you up if you don't keep yourself killing. And of course, just like any true Doom game, you should always be on the move.

The campaign has a nice story to it, but it rarely forces you to pay attention to it. You spend more time killing demons, which is a great thing. Most story segments you can actually just walk away from and continue killing.

Doom also introduces some new mechanics, such as glory kills - fully animated executions performed against your enemies, each one gory and...well, glorious. You have a variety of unique guns to choose from, just like any good first-person shooter, but in singleplayer, you get to modify your weapons with upgrades, new firemodes, and the like. You can also upgrade your suit abilities in singleplayer - anything from blast resistance, to a more intricate map. Further suit upgrades can be achieved through obtaining runes - gained by completing quick challenges. These runes let you get new benefits, such as armor-for-kills or increased mobility.

The multiplayer modes are, surprisingly, a really strong point of the game. Deathmatch game modes can get hectic and crazy, especially if you bring friends along. The "demon rune" - a powerup that lets you turn into a demon for a short amount of time - can be a real game changer, and ends up directing a lot of focus on the big guy.

There's also a level editor (SnapMap), which has it's design limits, but is still really fun and can be used very creatively. This level editor can make solo/co-op missions, wave-based survival games, bloodbath arenas...I even saw someone make a Harvest Moon-type game.

In both multiplayer and SnapMap, you're allowed to customize your weapons and armor to your liking. In multiplayer modes, things like colors, armor pieces, and extra loadout items are obtainable through an XP system. In SnapMap, though, you earn these by buying them with SnapPoints - an entirely in-game currency gained by playing and creating SnapMap levels. This system keeps you playing and creating for a good while.

Overall, Doom is an amazing game for both veterans and newcomers to the series, and has an outstanding amount of content to keep you going. I would say that id Software definitely delivered us the true return to the series everyone has been waiting for - because believe me, Doom 3 sure wasn't that.\t\t\t\t\t
