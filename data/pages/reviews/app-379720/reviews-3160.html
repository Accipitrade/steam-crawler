\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a man who has logged, some would say wasted, hundreds of hours with the original Doom and its sequel as well as the thousands of user-designed wads, both in my childhood and recently in ZDoom, I can safely say this is a worthy follow-up. It doesn't really approach the legendary heights of its forebears and the developers have made one monumental mistake, but it's as good as we were going to get when the devs were even remotely interested in making a modern game.

Playing through the game, I felt like the design philosophy of Doom 4 was to take the nameless marine from the original Doom and Doom 2 and drop him into a straightlaced modern remake of Doom, and laugh maniacally as he tears everything apart. While I thought Duke Nukem Forever was fine for what it was, one of the galling things about it was the 'modern shooter' trappings weighing it down, which Doom 4 completely avoids by having this approach to things. The Marine doesn't move nearly as fast as in the original games, but it's far, far quicker than any modern shooter. There is no 'realistic' limit on the number of weapons you can carry, and, perhaps most important and subtlest of all, there is no reloading. That's the one they usually forget about, and my hat's off to ID for that.

The combat is plentiful and fixating, and doesn't just rehash the original, being less about herding groups of enemies to follow you and more about outmaneuvering them, and borrowing slightly from Brutal Doom with the glory kills to make the game feel as visceral and distressing as the original felt. You couldn't do this by just translating it with modern graphics, because the gameplay in the originals now seems quaint. The game looks great, and although it is worth considering I'm slightly above the recommended specs, the game ran at a smooth 60fps with all setting maxed.

The story is goofy but I never wanted it to be anything but a backdrop for nonstop action, and I honestly appreciate the world the devs built, mainly in supplemental log materials but also in several super-metal narrations in which you learn how the denizens of Hell view you via the tale of the 'Doom Slayer'. It gives you a good feel for the cutthroat, martial, Nietzchean culture of Hell and how they regard you with respect as well as fear and loathing. Speaking of metal, although younger players will probably be into it, the music was a problem for me. I don't particularly like dubstep in my metal, although I suppose since the original was blatantly ripped-off tracks by metal bands, they had to do something else. I would have preferred to stick with the originals in updated synth form or a more traditional synth score. The soundtrack is a terrible mess as far as I'm concerned. Again, this probably won't be a problem for you.

There are several glaring issues with the game, but none of them are dealbreakers. I like the weapon mod system, as well as the various upgrades that can be applied, but they ultimately feel like just needlessly complicating things, and I don't see any real reason for them to be there. I played on ultra-violence, so it's possible they're required for strategies on higher difficulties, but I would have preferred something more straightforward. I'm a big fan of RPGs and turn-based strategy, but not so crazy about the modern proliferation of these kind of systems in more action-based games where they don't belong, and I think this is something Doom 4 would have done well to have left behind along with the other modern trappings it dropped.

Map design is also a problem. I enjoy it in principal. It's very Metroid Prime, which several things about this game are, curiously enough. However, there are too many points in the game where parts of the map randomly become locked off, meaning you can't go back to pick up secrets or explore. I can remember very few if any maps that shipped with Doom and Doom 2 that did this. The level design is based around open arenas with very few if any dead ends where you can maneuver and do battle with enemies.

This is fine, but I found myself missing the monster placement from the originals. Rare were the times in Doom 1-2 where you would see an environment and know you were going to fight in it, and monsters often surprised you, forcing you to think on your feet. Virtually every battle can be planned for in Doom 4. But I didn't hate being able to prepare and adjust my strategy to the environment that much.

The weapons are great. They all look amazing and at home in the universe Doom 4 creates, and all require different strategic approaches to use. Apart from the pistol, which is one of the most embarassing things I've ever seen in any FPS. I wasn't completely happy with the sound effects on all of them, in particular the dreaded Super Shotgun doesn't have the chunkiness to its reloading noise that the original had. But that's a minor issue.

I don't care about multiplayer in general, and when I did do it I played the Quake games, so I'll defer to others who declare it lackluster. Haven't tried it myself.

The one massive mistake that I think everyone will regret is not including a full suite of editing tools. I'm shocked by this decision and can't possibly imagine what the impetus behind it was apart from tremendous stupidity. Doom and Doom 2 still have active modding/level design communities. An entire generation of game developers got their start, at some level, in that community. If there's anything that holds this planned new series back, it will be this.

Still, I had a blast playing this game and I'll probably replay the originals now. I would recommend Doom 4 to everyone. If you've never played the originals, it's a different animal to Doom 3, and it'll give you a great taste of what the older generation is talking about. If you were a fan and you've had enough of Brutal Doom, this is as close as you can get in the modern era to those halcyon days.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo start off this review, I'd like to point out the one thing I dislike with this game and that is the fact that you can't reload your weapon.

Gameplay: 10/10

Bethesda really knocked it out of the park with the mechanics of the fight. The glory kill system integrates fairly well with the basic FPS combat we all know and love. Oh and it'll take you some time to stop pressing your sprint key, because you're always sprinting! But when you get right down to it, it's simple and stupid fun action. Shoot things, stomp things, squish their faces, tear off their heads, shove your hand where the.. well.. you get the picture.

You'd think that it'd get boring after a while, but together with the other elements of the game, it comes together to form a tight and close knit combat experience that can actually leave you a bit mentally fatigued. As for the levels in the game, they're huge sprawling affairs with lots of secrets and easter eggs everywhere. The devs really want you to explore the world and quite honestly, it's a lot of fun to the point that you sometimes forget you were supposed to beat the game.

And on that note, you can now upgrade your suit and your weapons, which after a while started feeling .. eerily familar. Then it hit me, it felt like I was playing Super Metroid again. Yeah, you're a badass that are running after powerup items that make you even more of a badass. Oh and when you're done upgrading your weapons, you have to master them too by completing challenges to unlock their potential. Can I get a "replay value" from the congregation? AMEN!

Graphics: 10/10

They're glorious, there's no other way to say it. Mars and hell never looked better and I found myself staring sometimes at the skyboxes because they were hauntingly beautiful. Then there's the things only graphics nerds love, like super high quality textures, more shading than you can shake a chainsaw at, lots of grit and detail and of course a MODEST amount of bloom thrown in for gameplay purposes. Yes, I know.. few games do this these days, but DOOM actually manages to use lighting in a very good way. And it's not pitch black either which makes it even better.

Sound: 11/10

Mick Gordon. Taste the name as you say it. It's going to get repeated a lot I'd bet. The music this guy has written is the living, beating, festering heart of DOOM. Really, the game could have been a complete dud and I'd still give it 5/10 just because of the soundtrack. To put it simply: The soundtrack is as if you carved it out of a huge block of metal. It's brutal, it's satanic, it's the force that propels you forward and the unyielding energy that keeps you laughing maniacally as you slaughter your way through the game. Being an old gamer that grew up with DOOM, I can say that Mick didn't just revamp the soundtrack, he pretty much disassembled and reassembled DOOMs sounds from the ground up. If you've played Quake 4, you'll remember the horrific conveyor belt scene. THAT'S what Mick did with the soundtrack and it's the most glorious part of the game.

I can only bow my head and go \\,,/ at the guy. He's totally metal god material.

Story: 10/10

Basically, it picks up where the old games left off and continues the storyline. Between your codex, the brilliant characters and the awesome voice acting, there's a lot told about the world of DOOM. And, amazingly, your protagonist, although silent, really gets his message across a lot of the time. I'm not going to say anything more about this to avoid spoiling anything.

Overall impression: 10/10

I honestly thought DOOM was finished as a franchise, that you couldn't make a sequel that would top the monumental two first games. But Bethesda along with ID did and it's truly magical in that way you only remembered games being as a child. Also, the game has a lot of replay value since when you're done beating it, you can go back and find all the codex entries you missed, do challenges, max out weapons and ... and.. find the ORIGINAL DOOM LEVELS as well!

So yes, if you people will excuse me, I do believe it's time I started finding the next secret.
