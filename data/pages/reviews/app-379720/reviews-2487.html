\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor most of my beginnings in gaming, I played the legendary game Half-Life and the other wonderful products made by Valve. Of course, like any other FPS gamer, I knew about an even earlier pioneer in the genre: DOOM.

I have never played the original games, but when I randomly decided to buy this game and played it, I immediately felt the legacy of the series and was blown away. Hardcore vibe, fast and unrelenting movement-based combat (like my other new favorite, Quake).

This is the best single-player FPS I have ever played. With all respect to the Half-Life series, I believe this game is better for its emphasis on intense gameplay over story. The story in DOOM is basically optional and, like Half-Life, thrives by never taking you out of the protagonist's shoes. Unlike Half-Life, the main character is a faceless entity the player fills, not a fleshed out character.

It's all about the gameplay, which feels sweetly "retro" even if you never played old shooters. No nonsense, just tearing up bigger and badder demons with bigger and badder weapons until you feel like an FPS god.

I regret taking so long to get on board. This game is a steal. Buy it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fantastic title, and a fantastic addition to the DOOM series, despite its shortcomings. Single player is where it's at, and it is very well done - especially the later sections. The weapons are great, though once you get to the bigger and better guns you won't use the lower tier weapons very often, if at all. The super shotgun, in particular, will be your bread and butter, and like a fine woman you won't be interested in another weapon - unless the situation demands the use of a more powerful weapon.

When my friend first showed me DOOM 2016, I was not impressed. This is primarily due to the first couple of levels, which honestly are quite linear. However, after finally picking up a copy myself, I am happy to see the labyrinth map design. Unfortunately, DOOM 2016 still suffers from the same issue as many modern FPS titles, namely blocked doors. Meaning, there are points of no return throughout the maps, making it impossible to go hunting for secrets after you've cleared a map. So if you miss even one secret on your first playthrough you'll have to replay the entire level in order to find it.

To make matters worse, the game commits a grave sin by using checkpoints, rather than save anywhere. This really brings down the experience for me, and I'm constantly reminding myself that I can't save until I hit the next checkpoint, or end the level. As I recall, this is something even DOOM 3 got right. Save anywhere would have made the impossibility of backtracking more bareable, and it's a shame the developers chose checkpoints over that.

Coming back to the map design, specifically the aesthetics look amazing. Truly, the aesthetics design of the entire game is fantastic. UAC environments look rightfully sci-fi, even industrial. The UAC facility actually looks like what they primarily do is harvest, process, and ship argent energy, while also maintaining a tough exterior appearance that one would expect to survive the harsh Martian climate. Not too sci-fi, but not too industrial either. Hell itself is wonderfully designed. I am honestly terrified by a lot of what I see, and I often wonder if the developers have nightmares by what they must have had to research for inspiration. Well, whatever they've seen, it's really paid off.

Music is meh, I wish the tracks were assigned to the maps and not to situations. I go through corriders all too often listening to ambient sound and creepy soundtracks. So after I'm through fighting and I'm trying to find my way through the labyrinth, I get what is essentially an eerie quiet. Which does nothing but creep me out, and kind of kills whatever buzz I got from fighting. The only thing that keeps me going is the thought of finishing the labyrinth and moving onto the next fight, so some good music will start playing again.

Multiplayer... We don't talk about that. Just heed my advice and steer clear of that, and snapmap too. Not that snapmap can't be used for some pretty creative things, it's just that the limitations it places on creators is too great. I'm sure it's good for some minor entertainment, but snapmap, and other map editors, just aren't viable replacements for modding yet, and I doubt they ever will be. So steer clear of it and multiplayer, especially multiplayer.

Ultimately, DOOM 2016 is a fine addition to any DOOM fan's library and an excellent game for newcomers, though it does have a few shortcomings that prevent it from reaching its full potential. Hopefully they will be corrected in the next installment of DOOM. Regardless, I highly recommend DOOM 2016 to anyone who is even remotely interested in this title, it will not disappoint you... Too much.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe singleplayer is amazing save for a few bugs, like the game always crashing at the end of one mission. The combat is inherently satisfying, but I'm not completely happy with the upgrade system. Forcing me to use a wepon I don't enjoy using to perform a complicated kill, and then repeat it like 10-30 times is frustrating and time consuming. Same with runes. Collect 500 ammo packs? Seriously? You spend so much time doing these frustrating chores, you sometimes forget how fun the combat can be.

The multiplayer however smooth and satisfying is ruined by complete lack of balance in matchmaking. When you've got 4 max lvl players it would make sense to split them 2-2, no? The game puts 3 better players on the team with more people, giving one team a massive skill and numbers advantage. This happens ocassionaly in most multiplayer games, but is too common a occurence in DOOM, sadly.

tldr: pretty fun singleplayer.\t\t\t\t\t
