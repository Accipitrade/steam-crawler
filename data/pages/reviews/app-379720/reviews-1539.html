\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Doom reboot is a great game. A lot's been said about it already; just know that it's a fast-paced, Doom-y FPS made with care, that gracefully merges the old Doom formula with some modern sensibilities. Movement is an important, central aspect of the game - the Quake-like verticality may be new to the series but the importance of movement itself is not - and it even takes the execution system from Brutal Doom and turns it into something cool and not cringey. It's basically a 180 from Doom 3's slow, dark pacing.

The story is actually interesting, but as is probably the right move for a Doom game, it's tucked to the side for curious players to seek out, while the demon killing is front and center. You're not so much a space marine struggling to survive the onslaught of Hell on Mars, as Hell is trying to survive the onslaught of your fearsome self.

A bug or two that screws up progression towards 100% campaign completion if you finish certain levels before completing optional objectives was the most severe issue I encountered, and that's pretty much one of the only issues. (Make sure you get all the Praetor tokens on Kadingir Sanctum, and interact with both rune trials on Advanced Research Complex, before you finish each level.) It takes a while to load, especially compared to how quickly you can fire up the likes of Doom 2, but I suppose that's to be expected from a 21st century triple-A game.


Multiplayer is actually okay for what it is; it wasn't made by ID and it's not what most people came for originally, but it's better than people gave it credit for. As of 2020 it's still possible to find decent lobbies on North American servers, even if people only play the team modes, but a lot of the players are highly skilled veterans who will crush you if they're teamed together on the enemy side. Since there's no anti-cheat punishment, you can expect to run into cheaters here and there too.

It's a fast-paced 6v6 arena shooter - something you don't see a lot of nowadays - with decent maps and a variety of modes including team deathmatch, albeit with some modern conceits too such as a two-weapon limit. You've got fast movement combined with mostly satisfying weapons and feedback, and the ability to transform into a demon and wreck faces, which is cool if you're skilled-slash-lucky enough to get to do it. With stuff like that it's obvious the multiplayer wasn't really designed with competitive play in mind, but it's pretty fun if you can find a good lobby.

Snapmap on the other hand is an admirable attempt at fostering user-created content and accessible content creation, and people have made some good maps, but ultimately I can't help but be disappointed with it. It simply pales in comparison to the kind of modding that's been done with classic Doom games.


Doom is ultimately a worthy addition to the Doom franchise that brings its own classic-Doom-slash-modern-game sensibility to the Doom table. Doom.\t\t\t\t\t
