\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll put the TL;DR at the front this time: DOOM is awesome. DOOM is so satisfyingly awesome you'll need to take breaks between levels from awesome overload. That's how great this game is.

Now for the long version:

At its core, DOOM is a game about gameplay above all else. It's a pattern you'll see in almost all aspects of the game. This core gameplay is fantastic. It's everything you'd want from a DOOM reboot. DOOM plays like a fast-paced, old-school shooter put into an modern FPS environment. The game tells you to "rip and tear, until it is done", and that's exactly what you'll be doing. The feedback loop of shooting, tearing, and dodging at breakneck speed keeps the tension and excitement high while still making you feel like a god. Health drops from enemies when you gib or glory kill them, and the arena-like rooms where big fights take place force you to constantly move and kill, reinforcing the gameplay loop. It's fast, keeps the action high, and is undeniably DOOM.

Music: I'm not normally one to pay much attention to music in games, but Mick Gordon's sound design is the best I've heard in any game. What you hear from the OST and trailers don't do the music the justice it deserves. DOOM's music is unique in that it constantly adapts to what you're doing. As big battles start, the music starts to build up, and it winds down as battles are finished, seamlessly. DOOM's fast-paced gameplay allows for an equally fast metal soundtrack as badass as Doomguy himself. The dynamic soundtrack means the best parts of the songs always gets plays during the best parts of the action. In smaller encounters the music will wind up a little bit to match the action without dragging it out of proportion. While you're exploring it'll play with a more atmospheric style. When you go for a glory kill in a big fight, the music even pauses for a second to give you a sort of "wait for it..." moment before carrying on as you rip and tear through more demons. The only complaint is on occasion at the end of big fights you'll have one smaller enemy left and the music will still play as if you're in the heat of the big fight while you try to find and kill it. Beyond that, the sound design for this game is flawless.

Setting: One of the things DOOM does differently than most FPS games is how it completely disregards its story. It's there if you want it. It gives you some context and explanation for the world, but it's never the focus of the game. You're there to kill demons. In the first minute of the campaign, you're given a bunch of exposition from a voice transmission, but instead of sitting there quietly while the NPC spouts off about how everything's quite literally gone to hell, Doomguy shoves the panel away and goes to find his almighty shotgun. When told to carefully remove a fragile lens to preserve it for later use, Doomguy just stomps on it with his boot, satisfyingly smashing it to pieces. Just like the player, Doomguy isn't there to debate philosophy or even save the world. He's there to rip and tear through demon hordes like a ball of rage and fury, and it shows through every first-person animation of the mute protagonist. In an era full of games that try and fail to deliver a cinematic experience, it's refreshing to see DOOM give traditional story elements the middle finger and focus purely on gameplay.

I haven't played the multiplayer, so I can't give much of an opinion on it. All I can say is that I don't think multiplayer belongs in a game so tied to its core single-player gameplay and design. It's not something that would translate well.

As for SnapMap, it's only okay. While making your own levels for a game like this seems great, it's done in a very spotty way. The biggest issue I had is that a lot of the mechanics just seem a bit off. SnapMap launches a completely separate version of the game, with different settings (controls included), UI, and gameplay mechanics. Upgrades earned from the campaign aren't available in any way, nor are they unlocked all at once like in Arcade mode. Map interaction is seems very rough and unpolished, since there are no scripted animations for that. It seems like the people who worked on SnapMap took an earlier version of the game and split off in development and adding things differently than the main campaign developers did, or in some cases, didn't add them at all. It also can get painfully obvious how important good level design is in a game like DOOM; bad level design can often ruin the core gameplay loop when levels are too sparse, too dense, or put enemies, items and objects in places that discourage the aggressive playstyle of the campaign, and this shows in meny user levels.

The alternative mode I enjoyed most was Arcade mode, which is incredible. Arcade mode essentially takes the campaign's fast, high-action gameplay and ramps it up to be even faster and more intense. You get a score multiplier based on how fast and consistently you kill demons. You get bonus points for doing it with style (such as glory kills, direct hits with explosives, headshots, death from above, etc.). You also start fully upgraded in each level so you can focus less on just clearing the level and more on clearing the level in the fastest, most badass way possible. It's immensely satisfying to play, especially in short bursts when you have less time to explore or try for weapon upgrade challenges.

Overall, this game is one of the best single-player FPS games out there, and for me one of the best games I've ever played. It's the perfect game for when you want to jump in for fast action without a care. I'm not going to give it a rating, since that's just not what DOOM is about. It's an awesome game. Go play it.\t\t\t\t\t
