\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere's not much I can say about this game that hasn't been said already. I didn't care about the multiplayer at all (which conveniently means I can ignore 100% of the DLC), but the singleplayer campaign is the most fun I've had with a shooter in years, possibly ever. If it looks at all interesting to you, go buy it!

The first thing I did after finishing the game was go back and watch some GDC talks about how they managed to pull this off -- every aspect of the game is expertly crafted to push you into a state of flow, and especially, to teach you to unlearn decades of cowering behind chest-high walls and instead play aggressively. It's a master-class not just in how to build a fun game, but how to trick players into playing it in the most-fun way possible: Charging forward, dodging projectiles, circle-strafing and punching your way to victory. It's probably the only game I've seen have anything like the "glory kill" system that _enhanced_ the flow of gameplay, instead of interrupting it. It's also ultraviolent and incredibly metal in the best way -- there may be horror themes explored here, but it's not scary, it's METAL.

Had a stupid grin on my face for the first hour or two of this game.

Plot: It's there if you want it (there's tons of great lore in the menus), but never gets in the way. Because the actual plot is: It's Doom. You are on Mars and sometimes in Hell, and here are some demons for you to kill.

Performance: Perfect. Maintained a _steady_ 144 FPS on my machine with all settings as high as they'll go, only occasionally dipping down to 60fps during cutscenes in a way that's only noticeable if you have a framerate counter on. Only thing I really missed in the options menu was a way to set a framerate cap.

I'm writing this having just played Wolfenstein: The New Order, which is a useful comparison. There's some of the same old-school DNA there -- you do carry around a ridiculous arsenal, you can dual-wield assault rifles and fully-automatic shotguns, and so on -- but some of the magic was gone. With its 60fps cap and 30fps cutscenes, partially-regenerating health, and unlimited sprinting forward but in no other direction, it may not be a modern cinematic military shooter, but it plays like one. And with long, emotional cutscenes in a game where you fight Nazi robots on the moon... that's a game that doesn't know what it wants to be.

Doom 2016 knows exactly what it wants to be: A worthy successor to Doom and Quake. A thoroughly modern one, informed by ideas from the decades of other games since Doom, but confident enough to lead, not follow.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's Doom, brought up to date for the new millenia - what else needs saying?

Ok, so lets expand on that. The combat is very fluid and really enjoyable the way you can chain kills (and thus heal) as it turns the combat into a frenetic dance that rewards aggression and thinking on your feet. The sound was good, but not an OST I'll be downloading. There's no plot to speak of (more than the original Doom, but that's not saying much), but it doesn't matter too much - you don't download a Doom game for the sophisticated storylines ;)

If I had a criticism it'd be the overuse of specific set pieces. You will walk round a corner and 'Oh its another kill x hundred monsters in an enclosed space to open the door'. I found these detracted from them play which is a shame as I'm sure there would have been a way to add more variety to these. As it was they occurred enough to make me think 'oh great anohter one...'.

That said though, its an enjoyable splatfest, mowing down demons left right and centre and all our old favourites from the original make a return. A nice Easter Egg is that you can unlock original levels via portals and jump into the old 1990's version!

Not one of the most emotionally impactful games in my library, but an enjoyable romp through waves of Demons and I enjoyed my play through. (Play it on Ultra Violence or above if you want it to be a challenge).\t\t\t\t\t
