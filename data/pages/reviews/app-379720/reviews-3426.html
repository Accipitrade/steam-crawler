\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNow this feels like Doom...
Gotta play it on the hardest setting possible, it's grueling,heavy and paced perfectly, combat feels smooth and destructive, enemies have a nice layer of difficulty if you allow them to live longer than you should, already a few times I've been caught out because I passed on killing a few lesser enemies thinking I'll just go on to that beefy guy with the shotgun first because the music has kicked in giving me a feeling of grandeur and invulnerability only to turn around and be cornered shooting frantically but missing as the enemies dodge accidentally whilst swiping away at me, clawing me to pieces...

Graphics are sweet as they come, crisp textures, high framerate and really attractive specular lighting and particles. Sound is nice and reminiscent of the first doom with hydraulic sliding door sounds, demons materializing in to attack then dematerializing as you send them packing.

Running on max setting and maintaining a high FPS and my PC isn't even sweating it, it runs cool without the heavy fans kicking in, just a nice near silent hum like it's idle, oh and I didn't mention the specs...

damn, i was so wrong expecting it to be bad. they really have nailed the nostalgic feeling of it. no reloading, carry all the guns you want, brutal chainsaw deaths, secrets, finding coloured keycards. and the best part, these special secrets by activating a lever and unlocking a small part of the original doom levels in its glory old graphics. they even play the original tune as well.
picking up all the guns we know from the original with its beefed up graphics just makes you smile.

the glorykills get a bit boring but at least they provide you with health and ammo which will be needed. its really gory and the blood effects look amazing. cutting an imp in half with the chainsaw is as gruesome as you as expect it to be.
there is a story but im too busy having a good time shooting down monsters. but then again, its nothing special anyway.

they have increased the speed that makes doom 3 a slow boring game, so this is a huge improvement from what we saw and played in the beta. they have added a little modern touch to it by being able to upgrade your weapons and your armor.

so yes, its good. im actually having a good time with it.\t\t\t\t\t

Lets see, where can I start?

Well I have played for what, 3 hours? I have over 200 Glory Kills. Yeah.... Glory Kills are pretty cool.

You, I, your mother, and her mother all know thats not a good thing. So we walk around and we kill demons in multiple ways. Rockets, Grenades, Bullets, Super Shotgun, Plasma Rifle. I mean what isnt there to like?

Fall in lava? You get the Terminator Thumbs up. Would fall in lava again just to see that. I've done it and proceeded to laugh a little then cry cause I was dumb.

So do I recommend this game? Well yeah I mean I would love to rip a demons arm off and beat him in the face with it again, and again, and again.

As for multiplayer? To Be Determined.

************** EDIT *********************

Well after finishing the Campaign and loving that last boss battle. I found myself with a friend of mine crusing my way into multiplayer.

Multiplayer is fun. Because I mean who doesnt love to glory kill and get glory killed by other people lol.

My only gripe so far would be the lack of achievements for Multiplayer for example such as and achievement for

Kill Streaks
Wins
And other achievements that would make sense for the game. Like killing another demon player with the chainsaw or something. Or killing a few people at the same time with the mancubus vent ability.

Other than that I would say that I am enjoying multiplayer a lot, brings me back to the time when I had a lot of fun with an online multiplayer deathmatch type game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t7 hours in might be premature to be reviewing this game, but given how mechanically-oriented the game is, and how consistent it's been hitherto, I'll risk being 'that guy'.

So back in November of 2013 or so, there was a discussion going on the Bethesda forums about what people thought the new Doom game should be like. My thoughts back then were, quote:

---

• Go back to empowering the player in ways that most FPS games don't do anymore. Make the player highly manoeuvrable and reserve hitscan attacks for just a few enemies and not all of them. One of the things I still love about the old Doom games is that on lower difficulties, an unskilled player might be able to muddle their way through without too much damage, but on higher skills, the sheer volume of damage being thrown at you is insane, but a player who prioritises their targets carefully and is quick on their feet can get through all of it without a scratch. In games like Cod and Rage, you don't feel empowered because you're stop-n-popping against bullet attacks and soaking up damage so much of the time, relying on an automatic health regen to get you through. Where most FPS games these days reward patience and caution, Doom rewarded the skill, precision and boldness of the player. Explain the player's speed away in the plot somehow if you must (I dunno, he got possessed by a demon spirit or something that gave him superhuman abilities or whatever), but just make sure it happens.

• Focus on satisfying visual and audio feedback in the use of the weapons. The actual feedback of firing the SSG into a pinky in Doom 2 is still one of my favourite sensations of any game! Subsequent id games did an ironically poor job of emphasising this feedback a lot of the time, though I found it heartening that Rage put more effort into this aspect than usual, and I do recall Carmack mentioning it in interviews so I'm glad that the effort was a conscious one. It's a good thing so keep doing it.

• Go back and study all the old Doom levels and look at all the things that made them work well. Sprawling, non-linear environments often with multiple pathways, the tenet that any area that you can see in the game is somewhere the player should be able to get to and be rewarded for their effort (no invisible walls and low barriers you can't jump over!). Think of ways to design levels that play with and emphasise the strength of the game's core mechanics. Set pieces were built around throwing the player into situations dynamically and then seeing how they'd deal with them rather than force you to sit and watch passively as something happened to change the environment. On the flipside, games like Serious Sam and Painkiller are oft thought of as games that bring back the old Doom formula, but as far as level design goes, I think they miss the point. I keep hearing Doom described as a game where you mow down wave after wave of bad guys in giant open areas but in truth, the first two games were way more clever than that.

• Attitude. I love the hell aesthetic of the games and it still works in Doom 3, but there was something flat-out rebellious about Doom. The way it all worked originally was controversial because nobody had dared to put satanic imagery in a game before and id did it because they were young, impassioned people and so much of how the game works is just throwing the big middle finger up at the industry in a way that was fun and productive. Don't feel constrained to retreading old ground from an aesthetic point of view because you feel it's part of Doom's legacy - take a few risks! Piss some people off!

---

Fast forward to the game I'm playing today there's a few things sticking out for me: one, after languishing in development hell so long and after Kotaku's article exposing just how terminal things had gotten over at id, the fact that this game made it to market at all is quite the surprise. That the game came out and is as good as it is, though, that's a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ miracle.

Almost every one of those main tenets I pined for back in 2013 is present in this game. Against all odds, the spirit of not just the original games but the Doom community as a whole, is present and whole. At every turn you're seeing signs that the game was made by people who don't just love the old games, but understand them, at least in terms of the combat mechanics and the game's tone and aesthetics. The core design follows the groove carved by games like Serious Sam and the recent Shadow Warrior reboot - combat combat combat combat combat. It does one thing, it does it very well and it does it a hell of a lot. The Doom mod community's works also seem to have made an impact - in particular, double-jumping and quick, rechargeable 'nade throwing reminds me of the Project MSX mod. Brutal Doom's influence is obvious of course, though it's more its irreverence that manifests in the game than any of its mechanics; the melee finishers in Doom 2016 here mirror Serious Sam BFE's equivalents far more than BD's long, drawn-out fatalities.

Movement is slow for Doom, but quick for modern shooters. The compromise in speed to better accomodate console gamepads is felt here but it's hardly debilitating - new for Doom is the verticality of movement, and that ties in to the level design too (which I'll get to in a bit). True to the original games, your player is a glass cannon, and your nimble move speed with projectile-throwing enemies, makes for a skill ceiling that goes up into the clouds. The so-called 'glory kills' really serve as a bullet point on the box more than they add to the gameplay, but they make sense as a tactic and don't outstay their welcome.

Rage started id's trend of actually trying to focus a little on making weapons satisfying to use again. Quake 3's guns, while well-balanced, felt weak and pithy compared to the chunkier guns of other games like Unreal Tournament. Doom 3's guns not only looked and sounded even worse but actually felt ineffective much of the time, with enemies not even breaking stride when hit. Rage tried to make shooting fun again, but Doom '16 perfects it. I'm particularly delighted to see giblets making a return in a modern shooter. Gibs! Delicious gibs! Gibs all over!

Level design is good overall compared to most modern shooters, but pales in comparison to the playful, subversive and often downright devious ideas seen in Doom 2 and is the only way I feel it doesn't measure up to the originals. While there's plenty of variety in the gameplays spaces ranging from halls to open terrain to platforms to vertically-oriented fighting, encounters follow the Painkiller formula: walk into a new room, 100 bad guys spawn in, kill them, find secrets if you feel like it, proceed. Doom 2 (and many user-made wads for it) delighted in toying with your expectations and putting you in odd situations with the tools it had at its disposal. Who remembers Barrels O' Fun, which had you running like crazy as the entire room around you filled with exploding barrels went up? Who remembers Gotcha!, where you had to take out a spider demon and cybie by tricking them into shooting each other? Almost everyone who played Doom 2 will know exactly what I'm on about. Will we be talking about Doom 2016's nutso level design head-screws in 20 years? Probably not.

There's so much more to say and I haven't even started on the audio/visual qualities, but I'm running out of room, so... Snapmap. It's cool and potentially really important. Multiplayer... eh, it was alright when I played the beta. Not bad, not great, but being an SP guy I find it hard to care.

Summarily: This game is a massive achievement in going against the industry trends and a much-needed return to form for id software. The game's ancient core gameplay design has lost nothing to time, and after expecting a soulless cash-in on the Doom name for so long, I'm so relieved to be able to say it's the most fun I've had with a shooter in years. Crazy.\t\t\t\t\t
