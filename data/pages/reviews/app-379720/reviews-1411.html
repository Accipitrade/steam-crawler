\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDo you ever just lay in bed and think about how ignorant you used to be? When I was a kid, I used to think that my teachers lived at school. I also used to think that the backroom in the supermarket was where they actually made all of the food. I was a stupid kid. It's easy for me to chalk up my terrible ideas to youth, but every now and again I'm humbled by my lack of intelligence as an adult. I've held a lot of terrible ideas, particularly with regard to video games. I used to think that Assasin's Creed was a good game, I thought that Lawbreakers would be huge, and (getting on topic) I thought that Doom 2016 was kinda mediocre. I played it back when it came out on Xbox and didn't really think much of it. However, the massive amount of hype surrounding Doom Eternal's release prompted me to revisit its younger brother. After all, this game singlehandedly brought Doom back into the public zeitgeist after a decade or so of weak output. Now I have yet another thing to cringe about while under the covers, because Doom 2016 kicks ass.

The first person shooter has undergone a lot of evolution throughout the years. The frantic room-clearing action of games like Doom and Quake has slowly given way to the cover-based combat of modern shooters like Call of Duty and Medal of Honor. Now don't get me wrong, I'm not saying that these games aren't good. In fact, I think that a lot of modern FPS games like CS:GO and Rainbow Six are great exercises in tactics, positioning, and teamwork. However, I think something was lost in the pursuit of more realistic gameplay. Sometimes I don't want to cower behind cover. I wanna explore nightmarish worlds that are straight out of an Anton LaVey wet dream! I wanna shoot guns so huge that they take up half the screen! I wanna charge right in the enemy's face and make them regret the day they growled in my general direction! I wanna be unstoppable! I want to rip and tear! I wanna be the Doomguy!!!

Doom 2016 brings back everything that was great about the arena shooters of the nineties but with a fresh coat of paint. No more fighting endless waves of generic terrorists in bombed-out buildings. Now you get to do battle with the terrifying and diverse hordes of Hell. Each of the enemies, from the nimble imps to the lumbering Barons, have their own personality and behavior. All of their attacks are highly choreographed, making it possible and highly rewarding to dodge. Doom rarely ever supports lame cover-hugging gameplay. Instead, the optimal way to play will see you swerving and evading through attacks while making good use of the massive arsenal of weapons that you build up. The levels are a natural extension of this playstyle. Arenas, in particular, take on a layer of verticality that very few FPS games achieve. There's so much â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥ to climb on, so many hidden nooks to discover, and so much to unlock that each level feels like a theme park. The firefights in this game are so intrinsically fun that it honestly baffles me I didn't enjoy them the first time around.

And you know that I have to gush about the music. The â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥â™¥ music. Game devs, you wanna know the fastest way to my heart? Get some kickass music for your game. Mick Gordon did a phenomenal job with the soundtrack to Doom. I'm pretty sure it's impossible to listen to a song like "BFG Division" and not feel a predatory bloodlust wash over you. This crushing mixture of metal, electronic, and industrial music will remain one of the best soundtracks to ever grace my ears. I also need to give a massive shoutout to the art direction. The environments, while not highly varied, are really detailed and well designed. The Hell levels, in particular, are visually stunning, with a great mixture of narrow blood-soaked halls and wide-open tarnal stadiums. Lastly, the guys who animated the glory kills deserve a promotion, because they're incredible. Glory kills are fast and snappy, they have amazing sound design, and they don't screw up the flow of the game.

This is usually the point in my writing when I list my issues with a game. But honestly, I have virtually nothing to complain about with Doom 2016. It's everything that I want from a single-player shooter. However, I should mention that a common complaint levied against Doom is that it can feel tedious at times. The game is hyper-focused on the gunplay, which is great for inpatient spastics like me, but it might start to drain on others. Fortunately for them, the platforming and secret hunting can offer a much-needed reprieve from the endless skirmishes. There's also a slew of data logs you can find that fill you in on the overarching story. But ultimately, the heart and soul of this game lies in the combat. If you're expecting a methodical and thoughtful experience then you should probably see yourself out, because you aren't going to find it here. This is a fast-paced and visceral experience. During the gameâ€™s numerous firefights, you often won't have much time to think or plan things out. You'll be making hundreds of tiny snap-decisions that add together to decide the outcome of each battle. This free-form combat is what made Doom 2016 so much fun for me. I'm so glad that I gave this game another shot because I ended up finding what's possibly my favorite single-player shooter of the past decade.

A BRIEF AFTERTHOUGHT: I recommend playing your first run on "Ultra Violent". I think it offers the perfect balance of challenging gameplay without being too frustrating. If it's too hard you can always switch to "Hurt me plenty". Just don't play on "I'm too young to die", because you'll be bored out of your mind. Also, I didn't talk about Snapmap and the multiplayer because both are basically dead. You don't buy Doom 2016 for the multiplayer, you buy it for the campaign.
