\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm not massively impressed with this game. Everybody is saying that they love it but I can't understand why. The combat is average, collision detection for environmental objects is lacking - objects falling through into other objects while others have no detection at all.

The checkpoint saving system is massively unwelcome. Why? After growing up with quick saves, why did they decide to remove them from standard gameplay? It forces backtracking and makes nightmare less fun (although having a checkpoint mode for nightmare + is a potential, I don't see who would ever run it).

Enemies are indistinctive. Their attack patterns seem all over the place aswell, and the lack of predictability can be awesome, but it just doesn't work well enough.

The graphics are fairly okay, but the colour palette is lacking and it quickly feels samey, even more samey than Doom 3.

The weapons operate fairly predictably, but they are not attractive to operate. The standard shotgun feels just as useless as in Doom 3 and the Plasma Gun looks like it is shooting water bubbles.

I gave multiplayer a go. It's average at best. Maps look okay but feel too large for team deathmatches, and there doesn't seem to be any players for free 4 all. The customisations are awesome, and if the weapons felt more fun to use i'd probably stick with it, but at my age I've played a lot of FPS, and DOOM doesn't tick enough boxes with me. I like the arcadey like feel but I don't like the execution. It's pushing me into a shallow direction of gameplay that doesn't feel fair or fun in single player nightmare, pointless to play on an easier setting, and doesn't bring enough players close enough to each other with exciting enough weaponary in multiplayer. Against other FPS games out there, especially considering how strong multiplayer FPS is at the moment, DOOM just doesn't compete well and it feels odd.

Certainly not the worst FPS shooter, but the glitz is overshadowing the shallow and unspectacular promise of what could have been executed with greater substance in place of style. The alternatives on the market just offer so much more.

6 / 10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDOOM is fun, but it's not for everyone; a lot of what you bring to the experience will define whether you like it or not.

I...like DOOM. It's frustrating to say that I just like DOOM as opposed to loving it, because the game has a lot to offer. It's a crazy run through sleek, sci-fi inspired environments laiden with a degree of "heavy-metal" inspired aesthetic that would feel right at home on the front of Meat Loaf or Black Sabbath album. The music and sound design is, simply put, ON POINT; Mick Gordon continues to show us in this game, much as he did in Wolfenstein: The New Order, how music can be used to heighten a game's experience. On that at note, the gameplay loop on display is nothing shy of masterful, subtly encouraging an aggressive playstyle while making the player feel just as powerful as playing as the Doom Guy demands.

So, since I like this game and doubt I'll contribute anything crazy and new to the conversation, let me make a case as to who this game is for and, more importantly, who it is not.

DOOM cleverly evokes the visual trappings and intensity of the 1993 original, complete with a bright color palette and enemies that interact in unique and interesting ways. Yet, on top of that, this quasi-reboot (I use the term loosely) bolsters the originals to new heights, mythologizing the exploits of the player-character in the original game to a legendary status, turning what would otherwise be a rocket-powered romp into a celebration of all things Doom. If I were being cynical, I'd call it masturbatory revelry, a self-indulgent celebration of John Carmack's work. More generously, the game has a good sense of humor, and it plays up its camp past the point of annoyance to the point of genuine enjoyability.

Therein lies the rub. Much of what makes DOOM so rewarding is the way that it reinvents the FPS of yesteryear for a current generation of players, and much of it succeeds. The lack of a reloading mechanic, the breakneck walking speed, and the variatble slew of weapons all evoke the original, while the use of double-jumping and "glory-kill" system bring the edge of current games to the experience.

The problem, for me at least, is that's all that DOOM brings to the table. The lore to this version of the world is deep, but it's almost all absent from the overall experience, save for exposition dumps buried away in the game's codex, all so technically written and lengthy as to be incredibly informative about the world's lore while remaining an utter drag to actually read. The Doomslayer is a fun character, but one who lacks meaningful nuance. The moment-to-moment gameplay is solid and enjoyable, but it fails to evolve substantially over time past learning the attack patterns of new enemy types. Even the villains are a unique sort of cartoonish evil that, while fun in small doses, eventually wears its welcome thin. It's hard for me not to compare this title to the Wolfenstein reboot, a game that at once reveled in its legacy while providing for it a new depth and complexity through its surprisingly human narrative. DOOM has no intention of following suit, choosing instead to bank on nostalgia and tight execution than reexamining what it means to be a Doom title.

I think that, in short, is DOOM in my eyes. It's fun but only in small doses, and the longer I play, the more I find small parts of the game less fun than frustrating. I want to love it; I really do, but it's a title aimed at a different set of tastes than my own. For those who love the shooters of years gone by, it's glorious return to form and a raucous good time. But for players like myself, individuals who grew up on PS2 and GameCube platformers rather than the circle-strafing shooters of the early FPS genre, much of the joy is lost in translation. The game's a lot of fun, but again, it's not for everyone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is the definition of ultimate badassery. You are the legendary Doom Marine. You awaken from your ancient slumber and the first thing you do is you break off the chains from your arms with your godly strength and crush the skull of the first unlucky demon. Rip & Tear.

Coming from the gods of FPS, id Software, is a reboot infinitely more successful than the third installment. It exchanges the slower pace and horror focus of the third game for insane speed, velocity and verticality of combat. There is no time for cheap jump scares here. The game still maintains a solid horror or terror atmosphere with all the gore, pentagrams and demonic inscription scribbled along the walls of Union Aerospace and Hell.

What will strike your eyes first is the unprecedented and almost perfect use of user interface. It is so cool, natural, versatile and lovely with all of that clean graphical reactivity. It makes killing enemies more satisfying when you have a unique dynamically changing crosshair for each weapon. And there are many weapons, each having two obtainable modifications and those modifications have upgradable properties and masteries, creating a ton of combinations and possibilities that you sometimes forget you have them all.

Another wonderful asset of this game is its Codex. It was so much fun to read as it unlocks new entries after collecting data logs, weapons and killing enemies. It offers rich backstory information written in hard sci-fi lingo describing the science behind the demons, weapons, demonic mythology, harvesting of Argent energy and the corrupt UAC conduct and policies. This might sound boring but it's super interesting to read about, for example, the transference of consciousness of the dying CEO Dr. Samuel Hayden into a three meters tall cyborg. One can only wonder what we will be capable of in 2143, minus the siphoning of energy from the portals of Hell, of course.

Minus all of this story stuff, the core gameplay is fun as hell. Each enemy can be killed in a glorious finishing animation and with all the weapons, mods and runes the combat is never ever boring or repetitive. It takes some getting used to, especially the movement speed and parkour, but it was refreshing to see such a multi-layered level design.

The game contains an advanced map and a vast collectibles and secrets system that actually rewards you for locating secret areas with weapon upgrade points. This offers a ton of replayability. Dr. Samuel Hayden sounds a bit like Lance Henriksen and the facility's announcement system sounds like GLaDOS. The soundtrack is a legend. The optimization is amazing given how good the game looks.

The game is awesome af. 'Nough said. Buuut, the overall impression and impact on myself is...

9/10

+ ripping and tearing demon body parts, amazing sleek interface, godly soundtrack, graphical maturity and optimization, rich hard sci-fi setting, story and lore, cool and badass, sufficiently long and replayable (not rating the multiplayer)
- the speed takes some getting used to, physically exhausting\t\t\t\t\t
