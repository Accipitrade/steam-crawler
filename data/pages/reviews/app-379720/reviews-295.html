\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played this before on the Xbox years ago but now that I've played all of the previous games it's easy to tell just how much id software kept true to making Doom about nothing but ripping and tearing demons apart. But at the same time they weren't afraid to keep up with modern games and innovate a little, and the finished product was incredible.

The gameplay is phenomenal. I can't express just how much fun it is to use every weapon in your arsenal to blast through demons while you fly across the arena in front of you. For starters in most fps games I would find myself using half of my weapons at most in any given situation, let alone the entire game due to most weapons being inferior in some way or another. But in this game (as well as Doom 1 and 2) I found myself using everything at my disposal, especially with the tougher enemies. They all serve a different purpose or mechanic, with some being great as crowd control and others great for focused shooting at one enemy, some useful for both and some serving a completely unique purpose, those being the chainsaw that instakills anything and gives you tons of ammunition and the BFG that does the same but on a wider scale and stuns bosses. It feels so satisfying to have a game where I actually have a reason to use everything, and without sprint or reloading to ever worry about I don't have to stop for anything except the demon in front of me.

Being able to kill enemies in unique ways and change up what you use to kill them while getting different rewards out of it was one of the best ways Doom 2016 innovated. Take a Hell Knight for example: You can shoot its head clean off with a gauss cannon, blast it with a barrage of rockets (or mini rockets), saw it in half, lob a mortar at its torso, harass it with a volley of super shotgun shells and so much more. Depending on the way you attack it you can stagger the Hell Knight and glory kill it in multiple ways depending on the angle and get health (or armor and ammo with certain perks) as a reward to keep you going in the fight. The glory kills are so great that often I don't even need the extra health; I just want to do it because it looks cool.

Along with the innovation is that you don't just get a weapon and that's it. In fact, through killing demons, finding secrets and completing challenges you can get points towards upgrading your weapons as well as yourself. By actively boosting your own arsenal you end up gaining even more creative ways to move through levels and kill demons. On top of this you can explore every level to gain new upgrades. In past games secrets gave way to extra health and ammunition or even a new weapon earlier than normal, and for many secrets in Doom 2016 this is still the case. If you're a completionist you might struggle a lot with having to sometimes go to the last checkpoint or possible restart a mission to get a certain upgrade, but if you play more casually you won't suffer at all. Many upgrades are in your path or sometimes on a path less traveled, allowing you to keep up with the larger amount and variety of demons. You can easily get all the weapon upgrades and most of your suit upgrades that matter by just playing the game how you please.

But the gameplay wouldn't be so memorable if it weren't for the masterful soundtrack in the game. Mick Gordon has made one of the best soundtracks I have ever heard in a video game, and in my opinion the best metal soundtrack. The intensity and crushing riffs are absolute perfection for the theme of Doom and scale exactly like they need to for whatever you may be doing, whether that be flying around an arena or just starting up a level. The music is so good that Doom 2016 has become one of the only games where I started listening to the soundtrack in real life, and the only game (except Doom Eternal) where I still regularly listen to it. Serious props to you Mick Gordon, this game wouldn't be anywhere near what is without you.

The story is not the main focus of the Doom 2016 but it is noticeably much more prevalent than the previous games (except for Doom 3, which didn't execute it well). It doesn't say much considering all the games are from the 90s, but it is prevalent enough to have a real purpose that you see in front of you while fighting but not too much that it distracts from the gameplay. The only characters you learn much about are the antagonist, the CEO of the UAC that helps you along the way and, of course, Doomguy. I think this was a great choice because the story isn't forcing you to be invested in too much but it still gives a great dynamic to who is still shown and their different motivations. Doomguy's expressions being shown purely through his actions worked extremely well and cemented his place as an iconic character beyond the 90s. But the story isn't lackluster outside of that; if you wish to learn more about the demons, environments or general history of the universe there are plenty of data logs you'll pick up throughout the game that give tons of information and backstory to everything from the perspective of the UAC, which in my opinion was perfect to not distract from the game itself.

Lastly I wanted to point out that the art style was also great for a modern Doom game. It looks pretty dang realistic and the different demon varieties were updated so that they immediately embody what they looked like in previous games while still being something new and gritty. It's pretty impressive how the art style, movement and attacks from each enemy pay homage to the old games while still fitting in perfectly with the game. Even the new Summoner added was made with the same things in mind so it still fits in.

Overall the game is perfect. It's the best reboot of an old game that I have ever played, and it was an absolute blast to play. I highly recommend this to both doom veterans and newcomers.

10/10\t\t\t\t\t
