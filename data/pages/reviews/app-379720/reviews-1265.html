\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me preface this by saying the following. Feel free to skip to the first breakpoint if you like.

I purchased this game before DOOM: Eternal was even announced, and I'm going to review it in a vacuum. I haven't played Eternal (EDIT: I HAVE NOW, SEE BOTTOM ADDENDUM) so if you're looking for a comparison between those two games: read a different review. The reason it took me so long to review this is because I left the game for a while, and have only returned to it due to COVID-19 quarantine. That isn't a knock about DOOM's quality: I still highly recommend it. I had simply found other games to eat up my time, and I was otherwise occupied.

With that (relatively irrelevant) segment out of the way, let's get on with the meat of the review.

-------------------------------------------------------

I'm sure you probably understand at least the basics of how DOOM operates. Go into a room, see the demon, shoot the demon. It's a very simple concept that's easy to pick up, and the game's core mechanics are simple to learn quickly. That said, they're very well designed allowing for each weapon to feel unique and have their own niches.

Each combat encounter will throw a horde of demons at you, but more will spawn throughout. As a result, it never feels like you're fighting an indestructible horde nor are you fighting a piddling group. Ammo, health, and armor pickups scattered throughout the area with the occasional powerup help the combat keep flowing, and prevent you from running out of gear regularly. However, it's not enough to let you rely on one weapon, forcing you to actually make use of the varied arsenal at your disposal.

Sometimes, however, you will find yourself out of ammo for everything, or seriously struggling in terms of health. DOOM's solution is the following: glory kills and the chainsaw. I believe these are some of the best additions to the game. Glory kills are a bonus for bringing a demon near death, and then using a special attack on them to finish. It will reward you with health, enticing you to actually push INTO combat when your health is low. Similarly, kills with the chainsaw cause enemies to drop tons of ammo which can help you get back into a fight instead of scrounging for scraps.

All of this coalesces into a very fast-paced push-forward type of combat, which fits the "powerful demon-slayer" attitude of the game. At it's core, DOOM is an FPS so this must be top-notch for a quality experience. I'm happy to report that it feels amazing.

-------------------------------------------------------

Mick Gordon's soundtrack playing in the background is a welcome addition to the game, helping pump up the player during combat. Reacting to the player's own actions is a very cool idea and it's decently well executed, but I find that sometimes glory kill riffs can sound out-of-place sometimes.

Complementing the rip-and-tear attitude of the soundtrack is the similarly gory visual style. Demons are bloody abominations placed in the very sci-fi UAC facility or the soaring but crumbling halls of the hellish... uh... hell. Ripping and tearing your way through demons (sometimes literally - using the aforementioned glory kills and chainsaw) is very much the spirit of the world design.

World design is, of course, also important for gameplay in addition to visuals. Areas are laid out mostly as battlegrounds and the pathways between them, with keycards sometimes blocking your path. The battlegrounds themselves resemble multiplayer maps: they're extremely open with sightlines that cross the entire battlefield and some degree of symmetry. I find this helps the speed of combat as it helps you see your next target and home in like a berserking demon slayer eyeing a particularly juicy piece of imp flesh.

-------------------------------------------------------

DOOM's storyline isn't exactly the focus - that handily goes to the fighting. However, if you're looking for some story on the side, DOOM provides. Logs are found throughout the campaign detailing life as a member of the UAC, and the main story delivers as well, but avoids (lengthy) cutscenes. I won't go into detail of course, but it's acceptable. I certainly wouldn't call it my favorite, but it was enjoyable nonetheless.

-------------------------------------------------------

At any rate, that's DOOM. If you're looking for an action-packed FPS game with great gunplay, this is definitely one to try. As I mentioned previously: I have not played DOOM: Eternal. If you're looking for a comparison, try another review. If you're looking to see if you should pick up this DOOM, however, the answer is undoubtedly the following:

Yes.

-------------------------------------------------------
UPDATE: January 28, 2021
-------------------------------------------------------

Played DOOM: Eternal (though not on Steam, so I can't review it properly). Better than DOOM 2016, if you have to pick between them I'd recommend Eternal by a long shot. Combat is more intricate with a lot more interacting systems and movement mechanics. If you do want to play both, start with 2016 or you'll feel constricted.\t\t\t\t\t
