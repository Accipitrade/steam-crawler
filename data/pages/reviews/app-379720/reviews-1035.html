\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen DooM came out in 1993, it was written for MS-DOS when Windows was a fledgling OS. The Commodore Amiga was king in the gaming world at that time. One would have thought iD would have written it for that platform. It never was, however. DooM was the first game that really showed off the IBM PC potential, beginning the push for better sound and gfx. I immediately latched on and started creating custom maps.

Far from the primary colors of the original game DooM 2016 still has the FPS mojo many software houses long for. It is, for all intents and purposes, a shooter. The cut-scenes add to the back story, however. The graphics are visceral and a feast for the senses. Everything about DooM 2016 is polished.

There are a couple of annoying issues, none of them game-breakers. First, some of the challenges are impossible to pull off, mainly because they are timed to a ridiculously short period. There is also no Quick Save or Quick Load, something that shipped with all previous DooM games. Not a big deal, as checkpoints are somewhat generous, triggered mainly once you've cleared the monsters from an area. The last irritant are the monsters' resistance to your weapons. I played on Normal and had to cheat to get past some of the swarms thrown at me. I supposed I could have eventually made it through the game without cheating, but repeating the same kill-or-die sequence over and over again just gets boring.

While the map function is a technical marvel IMHO, it stinks as far as figuring out how to get from Point A to Point B. Adding to the navigational difficulty is all the mini screens cluttering the screen. This makes it hard to determine routes, especially when a bunch of demons are all over you.

All in all, I'd recommend DooM 2016 to any fan of the genre.\t\t\t\t\t
