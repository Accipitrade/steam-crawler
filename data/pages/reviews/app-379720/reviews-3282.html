\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf your looking for a review on this game you've come to the wrong place, but if your looking for a outstanding cheese cake recipe you've come to the right place.

Required Ingredients:
1 cup digestive plain sweet biscuit crumbs
3 tablespoons sugar
3 tablespoons melted butter
40 ounces softened cream cheese
1 cup sugar
3 tablespoons flour
1 tablespoon vanilla
1 cup sour cream
4 eggs

Directions:
For The base:.
If you're using a dark pan, preheat the oven to 350. If you are using a shiny pan, 375.
Combine the crumbs, melted butter and 3 tbsp sugar well and pat onto the bottom of a 9 inch springform pan. I also like to very very lightly butter the sides of the pan so that the cheesecake will not stick later on.
Bake for 10 mins in your preheated oven and remove. Let it cool while you prepare your batter.
For the batter:.
Make sure everything is at room temperature.
Combine sugar, cheese, flour and vanilla in a big bowl and mix on medium speed till well combined. Don't forget to scrape the bowl as you're doing this.
Mix in the sour cream till its incorporated well. Then, add the eggs one by one, mixing well after each addition.
Pour the batter into the springform and let it bake for 1 hour and 10 minutes After the baking time, turn off the oven and leave the door ajar. Let the cake cool in there for about 1 1/2 hours.
Remove the cake and let it cool thoroughly. Refrigerate overnight.
Release the sides of the springform.
Eat.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI rarely write reviews but felt like sharing a few words on this latest iteration of Doom.

I'm only 4hrs into the campaign but it's plain to see, after just this short time, that Doom 2016 is a great shooter. The movement is fast, the enemies are plentiful, the guns are satisfying in both sound and feel and the level design is amazing too. While the graphics aren't stunningly photo-realistic, it has a strong art direction and very nicely designed environments, both interior and exterior. It's fun to explore the maps to find the collectibles just like Doom from the 90's.

The game starts you off in a room with a gun and some enemies. point and shoot is all you need to know. So rather than the game taking you through a safe bit of wandering around during some exposition of the story, you're dropped straight into the fray. You get a gun, shoot some easy enemies, get your armour and continue to blast away at enemies.

There's a tiny bit of tutorial when you collect something new, but aside from that it's very straightforward. You run, you shoot, you strafe, shoot, jump, shoot, shoot, shoot, etc until the room is clear and repeat. Sounds repetitive, but the combination of enemy types, weapon options and the pacing of the combat itself makes for an extremely satisfy experience when you can kite a room, killing everything and barely taking a scratch. Equally it goes the other way, finding yourself fighting for your last scrap of health. Both scenarios happen frequently and are wonderfully satisfying to come out on top of.

I'd highly recommend this game purely on the strength of it's single player campaign.

The multiplayer I've only played in it's beta form. I hated it at first, but grew to actually enjoy it despite its shortcomings.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a small child in the early 90s, I distinctly remember sitting on my Dad's lap and playing classic Doom. Running around looking for colored keys while blowing demons into pulp to the kickass soundtrack completely gripped me. Although I enjoyed Doom 3 for what it was, the survival-horror sequel/reboot didn't capture the tone or gameplay that made the originals great. Finally in 2016, the true Doom successor has arrived.

The campaign kicks off with a very brief couple lines of dialogue, and in only a few seconds the player has control and is already killing demons. This Doom doesn't try to fall in line with other modern shooters, its a fantastic translation of what games used to be before everything had to be cinematic. This Doom features only a couple of characters and it doesn't interrupt the action with lengthy cutscenes. This Doom is a real throwback to a simple formula: Find keys, find secrets, kill demons, and enjoy the pumping electro-metal soundtrack.

But that's just the foundation. This Doom has added layers of depth to it. Finding weapon mods that can be swapped out on-the-fly will make you even deadlier, and improving your base health, armor, and ammo capacities will give you an advantage as you progress to the later levels. Suit abilities can also be upgraded, which will protect you from environmental hazards (such as exploding barrels), make finding secrets easier, let you squeeze more use out of powerups, and more. On top of all that, completing Rune Challenges will reward you with bonus abilities such as increasing the range that you can absorb dropped items, gaining a brief speed boost after performing a glory-kill, and being given a chance to survive a death blow.

Levels in Doom provide multiple paths and layers to explore, combining tight corridors with large open spaces, offering a mix of approaches to fighting both small groups and large waves of enemies. This is not the slow-paced survival horror shooting of Doom 3. In this, you keep moving or you die. With Imps sprinting around, hurling fireball after fireball, Hell Knights leaping in with a swift ground-pound attack, Revenants hovering above with their jetpack, and a dozen other things trying to kill you simultaneously, you're going to have to strafe, double-jump, climb, and take advantage of powerups to survive.

Like the classic games, there are many secrets to be found hidden in levels. Some of these are lazily placed in obvious spots, but a lot of them are quite challenging to find or reach. Collectibles will unlock in-game models of enemies and weapons to check out, and data-entries add a ton of info and lore for those players who enjoy that sort of thing. My favorite secrets that were included here, are portions of Doom I & II levels, which when found, unlocks the full classic Doom level that can be played from the main menu.

To sum up the campaign-related content, Doom is a love-letter to fans of the classic games. You get 13-levels of fast-paced demon killing with a nice array of powerful weapons, brutal melee finishers, a badass soundtrack, collectibles, minimal story, and its all wrapped up in a GORGEOUS package. It took me around 15 hours to complete, and I absolutely loved it.

On to the multiplayer and snap-map modes. These are both disappointing blemishes on an otherwise crazy good game. Snap-map allows you to create your own levels, but the limits on level-size, enemy-count, and weapon-count put a giant damper on the concept. It feels random and tacked-on, and doesn't come anywhere near what true modding capabilities could've added to this game. There isn't much to say about the Multiplayer either. It isn't as fast or as fun as other arena shooters like Unreal Tournament, and it doesn't innovate in any way to make up for it. Its just...mediocre. I do like the degree to which your character and weapons can be customized, but there's no motivation to show them off in a bland, unneeded multiplayer mode.

Overall verdict: Although I didn't mind paying $60, many people may want to wait for a price-drop before picking up Doom. The campaign is fun and solid, but somewhat short. Because the multiplayer and snap-map modes shouldn't even really be included, I'm not sure I can convince someone that they're getting maximum value at full price. If you're able to find a key for $40 or less, I say absolutely do it. If you're a fan of classic Doom, I'm confident you won't regret it.

TL;DR: Campaign is great, feels like Doom. Multiplayer is bad, maybe wait for price drop, but definitely get at some point.\t\t\t\t\t
