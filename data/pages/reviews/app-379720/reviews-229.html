\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI probably remember it wrong, but I once read one of those stories of game design ingenuity about the original Quake, about how when you fire the rocket launcher, the missile actually spawns right behind you, because it turns out to optimize both game performance and the felt rhythm of play.

DOOM (2016) pulls this kind of tricks all the time. The game will snap you a small distance in space to make you feel and look good, giving you that last inch to grab a ledge, or letting you activate a finishing move at the last possible second, right when you most need the health boost it dispenses. Jonathan Blow talks about "generosity" as a design value, when you take a player gesture or affordance that might typically be kept scarce to make it exciting (such as time rewinds in Prince of Persia) and make it available all the time. DOOM is not a comprehensively generous game: it understands the micro-narrative impact of running out of railgun rounds two-thirds through a fight and having to revise your tactics in response (or taking a different approach on the next reload). But it is generous about the core experience of dancing around and shredding up hellspawn: you always have a shot, and it always look sick as ♥♥♥♥.

This game is at its best when I'm on my fifth try at a particular sequence and I'm reminding myself to just move fast and never stop. I know this song, after all.\t\t\t\t\t
