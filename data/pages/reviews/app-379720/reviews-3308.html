\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI may consider myself a veteran PC player - I am 27 years old now and started playing some games back in the first half of 90s. I do still play some old-school stuff to this day, such titles as: QuakeWorld, Unreal Tournament they have a great remaster which is free, Doom 1-2 Plutonia is my favourite.

How would I describe this new Doom?
Shortly speaking it is awesome! But let me get down to the details...

Graphics

Visuals in this game are top-notch in all terms. The only drawback the game really has is a stylized represantation which some ppl even described as a cartoonish. But in purely technical terms and overall quality there are only two games which can be placed on the same level: Uncharted 4 and new Tomb Raider. I've played new Unreal Tournament on ultra settings so I know what I am talking about.

Campaign

This is where game truly shows that this is DooM in its very soul - the game I played back in 90s. Yes, it has certain elements of modern shooters which are absolutely optional and if you want to make the experience even more hardcore and old-school by all means skip those innovations I'd like to see the person who completes the game on nightmare without ANY upgrades. The gameplay is extremaly fluid and full of unstoppable action. In fact, the major concept here is a constant movement - you must swim across the arena in order to survive you cant just shoot well. The campaign is around 10-13 hrs long if done with moderate exploration on a normal difficulty. Let me just address the difficulty for a bit. It is more challenging than previous doom titles in terms of general gameplay as I said previously you have to move around or you get hit and getting hit esp on higher difficulty nightmare and ultra-nightmare settings even once means almost instant death. The enemies have different attacks and you cant just spam them with bullets you have to change you approach from time to time. In fact, the enemies are not easy to predict to begin with.
The level design is not liniar but it has quite significant amount of lockdown areas where you have to kill in order to set yourself free. Is it good or bad? For speedrunners it is a problem but generally it makes the game very intense.

Multiplayer

Personally I kinda like this new vision of multiplayer. Yes, it is not quite traditional doom mp in terms of weapon selection, speed and certain power-up balance demon rune is what makes it very specific. But to be honest it has certain tactical elements which just work - if your teammates do what they are supposed to do that is. Clan Arena/FreezeTag are modes that have no demon rune btw. If you want to take a look at this new mp concept I recommend you to start with Warpath.
The only thing mp truly lacks is a classic mode without load-outs and kinda faster movement at least - in SnapMap you can create that by yourself but it better be included in standard mp list for easier/quicker access.

SnapMap

Mindblowing to say the least! Just let me be short:

1. You can create your OWN logic of your level:
- Did not like the multiplayer? Felt too slow? Loadouts are not what you like?
You can increase the speed, get rid of loadouts and specify how much damage weapon deal.
- Did not like how items drop out of fallen demons in campaign?
You can make your level super traditional in this respect so nothing will come out of killed foes.
- Just want to play a completely different game for some reason?
There are examples of musical simulator and sic! basketball game made entirely in SnapMap!
Shortly speaking, SnapMap has its own simple scripted visual programming language.
2. The creation process is simple enough - you choose preset modules from the pallete and snap them together. This is where certain limitations first appear:
- you cant create your own modules and I hope they'll add some sort of module editor so one could create custom modules similar to Sims game I believe
- it has no hell theme - hope they'll add this as well later on
3. SnapMap requires time to master so don't expect to create very original and complex stuff at the beginning.

Conclusion

As an old-school oriented person in many ways I think this game is great and deserves a lot of positive feedback.\t\t\t\t\t

PROS:

Fun, relentless, in your face single player campaign that definitely FEELS LIKE DOOM in its own way, while also feeling fresh and modern.

Will test your skills on Ultra Violence difficulty, beat you down and kick your butt on Nightmare, and break your will and drive you mad on Ultra Nightmare. (Use a new save file for Ultra Nightmare, as progress is NOT saved, whereas at lower difficulties progress and upgrades are persistent.)

Glory kills (sync kills) are not at all as distracting or intrusive as many (including myself) feared and, on the contrary, become incredibly satisfying, fun, "crunchy" moments that you leap between at high speed, fluidly and intuitively. They feel right at home in a game this fast paced.

Level design that is (at times) reminiscent of classic Doom visually and logically, but (the rest of the time) also contemporary, inventive, highly vertical, and fresh.

Lots of precision platforming at high speeds while fighting and shooting at range. (Very Quake-like actually, which was a surprise, but works well here.)

Boss battles break up the gameplay with fresh, thoughtful designs that, true to the game's old school ethos, are more about memorization and reflex speed than brute force.

Snap Map is full of promise and flexibility (and, id claims, will be expanded for free eventually,) but...

CONS:

In its current form Snap Map is extremely limited and thus cannot take the place of true modding. Especially in terms of aesthetic variety and single player level "feel." (The levels feel like multiplayer mode maps, even when taking pains to create solo stages. Playing as your multiplayer avatar rather than the Doom Marine from the campaign doesn't help that feeling)

Not quite enough hell levels (though what's here is GREAT imo.) Fingers crossed for a campaign expansion!

CAN become repetitive, IF you don't enjoy the core gameplay and pace of the game. (I do personally, and never tired of it.)

If you're not a completionist, self-challenger, or thorough secret hunter, campaign can be completed in probably as little as 8 hours on medium or lower difficulties. (Took me 20 hours on Ultra Violence.)

Boss battles may feel a little jarring in a Doom game to some.

No campaign co-op whatsoever.

WHO DO I RECOMMEND THIS GAME FOR?

1) Old school Doom fans who want a nostalgic game that EVOKES classic Doom and has the same relentless pace and focus on evasion and speed, but who are willing to accept a new, contemporary experience that does not PLAY exactly like classic Doom.

2) Non-Doom fans who enjoy intense, fast paced first person shooters, looking for something different from the many contemporary shooters with iron sights, health regen, and military settings.

WHO DO I NOT RECOMMEND THIS GAME FOR?

1) Classic Doom purists who want a modern visual face lift but the same gameplay flow and loop as the classic games (Doom/II/Ultimate/Final/64) or who love Brutal Doom and/or Brutality and expect this game to essentially play like them but with cutting edge graphics.

2) Non-Doom fans who want health regen, aim down sights/iron sights, aim toggle, vehicles, or an in-depth cinematic story.

3) Doom 3 (and possibly 64) lovers who want their Doom experience to involve survival horror or jump scares.

4) Those who want to buy the game PRIMARILY for Snap Map and level creation, or dream of recreating detailed classic Doom maps.

DETAILED REVIEW:

Caveat: I am concerning myself purely with the single player campaign and Snap Map level editor system in this review. I am not an online multiplayer gamer.

Full disclosure - My experience with Doom and its contemporaries, for what it's worth: I've been playing Doom for 21 years, both on console and PC. I've played Wolfenstein 3D, all the Dooms (including Doom 64, which I have a particular affinity for,) all the Quakes, Rage, one of the recent Wolfenstein reboots (The Old Blood which came with this Doom as a preorder bonus on Steam,) both Hexens, Heretic, Marathon, Duke 3D, and every Halo except 5 (not in that order obviously, & no - Doom is nothing like Halo.)

The first question on most people's mind is, "Does Doom 2016 FEEL like a Doom game?"

The answer, as I think we all probably knew, is "Yes and no." But the yes part of that answer comes in the best possible way in my opinion.

When I think of classic Doom up to & including 64, I think of fluid, fast movement, strafing in and out of enemy projectile fire; frantically trying to prevent them from getting close enough to trap me or inflict melee damage; keeping track of where I am in relation to health, armor, ammo, and powerups; & powerful weapons that make gibs out of demons.

Doom 2016, I am delighted and thrilled to say, achieves this with aplomb.

You see an imp. Rushing toward it, you strafe to avoid their projectile attacks as you close to super shotgun range. You blast them with a satisfying boom, then tear their lower mandible from their skull (one of the controversial & divisive "glory kills," which are not at all as intrusive as many feared, & actually feel great, fast, & natural in this game,) all in the space of a second or two. You jump, knowing his companions will already be hurling another fire ball at you, & taking fire from a revenant's missiles. You mantle up to a platform, run & leap off of it, & aim straight down at the floor, letting fly rockets at the skeletal monstrosity gunning for you. You glory kill him from above as you land, & side strafe out of the way of a pinky, before shotgunning its tail. Then you rip his butt off and beat him in the face with it.

This happens fluidly, & such a blatantly gratuitous power fantasy playing out amidst the constant threat of being overwhelmed & quickly killed if you don't pay attention to your surroundings is absolutely exhilarating. You find yourself thinking, "THIS is DOOM... only new."

Id indicated that about a third of the game would take place in hell. They were almost true to their word, and what's here is great, but I still felt I spent far too little time in the inferno.

There is a story. More story, in fact, than in any previous Doom game. The brilliant and beautiful thing about this, is that it doesn't get in the way of the action. There are very few cut scenes. Those that are here are exceedingly brief, & the rest of the game's narrative is conveyed through dialog (which you can run right by,) & codex entries found scattered throughout the levels.

There are also automaps, secret areas concealing ammo & powerups (and even weapons that you can get before you otherwise "should,") super secret nods to classic Doom, & rune trials. (Particularly challenging, timed gauntlets that focus on one particular area of skill in the game, which confer a certain small but significant bonus to a facet of your playstyle.)

In every level you can find upgrade drones which will, assuming you have earned the tokens necessary, allow you to purchase swappable modifications to one of your weapons. Examples include a shotgun that can fire grenades, or a lock on capability for your rocket launcher.

Traps abound. Large rooms lock you inside until you clear out aggressive waves of enemies. Some of these are downright daunting. There are hazardous surfaces that you must carefully platform across. There are small rooms that are already dangerous due to these hazards, which then suddenly reveal major enemies behind false walls.

Snap Map is technically brilliant, but woefully limited at this time. No elevators or hell assets & other constraints mean it fails to replace true modding. It is promised that updates will expand the mode. Let us hope so!

All in all, this is a new, fresh take on Doom, that is thrilling & exhilarating, while also being nostalgic enough to capture this old Doomer's heart. RIP AND TEAR!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is probably the Doom you were hoping for. (This review focuses on the single-player experience. The experience is slightly different for Multiplayer and SnapMap modes.)

A skillful blend of old-school sensibilities with modern polish, Doom takes what was excellent and fun about the earliest DOS incarnations of the series and gives it a superb fine-tuning for the 21st century. Gone are the hoary, industry-standard constraints upon the first-person shooter in the modern age: you have a bazillion guns that you can swap between at will, and not one of them requires reloading (except for the super shotgun, but, well, that one always required reloading, and it happens automatically anyway). Not counting your basic melee attack, there are eight standard weapons, all of which except the pistol have two alternate fire modes, and two special weapons (the chainsaw and the BFG), which adds up to 25 different ways to ruin a demon's day, and nearly all of them are a blast. This game also adds brutal 'Glory Kills', which are melee finishers that can be triggered on staggered demons--Glory Kills are fast, fun, savage, and even restore a little bit of health or armor, incentivizing you to get up close and personal instead of plinking from a safe distance.

It's not just the glory kills that are fast. The entire game is on speed, especially compared to most other modern offerings, and that's even before you pick up a Berserk or Haste powerup. Doomguy runs like a hellion and movement in the game is quick and limber: he's even learned to parkour up walls and mantle low obstructions, and this extra degree of movement gives the game's combat arenas and map layouts an entirely new tactical dimension. You will have so many options for getting yourself around that you will be hard-pressed to find yourself repeating them if you tried.

Speaking of fast, the game's performance is stellar. If this isn't the best-optimized AAA PC game I've ever played, I don't know what is. Using an out-of-the-box GTX 980 and i7 processor, with no overclocking, no tweaks, and no hoodoo, I was getting upwards of 200fps on the absolute maximum settings. Nonstop. DURING a fight. I think someone at id must have conjured a demon or two themselves to achieve this kind of performance, and if this game is meant to serve as a tech demo for id Tech 6, it's doing a damn fine job.

Some modern elements can be found in the game, in the form of collectibles, weapon upgrades, and Praetor Suit upgrades (the Praetor Suit being the official name for Doomguy's armor). Every level is littered with secrets, and all but the earliest ones contain challenges (such as 'kill five enemies with unique Glory Kills') and a Combat Rating that tracks how many demons in the level you've killed. Completing challenges and killing as many demons as you can unlocks weapon upgrade points, which can be used to improve your weapon mods--and improve those mods enough, and each one will unlock its own persistent challenge that grants even greater destructive power when completed. Doom games have always had tons of secrets to find, but linking weapon and armor upgrades to secrets in this iteration give everyone an incentive to keep a keen eye out. If you decide to go secret-hunting, it may slow down the pace of the game, but once you unlock a special ability for a weapon mod, you'll find that it's worth it. You may need them, too: Doom is a challenging game. I played on the default difficulty, Hurt Me Plenty, for the campaign, and I was able to feel suitably badass while still feeling like I had to focus and pay attention and keep my reflexes sharp. I also finished one level on Ultra-Nightmare, and, uh... well, that's appropriately named, let's just say that. Bottom line is that there is a difficulty level for everyone, including people who can beat all the other Doom games blindfolded and asleep.

The game also has a story, and while the story is, as expected, something of a light frosting on the cake, it is nonetheless coherent and interesting. There are no real twists or turns: you know who the good guys are and who the bad guys are from the start, and your objectives usually revolve around killing as many demons as you can wherever you go. But the voice acting is superb, the lines are delivered convincingly, the villains are menacing, and Doomguy himself, though a silent protagonist, communicates a seething rage through his actions that is pure fun to watch. There are a lot of entertaining moments throughout the story, and in particular the next-to-last level has a surprising and unexpected air of poignancy behind all the carnage. The writers were wise not to belabor the point, and not to let the story get in the way of the action, but there's something to be found if you want to look for it.

In the end, Doom is a superb game that more than makes up for the muddy mess that was Doom 3. It hearkens back to the series' roots without slavishly copying them, and delivers a fun, exciting, challenging experience that is surprisingly long--completing the game 100% took me twenty hours, almost four times what a modern Call of Duty would give from a single-player experience. If you like first-person shooters at all, this one surely belongs in your library.\t\t\t\t\t
