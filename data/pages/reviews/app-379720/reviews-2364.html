\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite growing up in the era of the original Doom games I'm not much of a fan, especially today. The games are lauded for their level design but IMO I think they are tedious, in particular, the larger more sprawling levels. Horde after horde of demons become boring after a while and just tend to bog the game down into a grind. The game looks terrible and plays just as bad unless you go to the trouble of using a source port. I do like the creature design though and I always found every weapon was still relevant, even after you've picked up the rocket launcher and plasma rifle. The last time I played Doom 1 & 2 I made myself invulnerable and no clipped past large portions of the game just to say I saw the end (I never did finish them when they were released).

Doom 3 is boring as hell (no pun intended). I've never been able to get past the first couple of levels. All the obvious and tedious jump scares put it on par with your average Five Nights at Freddy's. The game also seemed to be heavily influenced by the Half-life series. Maybe one day I'll get around to finishing it (must remember the duct tape mod).

When it comes to Doom (2016) my expectations were fairly low, albeit optimistic on the back of its overall positive reviews. In all honesty this one of the best games I've played in a very long time and one of the few in recent memory that has me going back for more.

The game is fast paced, harking back to the original doom and the story is largely told on the periphery through the codex. In most modern games the intro is a 20 minutes long video full of exposition and dialogue, but not in Doom. After a brief text intro almost immediately you start blasting away at the enemy.

The level design is more modern and linear than the heady days of Doom 1&2 but as part of its design, the game corrals the player into arena-like areas where you often have to kill lots of demons before being able to progress any further. This ebb and flow gives you plenty of action while also allowing you to take a breather every now and then and stops fatigue from setting in. In the arenas, many Quake inspired power-ups make an appearance such as the Quad Damage, Haste, and invulnerability.

The weapons, like many modern games, are fairly generic futuristic military looking things. Like the original Doom, I found most weapons were still useful throughout the game, even if they were superceded by superior weapons. All the fan favourites make a return as well as a Gauss rifle reminiscent of Quake 2/3's rail gun and grenades. Also like most modern games Doom 2016 features an upgrade and mod system for your weapons (and Doomguy suit) but also harkens back to the days of the original wherein you don't have to reload your weapon (except the Super shotgun). The upgrade system is fairly benign and is largely set and forget once you've found an upgrade you like. The BFG 9000 and chainsaw and separate to your weapon rotation due to scarce ammo/fuel drops but at the same time come in handy at times. I barely used the grenades and don't even know how to switch between the various types. I used them occasionally but more often than not I threw one out because muscle memory had me clicking my middle wheel to reload my weapon. The glory kills, scripted melee takedowns, had the potential to be an annoying gimmick but it fits fairly seamlessly into the combat mechanics.

Those enemies that make a return from Doom 1&2 mostly looked and acted the same for the most part with a few new additions. It was good to see monster infighting return and see them having a crack at each other and waiting for them to thin their own ranks. My biggest disappointment with the enemy demons were probably the bosses; When you're first introduced to the Cyberdemon in Doom 1 it's a real pants-♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ moment. In Doom (2016) the Cyberdemon seemed to be a shadow of its former self and the Spiderdemon was very underwhelming. The game also wasn't that hard. I played on the second highest difficulty (not counting the ones you have to unlock) and I only died a handful of times.

Other gameplay elements include finding secrets such little Doomguy dolls, upgrade tokens, classic Doom maps and rune challenges. Rune challenges award runes which can enhance the gameplay somewhat, but I found that only a couple were really useful and never bothered to change any of the three that I'd set. However, these challenges were a fun distraction, although they could get a little frustrating when you fail and have to wait for the level to reload for some reason and then wait on a countdown.

Graphically, the game looked fantastic on my rig and I was able to get 60+ FPS on highish settings. The only issue I really had, and it's very minor, is that when rotating weapons there was a lot of texture pop-in on the weapons themselves. The sound was ok, but it missed some of those meatier squishing sounds from the original and while the music was serviceable it lacked those iconic Doom riffs.

An honourable mention goes to lack of gorram quick time events. Aside from the occasional busted door that had to be forced open, there wasn't a single press A to hold to ledge or B to climb this thing or Y to rotate valve or X to do that.

Overall, if you haven't played Doom (2016) yet I highly recommend it. It certainly lives up to the hype IMO.\t\t\t\t\t
