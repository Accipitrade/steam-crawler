\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCONTAINS POSSIBLE SPOILERS

One of the most satisfying first person shooter's I've played in a very long time. Doom uses the style of fast-paced run and gun shooting that, as someone who has never played a Doom, Quake, or Wolfenstien game, I found incredibly refreshing.

Here are some of the few things you can do in this game:

Shoot demons.
Blow-up demons.
Smash demons heads into walls.
Rip demons heads in half.
Stomp on demons with their own foot.
Find secrets and easter eggs.
Shoot demons with penetrating railguns.
Find cute collectables.
Rip off a demon's horn and stab them in the face with it.

Things I liked:

On a more serious note, the glory kill system allows you to insta-kill any demon in a stunned state, which will drop health pickups. This takes away the frustration of running into a crowded room and end up scrounging around for health instead of actually fighting.
Each weapon provides unique and interesting functions and all, excluding the starting pistol, have two unlockable modes that can be switched between one another and can even be upgraded.
Every map has a number of collectables and challenges that can be found and completed to gain upgrade points for weapons, meaning exploration through the vast levels has a purpose.
The story is simple, yet funnily enough felt more satisfying than most stories I've seen.
There are tons of enemies that each have their own unique set of attacks, looks, and glory-kill animations.

A few problems I encountered.

The frames will occationally dip throughout the game, seemingly at random. I was replaying a level that I wanted to find all the hidden things in, and my fps wouldn't go above 20, despite previous playthroughs I was getting much better performance.
Some graphics options cannot be toggled or turned off. One of the more annoying ones being cosmetic particle and smoke effects, which lagged my game immensly if I ever looked out in vast distances. Very problematic levels were the ones that took place in of hell, where most of the levels I wasn't able to get a decent framerate.
Cutscenes would sometimes have sync issues, and the dialoge and sound would play and finish before the animations were completed.

The game has quite a few glitches and bugs as well that should definately be patched out, but what I found was not as game-breaking as what some people have apparently been having.

On the level following the first playthrough of hell, I fell through the floor for no reason, and ended up at the end of the level, and was unable to go back, resulting an an unwanted early finish.
Physics objects will stick together, or sometimes get stuck inside the floor or walls, causing constant clacking noises, and sometimes lagging the game if I looked at them.

Overall, the problems I've had are very minor, and the framerate issue is most likely because I don't have a powerful enough computer to handle the game engine and graphics, but I would unmistakeably recommend this game to anyone who wants to have a kick-ass powerhouse fantasy, if their computer can handle it that is.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ti've been playing doom since i was four years old in 1994 when my brother showed me the shareware version that came with our new computer. i've been hooked ever since.

at first, i was very cynical of this game, pre-release. why wouldn't i be? doom has had a hellish (heh) dev cycle with multiple versions canned for a variety of reasons, numerous instances of staff leaving id and overall a super bad scene from all evidence that was given. the e3 demo helped a lot to dissuade me from writing this off, but even then i felt uneasy. what if it's super rote? what if there is no soul?

i'm not sure how they did it, but id was able to bring back doom in the best way possible. this is a game that oozes confidence and knows exactly what it wants to be. it's not as groundbreaking for FPS as doom was back in 1993, but as it stands, this is my favorite FPS of 2016. now, it's important to note that this review is based entirely on the singleplayer campaign. i touched snapmap briefly and was left disappointed and the multiplayer felt very...standard and I wasen't impressed. so if you are comfortable with spending $60 on an extremely good single player campaign that lasts for around 10 hours, read on!

now, why is a remake of doom in 2016 possibly my FPS GOTY? let me count the ways:

1. the combat: while a fair chunk of the combat is regulated to closed-off arenas, the gameplay never feels anything less then super good and empowering as hell. doom approaches platinium games-level of fluidity and mobility, and the guns are super satisfying to use and have a, frankly astonishing, level of depth to them. i finished the game not even touching the rocket launcher, for example, only for a friend to tell me how much they used it and the scenarios it was super helpful in, and other friends have told me about other combat strategies they employ and each one of them is super different.

2. the personality and story: if you had told me that doom in 2016 would have lore and story that was legitmately interesting, i would have laughed in your face. but, again, i was proven wrong. while the story is nowhere near as good as something like The New Order was, it's an extremely entertaining and goofy story. i won't spoil anything, but there were moments where i caught myself laughing out loud at how bizarre, terrifying and ludicrous the UAC facility gets the deeper you go. also, the doomguy himself has an astonishing amount of personality just in the way he performs certain actions in the game. he never speaks, but we get a very clear sense of who he is and what he stands for, which is something that i never thought would be an element of a doom game.

3. the music and sound design: mick gordon returns from TNO and Killer Instinct to compose the soundtrack from doom and it is amazing. atmospheric and unsettling during quiet moments, loud and violent during not-so-quiet moments. the moment the soundtrack is released, i'll be getting it immediately. the sound design is also very well done, with super satisfying weapon discharges, grotesque demonic noises and the disturbing ambient hum of the UAC facility itself.

there is also an astonishing amount of replay value, with my 10 hour campaign not even scratching the surface of all the challenges, unlockables and secrets hidden in the game. this is absolutely a game to play through more then once!

this is a game that is worth every penny.

also, this was played on a i5 4670k with a GTX 970 4GB card and 8GB of ram and it was silky smooth on ultra settings except for the occasional hiccup every now and then from the crappy mechanical drive i had it installed on. i encountered very few issues, except for some annoying silent CTD's in Titan's Realm that I'm sure will be patched sooner rather then later.

it brings me great pleasure to tell you that doom in 2016 is extremely good.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: Overall worth it. Get it if you love past doom games and have the cash laying around. Note I haven't gotten to multiplayer yet. 7.5/10

Pros:
-Environments and textures look great.
-Weapons feel mostly solid. Rocket launcher feels like it lacks a little oomph, but its a minor nitpick.
-Levels are fun to traverse and provide a nice balance between blowing away demons and some exploring to find goodies.
-Doomguy's speed feels just right. Overall pace is great.
-Chainsaw kills are totally awesome.
-Little collectible Doomguys are so CUTE! Didn't quite expect that from DOOM, but I still like em'
-Backstory and tidbits are just enough to keep you interested. Perfect amount for DOOM.

Cons:
- Hurt Me Plenty mode feels a little too easy. Would recommend kicking it up one more notch from the get-go.
- Would like to have seen monster models a just a tad more.....traditionally demonic I guess? Some of the humanoid models don't feel overly distinct.
- In general, past Doom games as a whole felt more horrific and creepy in their overall environments and imagery. Like you were really everyone's last best hope against enternal damnation.

Undecided:
- Glory kills are kinda cool, but I find myself ignoring them after awhile and just running and shooting baddies. Could just turn them off I guess.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI should preface this whole review by saying that I haven't yet finished the campaign. As of this writing I have about 14-15 hours in, and I've just finished one of the later levels. At this point, I think I've played enough for my first impressions to give way to lasting, concrete opinions. So let's dive in.

Doom is a sincere callback to the titans of yesteryear: Quake, Unreal, and the original Doom. While it doesn't play exactly like a familiar arena shooter, it retains enough elements be familiar yet different.

The campaign, which is where the majority of my playtime has been spent, is fast paced and brutal. There's very little tutorial, no mandatory run and hide or stealth section at the beginning. The game puts a gun in your hand within the first 5 seconds of the campaign, and it's off to the races from there. Combat feels fast paced and exciting, but still familiar: it's easy to see that they've drawn inspiration in level design, combat, and enemy AI from the original Doom. This also means that your strategy will quite often be the same, constantly moving and side-strafing around enemies while laying waste to them with any one of your eight (eventually) weapons, if you include the garbage pistol which no one should use, ever.

The levels are designed with two things in mind: fun, fast paced combat, and deep exploration. The arena areas (a term id Software uses to describe the sections of a level where you'll have a lot of enemies spawning fairly continuously, and you're locked in until you kill them) are fun to jump around, and jump around you will. The nature of the enemy AI is that it swarms and pursues you until you are dead, so standing still in any of these areas is a quick ticket to a loading screen. As you move through the game these arenas become more and more fluid as your arsenal improves: later on I had an rune upgrade that afforded me infinite ammunition as long as I stayed above a certain armor threshold. Combine that with a supply of mini-rockets from the Heavy Assault Rifle that didn't require a reload, and the later arenas mostly consisted of me jumping from place to place, side strafing, and continuously holding down both mouse buttons to fire rockets that would home in on enemies (provided my crosshair was close to them.)

Boss fights, of which there are few, are unique and enjoyable. I died twice just figuring out boss attack patterns and how best to avoid the attacks, which is refreshing. Too many bosses in first person shooters these days are nothing more than bullet sponges, so it's nice to see that id Software remembers how to make things interesting.

Progression is fairly well done. New weapon discovery is tied to how much exploration you do. You can discover all 8 (pistol, combat shotgun, plasma rifle, heavy assault rifle, rocket launcher, Super shotgun, Gauss cannon, and chaingun) by the end of level 6, or it may take you longer. The chainsaw and BFG-9000 aren't a part of your normal weapon wheel, but serve a specific purpose: use the chainsaw to instant kill enemies (if you have enough fuel) and refill your ammo for other weapons, and use the BFG to instantly kill everything in line of sight of the blast. It's a fairly intricate system that works well: most weapons have two attachments which can be upgraded by finding secrets and completing challenges in each mission. Certain enemies have vulnerabilities that can be exploited more efficiently with certain weapons, and the chainsaw and BFG give you useful but limited "I win" buttons for "Oh ♥♥♥♥" moments.

Overall, the campaign is everything that a single player experience for a first person shooter should be, although that isn't to say that it doesn't have shortcomings. This is mostly related to the exploration experience, rather than combat or progression. Each level is so chock full of secrets that it's easy to miss a few, and yet there are numerous areas in each level that become inaccessible once you advance past them, many of these containing secrets. If it were as simple as taking a long fall to a new area, making the last one out of your reach, I could give this a pass, but many of these are simply behind locked doors that appear behind you for no discernible reason, and seem to have no way of getting back through. This forces you to replay levels if you want to be a completionist, which isn't terrible, but it is a less enjoyable model than that employed in the original Doom, where you could backtrack through the entire level all the way to the entrance at any point. Also related to exploration, these maps are quite large, and the ability to set custom waypoints would have been appreciated for those times when you're trying to backtrack to an area you missed, and its absence is glaring.

The multiplayer is passable. We've all read reviews, or maybe played a little bit, of the multiplayer beta, which is more or less representative of the final product. I enjoyed my time in multiplayer, but it's a bit simplistic. I won't begin to pretend to have any skill in first person shooter online multiplayer, yet I easily led my team in kills several times simply by jumping around with a rocket launcher and shooting at anything red. The demon runes introduce an interesting component, allowing a player to turn into an extremely overpowered demon form, which results in even a mediocre player like myself racking up ridiculous killstreaks. It seems a bit unbalanced, since you not only do additional damage but gain additional hitpoints as well. I thought it might have been a bit more balanced to pass over the demon rune entirely and just spawn random powerups around the map (like quad damage, haste, berserk, etc.) which grant you an advantage, but not an insurmountable one.

SnapMap is a useful tool, and should be picked up easily by anyone who used to (or still might) design custom maps. The tutorial is a bit basic, leaving the many complexities of the system to be discovered by the player during use, but once you get to know the system there's a great deal you can do. However, the entire system suffers from a limitation similar to one encountered in Fallout 4's settlement building: that the engine can only handle so many assets on a given map, and any asset you load into the map is constantly present whether it's visible to the players or not. So, if you want to build a survival mode where players deal with wave after wave of demons, there is a hard cap on the number of enemies you can populate the map with. This put a hamper on my SnapMap usage, since the mode I really wanted to create was rendered impossible (survival mode, increasing difficulty, virtually neverending, designed to end when the players died.)

Even for it's shortcomings in all three phases of its existence, I'd be lying if I said I wasn't just nitpicking here. DOOM is a great buy for any fan of old first person shooters, especially those who enjoy a lengthy, challenging campaign. If you're looking for all the secrets or playing on a high difficulty setting (or both?) you'll get at least 15-20 hours out of the campaign alone. Why are you even reading this? Buy it now.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI haven't had this much fun with an FPS in a while!

This game just makes you want to keep running, keep moving, all the while attempting to serve up some tasty buck-shot sandwiches for your polite guests from Hell.

A few little bugs, but nothing that breaks the game (some scrolling issues occasionally in the Codex - like I said, no biggie), but nothing that can detract from the sheer joy of the Glory Kills, great weapons (and their customisations), and a great feel when moving around in the interesting environments.

Additionally, while this is an FPS that encourages you to move quickly and dispatch your foes with overwhelming firepower (see Super Shotgun in mouth), the Codex also speaks about weaknesses that can be exploited in your enemies. It strikes me that while the publishers want a fast, flashy, gore-dripping game, they also want you to have a think about where you stick that shotgun; considering side stepping the Pinky and putting two shells in it's flank, instead of wasting six in it's face.

I think I'll leave it about there; I'm off to have another romp.
