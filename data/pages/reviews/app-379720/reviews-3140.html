\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDOOM makes a comeback after four years. Did id Software and Bethesda succeed at making it worth your time? ♥♥♥♥ yeah, they did. First off, the best part about this game: The singleplayer campaign. Took me about 12 hours to finish on the "Hurt Me Plenty" difficulty setting (which is basically the "Normal" difficulty in most other games). It never got boring as you constantly face new kinds of demons and you constantly get new weapons to fight said demons (such as the Super Shotgun from older DOOM games, the BFG etc.). There's many different execution/ "Glory Kill" animations for every single type of demon and overall the combat feels very fluid and satisfying. And then of course this game has incredibly beautiful visuals and stunning effects. You'll be surprised (or at least I was) how amazing a game like this can look. To top it all off, the campaign has a great, intense soundtrack which just makes everything even better than it already was. And then there's Snapmap and...the Multiplayer (which is pretty much dead at this point). Snapmap is a great idea and there's tons of different custom maps designed by players for players, with every kind of gamemode you could imagine. Especially fun when you have someone to play them with. The multiplayer, though? There's not too many players and it's uh....not exactly a masterpiece. But in the end, you'll have to decide whether you like it or not.

tl;dr: You like action games? You liked the previous DOOM games but you're also a fan of modern shooters? You like gore and Electronic Metal music? You like beautiful graphics?
Then buy this game.
Now.

It gave you a little taste here, a bigger fight there and finally you were ready for the big leagues. The game is paced using nothing more than arenas and transitional spaces. You are constantly moving from one arena to the next with the lure of hidden upgrades dragging you out of arenas on the far side.

The attention to detail in crafting the gameplay is worthy of the Doom legacy. Each weapon the game hands the player feels like a different tool in the toolbox, not an upgrade over everything you had before. Enemies all behave differently and engage you at different ranges and different speeds, encouraging you to pick the right weapon for each job. Upgrade modules for the weapons provide useful alternate functions, further adding to the variety of the weapons and your engagement options during combat. At no point did combat feel stale; in fact, I was consistently excited for the next arena.

Movement through the game is fluid despite larger, more open levels than in any other Doom game to date. The game provides visual cues to players that help them find their way. There is a modern "compass" option in the game, but I hardly found myself needing it. As the player traverses the map the developers consistently placed upcoming arenas, corridors and secrets squarely in the player's field of view. It made entire maps feel connected and organic in a way that many games fail to achieve. Most secrets in the game are obviously visible on normal paths of travel, leaving only the pathways to them hidden. The game also uses simple lighting cues to draw the player forward in the map. Things as simple as green lights, an almost universal signal for moving forward, are placed to indicate the next platform, door or path. They're obvious yet subtle enough to never truly interfere with the player's experience.

If there is one thing I had to take issue with, it is the sound design for some of the monsters. I consistently found myself being surprised by Hell Knights, Mancubi and other minor demons in the midst of battle because I never heard them arrive. In classic Doom, each monster had unique and distinct sounds that it made so that the player knew that a new threat was rolling in. I think the primary issue here is that battles are often just a cacophony of sound - mostly groans and gurgles from Possessed and Imps, and the sounds of the more formidable demons are being drowned out. It should be the other way around, however. Strong demons should dominate the soundscape with lesser demons only drawing major attention once the strong ones have fallen. That's an overall minor gripe with what is otherwise brilliant sound design on part of the game. Weapons all sound destructive with no two being criminally similar even when they are both firing the same ammo types (shotgun/double barrel or machinegun/chain gun). One touch in particular that I liked was how the sounds of hell were infused into the very core of the UAC facility. Doors, lifts and switches all echoed the cries of the damned behind normal sounds of operation. It's a small touch that helps build an extraordinary atmosphere.

Doom's story is something more akin to a veil over a legend. What lines and motivations that make up the story aren't as significant as the underlying cycle. The tale of the Doom Slayer is bound to repeat itself over and over again. It's a wonderful commentary on the relationship between the game and the player.

Games such as Doom, Wolfenstein and Shadow Warrior not only show that the classics can be returned to relevance in this new age of gaming, but that a worthwhile and engaging story experience can be shared with the player through the lens of a first person shooter.
