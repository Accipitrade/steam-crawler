\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYes.

This game made me realise how utterly boring single player campaigns in other games actually, truly are. It's almost a shocking revelation that has been bestowed upon me, leaving me feeling shocked, sad, confused, and speechless. But one thing I certainly know now: this is what a first person shooter is supposed to be all about.

The game immediately starts you off with shooting demons in the face. As you progress through each level, the game throws a completely new demon at you, each with their own set of unique abilities. The amazing thing is however - it just continues to do it. Each level tells you "Here, now deal with this new creature. Here, try and figure this guy out. Here, try handling two of them. Now three. Now a mix of all of them at once." It turns into absolute chaos, guys. CHAOS. The demons they introduce keep getting bigger and stronger. You're dashing around the place running for your damn life as a two-story high demon comes racing after you, weaving through other demons, and doing running drive-bys as you try to avoid getting smashed into a billion bits. It's like a grand finale to a fireworks show. It's actually, quite simply, incredible. I'm also very happy to see many of the classic doom demons make a persistent appearance. The demons themselves, especially as you progress later into the game, can be extremely intimidating. There is a certain demon, that whenever I see him, I audibly say "oh F***K", pull out the biggest gun I have, and hope to jesus I'm able to kill it.

The weapons are characters in of themselves - and you are introduced to them usually very quickly, collecting a gigantic arsenal to blast demons away. You will constantly be switching weapons in this game. You'll never know what enemy they are going to toss your way next - and in what combination - and sometimes certain weapons work better than others against certain demons. Rockets are great for giant demons with large hit boxes, while perhaps a less messy, loud, automatic weapon is better saved for the smaller guys.

The level designs are also pretty damn cool. One map in particular early on felt VERY Doom-like, running around collecting the blue, yellow and red key to unlock doors. But not too many are like this, which can be a good thing.

As for the multiplayer, I have heard people say it feels like Call of Duty / Halo. I quite frankly don't know what on Earth these people are talking about. Just because the armor looks like Master Chief - does not mean it plays like Halo. Just because there is a leveling system - does not mean it plays like Call of Duty. This game feels to me more like a mix of Quake and TimeSplitters.

Snapmap, while I haven't created anything quite yet, (need to watch some tutorials), is a pretty awesome addition. Unfortunately it seems a bit hard right now to find many people playing custom maps in the matchmaker which is built in. I assume it will become more populated over time when more people finish the campaigns and want a break from the standard Multiplayer modes. Another huge plus to Snapmap - 4 player coop missions.

I could get into the runes, the weapon upgrades, the challenges you find in each map, the secrets, etc. But I just wanted to review the parts that really stood out to me the most.

If you haven't already, buy this game. See you in hell.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCampaign:
Instant action, some platforming secret finding and good deal of different upgrades to deal with.

Finding health upgrades and weapons as you progress takes me back to Metroid Prime. Takes me back even more when I am getting lost and using the map alot to find out where to go - though I love trying to get all the secrets!

This game is not messing around, you need to constantly be moving around, utilizing glory killing (melee attack) to regain health and chainsaw to regain ammo in a pinch!
Did not expect to be killed the first hours but some monsters can easily deal 100 damage in a short amount of time if you get caught off guard.

Campaign has alot of replayability, either to replay it on harder or just going back to find all secrets!

Multiplayer:
Fast paced (not frantic) and easy to get into. Every other game you get different unlocks (there's 150 taunts for the game and you can equip four (4) taunts at the same time).

Good variety of multiplayer modes;
Team Deathmatch - Get more kills than the opposing team,
Soul Harvest - Team Deathmatch but a demon player is always being active
Capture - Capture and protect zones to aquire points
Mobile Capture - One moving zone to capture and protect
Clan arena - One life, 100 hp 100 armor, less powerups than Team Deathmatch, first to three games won out of five (5)
Freeze tag - Clan arena but instead of dying you freeze, and team mates can thaw (revive) frozen friends. First team to
freeze lose, first to three games won out of five (5)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm one of those people that looked at the E3 trailer footage and the later released gameplay footage and thought it wasn't going to be very good.

I thought the Glory Kills would get old
I thought the gunplay wouldn't be very good
I thought there were too few enemies

Well, after beating the singleplayer campaign at about 9 hours in length, while missing plenty of secrets and challanges,

I'm very, very glad i was wrong.

This game features some of the best level design i've seen in a first person shooter in the last decade. I found myself combing over the same levels over and over for all the extremely cleverly hidden secrets.

And in those levels are more than enough Demons to kill with your extremely satisfying arsenal of weapons and equipment. The Glory Kills are satisfying and quick enough to where they don't break up the pace of combat, and offer a second of a breather in what can be extremely intense arena sections. The way health, armor, and ammunition is managed makes for an intense experience where constant weapon swapping in combat is encouraged.

Now, I will also say that I am one of those wierdos that plays the old Doom games still with mods like Brutal Doom and other such things. So when I saw that this iteration featured character progression, i was a little skeptical at first.

But, once again, i was wrong. And better for it.

There are 3 main methods of improving your Doom Marine. Praetor Tokens, Weapon mods and Weapon mod upgrades, and Argent Energy.

Praetor Tokens allow you to increase elements of your armors functionality, making it blink when your nearby a secret, more resistant to fire damage, or completely invulnerable to explosive barrel damage.

Weapon mods are collected from Field drones throughout the campaign, they allow you to select one of two mods for any gun in your current arsenal. For example, and likely the first choice in weapon mods you'll get, the charge-up 3 round burst mod for your shotgun, or the explosive shot mod. Both are highly effective and useful in different situations. You can and are encouraged to seek out as many of these field drones as possible to collect both mods avaliable for each weapon, allowing you to switch fairly rapidly mid-combat to adapt to the situation.

Once you've selected the weapon mod of your choice, you'll be rewarded with weapon upgrade points as you play the game. You can use these to give the weapon mods themselves faster charge up time, smaller gap between firing, and many other fairly useful upgrades that, in most cases, contribute to the overall speed of your character in combat. These mods accumulate in a "Mastery" upgrade which can be unlocked by purchasing all previous upgrades, then completing a challange specific to that mod. For example, the mastery for the Explosive Shot mod for the Shotgun is to get direct hit on 30 imps with the explosive shot, and that unlocks cluster explosives for the explosive shot.

Argent Energy allows you to increase the maximum amount of Health, Armor, or Ammunition you can hold at any given time. You can choose to allot this energy into these three catagories as you wish.

Another method of progression which is a tad more minor than the previously listed three methods, is the Rune system
As you play the game you'll find Runes which let you play wierd, often time based challanges. If you complete the challanges you'll unlock these ruins which will offer you a passive bonus. Like being able to execute Glory Kills faster, from further away, or have more control while in the air.

It might seem complicated, but its all presented to you very intuitively and offers dynamic progression through the game. adding replay value and plenty of variety to the combat.

It is also worth noting that the game runs beautifully. I currently have a quad-core i5 3570K 3.40GHz, 16 gig of ram, and a GTX 970 and it did not drop below 60fps on all maxed out settings at any point in the campaign.

As far as story is concerned the writing is excellent. Doom Marine is all that he needs to be, a pair of armoured hands to hold your rocket launcher. Story elements are not extremely intrusive. There are only one or two instances where you are forced to sit in a room and listen to an NPC talk to you. Other interactions with the story can be completely ignored at the players behest

This game's singleplayer element clearly had love and passion poured into it. Its well polished, rock solid and relentlessly fun to play. I know I will be giving the campaign another spin on Nightmare, and Ultra-Nightmare difficulties in the future.

As for the multiplayer element, of the little i've put in over the Beta and now the full release, unfortunately I cannot say that the seperate development team tasked with the Multiplayer has done an amazing job. It really is just a washed down arena shooter. While some may still derive joy from it, i found the single player element to be the main driving point for my purchase.

And unfortunately I haven't given SnapMap a try. It seems the tools are very open to people being creative with them, and while SnapMap multiplayer is limited to 4 players only, I can see some potential there for fun provided the variety in room sections is wide enough.

Overall, if you're planning on playing it exclusively for the singleplayer campaign, it is very good, but you may want to hold out for a price drop as paying 60 dollars for a 9-10 hour campaign (assuming you opt out of second playthroughs) seems a bit shallow.
