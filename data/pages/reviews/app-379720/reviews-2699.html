\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wish my only options weren't thumbs up or thumbs down, because I'd give this game 3 out of 5 stars: not bad, fun in a lot of ways, but could have been better too.

First off, the minimum system requirements are way off. Though I own this game now, I started playing it on a freeplay weekend, and just for fun I tried it on a dual-core Pentium Extreme Edition (3.73GHz, 2M L2 cache). It runs like a charm on here, despite the quad-core i5 (2.4GHz, 6M L2 cache) processor Steam claims is a bare minimum for this game. Granted, it's paired with a 700-series GTX Titan, and we all know that it's the graphics card that does the heavy lifting in modern games. Still, I never would have even taken a chance and bought this game due to its nominal quad-core requirement, had I not tried it on a freeplay weekend first; Steam, I get that you don't want people complaining because they bought games that their systems can't possibly handle, but you're hurting your sales by overspecing the minimum system requirements. Amusingly, you actually *recommend* a quad-core i7 3.77GHz for this game, and for the life of me I don't understand how or why you think it's at all pertinent.

Now on to the game itself. Checkpoints. Really?! Yeah, I'm about to go into this crazy-hard area in which I know I have a good chance of dying, but if I do die then oh well I guess I'll just have to repeat the half-hour I've spent getting here since the last checkpoint. I mean, it's not as if I have a job, a house to maintain/repair, pets to take care of, friends or family I might like to see, a gym membership, or any other hobbies whatsoever besides playing this game. I have games from the 90s that allow me to save anywhere/anytime, that additionally allow me to restart the level (using a quiet autosave that the game handles in the background) in case things go catastrophically wrong. Is this 1995 or 2017? Am I playing a console or a PC? There's no excuse for this.

The graphics in this game are quite amazing. I've experimented at different resolutions, and things even look stunning at 1024x768, due to the miraculous shadings and reflections. The gore that accompanies melee ("glory") kills would impress a mortician or ER doctor with its realism.

For the most part, I actually do like this game; the first two hours felt a bit uninspired, but it definitely picked up impressively after that. This checkpoint thing is beyond ridiculous, though, considering that I'M NOT AN UNEMPLOYED TEENAGER LIVING IN MOM'S BASEMENT AND DON'T HAVE TIME TO REPEAT 30+ MINUTES OF GAME TIME EVERY TIME SOMETHING CATCHES ME OFF-GUARD OR I MISS A JUMP.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDOOM is hands-down the best single-player experience I've had with a shooter in... maybe ever. It's a game that has mechanical perfection -- the actual shooting cannot be improved upon. Combine this superb gameplay with great level design filled with great encounters, great enemy design with tons of variety, great weapon design and variety, great presentation, and an absolutely phenomenal soundtrack, and you get a shooter that is going to set the bar for quite some time.

DOOM's gameplay is in many ways a return to form for the genre: it plays with the speed of 90s-early 00s shooters, but with modern conventions that we've grown used to. These include a time-slowing weapon wheel, some RPG mechanics that allow you to augment your weapons and abilities, a more modern map system, etc. A core mechanic is the concept of the glory kill. When a demon is low on health, it glows, and you can approach it and use a melee attack to beat it down in brutal fashion with yoru bare hands. This is more than just a fun aesthetic mechanic; glory kills immediately grant health. Whenever you're low, you can go for a glory kill to get a health boost. The lower your health, the more health you get,

Shooting is tight, and isn't bogged down by aim-down-sight or other zoom mechanics aside from a few specific cases. Instead, your left trigger activates your active weapon mod.

Each gun has two weapon mods that you can switch on the fly for the right situation, though I found myself quickly choosing my favorite between the two and never bothering to switch to my alternate option. These include things like Siege Mode for the Gauss Cannon, which lets you charge shots at the expense of your movement, and then unleash it for high damage and enemy penetration. Or Turret Mode for the Chaingun, which allows you to rapidly use all your ammo. Or my favorite, the Micro Missiles for the Heavy Assault Rifle, which allows you to fire a swarm of tiny missiles that stick to your enemies. And on top of all that, every mod for every gun can be upgraded three times with powerful new effects, which in turn unlock a challenge (usually killing a certain number of demons with that mod) to get a fourth, dramatically powerful upgrade.

But the guns aren't worth much if the levels aren't fun to go through and the enemies aren't fun to fight -- which isn't a problem in DOOM. Level design is excellent. It's mostly linear, but has enough wiggle room and just the right amount of exploration where it still feels like a shooter with constant action, but with some secrets to uncover. This is bound together by an excellent 3D map UI that's easy to read and navigate, and can be upgraded (or holomaps can be downloaded) to show you the locations of key items such as the currencies for the various types of upgrades to your weapons and armor.

Each level is packed with carefully-crafted encountered featuring a TON of different kinds of demons. Every encounter is essentially a mish-mosh of different demons, and depending on the makeup of the encounter, gameplay is changed. If you're poitted against weaker, long-range demons, you might be good using your pistol. But then a melee-range demon that charges you down appears and suddenly you're on the run. You fight new types of demons in almost every level of the game, so everything stays fresh throughout.

The whole experience is wrapped in a narrative that is self-aware that it's a story about demons opening a gate to Hell on Mars. The protagonist frequently and repeatedly ignores demands of NPCs squawking at him over the radio, or interrupts their attempts at exposition in favor of getting back to the action.

Perhaps one of DOOM's greatest strengths is its soundtrack, which kicks in and slows down seamlessly as combat starts and ends. DOOM 2016 features probably the best heavy metal soundtrack I've ever come across in a game. It has to be heard to be believed, preferably in tandem with the rest of DOOM's excellent sound design as you're shooting demons to death.

Really, I can't praise this game enough. It was an absolute joy to go through and a throwback to an older style of shooter that clearly still holds up. I don't often finish a game and then immediately hope we get a sequel, but if the next DOOM is even half the game that DOOM 2016 is, I'll be there day one.

Totally Awesome. Graphics on ultra look great and the game runs at really high framerates with a decent up to date rig. Even with FOV at 110 my fps was still always over 160 frames. Loved it

All good. Directional audio is pretty good too.
