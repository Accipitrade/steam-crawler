\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLooks like Doom, smells like Doom and sounds like Doom, it is DOOM!

Positive:
id got it right. The game is polished, the action is fluid, the environments are incredibly detailed. Monsters look, move and sound awesome.

There is even story and lore, funny jokes and references all over the place. The universe feels tight and you can't wait to see what's behind the next corner. The progression feels natural and the learning curve is just right to keep the game from feeling too easy or too hard.

Actual combat is designed to keep you moving in a way that no game before has ever done. Simply put if you don't move you die, but if you do you find it oddly familiar (for those who played the original 2 games).

Negative:
Some of these are personal quirks some are overarching design decisions.

- Each combat encounter feels like an arena. You enter the pit, monsters spawn left and right, You fight like hell until no more come, done, area cleared, demonic presence is at an "acceptable level". This whole game mechanic feels contrived and for me at least breaks the immersion. The original games had a much more natural progression, monsters were not clustered into a designed arena but spread evenly across the levels.

Doom 3 sinned in the opposite direction. It put all the monsters in closets that opened when you had your back to them. Doom (silent 4) puts them all together in plain sight for target practice mayhem having you jump around while you run and gun.

Some mix of these 2 mechanics would have felt much more natural plus the "normal" shooter encounters. So far I also haven't encoutnered any of the orignal Doom trademark "trap pits" where you stumble into a room through an elevator, everything goes dark and you have to fight for your life. It is a bit like this but without the claustrophobia, darkness or sheer panick.

- Weapon design is clearly progressive and each new weapon you encounter is more powerful than the one before. Ammo capacity is tight so you'll empty your rounds quickly. Problem with this is that as you progress you need to play rock-paper-scissor with the game: encounter hell-knight -> use plasma gun. Encounter revenant, use rockets. Did an imp spawn ? quickly switch back to shotgun.

Basically each demon has an optimal weapon for it. If you try to kill a revenant with your shotgun you will most likely die. And if you hunt fast imps with the plasma gun you'll empty most of your clip shooting air. Basically you learn which weapon works where and switch depending on what you're facing.

My gripe with this is that it breaks immersion and forces you into a weapon-switch-fest that becomes tiresome.

- Something others have criticized aswel is the emphasis on melee or (glory-kills). If you don't do it you don't get the pick-ups, and die...

- And lastly, while the chainsaw is clearly OP it is also nerfed by the tiny fuel capacity and the absolutely arbitrary 1-2-3 fuel per monster type. This makes the chainsaw part of the weapon-switch-fest (and part of the problem) -> are you low on ammo on everything ? pull out the saw and replenish... I admit I would have much preferred to be able to use the chainsaw at will even if it needed fuel as ammo.

Despite these issues the game is rock solid this is just what would make me rate it 8/10 and not a perfect 10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'll update this as I continue through the game, but here are my initial thoughts:

This game instantly feels more like DOOM than any game since 1996. Combat is extremely fast-paced, and by the second mission, even on the normal difficulty, you're circle-strafing around the room just trying to stay alive while you pick off anything you can. It feels like the most intense Ultra-Violent maps from the original games, with movement speed turned up beyond Nightmare! levels.

One of the major strengths of the originals' design was the weapon balance, in that every weapon was a tool for a specific job. That has returned in an even more pronounced way with the new DOOM. It's clear the developers understand, gameplay-wise, what made the originals so good. From what I can tell, they've captured and refined those qualities here.

UPDATE:

My initial review was written while I had something like ~7 hours in the game. At the time of this update, I've played about 40 hours, the vast majority of tha being the singleplayer campaign and maybe ~10% of it in team deathmatch.

I've completed the singleplayer campaign from scratch twice now, once on Ultra-Violence and once on Nightmare.

I'll elaborate on what I've already written. DOOM (2016) gets everything right that the first game did, and in fact, does it better. Aesthetics aside, what made the first game so cool was its gameplay elements consisting of harmonization between the weapon balance, player movement, and enemy types and what they brought to the table for you to work with.

The first game had enemies that caused you to use certain weapons depending on your position in relation to theirs, as well as affecting your movement. Dodge projectiles by strafing, evade the demon by backpeddling, keep enemy positions in check by charging forward. Take cover against hitscan weapon enemies. This game features all of this and more. Well, actually I guess not hitscan enemies, but I digress.

Player movement is extremely fast, lack of bunnyhopping notwithstanding, and movement is absolutely essential to success. This is the only game I've ever played that kind of felt like the original DOOM's movement. The inertia isn't quite there, nor is wallrunning, but it feels right, and it feels good.

Combat is very hard until you have enough experience to understand what to upgrade and how to leverage your strengths. I recommend starting on Ultra-Violence and moving up. It will challenge you hard, and it's fun every second of the way.

The soundtrack is brutal and intense. The compression on the drums, especially, makes for some psychotic slams and it fits the combat very well. There are hints of old DOOM songs in there, too, so veterans that listen closely might be able to pick them out. Off the top of my head, I can think of four references that are present in varying degrees of completeness.

Commentary on game systems and multiplayer might be coming in the future with another update.

Bottom line is this: I've only fallen harder for this game after spending ~30 more hours playing since the initial write-up. I still highly recommend this. I believe this is probably the best singleplayer shooter ever created.

UPDATE 2: Multiplayer

I've spent the last three days or so playing a lot of team deathmatch. I've managed to level up to Echelon 3 rank 20-something (that means level to 50, then level to 50 again, and again, and then 20-something more levels) (updated: I've since leveled to echelon 6 and I might get to 8 before I sleep tonight). Somewhere between 15-20 hours of play I think (now over 30).

DOOM TDM is the best Halo game ever for PC. Why do I (and others) compare the game to Halo? There is an unmistakable influence found in everything from the announcer's tone, to the game design, down to the player models and maps. Everything in the MP game's design has been touched by the influence of Halo. Let me elaborate before rambling on random multiplayer thoughts.

Let's start by pointing out that it just doesn't feel like id deathmatch. Two-weapon loadouts. Power weapons. There are only team modes. No duel modes. Only huge, extremely complex maps. The weapon set has been expanded with several new items that specifically feel very Halo. There's not only one sniper weapon (the vortex rifle). There are like three. That's very Halo. There's even one called the BR that shoots in bursts of three, which ought to remind any Halo veteran of a certain infamous weapon from the past. While there are (thankfully) no vehicles, there are demon powerups, which are a kind of analog to Halo's vehicles. The person controlling the demon can absolutely dominate unless the opposing team coordinates a focused attack. Nevertheless, the inclusion of explicit demons from the campaign (baron, revenant, mancubus) is one of the only distinctly DOOM things in the multiplayer. Hell, they can't even bring themselves to use the word "frags," something found in every id Software deathmatch game since the original DOOM. Until now. Now, they're just "kills," just like Halo.

Is this a bad thing? It's not bad, per se. It's very fun. But I have to warn you, if you're looking for the next installment of classic id Software multiplayer, this is not it. But again, it's very fun and addictive. So whatever issues I raise from here on out, keep that in mind. And keep in mind that I'm nowhere near done playing it; I plan to level to Echelon 10 level 50 (the current cap). It should probably take 1.5-2 weeks if I play every day. So look, it's cool. Now onto some commentary about mechanics.

This is where my thoughts get weird. This is a video game, so does anything matter other than the fun factor? The game's MP is fun. But I have to say, it's like the junk food of game design. What I mean is, it's a team-based shooter (no duel mode in sight), just like Halo. But the rewards systems are all about your individual performance. It hardly matters if your team wins. Hell, it doesn't really matter that much if you die a lot, provided you get roughly as many kills as deaths. You get bonuses on top of bonuses when you kill with the absurdly overpowered "power weapons," a borrowed maxim of Halo. The team as a whole doesn't really benefit from controlling the map, other than "winning" but who cares about that? It's all about you and your points and progression. Hell, there's a large health pickup literally around every corner, so you don't really even have to know the item layout to survive. You just sort of jump in and play.

And that's really weird.

After spending several hours in a few Overwatch beta weekends, I've come to appreciate the required teamplay aspect that game brings to the table. This game is almost exactly the opposite. What can I say, it's the ultimate selfish team shooter. But it's fun.

There's nothing like knowing the power weapon spawn points and trying to capitalize on them and just destroying other players with the chainsaw. It's so fun and visceral. Actually, the MP chainsaw is my favorite powerup in any game, ever, no question.

But did we want this? I didn't. I've played a fair amount of Halo Reach multiplayer (50 hours? I can't remember) and I like it. But this isn't what core id fans want, for sure. On the other hand yet again, an entire generation of shooter players are probably jumping into this thinking it's totally normal and par for the course. Things change. I kind of doubt that if they did a Quake style deathmatch game, it would be as popular, and it would certainly not be as accessible. Nevertheless, I feel like they left behind the hardcore competitive scene to deliberately cater to a different style of player to a degree that this game will never be an esport, and I think that's sad. So, mixed feelings.

This isn't the MP I wanted and dreamed of all of these years since DOOM 3. It's still very fun. Just don't expect the successor to Quake Live.

More updates later? I don't know.\t\t\t\t\t
