They are rage. Brutal. Without mercy.
DOOM is a first-person shooter, serving as the eponymous franchise’s return to the fast-paced, demon-slaying action and sprawling map designs.

Everything has clearly gotten out of hand, yes, but it was worth the risk.
id Software certainly knows how to make great looking games. Lovingly detailed textures and models adorn every level, with especially convincing post-processing effects. I’m still majorly impressed by how sparks bounce off your held weapons. The lighting is top notch, from the brightest research labs to the darkest pits of the underworld, though a small number of rooms have unnaturally dark lighting. Enemies are recognizable immediately, thanks to their splendid animation and visual design – and they all chunkify gloriously when subjected to enough violence. Some form of texture streaming is most notable on your weapons at the start of a level, where they progressively load higher quality assets over about a second or so, and there’s an unfortunate tendency for several cutscenes to be full of harsh flashing images and lights.

Yet not only is the game positively gorgeous (even goregeous), it runs fantastic as well. Unskippable starting movies aside, on maximum graphical settings, my Ryzen 9 3900X, 64GB RAM, 1070 Ti, Windows 10 Pro system easily keeps a locked 60 framerate at 1440p in even the densest scenes the game has to offer.

Oh, but the feast is yet to stop. Mick Gordon proves, with just the main menu theme, that a chainsaw is a fantastic musical instrument. This title’s soundtrack is an awesome collection of some of the finest synthesized metal that one’s ears can ever be blessed with. Even the quieter sequences of the game are backed up by excellent atmospheric ambience. In short, the music absolutely rocks from the start and all the way to the end.

Really, no expense is spared on the sound front. Shotguns, heavy rifles, gauss cannons and big-freaking-guns: a splendid cacophony of thunderous death, all backed up by the chilling shrieks and roars of hell’s denizens. And before we move on, a special mention to the dialog, most notably Samuel Hayden’s intimidating voice.

May your thirst for retribution never quench.
All there is to know to get started is that demons have trashed Mars, thanks to exploitation of Hell’s resources and suspicious practices within the Union Aerospace Corporation, and you’re the only one around who can stop them. The story feels like a subtle parody of FPS trends, particularly through your character’s mannerisms. Impatiently knocking over a communication screen to cut dialog short, and smashing delicate equipment against the desires of the facility leader, are but a few examples of the triumphant characterization on display, with nary a spoken word from your marine.

Yet take a minute to read the ever-expansive Codex, and you might find the lore within to be simultaneously captivating and awesome. On top of this, copious amounts of dark comedy linger within the bowels of the UAC.

But you… You will be worse…
Ever missed the classic high-speed, projectile-avoiding shooter gameplay style of yore? This title has you covered. You have no need to worry about such petty matters as reloading, running or accuracy. There’s huge emphasis on staying on the move and being aggressive - if you dare stand still, you’ll be eviscerated blindingly fast, even with full stacks of health and armor, and to further drive the point home, getting close and applying extreme violence to your foes is the best way to recover resources.

https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2658002220

Ah, chainsaw! The great communicator!
Ideally, you’re swinging from mook to mook, bouncing from near-death and making use of your entire arsenal to survive engagements. It does feel like the game isn’t quite committed to such a direction, since fallen demons still drop health pickups for you if your own life’s low. The chainsaw is meant to restock your ammo in case you run dry, yet the static pickups and random enemy drops ensure you won’t have much need of it, even on Ultra-Violence. It might not be wholly practical, but it’s still hugely cathartic to rip open a troublesome Hell Knight when you get into a bind, then see the shower of goodies rain out of the gore.

Just make sure you mind your step. Platforming sections are a thing, and they’re… not that good. One small slip-up, easy to perform with the sluggish default air controls, and you’ll have to reload a checkpoint.

https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2659660473

Your affinity for guns is apparent.
The classic FPS-enthusiast toolset is provided within DOOM. You barely have to use your infinite-ammo pistol when one minute into the game, you’re given a shotgun. Your arsenal will expand quickly as you progress through the story; most of them have a pair of unique mods that provide additional utility when needed. Be sensible by slotting a scope on a rifle and remotely detonating in-flight rockets, or unleash a storm of lead with triple-barreled chainguns. Plenty of opportunity to make use of everything, with not only well over a dozen enemies to tear apart, but tons of challenges per level to upgrade your weapon mods with.

And how would you fancy expansive environments with sprawling maps? There are plenty of secrets to be found, including early weapons and upgrade tokens. Each level also has a lone lever you might walk past several times, even when replaying the level and looking to acquire all collectibles, but pulling them open will reveal some highly welcome trips down memory lane.

https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2658001746

You think the only way is to kill them… and you may be right.
As you upgrade your equipment and turn levels upside-down, the difficulty curve seems to plateau a bit past the mid-point of the story. Even as Hell throws its mightiest warriors your way, you become so absurdly powerful with just a little bit of exploration, that fights blend together in the latter parts of the game. Certain weapons also become nigh-obsolete, outside of a few niches – the poor plasma rifle gets little love compared to its railgun cousin, as a notable example. Not helping matters are the combinations of runes you can equip that make things like armor and ammunition a non-issue. You’re still advised to keep your wits about you for the awesome boss fights sprinkled around. These are proper tests of skill and pattern memorization.

Despite turning a bit too easy by the end, the campaign remains the star of the show. Eight-to-ten hours of visceral demon-slaying action, easily extendable if you’re looking for secrets. Arcade Mode is especially worth considering when playing through the story again; with only a few changes to map layouts and the inclusion of scores and multipliers, you’ll be making one Hell of a mess out of demons with speed you never knew you had.

Meanwhile, SnapMap is a platform through which users can create their own levels, complete with scripting tools to make new game modes. Its potential is there, but even the featured levels feel like they’re well below the quality caliber of the singleplayer. Multiplayer is also available and still reasonably active, should you crave for the standard affair of deathmatches and objective-based missions with others.

Rip and tear, until it is done.

With superb presentation, excellent gameplay and a wicked soundtrack, DOOM is a triumphant revival of the classic FPS. Nothing short of a glowing recommendation.
