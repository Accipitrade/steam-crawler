\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game... gets close to the sort of action-packed rampage of sheer, blissful fantasy violence satisfyingly against the elements of some clearly defined malevolent force that used to be, at least, more common than it is these days. And yet... something's off.

It could be the sad attempt at a full narrative that's been tacked onto a game that used to slap a few text screens in front of you at the start and end of its many-levelled episodes, more as punctuation and framing than full-on storytelling. The story of the original Doom was "growly man, covered in blood, blam blam, yay, he's dead and I'm not", varied slightly when demonic fiends start showing up. Doom II's story was essentially the same, only with more kinds of demons. The new Doom, this game, tries to make up a bunch of concepts and commodities and characters and motivations, and it just showcases how bad it is at doing those things. That was never what Doom was; the person you were playing wasn't some mythic hero, nor did he become one; he was a grunt with a gun, in a martian base full of monsters, who would rather be alive than dead. That was all the context and motivation you needed. Now, in the new one, we have these attempts to iconize the "Doom Slayer", which is a really silly title when you think about it... how do you slay doom, exactly? Or alternatively, doesn't every kind of slayer bring doom to the soon-to-be-slain? It's dumb either way. Growly, mythic, dumbly iconic, supply-drone-punching Doom Slayer is kind of a lame guy to play as, besides being utterly unrelatable... except perhaps to the steroid abuser crowd.

Then there's the graphics. They look gorgeous, sure; high polycount, pretty good frame rate, gore and twisty flesh with textures that look so gross a non-gamer might get queasy from looking at them. The monsters are very detailed, for what it's worth. The kicker is, you won't be seeing all that many of them, and not for very long. The combat is fast-paced, so you're in for exhilaratingly intense running-around-shooting-things challenges before you breathe out, savor the after-twitches, and survey the carnage that you have wrought.
Except you can't.
There are no persistent bodies in nu-Doom.
You may think this is a reasonable price to pay for fast, hi-poly action, but ask yourself: do you even have time to look at the monsters, to take in all those fancy sculpted knobbly bits? No, you don't, because if you do, they'll maul you. Well, you'll probably survive the earlier ones for several seconds, but you'll feel stupid standing there grunting in pain as you try to get a look at the fruits of some poor artist's most passionate efforts.
Just picture it; you've just killed, like, forty demons and zombies... their bodies line the floor of rooms and corridors, each macabre arrangement a testament to an ambush survived, a charge broken, a well-timed shot to an explosive barrel. As you double back with a new keycard on your belt, you pause to smirk at your earlier handiwork before making your way back to that locked blue door you passed earlier.
No, wait, that's the old Doom. Let me revise:
You've killed forty demons and zombies. They vanish like holograms and you regard the various blood-stained décor and civilian corpse props of the level with a strange sense that you are playing a computer game which someone thought would work just fine without any sense of congruity, immersion, satisfaction or accomplishment. As you double back through the area with your new keycard, you can almost picture tumbleweeds bouncing merrily across the utterly deserted levelscape. No trace remains of those twelve imps you hunted down, that big demon knight thing that was so much fun to do your melee finisher move on. You wonder if that was even in this room, or some other, very similar room -that happens a lot, you've noticed. All of the generic level pieces look alike, though some places do seem more sculpted than others. You track another collectible on your minimap, and eventually figure out the confusing path you have to take to reach the darn thing. That much, at least, remains largely the same...

Which of these games sounds like more fun? That's right, the old, ugly, pixellated, sprite-based Doom. If you really want to play Doom, go play the real one... or try that Ultimate version the fans have cooked up, maybe. Anything but this mirror-shine-polished Triple-A turd.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a modern take on Doom, to keep up with the times.

Story is pretty deep, however it's gonna be a lot of reading if you wanna immerse yourself on that level, and let's face it, we came here to play not read. Seriously it's THAT much text.

Otherwise gameplay is pretty solid. Monsters are a good challenge on any level, however Ultra-Violence and above has an emphasis towards you getting killed in very few hits, so..take that as it is.

Controls are on par with most modern-day shooters. mechanics are smooth, if you're struggling, you need more practice is all.

Levels are well-crafted with the atmosphere true to the Doom franchise. Plenty of exploring to do, with so many secrets tucked away for you to find.

Power-ups work as they should, and on the Normal to Slightly Hard levels they kind of turn the tables in your favor..by a lot. Think gore-fest. It's all good fun hehe

There is some replay value, especially if you found the Normal level somewhat unchallenging, crank it up and go through it all again. Faster.

Multiplayer seems fun, I started off with a few games ranking mid-table on every game, so I guess with some work and grinding I'd probably get a top 3 easily enough. But for me, with quite a few games in the to-play list, I normally push MP games away to avoid unnecessarily being stuck in one game. Not for me, with raising a family and making a career in between.

*******SPOILER*************

Boss fights are amazingly crafted, but I thought the Cyberdemon was a harder fight than the spider thingy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a fan of the original two DOOM games since my childhood (although less of a fan of the series' third entry), when I'd heard the series was getting a reboot, I was skeptical. I hadn't played more than an hour of DOOM 2016 before that skepticism had washed away entirely. This game brings back what the original DOOM had: speedy, satisfying gameplay, tight controls, and action and speed-oriented goals to meet, and a wonderfully mixed and dynamic soundtrack that sets the game in its time just as the MIDI thrash homages in the first game did. There's even a single-player Arcade mode that's a definite blast once you've finished the campaign. This game makes you feel like an action movie protagonist. True to its name, the game doesn't relent. Play this if you like fun video games.

Note: I didn't even touch the multiplayer. Still worth the money.\t\t\t\t\t
