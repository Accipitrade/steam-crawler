\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI can't not recommend this game, the combination of akimbo weapons and swords is loads of fun, soaring through the air, landing into a power-slide and clotheslining enemies with a katana in one hand and grenade launcher in the other, wallrunning and pulling crazy stunts with bullet-time and stuff. It's rad. If you have your VR legs you will love it; and if you don't, the locomotion system makes this a good game to GET your VR legs with. There is a lot of great things about the game. My gripe with it is that the 10 mission campaign is so short you will barely unlock a fraction of the skill upgrades (at the end of the campaign I had invested like 1 skill point in each of the branches of the tech tree, which is similar to the Mass Effect skill trees but imagine one character having the entire cast's worth of upgrade options), and once the main campaign is done there isn't a lot of replay value to playing the randomly generated missions, the small number of enemy types and mission objectives aren't really pulling me back into the game to keep upgrading the skill tree (something that would usually be a motivator for me in and of itself), the randomly generated artifact upgrades and upgrading the skills aren't necessary or motivating enough on their own (the campaign is easily complete-able without upgrading anything at all). Now that the dev is working on its second VR game (which I will likely buy based on how good this game was) I'm not confident there is going to be substantial content additions besides maybe maps and weapons. Like the Assassin's Creed games, this game gives you a great sandbox with some wicked cool toys but no really compelling reason to play. Don't pass this up, though, I'd just say $30 is too expensive for this game when you compare it to similarly priced VR games like Boneworks. I bet this game will go on sale when their next game comes out, snag it then if you are on the fence.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI want to preface this review by saying this is from the perspective of someone with a Valve Index - which for folks who dont know is a much different experience as a tethered system (attached to your PC).

So the concept itself of Sairento VR itself is awesome and the moments it worked (jumping through the air for kills, quickly traversing levels and wall running like a full on ninja) were truly immersive. The graphics on my Index with a 2080 TI were stunning, and enjoyable - so all pros there!

The issue for me stands that in a game that requires you to be nimble, accurate, and agile in your way of turning (physically with your body across anything from reloading your guns, swords, and mapping things to your body and movement) - things are just not mapped out accurately or with the Valve Index in mind.

This would not typically be an issue I find with VR games that list "Valve Index" as supported, and so while I appreciate the games premise/would love to actually play it properly - I would hold off on playing this game if you are an Index user.

With the Index, stuff truly feels half finished and janky - even mapping in game shows other VR systems, and when aligning my guns to my hip - the system could barely understand where the Valve "knuckles" were standing.

All in all, hope this improves and I can change this - but figured I would warn future Index users at this time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe movement system feels great. I found I could double jump, wall run, and climb around pretty easily without feeling motion sick or losing my sense of balance.

Combat was a different story for me though.
- Guns and explosives lack impact. I also found aiming to be pretty difficult. Many of the weapons have sights, but for some reason I found these harder to use than in other VR games. It would have been nice to have the option of adding laser sights to some of the guns.
- Throwing weapons simply did not work for me. They either refused to leave my hand or they flew off in some wild direction. I don't have this problem with throwing in other VR games.
- Swords get the job done, but I didn't find them especially interesting to use. They seem to work based on contact damage, so it's pretty easy to just kill enemies by waggling at them or by walking into them. Also, maybe you have to unlock stuff, but I was surprised by the lack of variety in melee weapons.

Performance was pretty inconsistent for me. Some levels ran perfectly smoothly, others tanked. I'm not sure why this happens since the graphics, while serviceable, don't look like they should be too demanding. The AI doesn't seem especially complex, and there's very little physics simulation that I'm aware of, so it's not clear to me what the problem could be. Also, I ran into a bug in a campaign mission where I couldn't proceed due to an enemy that was way outside of the playable area.

The art direction and level design leaves much to be desired. The game really looks and feels its best when you're surrounded by enemies and there's special effects all around you. But as soon as you're just exploring the levels, sneaking around, or moving onto the next room, it becomes clear just how empty the levels are. They feel like they were put together arbitrarily out of reusable assets rather than handcrafted by a designer. The levels are decorated just enough to fit a basic theme (e.g. cyberpunk street, cyberpunk warehouse, cyberpunk etc), but beyond that there's not really much in the way of environmental storytelling.\t\t\t\t\t

Sairento VR is exactly what I wanted and expected in a early VR game and then some. With swords, shurikens, guns and more, you are not limited in ho you dispatch your foes.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDidn't think that you could do a VR platformer without foot trackers. Well, I was wrong. If you want to play Shadow Warrior in VR, this is it. I don't know why it took me so long to buy this.

My only complaint is that by default this game disables turning by controller, which is strange like Doom VR and Vanishing Realms. When you have limited space even in a room setup (8 x 6), and you're leashed by a headset wire, it's not a good default. It's a good thing that you can change the controls to allow turning. When I get my ceiling wire management hooks, I'll update the review with controller turning disabled.

UPDATE I still like the game, but I really enjoy it much more on Oculus Quest 2. Why? Because this is one of those games that need to be played wireless. It's too frustrating playing games like this with a wire that needs untangling. I played both the standalone version and the PCVR version through Virtual Desktop.\t\t\t\t\t
