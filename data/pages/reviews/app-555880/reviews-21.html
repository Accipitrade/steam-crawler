\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFast paced and exhilarating, Sairento is a shooter with emphasis on verticality and movement.

With a little time, making dashes, jumps, and performing slides becomes second nature which frees up the mental space to strategize. Managing ‘chakra’ between movement and bursts of damage in bullet-time is key to your survival and entertainment. Bullets’ status as physical objects makes slow-motion look outstanding and allows for badassery like ‘matrixing’ and deflecting projectiles with a sword. Combat arenas each hold a unique visual style and offer diversity in terrain. Outside the more generic hallways and indoor segments, the game can be quite pretty.

…

As a tall and lanky boy, I couldn’t really find a comfortable height/waist calibration setup. This made getting a feel for grabbing, holstering, reloading and sliding a tad difficult. I regularly experienced minor visual glitches, although that may have also been a problem regarding my PC.

Levels, even in the campaign, are procedurally generated. Despite being different with every play, the limited variety in the types of rooms available makes sessions feel repetitive which subsequently limits the game’s potential replayability. This ends up being significant as the large chunk of the game’s content resides in the loot system and its association with those generally unoriginal missions. It was a joy to play through Sairento’s excellent campaign, yet it’s disappointing to see such high emphasis on what is essentially grinding.

Sairento incorporates a complex system of weapon, armor, rune, and skill modifications which has high variability and depth to it. On the downside, moving through the skill tree can take some time and runes begin to stagnate in terms of power and interest after a while. I never found myself pushed to seek out or purchase runes and, after finding a solid loadout, I never felt motivated to try new weapons. [As a side note, it would’ve been nice to see the campaign introduce each weapon. It’d be a chance to create memorable set-pieces while showcasing the game’s range of options.]

…

Anyone who’s on the look for more VR shooters should definitely look into Sairento. I appreciated what the game tries to do with some of its systems, but many ideas fell flat in several places. It’s the core gameplay and the joy of shooting, dashing, and creating carnage that makes Sairento fantastic.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the best VR combat and fighting games period. Amazing fun, this and Beat Saber was what sold me on VR gameplay. The guns even have working sights on them if you choose to use them that way. The game has Swords, Kunai's, Katana's, Bows, Pistols, Snipers, Assault Rifles, and shotguns, plus some other ninja weapons like chakrums. DLC adds things like wolverine claws that can shoot the claws sort of like a crossbow (they can be made into explosives too) as well as a weapon that is similar to the chain scythes Reapers use on Tera online. For the infancy stages VR is in this game is incredible and a lot of fun. The biggest drawback of this game is the bigger the room you have the better, and if you own the wireless attatchment its even better. NEITHER OF WHICH ARE REQUIRED, I play Sairento in a small space generally standing still I just highly recommend a big room for it.

TLDR 10/10 would punch my $2k monitor again (literally) from lack of play space.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: "C'mon, stop trying to hit me and HIT ME!"
10/10 would bang-bang, in bullet time.

Sairento - which, in case anyone besides Captain Obvious was wondering, is actually a Japanese gairaigo (loan word / anglicism) for "silent" (get it? because ninjas like sneaking up on motherfeckers) - is the number one reason to:

- consider a proper room-scale VR setup (Oculus and PeaSantVR GTFO)
- get a wireless VR adapter like TPCast or HTC's official accessory and go full Ultron (there are no strings on me)
- put rubber condoms on your Vive controllers to absorb (very likely) impacts
- eliminate all furniture from wherever you may be playing, maybe tear down a wall or two
- disappear from RL society for a while because they don't deal with katana and guns very well
- remind yourself that quintuple walljumps and floor slides don't work that way IRL every time you exit VR

In Sairento, you are Chieko - a cyber-kunoichi kicking all kinds of ar$e using all kinds of things that hurt, badly, either from a distance of up close and personal. You will quickly learn that physics is a concept the developers decided to draw some inspiration from, but mostly ignore... because it's more fun that way. If the trailers haven't convinced you, I really don't know what else to say, except: don't even THINK of playing this if you have issues with locomotion in VR. Because you will freak the ♥♥♥♥ out.

If vertigo isn't an issue for you, strap in - 'cause this ish is WILD. Like, if Gorn were fast, with more weapons, actually challenging enemies, large arenas and a LOT of vertical movement.

It's fair to say I'm experienced with intense VR gameplay, and my setup is probably one of the largest wireless home rigs out there. Despite this, I found myself getting so immersed in the game that I actually managed to smash a controller into the wall. This basically never happens - not even in Gorn, which I play like a possessed mass-murdering madman (RIP AND TEAR!). My main piece of advice would be: always, ALWAYS play with the chaperone floor bounds permanently visible. You need to know where your real-life walls are as you're flailing your arms around fighting dozens of enemies, or you'll break something. And if this happens to me in a ~5x4m play area, it absolutely WILL happen to you.

This is a great game overall. I really wish it were possible to rely on slo-mo a little less, and play in a much bigger space, but that's the trade-off with any currently available large-scale VR experience. Reality simply hasn't caught up to the devs' crazy ideas yet. But once you adapt to the mechanics... yo. I'm almost glad the battery powering my wireless adapter dies after 2-something hours and introduces a mandatory break, or I'd spend days in there, slicing suckers into tiny bloody bits in glorious bullet time.

All that being said, I haven't even begun exploring what's possible with the weapon/accessory crafting & modding system built into the game. Long story short: just give these guys your money (and get the weapons DLC, too). They've done a marvelous job.

UPDATE: Now at level 400+, I *have* spent some time upgrading skills and weapons using the loot drops (and crafting). The system is extensive, allowing you to adapt to almost any play style you desire - guns in all flavours, throwables, heck: even fist-fighting is possible. My favourite remains swordplay & #AGGROBATICS. My character can now QUINTUPLE jump, hold to on walls (which sacrifices wallruns, but screw it, #SPODERMAN), and is wielding two double-bladed plasma swords with over 1400 DPS each. Those could be called "lightsabers" if Disney weren't ar$eholes about their trademarks. In short, Darth Maul can SUCK IT and I'm moving on towards the highest levels of difficulty. Still wish I had a warehouse-sized play area at my disposal though - I have to periodically check where I am in the room (again: FLOOR BOUNDS ALWAYS ON!) because I get immersed like crazy. Can't even imagine playing this on PeSasantVR... you poor, poor people have no idea what you're missing ^^

Oh, and shut up already about the protagonist being female. You were perfectly happy playing Metroid as Samus Aran, weren't you? Remember that aside from Story Mode, when playing extra missions and challenges, you can always change the character to Hiro (inspired by Hiro Protagonist from Neal Stephenson's "Snow Crash" maybe?) - and you will be doing a lot of these if you want to level quickly. Which, amazingly, happens at a very nice pace without the need for microtran$actions. AAA developers, take note!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSairento has significantly exceeded my expectations. Jumping around blasting bad guys in slow motion is awesome, and a very impressive inverse kinematics body model allows you to see your arms, legs, body which is way more immesive than just floating hands. Tons of variety and customization available, from an extensive settings menu to a variety of game modes, difficulty levels, skill trees, and loot.

Some of the items can be really game changing - I found one item that makes bullet time permanent except when moving (like SuperHot), but reduces max health by 90% making you a glass cannon. Another gives the SMG infinite ammo reserves, making the "spray and pray" tactic viable.

Despite being a "ninja" I've found the gunplay to be better than swords. I had trouble the first couple missions playing with swords - I kept trying to jump to an enemy to slash them and finding myself a couple feet out of reach or behind the enemy (facing the wrong way). Other time I would expect a dash with my sword held out to kill enemies I passed through, but it did not appear to do so. Then I figured out I could equip all sorts of other weapons from the loadout screen and never looked back.

Voice acting is unexpectedly good and the story is interesting, although not the main focus of the game. Jumping locomotion works very well, and doesn't make me motion sick at all. There are options to use teleport-only locomotion, but I think that would take a lot away from the game. There's also an option to use touchpad locomotion for the iron-stomached among us, but I didn't turn it on because I can only barely tolerate touchpad motion and the jumping is plenty effective to bounce around the map.

Graphics are servicable, but are the worst facet of the game. They're slightly worse than standard for VR games (which is already fairly low fidelity due to high performance requirements).

Overall, best VR action game I've played. I wish I had bought it sooner - it's been on my wishlist for months.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to like this game. I really wanted to. I mean, who doesn't want to play as a cyber ninja? That's fricking awesome right? And the trailers look so fun. And during the tutorial even, it's so promising learning about triple jumps and slides and whatnot. Reminded me of Titanfall in a way, how you feel like an utter badass! And for someone who's sort of experienced in VR games, the fact that this game has a lot of quality of life features that aren't really featured in VR specific games yet, such as a bunch of modes (multiplayer, arcade, campaign, daily challenges, etc), currency, visual upgrades, a bunch of content, is to be commended. The developers have done a great job in that respect.

Unfortunately, I have to give this game a negative review. The moment you start putting the pieces together, it ultimately falls apart.

First off, for a game that looks okay at best, it sure does use up a lot of power just to run at the lowest settings. Even at lowest settings, with up to date graphics cards from just a year ago, and a good processor, this game still runs at around 30 to 40 frames per second for me. Of course, this will depend on what computer you have, but for such a limiting game in terms of graphics, it confuses me as to why it is optimized pretty poorly. Not to mention random stutters all the time.

But that's fine. Let's assume your copy runs perfectly fine. Up to date, technical hiccups are a no go.

I can really see the idea the developers had for this. And it's a great idea. But this game is so dumb, I'm sorry. The guys in the trailers must have a PhD in playing games because you will never feel like that throughout the entire game. What ends up happening, at least for me personally, is you start out the level, think to yourself "alright im gonna be such a badass, do this thing, jump off of this wall, slo mo slice that mofo while in midair, and then shove a gun into the last guys throat!". In reality, what happens is you slide into battle, awkwardly stand there for the duration as dozens of guys swarm you, as you spam the slo mo button, fumbling with your pistols that are hard enough to aim with guys that take a good amount of hits to kill, run away, try again to look cool, only to repeat ad nauseum. I can really see how some people might be gods at this game, and get really good, but for 99% of people out there, that will not be the case. It'll be gameplay like this, 24/7.

That's not to mention all of the little hiccups with the game that really add up. The submachine gun has a crazy muzzle that basically makes it unusable, feeling like you just picked up a stormtrooper's gun. When you turn fast enough, you can clearly see the game outline, as it cuts off into black nothingness. I don't know if this is a specific issue with my headset (Oculus VR), but I have never had this issue with any other VR game. The wall jump mechanic seems super incosisent, where during the tutorial it took me multiple tries to do it, even though I was pulling it off correctly (inb4 "hah git gud"). I even looked up tutorials to see if I was actually doing it right. Etc. etc.

I can't really blame the developers to be honest. For those 1% of people that can pull off crazy ♥♥♥♥ like in the trailers, good for you. I wish I could have that much fun. And they have made an experience that is to be praised for what it has done, especially considering this is also a very small company if I remember correctly. But for the rest of us, this is a game that looks fantastic on paper, but will ultimately leave you disappointed in terms of the execution. I still give credit to the game developers for the amount of passion put into this, and it saddens me to say this, but put your money somewhere else, or at the very least look into the type of game you're getting into, while asking yourself "can I do this?"

Edit: As of right now, the developers have responded to my review, being very cordial. Once again, developers, you guys are awesome. I'll see if these fix the game for me, and I'll admit, I got a bit trigger happy with my initial review due to the frustrations I experienced.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOk first of all allow me to clarify that VR was NOT made for this.
Now, this game is good. Not great, but good.

It's got the most replay value (in VR gaming) in my opinion, as there is a good variety of maps along with enemies.
Rewards and enemy spawns are randomised, so you will unlikely have the same experience each play.

However, like other VR titles, the weapons feel weightless thus slightly ruining the immersion.
You can slice enemies into half just by touching them with your blade, without the slightest force.
And this is where I'm looking forward to Blade and Sorcery, but that's another story.

The new update which added backflips in mid-air just makes me feel sick. It's often triggered when not intended. And there seems to be no option to revert to wall backflips only?

There are other bugs too which I will not go over as there are quite a few.

ALTHOUGH, I like how the developers are releasing frequent updates which actually make a difference to the gameplay and performance. They also add detailed patch notes, showing exactly what has been modified.
It's nice to see that the developers care about their game, and I think that is very important. Some bigger developers like Bethesda, don't really seem to care about their VR products.
