Don't Ghost Out of this Game

Story

Karen is an amnesiac ghost who must search for her Heart Fragments in the Driftworlds, allowing her to regain her humanity and memories. Throughout the story, Karen questions her identity, since she is currently much different from her past self as shown in the flashback events. This all culminates in a nightmare segment that may or may not have made me scream in a high-pitched tone before leading into Karen realizing what makes her Karen. The ending still has plenty of unanswered mysteries to lead into the sequel, and I hope the sequel expands on things like the Elicio Church, Mika's connection to the Driftworlds, and psychic powers in general.

Gameplay

As a warning, I couldn't play this game in F4 fullscreen mode without massive frame drops, but got good performance in windowed mode. That's a shame, since I feel that action RPGs benefit the most from fullscreen. Turn off G-sync if you have it, or it'll cause performance issues in RPG Maker MV games that use a later version of Node.js.

The game has both Action RPG and Mystery Dungeon turn-based combat, though you'll likely spend most of your time in the latter, since the former is essentially a more complex preemptive strike system when it comes to normal enemies. Combined, these two systems makes the player consider their positioning before they attempt to ambush an enemy, since it's possible to lure the enemy and isolate them or accidentally start a fight in the middle of several enemies. Since most enemies have long-range and rush skills, it's very important to be mindful of positioning.

For ARPG actions, you have to press the B button to use your default action, which is Foxtrot (Dash attack) for Karen. However, some actions, such as Summonstone extra actions and Astrake's Vastel Charge, require you to hold down B to charge. Normally, doing this causes the default B action to activate first, but holding L allows you to skip the default action. Sadly, this part doesn't really get explained outside of the Nameless Tower arcade dungeon.

Grand Bosses, aka Gangers, have a mechanic where you'll be stuck in ARPG mode until you break their shield, and there are often puzzle mechanics that you need to be aware of to pull that off. This means you won't be able to do much damage to the Gangers in ARPG mode, but you benefit from passive healing when moving, so you have some room for error to figure out what to do. Once you force the Ganger into turn-based mode, you can start dealing more damage, but the Gangers will have access to stronger moves as well. I found most of the battles have enough margin for error that you don't need to spam too many powerful consumables, with the exception of the postgame superboss.

While individual boss battles can be considered balanced and moderately difficult, the true challenge comes from getting to the bosses in the first place. Every Driftworld dungeon is made of randomly generated floors with randomly generated loot. You can pick up equipment along the way and any duplicates you find will upgrade all instances of that equipment permanently. However, upper and lower body armor has durability, which goes down not only from damage, but from taking up turns, forcing the player not to waste too much time exploring. The movement penalty is kind of harsh in the long run and there are enemy abilities designed to break or remove your armor, so it's necessary to prepare multiple sets of armor.

The trap system is probably my least favorite mechanic, since it can be very difficult to spot them. When the spirit cat, Tarte, is in the party, you can get a brief glimpse of traps upon entering the floor, engaging an enemy, and killing an enemy. However, if you fail to memorize all the spots or can't fulfill the conditions to scan for traps, you could end up taking unnecessary damage that could wreck your armor durability.

Late-game, there's a mechanic where floors can randomly have a "Monster House," where several monsters will spawn and aggro on you. IMO, this adds a bit too much RNG into the game, since it's very possible for them to spawn in a way that leave the player with bad positioning. The first time I got a Monster House, they spawned in the same room I spawned in, mkaing it hard to escape.

You can earn currency known as DP by getting high scores in dungeons, picking them up off the ground, and even examining events in the real world. DP is only used for restoring levels at Warp Gates and upgrading your skill trees. You can also convert unneeded items into DP, but sadly, there is no way to use DP to acquire essential consumables, which would have made the luck-based aspects of the game more forgiving IMO.

The game advertises itself as a Roguelike where death is penalized with equipment loss, but finding that equipment again results in the equipment getting stronger, making it a Roguelite in practice. However, there's also an autosave mechanic, where you're allowed to load a dungeon autosave file exactly once. IMO, it's usually better to just load the autosave and try to find a way to escape, since there's no guarantee you'll be able to easily find that equipment again.

As you advance in the story, you gain the ability to interact with map obstacles in various ways, such as launching logs, pushing iron balls, jumping off walls, and hitting water pumps. In some breakpoint areas, you'll be able to use these mechanics to solve optional puzzles and obtain Sado Band BGM, some of which aren't in the soundtrack DLC, so I'd say they're worth doing.

Verdict

The action and turn-based mechanics blend together surprisingly well and the story is half-complete, yet very engaging. The music is also soothing, so I recommend buying the soundtrack DLC both to support the developer and have something nice to listen to. I'm someone who normally doesn't play the Roguelite genre, but I still highly recommend picking this game up.

9/10

Edit: The fullscreen issues can be fixed by turning off G-Sync if you're using an Nvidia GPU.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSurprised this game didn't get more reviews.

This game plays somewhat like a Mystery Dungeon game, but at the same time an action RPG.
First of all, when you start the game you are greeted with an extensive options menu. You are able to optimize the game's screen and sound by quite a bit, which feels really neat. Once you start in the game, it immediately brings you to it's gameplay. The gameplay consists on moving around the field, when encountering enemies, you have the options of attacking head-front or performing counter attacks when they turn yellow, which happens when they attack you and miss.

The part the got me the most is that once you initiate combat in any way, you enter turn-based mode, where you can think about your actions more before performing them. The two gameplay styles intertwine absolutely beautifully, leaving for a fast paced game with lots of fun.

The game also consists on dungeons which are randomized. Just like in Mystery Dungeon games, you will start as Level 1 in each, or most, of them. Items you collect make you stronger in the long run, but you drop half of them upon death. But worry not for losses, as there is a skill tree that'll help you come back stronger, and specifically the items you have dropped will come back in the next run but in stronger values.

This game has a lot going for it basically, and it's lots of fun for me. On top of it all, there's an Arcade mode for the truer Roguelike experience. I'd highly advise checking this game out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a roguelike in the original sense of a top-down dungeon exploring game but it operates in real-time until you engage in a battle. That speeds things up and also offers opportunities for things like going behind a creature and ambushing it to engage at an advantage. Some actions move you several spaces so fights are less static than some other games of this type

You find items and all your clothing and head equipment shows up on your character model. There are tons! They also have degrade mechanics that show up visually. Your armor degrades as you use turns so taking damage and then waiting to heal consumes your item durability. Items have skills attached that you can then use. You can swap items during fights.

As you play more, every time you find a duplicate of an item (even if you tossed the old version) it upgrades all of that type of item. You can stuff consumables into equipment to buff it. Between that feature and permanent upgrades bought with points from dungeon exploring, there is incremental progress even if you wipe and lose your items.

The menus and ui are well designed and you can accomplish things quickly and with little fuss.

The pixel art has an interesting and distinctive palette and the cutscene art is nice.

Overall, its fun and less fussy than most roguelikes.\t\t\t\t\t
