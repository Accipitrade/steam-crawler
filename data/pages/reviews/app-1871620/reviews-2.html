\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCovid Simulator is visceral. It is detached. In as much as it is possible for art to reflect reality, Covid Simulator has reflected the darkest nature of modern human impulse.

Covid Simulator is also the most realistic simulator ever created. Sheer genius in its simplicity: it reduces human beings experiencing the office work environment during the pandemic of 2020-onward to their most fundamental productive units. Paradoxically, the inhabitants of the simulation are gifted with a sort of perverse free will, where their decisions have potentially catastrophic consequences to the larger collective, and potentially to themselves. The decisions are made nonetheless, perhaps or at least seemingly at random, and what unfolds is a vision of collapse from every angle: total and complete collapse.

As in life, it is temporarily possible to stave off the collapse through adequate preparation, but the impulse to ignore some apparently mundane element which may drive spread under certain conditions for the sake of maximizing profit is too great to resist. Slowly the workers are decimated, and then dessicated, as the workforce succumbs to the disease at first in dribs and drabs and then in torrents.

Masks help, but people take them off to eat. Vaccination helps, particularly against Delta, but less so against Omicron. You can institute quarantines or work in shifts, but your efforts will only limit the spread so much. And the most important thing is your score (profit) and you have to maximize that.

A sense of eerie foreboding pervades the music, and it instills appropriate dread and vexation as you wield the lives of the inhabitants as tools of your own personal aggrandizement. At first I found myself speeding through the days, unable to bear witness to the folly I was driving. That is, once I realized I could increase the speed. Prior to that, the inhabitants bumbled along placidly, almost blissful, taking off their masks (safely) to eat lunch, chatting about their city name and sports team, or blithely generating profit at their workstations. I tended to slow down a bit at night and reflect on the splendor I had granted these beings, through the purpose I provided them.

Eventually things began to get ugly. And no matter what I did, I could not get the level of infection to a place where I could manage it. Even with the entire office pro-vax, masking, and working in shifts, there was no way to eliminate the risk of exposure. Even worse, my profit was tanking. Even when a level of herd immunity was achieved, with most of the infected remaining asymptomatic, there was no way to ensure an increase in profit. While the infection of the workforce was certain and therefore a known quantity, the uncertainty around profitability left me feeling empty and desperate for a way to turn the red number green. In truth, playing in a way where that was the only important thing to me was the most difficult road to travel, yet I strode on into the face of my workers' destruction.\t\t\t\t\t
