\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVisually pleasing aesthetic, charming sounds etc but gameplay leaves a lot to be desired when comparing it to nigh on every platformer in existence. Ignoring the blind loyalty is a key thing to do as you will just get "i liked it therefore ♥♥♥♥ you" which is fine, but then you get "You just gotta get into the mindset that it's NOT a race, you can combo costumes with PATIENCE" and so on, which is the problem really. 'Patience'. Takes a lot to want to completionist this game and not for difficulty, everything is just so slow and tiring and most costumes shouldn't exist either because they get outclassed a stage later or are just 1 of a few that do the same crap. Most other platformers will let you explore at your own pace and let you zoom through if you so happen to want to thanks to actually fun mechanics. Balan has like 5 costumes you will never put down if you are like me for sequence breaking, general fun times exploring, but the rest feel like a reason to advertise 80+ costrumes. genuinely using the 'mobility' costumes is nice, still suffers some clunk of changing costumes, losing them one death etc, but they do make the game that bit more fun because of how free you suddenly become in comparison to the other horrid outfits. The game is very inoffensive, but if you love to run free around a 3d environment without a healthy dose of clunk this isnt for you. HOWEVER, if you want a nice, chilled out adventure with nice music, visuals and a steady paced casual platformer this game will do you no harm. however, it will harm your wallet. for 50% it's a good grab, but if you paid £60 like me hoping it was good and wanting to support people like naoto ohshima and not the other weirdo then you'll have a nice dollop of dissapointment. If steam allowed a vote of "Hmm... EEehhhhhhhhh *palm down hand wiggle*" i would do that. But as it stands it's GOOD if you want a slow casual charming experience. TERRIBLE if you've ever played a platformer that knows what it actually wants to be/do and rewards you for messing with its controls.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAlmost 100%ed the game, figured that's worth mentioning at the start.
Balan Wonderworld feels like if a lost game from the late 90s - early 2000s was found from Yuji Naka's PC, upgraded to HD and shipped out, and honestly i wouldn't have it any other way as a fan of those early 3D titles.

For a platformer of it's caliber, Balan Wonderworld has a ton of freedom, whether it's the difficulty of the game reacting to how you play, the robust catalog of over 80 costumes to choose from or the level design that's been deliberately built to have puzzles that can be solved in more ways than one and more.

The story is a joy to experience and i appreciate that a good bit of it was kept to a novel to not make the main game too exposition-heavy (Very much recommending the novel, btw. It's awesome.) in order to keep the game's feet more on the ground. The story and lack of voice acting, at least one that's from this world screams NiGHTS Into Dreams and while it's only in those two areas, the return of such way of storytelling is very welcome at a time such as this.

The cutscenes of the game are more than enough to convey the torment of the game's characters, not even counting the amazing visuals. Even the levels incorporate some of the themes the game presents, from heavily walled-off farms to art exhibits in the verge of breaking down and empty towers made of stone and glass.

Speaking of levels, the level design in Balan is great. At the first glance, much like in a number of games, the levels may seem extremely linear and you would be right....
...if you were just getting to the end of the level. The game is riddled with all sorts of collectibles, some most definitely in places you wouldn't look at at your first playthrough. While you're absolutely going to come across an area you can only enter with a costume you haven't unlocked yet on multiple occasions, the element of backtracking at the player's will to collect any remaining statues with new costumes are really fun. I can count the times when i found Balan Statues in places i couldn't even imagine there could be one. At one point, i found myself scaling the walls of a cave for a statue, jumping on small platforms over a bottomless pit instead of running through the level itself. The lack of invisible walls is very apparent. Most places in the game can be walked on.

One more thing now that i was talking about levels, the graphics. They're pretty cool and manage to help the atmospheric level design reflect the game's characters even better. If i ever felt like i was bored running from place to place i could just look around myself and be entertained just by the beautiful environments alone.

The amount of costumes in Balan is impressive. I'll admit that some of the costumes that exist only to activate a certain thing and nothing else and that does kind of suck, the game makes it up with a variety of different costume types ranging from mobility to combat, and even some gimmicky ones that may completely change the way you play. These make up most of the outfits in the game.

Do you want to go hard and ram straight into your enemy? Consider it done.
Do you want to directly attack your enemy by punching them in the face or do you want to use a boomerang to catch enemies with shields off-guard by striking from the side in exchange for slower throwing speed? Your choice!
Do you want to launch yourself at enemies after jumping a la blue rodent? No problem.
Need to stop time or become invisible in order to catch your breath mid-battle and plan out what to do next? You've got it!
Wishing for a way to clear a whole bunch of enemies with a single big attack? It's yours my friend.

The strength/usefulness of a costume is based on how much effort you put into reaching said costume. Some costumes may be spread out across a level to help you make it through, but those aren't quite as good as some hiding around.
In my journey to 100%, i had to change my approach to the game multiple times through my costume loadout and i had a lot of fun doing it. By the end i had even circled back to the old common costumes since i realized that i really needed them and couldn't rely on postgame costumes.

The gameplay in Balan is pretty friggin' cool. Please put down your pitchforks.
Costume management in Balan is key. If you think a costume you already have is going to be worth having later in the game, pick it up. You'll need it. Maybe you'd wanna grind on it for a while, you can do that.
Out of the multitude of costumes the game presents the player with, you can only carry three at a time. This introduces an element of strategization into the game, with the player having to figure out: "What costume am i going to need for what's coming up? Do i need a specific costume to get through this part? What am i going to counter this level's enemies with?" to minimize the amount of visits you'll take to the changing room, found from checkpoints. The changing room is the place where all costumes other than the three you're possessing are placed into. Visits to the changing room are quite quick thanks to how everything works and how often the player comes across checkpoints.
The jump arc is very reliable and even more so with the more floatier outfits that the game has.
I don't think i could honestly enjoy Balan without the Day 1 patch that increased the movement speed among other things. It's just right.
Enemy encounters are awesome, especially on Hard Mode, which i'm going to talk about later.

This is the part where i want to talk about the "bad" stuff.

I love the one-button system that Balan introduces. While the changing time between costumes could be faster, it still emphasizes the strategization system that i mentioned earlier. My favorite combo is to always bring one mobility costume, one combat costume and fit whichever costume i think is most appropriate at the moment into the third slot, it's great.

The game isn't perfect. Everyone, even the enjoyers such as myself know that. However a lot of the things i found as negatives don't persist thruought the game and are rather level-specific.
However, there are two things i can't excuse. These two things do have a workaround for them, but i shouldn't have to go out of my way to do that workaround every time.

First off, Balan's Bouts. These quicktime-events require the player to do a perfect run or they'll have to beat the boss of that chapter in order to try again. This can extend to the completing of a stage. To truely complete a stage, the player has to touch the goal, but after touching it once, they can't touch it again without having to beat the boss again.
Second, Hard Mode. A single hit reverts you back to normal mode and you´have to earn the mode again which takes a while. For a true 100% of the game, every level in the game has to be fully completed with Hard Mode on.

The workaround? Restart your game before exiting the level. The game won't autosave if you don't leave the stage and allows you to try again. It's a solution but it shouldn't be one.

Now, while it may seem that i hate it, Hard Mode is BY FAR my favourite thing about Balan. Activated by fighting enemies for long enough without getting hit, the stakes can't get much higher than that, with much harder enemies and mid-ambush minibosses that while do go down in one hit are very dangerous. Strategizing is much more important than before, and one awesome inclusion to it is that you won't lose Hard Mode if you fall off a stage.

After 2020 Balan succeeded in bringing my hopes for the future back and encouraged me to better myself. Despite the obvious problems in game development that come with a pandemic, Yuji Naka still managed to create a beautiful swansong that i'll definitely be remembering for many years to come.

If you're a fan of 3D-platformers from around the start of the century, i recommend Balan to you. Otherwise wait for a sale. The 100% takes a good bit of time.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhoo boy, this game is... something, to say the least. As much as I like the world within it, the gameplay isn't really spectacular or something to write home about.

It's not that I like the gameplay (I think it's mediocre, but passable if you have nothing else to do, at best), but it's rather that I like the rest of it as a whole; the characters, morals, themes it tackles, soundtrack, and novelization. The novelization, despite having some questionable translations at some parts, is actually a surprisingly good read and gives a lot more depth to every character who's inside or entered the BWW theatre, so much so that I'd recommend reading it over playing the game (and it's considerably cheaper as well).

The game and its dreadful execution, however, is where things begin to fall apart and the seams of this supposed wonderworld really start to show; it's pretty slow-paced, vapid and boring, you have to do tedious minigames and bouts (which you can't even restart if you get them wrong, lest you restart the game from the title screen or beat the world's boss) if you want to finish the game 100% (or even finish the game at all since you're going to need statues to beat the game, and you get some of these statues by perfectly finishing these minigames), and even the story and characters in it are lacking compared to the novel, in which the game gives little-to-no explanation to either (especially Leo and Emma, who are essentially just blank slates for the player - the former is egregious since in his intro cutscene, he seemingly awkwardly leaves after some people try to get his attention, never actually clearly stating his reasoning inside of itself; the official site explained that Leo had an argument with a former friend a long time ago and prefers to be alone since), and costumes are often very samey (which is to be expected if you have around 80 of them).

Overall, if you really want to experience Balan Wonderworld for yourself, I'd recommend purchasing the novelization and/or soundtrack and not the game itself. It'll likely be easier for you (and your wallet) rather than playing something that most people consider cumbersome.\t\t\t\t\t
