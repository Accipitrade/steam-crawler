It's like having an open world racing game, but you have cars that either goes forwards or backwards, and you have to exit, find and enter a particular vehicle each time you want to do any of those actions. Sounds pretty stupid, right? Now imagine a 3D platform game with this same concept: you have costumes for either jumping or either attacking enemies, and of course there are a few which can do both but when you get hit once, you lose it, so you better stack up on those, and play over and over that particular stage where you can earn that costume again. This is the prime example of artificial grindfest, and implementing the worst gimmicks. 90% of the game should have been scrapped and cut out for being dumb and unnecessary, instead (to have content in the game) they just made every idea they came out with, and this incoherent mess came out. A really buggy game with a truly dump concept riddled with bad camera controls, boring music, awful art design, disgusting colors, garbage quick time events and annoying boss fights. I had no joy playing this game at all, and I could not recommend it under any circumstances to any person whatsoever. Avoid it and don't trust gamers with Stockholm syndrome, trying to somewhat redeem this game as an "artsy game people just don't get". It is not. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tBalan Wonderworld is a 3D adventure platformer that offers no freedom to its players. In a genre that is all about losing yourself in vast worlds, looking for collectibles and using the tools at your disposals to overcome challenges, this game offers tiny areas connected by narrow passages, collectibles that don't feel like they matter and such a restrictive amount of tools that player skill becomes irrelevant. Movement is slow and jumping feels heavy and awkward.

What you can do entirely depends on whether you have the right costume or not to clear the challenge ahead: the costumes only do one thing (some even taking away your ability to JUMP IN A PLATFORMER) and they are not fun to use. It feels like playing Super Mario Odyssey, except you're only able to use one ability at a time. You can't combine the costumes to pull off tricks or freely explore your surroundings. The whole game is about bringing the right power to the right location. It's fitting the square in the square hole in a genre that is supposed to be all about freedom, wonder and exploration.

And that's without mentioning some of the worst offenders, namely the atrocious QTE sequences with Balan or the random dancing NPCs that vanish as soon as you approach them in every level.

It's a shame, because some of the designs for the bosses and characters look pretty fun and the cutscenes, especially the opening cutscene, are beautifully animated. The artists did a good job on this. It's the level design, core mechanics and general concept for the game that just don't deliver in any way, shape or form.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBalan Wonderword is an aggressively bad game with a lot of "content".

Gameplay takes place across 12 Chapters each with two acts and a boss act along with an unlockable third act after you beat the game. Each chapter has its own specific costumes out of 81(!) total. They don't do 81 different things, generally the early ones are explicitly worse versions of later ones, with a few being duplicates. A lot of abilities are also chapter specific, but those can usually be circumvented with better later costumes that make platforming much easier. Switching costumes takes a long time and can only be done while on the ground and while your character isn't doing their ability. So, despite having up to 3 equipped, this game isn't about chaining abilities or entering any sort of fun flow state. Fun is in very short supply.

Going through the acts you'll have combat, when you enter trigger volume that causes enemies to spawn. Wait long enough and enter that zone again and the enemies will spawn again. The game has its own dynamic-esque difficulty where if you beat enemies without taking a hit consecutively, you'll go into hard mode where red enemies spawn that are faster and more aggressive. This really highlights the weaknesses of the combat suits. They seem to lock on when they feel like it and if you miss, you've got to wait for whatever attack to disappear before you get another shot. Balan Wonderworld is a one-button game, so often you can't jump if you've picked a suit that shoots. The hard mode will turn the mini-bosses into tougher versions with a chance to spawn Lanced versions of them that are linked to achievements.

As you go through each level you'll collect golden (or rainbow) statues of Balan that are what gate the unlock of further chapters. There's 228 gold ones. 48 of these are from succeeding at a mini-game called Balan's Bout. When you collect a golden hat, you'll be transported to a nondescript world in which Balan flies around and punches rocks or Lance, the villain of the game. You'll see a short animation setting up the threat, then Balan will be framed on screen with a transparency of his pose sliding into him, when his model and the image are perfectly lined up you hit the button to succeed. They start out with 4 parts but end up going up to 6 by the end of the game, so it's about 250 QTES total, but the game has maybe... 20-30 different events. So you'll end up seeing Balan punch the same rock a lot of times. Miss one, and you have to fight the chapter's boss to reset the level's pick ups and try again. This is the worst part of the game, made worse by the sheer number of times you have to do it.

Bosses have three statues as well for different fighting styles. This part is a bit clever, basically there's multiple ways of doing a hit on a boss, find all 3 and you get all 3 statues. You don't have to do it all at once, but you often can. Sometimes you'll need a suit that isn't provided in the boss fight to do it. A lot of the bosses have a "hurry up and wait" design, though, that just has you sitting and waiting during limited or often no threat at all as you wait for your chance to do damage. Beat the boss and you get a dance sequence as a reward, beat all 12 and the final boss and you get the ulitmate dance sequence and get to stop playing.

There's a small hubworld, just basically a circle with spokes off of it for each of the 12 chapters. They're out of order which bugged me to no end trying to find specific ones to return to. From the hub you can enter any act or fight any boss. There's also small creatures called Tims you collect, similar to chaos from Sonic Adventure. You feed the tims gems from the world, with each colour modifying them and letting them do different tasks in the world. If you feed them enough of the 3 alternate gems you can eventually unlock a King Tim with a crown who can do all the tasks of all the different colours, and also lets you unlock the secret costume. Oh also, the tims interact with a rube goldberg device called the tower of tims, incrementing a counter with each cycle. The counter maxes out at 24000, completing the hub world challenge.

I was really interested in seeing how this game would play out when the demo dropped. It seems to have no shortage of funding as the art, model work, level art, music, and cg is all decent quality. But the game design is quite poor and unfun. I don't even think the one-button aspect had to be as stifling as they made it in the final design. It's a shame.\t\t\t\t\t
