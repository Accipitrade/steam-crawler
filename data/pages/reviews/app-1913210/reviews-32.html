\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't usually write reviews, but I'm immensely disappointed with this.

I played through a couple of matches, and found the controls to be clunky. The game isn't as responsive as I'd expect in some situations. The gameplay is slow. I'm a huge fan of the WCW/NWO Revenge style of games from back in the day, but this ain't it. These are things that can be patched.

As of right now though, the game is in an unfinished state. The rumors that came out that the EVP working on this and THQ not getting along seem to have been true. And THQ's developers stating that the game wasn't finished. It seems like AEW rushed the game out, and said, "We'll patch it later." Which is disappointing.

It *is* cool that the entire AEW music library is in there. Big up.

The severe lack of content in character creation (there was more variety in Legends of Wrestling), the lack of a *cage* match, the fact that ladder matches are only 1 on 1 affairs, the story mode is very linear. I'm just very whelmed by this game at the moment.

Maybe I'll come revisit it in a year or so when it's received patches and more content, but right now I can't recommend this. If you want to support AEW, cool. But don't simp for them when this product is clearly subpar - *especially* given the anti-consumer practices they've tacked onto the game with the EGS / Anti-Cheat, etc. The game isn't even open for modders at the moment, something that would have made this worth a purchase because the community would have picked up the slack where the developers left off.

Come back in a year or so. It might be finished then.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat fun! The controls and systems are tutorialised well, and are easy enough for a wrestling game newbie like me to pick up, but complex enough to feel tactical and satisfying. Career mode is great fun, and has a charming sense of humour. Being able to play as Yuka Sakazaki and beat a defenseless Jon Moxley almost to death in Training mode is almost worth the price of admission alone! There's no getting around the fact that the graphics are a mixed bag; quite a few character models are almost photorealistic, many more are somewhat uncanny, and the background scenery in some of the career mode locations is extremely basic. Personally I don't mind this, as it somehow feels right for the casual, just-for-fun mood the game is going for, and gives the game a scrappy, almost DIY feel that matches AEW as a company. The delays the game experienced are definitely evident, as it almost feels like an AEW game from mid-2022 in some ways (trios and International titles not included, old Women's World Title design, William Regal prominently featured, many currently-popular roster members not included yet, etc), but they've already explained that this game will have a 'games as a service' style ongoing trickle of updates and new content, so I'm sure all the obvious stuff people want to see added will arrive eventually. In terms of potential future updates, I would love to eventually see the inclusion of wrestlers from other companies AEW has relationships with; ROH is obvious, but I would personally pay almost any amount of money for DLC of the NJPW, DDT, or TJPW rosters! But even in its slightly bare, day one form, the game is still a lot of fun.

TL;DR - If you go in with sky-high expectations you might be disappointed, but if you're just looking for an easy-breezy, fun first wrestling game featuring AEW talent, this game is a delight!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHere is my honest oppinion on the game. First of all, let's start with the positives:
- The gameplay is really amazing! It feels super smooth, the hits and kicks connect really great! Sometimes even too good haha. anyway, the gameplay is really amazing and super smooth and just feels great to play.

- The controls are as advertised, easy to learn but hard to master, i figured out the controls really quickly, but oh boy are they difficult to master, takes like 5-6 matches until you can do some real damage to the opponent. IMO, this is not a bad thing, its a good thing, because you keep on replaying the game until you master the controls.

- The match types are limited, but the ones we got are really fun! Exploding barbed wire dm is really fun to play, the best moment is when you have a finisher (like Malakay Black) where after he hits it, the opponent just keeps on moving (i had a match against Jericho, and after hitting Malakay's finisher, Jericho walked stunned into the barbed wire causing an explosion, really great physics!)

- Hitting a move on your opponent just connects! The hit detection is simply amazingly done!

- Road to elite is also very fun to play and has great replay value, you encounter many different scenarios, make your own decisions which affect the playthrough.

- The game is fast paced, not so much simulation based, but this in my oppinion is much better! There is action constantly, you always need to think on what you should do next, you wont be just there standing arround and waiting for your opponent to recover, they get up in a couple of seconds, so no break for you while playing a match, which i really love!

- No stamina. Meaning you can perform every move one after another and run for kilometers without stopping, this is really great because it doesnt break up the action (you dont need to wait for a meter to fill up in order to perform the next move or to run)

- The entrance interactions are a great detail, switching camera and setting off pyro on your own, really great touch by the developers.

- You can have up to 5 finishers and signatures

- Every wrestler feels unique! You will feel a difference in gameplay, if you play as a high flyer, your character will move super fast, fly fast and far and perform actions non stop, but if you play as a heavyweight, the difference is immediately noticable! If you play, for example, as Luchasaurus, he will start running a bit later and a bit slower, also a heavyweight will also perform attacks slower and he will not jump over a grounded opponent, he will step over him.

- A LOT of unique and fun weapons!


Now onto the negatives:

- I don't really like the button mashing for pinfall and submission, it would be nice if there is a meter that would display how much you have to mash the button in order to escape or it would be nice if we could choose a different way of kicking out.

- Limited creation suite items for create a wrestler/arena, i hope they will expand the creation suite items as updates get released.

- In Road To Elite it would be nice that workouts would be a minigame (Like in GTA San Andreas)

- No community creation suite (i hope they will add this in the future)

- No steel cage match

- The weapons break after 2 uses. The weapons should last a lot longer, as for the tables, they should make them more stable and not collapse after just touching them with 1 body hair

- improve the referee AI, the referees take too long to start counting and they count too slow


Overall the game is Super Fun to play and i can only recommend it!
The people that "hate" on this game are just disappointed about some limitations of the game, but the developers said they will patch in more things as time progresses, so we will see more ccontent as time goes on!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDon't come into this expecting it to be a 2k WWE style game, it's not.
It's rough around the edges for sure, but there are no game breaking bugs that would limit your play and there's a solid core here that's good fun button smashing wrasslin.
It likely won't ever have a CAW like suite like the 2k games, but that's fine by me, I'm not a tinker, I just smash things and have fun.
I'll need it to flesh out over time in terms of match types and roster and to smooth out some of the basic bugs going on at the moment.
Worth a buy if you're a wrestling fan and are looking for a less simulationist style of play.
But wait if you're on the fence to see how additional content spins out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBack in the mid-2000s, Yukes' wrestling games became annualised with the release of 'Smackdown vs Raw 2006'. This began a long period where Yukes would crank out a new WWE game every year (under the banner of Smackdown vs Raw, WWE or WWE 2K) and they'd all be pretty much the same, with the same bugs, gameplay issues, dodgy animations and so forth.

That's why I was so excited about AEW Fight Forever, because for the first time in nearly 20 years Yukes finally had an opportunity to take the time to craft the best wrestling game they possibly could. They could finally take their wrestling game formula and give it the 20 years worth of polish and improvement that they weren't able to do before... or at the very least, give us a nice polished-up version of Smackdown: Here Comes The Pain (PS2).

At least, that was the theory.

The first thing you notice about this game is that, even though it looks nice and sharp on a 4K monitor, the actual character models look like something from the PS2 era. This is especially noticeable with the facial expressions on the character select screen, which range from 'passable' to 'nightmare fuel'.

Once you've gotten used to the facial expressions, you may notice that the roster is woefully incomplete (even if you take DLC and unlockable characters into account). Major stables like House Of Black and Blackpool Combat Club are woefully under-represented, and I noticed a lot of my personal faves were missing too (including ones that were with the company from the start). Notable omissions IMO include Christopher Daniels, Claudio Castagnoli, Evil Uno, Stu Grayson, Private Party, Jake Hager, Kip Sabian, both Santana AND Ortiz, Shawn Spears, Swerve Strickland, Austin Gunn, Penelope Ford and Toni Storm. So... yeah.

But none of that matters if the game plays well, right? Well, let's get into that now.

Unless you've been living on Mars for 3 years, you'll know that the main objective of this game was to replicate the gameplay of WWF No Mercy (a classic N64 wrestling game for those who don't know). But for some reason, this game also insisted on copying some of the worst parts of that game, things that only existed because of technical limitations. Entrances consist of a 5 second pose on the stage, so if one of your favourites has a really cool entrance or song (such as Hikaru Shida, Kenny Omega or Adam Cole) then you're fresh out of luck because you only get to see/hear a tiny sliver of it. You also don't get to hear Justin Roberts' amazing introductions during the entrances, there's no commentary during matches, and matches are limited to 4 wrestlers at a time which not only makes the Casino Battle Royal less fun but also prevents you from having 3 on 3 tag matches, even though AEW literally has a title belt for Tag Trios.

The game itself is an odd hybrid that tries to merge the basic gameplay mechanics of No Mercy with the look and feel of a Smackdown or Smackdown vs Raw game. And just like the old SDvR games, a lot of the animations and mechanics are disjointed and awkward (which was, ironically, never an issue in No Mercy even though it is the older game). Some of the moves are difficult to connect with (such as Hikaru Shida's finisher which I missed with twice before finally landing one), hopping over a prone opponent is laggy and clunky and you never end up where you were aiming (again, not an issue in No Mercy), and while the strikes are pretty responsive there seems to be massive input lag when trying to grapple (especially if you try to do a light grapple straight after a strike).

Ah yes, the famed Grapple System. An archaic relic of the past that demands that you do a collar-and-elbow tie-up before executing most of the moves in the game, even the ones that are NEVER preceded by a grapple IRL. It ensures that not only does most of the game not look or feel anything like real wrestling, but that all of the characters play pretty much the same since a high-flyer like Penta is just as reliant on standing grapples as a big guy like Miro is.

In the past, some wrestling games have tried to do away with this system. We saw that TNA game which, for all it's other flaws, at least showed that it was possible to do standing grapple moves without standing grapple animations in a videogame. We also saw WWE All-Stars introduce a system where technical characters could chain 4 or 5 grapple moves together (in a way that almost resembled actual chain wrestling) and high-flyers had a style of play that felt more natural. Well you can forget about all that, because the 'classic' grapple system is back with a vengeance whether you like it or not. And even if you DO still like the grapple system, this incarnation of it feels slow, clunky and un-intuitive compared to a game like, say, Def Jam: Fight For NY (which actually WAS made by the studio that made No Mercy and felt extremely polished too).

As you play longer you'll see other issues pop up too. When I tried to pause the game to see Kenny Omega's move list (because I was playing as him) it instead gave me Adam Cole's move list, which is extremely unhelpful when trying to figure out what they've picked as his 'signature moves' and how to position yourself to execute them. The camera likes to zoom in during big moves to make them look more impressive, but sometimes it zooms in so much that you miss part of the move and lose your bearings in the process (which matters if you want to follow up with something that requires specific positioning). The soundtrack seems to consist of about 5 songs that play on an endless loop, including during matches, so within an hour you'll start getting bored of them. And yes, the 'mini-games' suck which probably explains why some reviewers don't even bother mentioning them (and the one I actually wanted to play- the Home Run Derby one- is not unlocked from the start for some reason).

I understand that most people will love this game, because all they want is a wrestling game that plays like an upscaled version of No Mercy or Here Comes The Pain, and that's fine. But even if that's all I wanted from a wrestling game, I still feel like this one is too slow, too laggy, too clunky and too janky to be consistently fun (not to mention the poor presentation and small roster), especially for a game that was being promoted as an arcade experience rather than a true sim. I already played an upscaled version of No Mercy's gameplay with DJ: FFNY and I got an arcade wrestling experience with WWE All-Stars, and this game doesn't feel like a step up from either of those. After an hour of gameplay I felt like I'd seen pretty much everything that the game had to offer, and I had no desire to jump back into another match.

If you're a fan of Yukes' PS2 wrestling games or AKI's N64 games and this is somehow the game you've been waiting for, then I'm happy for you. Play it and love it and get years of joy out of it. But for me, this feels like an average, unpolished attempt to recall an era of wrestling games that I was ready to move on from 20 years ago. I will continue to wait patiently for my dream wrestling game, one that feels like a true evolution of the genre while also being a polished and feature-complete product at launch... and if that game ever arrives, I'm pretty damn sure it won't be developed by Yukes.

"Elite"? More like "Obsoleeeeeete!"\t\t\t\t\t
