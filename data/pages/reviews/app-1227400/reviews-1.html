It does suffer from the same problems as those books in that you can end up choosing the "wrong" path in a way that suggests you should restart the whole thing just to see how it could otherwise go... \r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you’re a fan of Choose Your Own Adventure books, there’s a good chance that you’ll enjoy interactive visual adventure novels like Over the Alps. The last time I played Over the Alps was almost a year ago in July 2021, when I finished two of the three planned standalone installments of the game: “A Case of the Chateau Vercoli” (released in September 2019) and “King of the Mountain” (June 2020). At the time of writing, the official website for Over the Alps has been shut down and there seems to be no news of the final chapter’s release date.

Having sat on this review for months now, I’ve decided to finally publish it, assuming that the third story -- “The Devil’s Quill” -- will not come out at all. Even without the last installment, Over the Alps is a fun adventure game. Set in pre-World War II Europe, the game tells of spies who navigate the treacherous and deceitful world of espionage. Each installment stars a different secret agent, and the stories are told through the agents’ postcard communication with their mysterious handler, Aubrey. Aubrey was intended to be the starring character of the third chapter.

The first installment was written by Jon Ingold, who is the game writer for Inkle’s 80 Days, Heaven’s Vault, Pendragon, and Overboard!. This elaborately written chapter took me just over two hours to complete. Harry Tuffs, known for his work on Sunless Skies and Fallen London, wrote the second installment. I completed this chapter in about an hour.

Ingold and Tuffs did a great job in telling engaging spy stories that, while written separately, feel connected and similar in style. I was compelled to replay them not only to collect achievements, but to see how different decisions would affect story development and the outcome. It was too bad that at the time of playing, a few achievements were bugged and couldn’t be obtained. Thankfully, Ingold and Tuffs’ charming writing, as well as the colourful, picturesque and calming minimalist art style made those moments less frustrating.

In Over the Alps, players make decisions by selecting stamps, the colour and symbol of each represent a specific type of response. Absence of a tutorial or hover labels meant learning this game mechanic through trial and error. It was only later that I learned that the more often a type of stamp is chosen or used, the more effective it becomes in solving in-game complications. Just when I thought I got the hang of it, the second installment introduces new stamp designs. How annoying!

It’s a shame that Over the Alps may not get the ending that I’ve been looking forward to. However, the two existing playable standalone installments are well done, offer complete enough of a narrative experience, and provide a few hours of light entertainment to those who enjoy visual novels, adventure games, and/or Choose Your Own Adventure books. Over the Alps is a game that you can easily enjoy and finish in an afternoon or evening.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA wonderful text based experience where all you do is choose dialogue options and in-turn progress the story. The stories are neat and entertaining.
I love the fact that your choices don't have obvious outcomes. the results of your actions can only be somewhat half-expected and it's good because it keeps your interest up and doesn't make it boring. A kind of a choice and consequence system that involves chance too in a good way.

The art style is definitely worth mentioning. Not only is it strikingly beautiful, but also it adds to the immersion by using the 40-s/50-s postcard design style in a way that it corresponds with the period in history that the action takes place in. Plus it's intuitively logical for a game about communication and information exchange via text to use postcard/mail elements – really lets you feel like you're communicating with the game characters.
