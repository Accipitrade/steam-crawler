\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm not really sure if I can recommend that game or not. It's certainly a small piece of art, has a lovely art style and depicts a story you probably have either never heard about before or you know, because you come from that region in the world and will be familiar with. In the latter case it's probably not worth a buy, unless you REALLY dig the audiovisual presentation, because, sorry, guys, but there's just NOTHING else here. Where is the gameplay? The game has you walking right. For the entire time (which is not even 1 1/2 hours). You can't run. You can't jump. "Later" on there are some short segments where you get to "shoot" a bow and fly on an eagle, but that's about it. The rest of the game is a walking 2D walking simulator with the occasional environmental "puzzle" for which you have to use your ability to change between man and mooseman vision or whatever they want to call it.

Look, it's a short game with almost no gameplay. It's also not very expensive, especially when on sale, but buy this only if you're interested in learning more about...whatever I just learned about. I'll probably forget it again very soon. Some stuff about a local creation myth. Still a thumbs up for being different with interesting visuals I guess. Funny thing is that I think I could still refund the game after not even playing it for 2 hours...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: It's a (2 hour long give or take) narrative game conveyed as much by imagery and sound as it is by text and narration. If you're the sort of person who uses the term "walking-simulator", you won't enjoy this. As someone who enjoys the odd interactive experience, I would still wait for a sale and not set my hopes too high. You may love it, you may not. Main points in bold.

I respect that The Mooseman is covering new ground subject-wise here and there is some fascination to be had with the otherworldly setting of these ancient people and their deities, but that isn't to say it's delivered perfectly.

For the vast majority of the game you are walking, left and right, which rather hampers many of it's attempts at puzzles. Without further means to work with, these often seem over-simplified, and in a two hour experience where you're mostly walking right (there is an auto-walk) this makes them seem like busy-work to lengthen the experience. Unfortunately, I didn't enjoy any of the puzzles, it has to be said. To bring this into focus, a good deal of the game's collectables are found by walking left at the start of each area (minor spoiler).

Speaking of collectables, there are too many, and along with the extra text you get every time you hit a checkpoint, I felt they detracted from an otherwise reasonably immersive experience. What feels like every 30 seconds, you're opening a menu to read something, which thankfully isn't a bulky text block but regardless pulls you out of the game constantly. The collectables don't even have anything interesting to say and have all been researched from a single source, which got on my nerves for no good reason. The checkpoints do fill out the story but often this information is reiterated at the end of each section in a much more pleasing manner; a spoken word piece in (presumably) their native tongue. My advise would be to ignore these menus and extras, if only until you reach one of these spoken-word parts. Do your reading in one go, every now and again, if at all.

The art-style is intriguing. I imagine it was attempting to capture the simple motion and feel of the cave-paintings and many treasures of these early tribes which the collectables detail, and it is not without it's moments of beauty. However, for me these moments were fleeting. Mostly this style reminded me of wax-crayons or most of all, the spray can from the old MS Paint, and as glorious and legendary as that spray can is, it doesn't quite conjure the imagery I think they were hoping for. For the most part, it feels somewhat cheap.

Despite all of this, I can't help but feel it was a worthwhile experience. The price, while low, may still be too much for some. The gameplay was less than scintillating. The puzzles were simplistic. But where The Mooseman shines is in atmosphere, it has flashes of brilliance in this regard, the sound design and spoken-word segments are wonderful. We get a glipse into a world we would never ordinarily have seen and learn of a culture most of us likely never knew existed, and for that, it's priceless. For all of my gripes, I'm still clicking this here "Yes" button, so take from that what you will.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t The Mooseman is a beautiful walk through the myths of the Perm Chud tribes, of whom I knew nothing before playing this game.
You play as Mr. The Mooseman, one of seven The Moosemen Brothers, and a shamanistic figure who can (a few minutes into the game) see freely into the spirit worlds. You are tasked to go through three worlds, discovering the ways your spirit vision, and later another ability, can help you solve puzzles and move forward. There are some skill-based challenges, but they're very gentle, and if you just want to see the way everything plays out the puzzles you *need* to solve to get to the end are pretty simple. Things get a little more complicated if you want all the collectibles, with more exploration and observation of subtle environmental cues needed.
The art is wonderful throughout, with a simple but scratchy, handmade feel. The music is gorgeous as well, and has the very welcome habit of breaking out into choral arrangements of folk songs. But to me the real star are the myths themselves, which are not just great but beautifully told and extremely well integrated with the game. One recommendation, whenever you walk by any totems (which function as checkpoints), make sure you read the lore they unlock; these lore bits add a lot to the experience, and are usually tied to whatever you are doing at the moment.
It's a short ride - longer, if you want to find all the collectibles, but as with many of these type of games its short duration works well; there's a lot of variety here, but nothing feels like padding. It does an amazing job of making you feel immersed in a folk tale; if you enjoy reading about mythology it's a no-brainer.\t\t\t\t\t
