\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a negative review because I did not enjoy The Mooseman.
That is not to say that Mooseman is a bad game.
I consider my tastes to be pretty average and so in my opinion, The Mooseman is not fit for a general audience.

Without waxing lyrical, here's why I did not like the game:

a) It's gameplay did not capture my interest. For the most part it can be described "as walking slowly to the right". There was a few puzzles in there but they of a "notice a thing" or "remember and reproduce" sort rather than "figure it out" sort. Two or three action sequences kept the same unhurried pace and controls in them felt floaty. They mixed up things a bit, but ultimately failed to thrill me.

b) The story did not grab me either. For me to like the writing in the game it has to have either colourful and relatable characters or feel really grand and epic. The Mooseman basically relates ethnic mythos, which don't really fit in either category.
Again, to underline the subjectivity of this review: if weird myths and legends about world and makind and spirits is you jame, you will probably enjoy the story. Otherwise - not so much. One thing, however, is less subjective and more of a general drawback: for the most part lore in the game is presented in the form of short text entries, unlocked throughout the game one at a time. To read those entries you have to pause the game and go into a menu, which disrupts flow of the journey.

Now if you got to this part of the review and you're looking for some positives, they in fact are there. I liked the art style of the game: the greyscale scheme felt appropriately dreary for the subject matter and design of spirits and various mythological entities in the game reminded me of Studio Ghibli's Princess Mononoke movie. Voice acting, although I didn't understand a word of it, felt very authentic and certainly helped to cement the mood. Music, however, was the standout part of the game. It wasn't the kind of tune I would put in my playlist, but, again, it fit right in there with the story and developers managed to employ it to the great emotinal effect in a couple of scenes.

This is my experience with The Mooseman and I hope I gave you enough information to make a decision whether you'd like buy this game.

Couple of side notes:
1) Entire playthrough took me under 2 hours, although 100% the game would take more, maybe up to 4 or 5.
2) Steam says there's no user reviews for this game which
a. seems wrong
b. is kind of daunting as I wouldn't want to be the first to write one, but oh well.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCool idea, but the input problems kill any fun from the game, for linux gamers.

First, the menu items unclickable in fullscreen, so I have got to play in windowed mode, to get started.
For a game that uses only four input keys, it sure has a lot of problems. The gamepad controller has no button for accessing the menu, so I have got to hit the keyboard's ESC key.
Then, resorting to just the basic keyboard, the often used Myth Menu is accessed through the F2 key (seriously?), but then that menu cannot be navigated with the keyboard. The game has you go there for each of the forty myths, which describe the level's area. How did that get overlooked in testing?
Then, returning from the Myth Menu, the player begins auto-walking and a direction key must be pressed to stop. Not great for a game with death pits.

Otherwise, the animation is pretty good. The graphics are solid. They did a good job capturing the feeling behind the myths. There is not much to the gameplay - just left and right directionals - which makes me guess this was designed for mobile gaming -- desktop support was an afterthought.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was not disappointed.

This is not a long game, and if you're cool with that it's well worth it. This game probably won't mean much to people who aren't into history, mythology, culture, or language. Personally, as an anthropology/linguistics dork this game was spectacular.

The artwork is pretty, the music is nice, the puzzles are (relatively simple) wonderful.

It's an atmospheric game, and if you can catch it on sale, it's definitely a worthwhile purchase.

I've never been exposed to Finno-Ugric mythology before (Komi, specifically). I've always been into this stuff, and the fact that the content appears to be based on real lore and research from somewhat respectable institutions says volumes about the game.

Buy it.

9/10 only because I once managed to fall out of the world, which didn't kill me, just resulted in me wandering around nowhere stuck. It was easy enough to reload to the latest scene though.

P.S. You can apparently find it on mobile. Shhh, don't tell Steam. I haven't played it there, but considering the pace and style I think it'd be the perfect mobile game for those who don't care about achievements. (I do!).\t\t\t\t\t
