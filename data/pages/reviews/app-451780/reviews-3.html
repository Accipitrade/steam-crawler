\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis could have been a good game, if it didn't drive the player nuts by freezing and crashing almost every single time a certain a.k.a. death skill is being used.

The Good:
  • the characters and their designs
  • interesting concept/premise. There is one enormous boss monster (Trillion) and you train one overlord at a time to defeat it
  • the dialogues. The humour is what you would expect from an IF and CH game (see Neptunia)
  • the feels. I was actually genuinely sad a few times (because of the story and not because of the crashing)
  • quite enjoyable with a strong overlord
  • defeating Trillion with your overlord is very liberating

The bad (/awful):
  • personally didn't like the battle system
  • the grinding. oh... my... lord... the freaking grinding
  • the valley of swords drove me nuts before my overlord had enough skills
  • the freezing and crashing. Judging by the other reviews this is a known issue, but there are no patches. Thanks. (I'll explain how to counter the crashing a little further down.)
  • watching my waifu die multiple times because of the freezing

Sitting through this game gave me a headache. I played through it once. Used a guide to get to "new game +" as fast as possible. Planned on getting to the true ending but the game crashed again and I just didn't want to redo a certain battle at the lowest resolution in order to get through the crashing, so I simply spared myself.
Also yes, to solve the problem with the crashing once you encounter it (and you probably will), either delete and download the game again or turn down the resolution completely.

Please, if you still want to try Trillion out (for whatever reason), buy it atleast at a 90% discount. Otherwise it's just not worth the pain.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm gonna start by saying this game isn't for everyone. Also the negative feedback of the game is, for the most part, warranted. The game play is basically like an incremental game except you gotta do everything instead of the game doing it for you but it isn't as slow paced as your normal incremental game at least, it merely borrowed some ideas from those games. You grow an overlord in power, they attempt to fight Trillion, if they die, a portion of their exp, all the gold and tokens and unused items transfer over to the next overlord. You will slowly, in this way, become powerful enough to kill Trillion.

The pacing is slow, but the dialogues and the artwork are pretty fantastic. It's a shame that, while the game has voice acting, it's rare.

Honestly if you liked Long Live the Queen or was turned off by Long Live the Queen having too many training choices and no practical use for all the training beyond opening new story dialogue BUT appreciated the game play concept, you will probably like this game.

I was turned off by this game for the first few hours, decided to try again. The story writing ain't bad tbh and the character designs are amusing.

Should you buy this game? Only if you know exactly what you are getting into. Then again, I didn't know what I was getting into and actually liked it. There's a 2 hour refund policy on Steam and it won't take you very long to decide if this game is for you.

I would rather rate it in-between yes and no but since I have to choose one, I'm giving it a positive. It's a niche game genre that feels really unique and different from any other game out there, that alone will trigger negative reviews because it just isn't for everyone. I don't mind it so I'll give it a Yes.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's obvious that this game is designed to be frustrating on multiple levels, which just doesn't jive with what I want out of an RPG. That said, there is an art to making a difficult game compelling and interesting; Dark Souls or Ninja Gaiden this is not.

The jist is, you need to throw waifus into a bonfire to kill a generic force-of-nature anime bad guy here to eat your hell kingdom. You accomplish this by training them (you choose a stat to focus on, then with no input the game randomly decides how successful you are), to gain experience points, which are then spent on stats, passive buffs, and new active abilities. This somehow manages to be very dull and obvious, and incredibly confusing at the same time. And boy does it not feel like it matters. Your characters are going to die, repeatedly.

That said, this isn't a "Dark Souls style" you are going to die. In Dark Souls, you gain skill and experience through your failures, and subsequent success feels earned. Here? You get a cutscene from the archetype-conforming shady scientist character telling you how obvious it was that you'd fail, then you allocate dizzying numbers of points into categories that aren't adequately explained to you until it's time to fight the boss. Then the character you're playing as dies and you get to do it over again with someone else, who starts a little bit stronger. Repeat until you actually manage to accomplish anything after sitting through hours and hours of pachinko-esque cutscenes nonsense randomly deciding how successful your training is, random events (often with punishing and unpredictable outcomes), and losing your shirt in what is sold to you as training.

The game is very up-front in how it sells this to you, but that doesn't make it fun or enjoyable. In my opinion, this is a significant waste of time; stick to the Publisher's other series, Disgaea for example, in which you have full control over your grind, and it pays dividends rather than disinterest.\t\t\t\t\t
