Trillion is about chipping away at a giant god that has *drum roll* a trillion HP. You train your overlord(s) by choosing which stat to build, and eventually you'll face the giant god. Upon defeat of your overlord you choose another to train until you beat the giant God. The training sections are fun, but it also has a visual novel element to it, and the combat is grid based.

There's a lot of different pieces and systems that make up Trillion, and it really made things more difficult for me as time went on. I want to say I ultimately like Trillion because it's so very different from anything. I also got this for about 3 dollars on a steam sale, so I'd say it was certainly worth the try.

If you like complicated tactical games with a time crunch, give it a try\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPositive: Controller works. Japan again uses religious lore expertly to create interesting ideas unique to westerners. Faust and every one of the avatars for the seven deadly sins all are fun and interesting characters. I have enjoyed interacting with all of them so far... "Cerberus" being Cui 2.0 (That's a Fairy Fencer F reference)

Neutral: Reminds me of Princess Maker/Long live the Queen with stat raising and brownnosing your character.

Negative: The combat x 1 Trillion. Trillion isnt the enemy, he is just trying to stop us from the fart in church combat system. No movement limits would have solved it. That way, you could just run from Trillion's attacks and not be forced to watch the enemy slowly charge to kill you. Plus real-time would make for even more strategic gameplay. The pauses bring things to a snail's pace and really kill an energetic atmosphere. My weapon behaves like a tomahawk chop. Was a bit awkward without a trainer. Is actually decent with one.

Overall: Will complete it, but felt the need to mention these ideas to others before they buy. A steal on discount, but take a think or twenty before paying full price. An Iffy/Compa lowpoint imo, but they can do little wrong to me. Worth a try. I'd suggest using Cheat Engine or its ilk however...

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTrillion is what I would consider to be "double-niche", as it is a mish-mash of (mainly) two genres: SRPG and Raising Simulator. About 80% of this game is navigating menus and reading the VN-style cutscenes as you train and raise Affection with the Overlord of your choosing (at least for the first 3, the rest after that are in a set order). The other 20% is mystery-dungeon-like gameplay where you navigate on a grid through the Valley of Swords for training or to fight Trillion; anytime YOU move, the enemies will make a move of some sort as well, each move you make being a "turn."

For your very first run, I'd STRONGLY encourage you to forego any sort of guides and just do it blindly, and take your best whack at Trillion that you can, because while there are various ways to go about defeating him, there's a pretty easy way to do it that will suck all the fun out of trying all sort of different playstyles. I had originally played this on the PSVita and, after my first run or so, could no longer play it due to it crashing all the time. I picked it up again here on Steam and I'm pretty glad I did. In addition to the bugs from the Vita version being next-to-nonexistent, I thoroughly enjoyed the story and getting the 10 different endings. There's plenty of everything to go around here: comedic relief bits, fanservice, and heart-wrenching despair as you watch the Overlord, that you trained and who fell in love with you, get eaten like yesterday's leftover potato casserole. If you have the fortitude and willpower for grinding, there's a nice bit to be appreciated by 100%-ing this. That being said, if you just picked this up for cheap on a steam sale, I'd say just just play two or three runs, making your final run the True Ending run for good measure so you get the closure you, and anyone else playing this, would deserve.

Interesting fact about this game is that while it was developed my Compile Heart, quite a few Disgaea staffers were on board with the project, such as the director of Disgaea 4, Masahiro Yamamoto, and the composer for Phantom Brave and Disgaea: Hour of Darkness, Tenpei Sato. Sato's signature style is very present here, as just like in Disgaea, you'll find yourself humming the tracks that play in the menus, when fighting Trillion, etc. even long after you've been done playing the game.

I very rarely 100% games. I've only ever 100%'d three games in my life, but this one was worth it to me. Getting to see each Overlord's ending, and watch them die again and again and again and again all for the climax in the true final chapter was all-too satisfying.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBought this very cheap during the autumn sales but I have to say, if I'd known how good it was beforehand I'd probably have bought it at full price a long time ago. This game is quite underrated and I was pleasantly surprised to find a hidden treasure like this.

The concept itself is quite unique. Throughout the game, theres really only 1 real boss, which is the titular monster, Trillion. You spend a lot of the game just preparing your character to fight this boss, one at a time. Keep in mind also, that the game doesn't joke around when it asks "how many overlords will you sacrifice?" because your characters, upon dying to Trillion are dead for good for the remainder of that playthrough. This can be quite heartbreaking especially from your second playthrough onwards if you know you have to let a character you really liked die, but if you can stomache through it the overall gameplay, concept and story is quite good. It seems quite evident that its a low budget game, but I'm overall quite satisfied with the quality delivered and the sheer volume of content available. 15 hours in, and given how many endings and things there are to do I'm nowhere close to done with this game.\t\t\t\t\t
