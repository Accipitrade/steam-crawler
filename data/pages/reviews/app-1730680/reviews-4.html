\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKlonoa's original release was a classic, and still is; a game that spends that much time on so many "best-of" lists has something to it.

As a platformer it's very much in the mold of a Mario or pitfall: you're introduced to the main abilities you'll have early on, and the game's challenges overall involve using your limited abilities in interesting ways.

Unlike a lot of games of this genre, it uses the idea of 3D and 2D in interesting ways: movement is constrained to left or right, but things may be in the foreground or background, or may involve shooting something perpendicular to your movement. Enemies might come from the background to the foreground or vice-versa, and you might be swining on a bridge or some suchas. While it sounds confusing, the game does a good job of making things obvious and understandable, helped by the gorgeous and bright art style.

Another thing to say is that while the game looks like mascot platformers of the past, brain dead and simple, the game hits hard with some of the hardest platforming and execution of it's era. IT comes from the era of "Short, but brutal" and lives up to it: with only ten levels in the first one, the game makes sure to pack as much as it can into that.

Overall, this is definitely a game for both new and old gamers; both those who have nostalgia for the original release, and those who want a new and fun game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tKlonoa Phantasy Reverie Series delivers remakes of the main two entries in the cult classic Klonoa series. These are both 2.5-D platformers revolving around picking up and throwing enemies, wrapped in colorful aesthetics and hiding melancholy stories. The versions in here have much higher resolution assets than their respective PS1 and PS2 counterparts, but hit detection feels a bit finnicky in here. Still, these are quite solid versions of these games, and the games in here are quite good.

Klonoa: Door to Phantomile was novel in its original release for combining 3D environments and 2D gameplay, lending its world some literal depth in comparison to other 2D platformers of its time. It's put to good use with branching pathways and elaborate settings, but in this first outing, it doesn't quite reach its full potential. It's a solid start, but it's not quite an all-time great.

Klonoa 2: Lunatea's Veil makes a much stronger case, though. It keeps the basic mechanical foundation of the first game but expands upon the types of enemies and puzzles encountered through the game, giving it more variety and pushing into more creative territory. It also introduces snowboarding levels which provide a faster counterpoint to the generally slow platforming levels. Even the story seems more substantial this time around, with more complex characters and motivations. A proper Klonoa 3 never materialized, but I'm not sure if one is really needed with a game like this.\t\t\t\t\t
