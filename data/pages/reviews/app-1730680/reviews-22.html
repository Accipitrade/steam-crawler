\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTwo beautiful platforming games in one package. If you're a fan of the genre, they are so worth it. Engaging platforming, beautiful art direction and music, and storylines that, despite opening up with the usual "save the world from the bad guy", become surprisingly deep, especially in Lunatea's Veil. It's such a shame this series has been so overlooked.

When it comes to the quality of the remaster effort, I've only played the original version of Door to Phantomile, so I can only comment for sure on how that one is. Despite some charm lost in the new visuals, as well as the awkward pauses between dialogue boxes, the QoL gameplay tweaks are very welcome, and the game's otherwise just as playable. Lunatea's Veil, I played for the first time through this remaster, and I think it made for an excellent first impression of the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's okay to remember to be sad.

Klonoa's a bubbly game with some of the best platforming I've ever had the joy to go through. But in a lot of ways, it's more than that to me. What initially starts as an innocuous journey of a little cat thing trying to fight darkness and sorrow becomes something so much deeper.

There's a level deep into the sequel, a game that holds for me as one of my top PS2 titles of all time, called the Maze of Memories. It's a place where people spend their entire lives reminiscing on the good times they had, no longer building what was new. It hit me hard as a kid and still hits me hard now. It's a reminder that even though we attempt to distract ourselves from harsh realities and sorrows of the present, we can't just pretend it doesn't exist.

Klonoa is probably one of the best things Namco's ever made in my eyes. It's a sharply designed game with graphics that hold up even now... But it also possesses a heart that few games have ever been able to attain. If there's one thing this story deserves is a chance to give Klonoa a chance to find his way home.
