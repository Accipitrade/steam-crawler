\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you love platformers, especially ones with cutesy mascots, then this may be for you. The remake of both games sets itself to be balanced and enjoyable that you can pick up a controller and know you'll pretty much need only two buttons, or a old school NES Controller. If you want to go easy, there's a easy mode that provides the player with infinite lives, and normal with a set number of lives, which can be said for hard mode. However, if you want to play on the hardest difficulty, on either game you pick, you'll need to beat either on easy or normal mode first. Each game allows the player to change costumes, but are limited without DLC, with only two. (Classic and Moo Impersonator). For Door to Phantomile's case, its a disappointing downgrade, as I would have loved it to run around with the Costumes presented in the Wiimake's version for both games. Maybe that's something that can be remedied in a future update or DLC in the future. The games will have you collecting gems, solving puzzles and saving multiple prisoners and dolls that are trapped and lost. Al while you go out to save a singing Diva from a Mystique Figure and his henchman and go out and travel various locations to collect Elements to save Lunatea, Collecting gems, prisoners and dolls will present the player with rewards if they collect a max limit, with prisoners being indicated by the HUD. The HUD, while it is workable and clean, could have been a little better for Gem counting. As if you miss some, the games may not directly let you know until you reach the end of the level. What is nice, you can revisit any levels as soon as you finish them without having to beat the games first, an issue first presented by Door to Phantomile's PlayStation 1 version. The games will have extra levels once you have met certain conditions such as collecting dolls and being the main stories of both games. The extra levels will test your skills on whether or not you can get to the end. Doing so will grant a music player in Door to Phantomile, and a blue check in Lunatea's Veil. Now, if you plan to get every achievement in this game, you won't have to worry too much, as the achievement list focuses more so on whether you can tackle bosses and collecting dolls and prisoners. No gem collecting achievements, as i'm sure that some would be fuming over. Also finishing the extra levels too, with Multiplayer granting you and a buddy to strategize unique jumps. Overall, a fun game to pick up and blast through with moderate ease.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a review from someone who has played the originals, so I am definitely biased in the regard that I like these games. I'll also be writing this review mainly from the perspective of someone who has played one or both of the originals and is considering getting these remakes.

Starting from the conclusion: yes, you should buy it. Even if you're not going to replay them, it will show Bandai Namco there is interest in the series. Now for the details:

Klonoa: Door to Phantomile
I personally love the treatment this game got. The environments look amazing, the sliding present in the Wii remake is completely gone, and the music sounds as wonderful as ever. Some people take issue with the fact that there was a lot of reuse of the Wii remake's textures, but I personally thought the Wii version looked fine so I'm perfectly happy with it. I'm also happy they decided to bring back the fantasy language instead of the English dub- it's always been something that added to the atmosphere of the series for me. Another thing that's been pointed out, however, is that they use the original audio from the PS1 version of the game for the dialogue, and it sounds very crunchy and compressed. It would have been nice to get a re-dub and potentially allow you to choose which version you wanted to listen to, but I understand they may not have been able to get ahold of important voice actors, especially considering how much time has passed. This may however be a turn-off for some people. Finally, one caveat- they really cranked the saturation up in both games, and while that works wonderfully for most areas, there are some that I would have liked to see have a bit more of a muted palette. Sometimes the overly bright colors detracted from the serious tone the game was trying to go for.

Klonoa 2: Lunatea's Veil
For many people, I would say that this is the main reason to buy this collection. At the time of writing this, the original on PS2 goes for about $80-100 on eBay which makes it inaccessible to most people, and that's a shame because it's an amazing game. I personally know a lot of people that have played 1 but not 2 precisely because of that accessibility issue. The fact that it's now only technically $20 is big. That being said, however, from what I've played it feels like more effort went into Door to Phantomile than Lunatea's Veil. There are good looking areas in 2, yes, but there are also ones that I quite frankly dislike in comparison to the original. A lot of these areas are at the start of the game, too, which is a shame because I'm afraid it will turn off new players. In addition, some of the sound effects (most notably the wind bullet grab) have been altered. This sounds minor but they're important SFX you'll be listening to often so I do take some issue with it. Overall, though, this is likely the definitive way to play 2 for the majority of people and has made it much more accessible, and just for that I'm willing to give it a pass on some of its rougher edges. It's a really fun game, definitely play it.

Other notes that apply to both games:
- The tutorials are annoying but you can thankfully turn them off
- The question of difficulty is hard to answer. I enjoy normal difficulty for the harsher health restrictions, but at the same time I feel like the wind bullet hitbox is too small. You can't even pick up enemies if they're going down a slope whereas in the originals you definitely could. This would be a trivial thing to fix so I hope a patch eventually comes that addresses this, but unless you're really good I'd recommend playing on easy mode for now. Normal can be frustrating and unfair sometimes.
- Another minor QOL thing, but you can't see how many gems you've collected in a stage until the end. 2 had this feature and it meant you didn't have to second guess whether you'd found everything or not. I'm sad it's gone now and I hope they bring it back.
- There are minor sound glitches, namely some sound effects don't play if you perform multiple actions too quickly. It fixes itself so it's not serious, but hopefully a future patch addresses this.
- The DLC, as others have talked about, doesn't give you the music and art files, it simply allows you to view them in-game. I'd say only buy it if you really enjoy the series and want to show your monetary support.

All in all there are some rough edges, but I really want to see this series come back and I trust in Bandai-Namco to make improvements if we get a sequel, so I want to ask everyone who is interested to buy the game.

Do it for the fluffy boyo.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis franchise provides me a lot of comfort, and it means a lot to me, so I'm gonna geek out for a bit and hopefully write a review that expresses what makes the original titles special, and how Phantasy Reverie maintains all that.

Klonoa is a PS1-PS2 era mascot that, unfortunately, didn't really catch on. Likely due to overwhelmingly poor marketing, neither Klonoa titles sold well, especially in America. It makes me really happy that Klonoa is getting some attention these days, as both Klonoa 1 and 2 are exceptional titles, and I'm even happier to say that Phantasy Reverie is a very serviceable way to play them.

Door to Phantomile is the first game in the series, and what made it special was it's surprising message and melancholy tone that existed perfectly alongside cheery levels to create a very memorable story. Ask anyone what the most memorable part of Door to Phantomile is, and chances are, they're going to say the ending cutscene. As such, I think one of the most important aspects to take into consideration when reviewing this remake is to see how well that story and tone remain intact. The Phantasy Reverie remake is built off of the Wii version of the game, but fully adopts the original's voice acting and sfx to create a very charming experience. While all of the PS1 original pre-rendered cutscenes have been replaced with in-engine recreations, the remake does a very serviceable job maintaining and evolving upon the original's cinematography. It's not without flaws, there are occasionally awkward moments that I believe could have been handled better. For example, Balue, a character you meet in the first level, certainly doesn't feel as animated as he does in the original, and there are moments like this throughout the game. In spite of that, the story and tone, in my opinion, feel totally in tact, which is awesome. As for the gameplay, Door to Phantomile features some of my favorite puzzle platforming gameplay ever, and I'm not typically a fan of that style of gameplay. Movement is very tight, and doesn't allow for much freedom, but the game engages you with enough puzzles and obstacles to make it a plus, rather than any sort of negative. Klonoa's core gameplay sees you using enemies as weapons and double-jumps. You grab onto an enemy, and can use it to gain extra height, as a weapon, or both. There isn't really any game quite like it, and it gets really addicting, especially in the harder levels near the end. Once you begin nearing the end of the game, I'm confident in saying that you'll wish there was more.

Well damn, there is more! The way I see it, Lunatea's Veil is the star in terms of quality in the Phantasy Reverie series, which is saying a lot because the first one's really good. Much like the first game, Lunatea's Veil is well-known for it's excellent writing and awesome gameplay. While Door to Phatomile's remake cutscenes occasionally feel awkward, Lunatea's Veil's feel more natural. This ends up being really nice, as the environments in this game are particularly well-crafted. Much of the cinematography in this game is really something, so to see the cutscenes preserved really well is a massive plus. Gameplay wise, Lunatea's Veil takes the foundation that Door to Phantomile established and builds upon it by adding countless new mechanics that feel right at home. It seems to me that people are heavily fixating on Door to Phantomile, but I hope people also continue onto Lunatea's Veil, it's spectacular, and may be my favorite of the two.

It's a bit off-putting that Bandai seemingly pinned Klonoa's status as a mascot on whether or not this game would sell well, but I was thoroughly surprised with the overall quality in this remake in spite of that. I hope Klonoa can finally end up cementing itself among the greats.

TL:DR; The original games were masterpieces in both writing and gameplay that simply got neglected, and this remake provides a serviceable way to play both mainline entries in one place. The price-point is definitely steep, but I'd argue that just one of these games is worth $40 by itself.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t~BENEFITS~
~Klonoa: Door to Phantomile-Phantasy Reverie Series~
Fun gameplay similar to the original Klonoa: Door to Phantomile.

Epic level design that fits with your pace of playing.
Good music you'll want to listen to all of the time.
Characters are cute, memorable and endearing. (The villains are of impeccable design)

New modes to play in and a two player feature that can be quite fun.
Fun to play if you're casual or serious!



~Klonoa 2 Lunatea's Veil-Phantasy Reverie Series~
Very close to the original Lunatea's Veil.

A fresh adventure that is as amazing as it's predecessor.
Characters are likable and playful but somehow holds down a serious tone.

It spices things up with snowboarding segments.
Normal levels are vast and a joy to explore.



~DOWNSIDES~
~Klonoa: Door to Phantomile-Phantasy Reverie Series~
Easier than the original. (even on normal mode)
There are tips that pop up on the screen constantly. (However you can remove them via the options screen)

The first boss Rongo Lango, has his textures mapped incorrectly.
Some of the NPCs (Jugpot soldier,Pamela,Karal,Grandpa,Huepow) are from the Wii remake that doesn't match the original art style.

The colors and bloom effects can sometimes obscure the characters facial expressions/mouths.
Expressions of main characters are out of kilter and do not give the emotional story justice.

Most if not all of the dialogue has been unnecessarily and bizarrely edited, they should have simply kept the original dialogue.(it now feels like I'm trying to read a run-on sentence poorly translated into English.)

The dialogue auto scrolls, it pauses on short phrases and quickly skips long sentences.(Very irritating if you are trying to enjoy the story at a slower pace.)
The music often loops incorrectly and plays at the wrong time during important sequences.

The roof of Klonoa's house changed to blue instead of the original orange. (why? Now it blends in with the windmill.)



~Klonoa 2 Lunatea's Veil-Phantasy Reverie Series~
As with Phantasy Reverie's Door to Phantomile remake, this also suffers from over saturation and heavy bloom.

Klonoa's mouth and eyelids do not emote in certain situations that are integral for us to understand Klonoa's personality.
A small amount of textures are looped oddly, but nothing majorly jarring.

Leorina's outfit was changed to purple instead of the original blue. (why?)



~CONCLUSION~
Klonoa was like a phantom until it was unveiled to many with Phantasy Reverie Series releasing.
While it does have inherent flaws that are annoying to longtime fans of the original games (such as myself) it holds up as a remake of two amazing games that will hopefully never be forgotten again, and to be enjoyed by a new generation of Klonoa fans.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoth Klonoa 1 and 2 are still solid platformer entries. Nothing that redefines the genre, but good for anyone who's looking for something else to play. Presentation for this series is one of its big strong points with the cute art direction and amazing soundtracks carrying the experience for both games quite a bit. The games themselves are short & sweet adventures that are to the point.

Klonoa 2 as far as I see it is going to be the main reason to get this collection. It's the type of hidden gem the PS2 era was full of. Besides being the better game in my opinion with its more involved platforming and inventive level design; it just gets treated by this remaster the best. Klonoa 2 gets an almost complete overhaul here: Remodeled environments, new character/enemy models, completely new textures, modern rendering pipeline with lighting, shadows, materials, etc. It almost comes across as a brand new game at times, the uplift is pretty impressive. My main complaint is they changed the art style from the original which went for darker color tones and an almost cel-shaded aesthetic with its character outlines.

Klonoa 1 is based off of the Wii remake and still feels very close to it graphically with the main differences being the few new character models, slightly sharper textures (with a few being redone), and lighting/shadows (the implementation of which is kinda spotty). Improvements from the remaster are more all over the place here compared to the second game. Also it looks a bit worse than the Wii ver. in some areas? Like the water in Jugpot lacking its reflection/refraction or the bloom missing from certain interior areas like caves. That said these are mostly nitpicks in the grand scheme, it still looks perfectly fine and is more than serviceable for anyone who's playing it for the first time.

Keep in mind these games are short, like VERY short. Beating Klonoa 1 will take 3-4 hours casually and the second game is a bit longer but still is only a 4-5 hour playthrough. Going for 100% can a add a few hours to the runtime, but you're still looking at just 15-20+ hours total and that's a high estimate. $40 is probably going to be a big ask for a lot of people realistically speaking. If you can manage to get it on sale for $20 or less then it's totally worth it.\t\t\t\t\t
