\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJourney back to a point in time when games just wanted to be games, and platformers just wanted to be platformers, by playing one of the most honest platformers of the time.

Simplicity and accessibility are the name of the game here. At a time when everyone and their mother were trying to figure out 3D controls, Klonoa put you on rail that twists and turns through a 3D world. Which let you skip the learning curve and experience it using familiar 2D controls. To make the experiece worth it, it dialed up the charm of its artstyle to a 110%, and threw in a grandiose, albeit simplistic story. The end result was a breezy romp that you got through quickly, but didn't forget for the rest of your life. And now you get to taste it again, in the current year of full HD.

And it should not be said that the simplicity comes at the cost of variety. The game gets a lot of mileage out of its double-jump mechanic, which is infinite so long as you're skilled enough to grab enemies mid-air. As well as out of putting multiple rails in the same scene, which you can interact with by throwing things either toward or away from the screen. Taken together, those make for some fairly satisfying puzzles and obstacles.

But now that we said the nice things about the package, lets adress a few elephants in the room:

It is obvious that the two remakes did not receive an equal amount of effort. While Klonoa 2 is mostly re-done from scratch, Klonoa 1 is a mishmash of re-used assets from previous releases of the game, which at times makes for a really jarring contrast. Especially when it comes to the audio, which is super-compressed and badly mixed.

Secondly, it being an honest platformer of its time is both to its benefit and to its detriment - it suffers from many of the trappings of arcade-era design. Most of the challenge comes from precarious jumps over instant-death pits, which will eat into your limited lives that you need to keep accumulating just to keep up. The bosses, while impressive, are mere gimick fights that you beat as a formality. And past the 10 hours or so that you will get out of the two games combined, replayability is an afterthought - the most you can do is replay levels to unlock inconsequential extras.

Such old-timey sensibilities might not be palatable to newcomers going in fresh in the year of 2022. Not at full price at least. Depending on your tastes, you may want to wait for a sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was excited for this game to come out, to the point where I was refreshing the Steam store page constantly on the day of release just trying to buy it as soon as humanly possible. I've now played through the whole game and earned all Steam achievements, and I can safely say that it has lived up to the hype. The gameplay is fun, yet challenging at times. Certain levels took me a fair bit of time and attempts to get through, granted I don't have a ton of experience with 2D platformers. I found it hard sometimes to gauge when things would move from the background to the foreground, but that may just be a me problem. The visuals are fine enough, I like that they kept the map from the Wii Klonoa in Door to Phantomille, though I do wish that they had gone with something a bit different for Lunatea's Veil, I liked the style with the outlines from the original. The story of the Door to Phantomille is pretty basic for the most part, though it does have some surprisingly sad moments. I really liked the story of Lunatea's Veil, though it can be a bit of a whirlwind at times. Overall, I'd say this game is definitely worth a try if you're a fan of platformers, and if not it's probably still worth a try honestly. Wahoo!\t\t\t\t\t
