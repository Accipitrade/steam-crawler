\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love this game.

It's purest expression of 'Risk for computers' in the Total War series. In later games, the strategy map is cluttered with elements that defeat true turn-based play. This one is delightfully abstracted. No walking avatars, no silly animations, no ships, just chesspieces. You see the results of your decisions immediately. It's compelling enough that I play most campaigns without ever leading a battalion, flying through 150 turns in less than an hour.

The only limiting factor on replayability is the AI. Oh, for this game to have better AI. It's weak enough that I tend to start in 1580 so they've got enough pre-existing infrastructure to have a chance.

Here's how I've won:

* Takeda. Use my wildly overpowered starting armies to destroy the home base of every other faction. The AI all but gives up. I settle anywhere and start taking over provinces. The AI fields peasants to counter my horses. /game

* Shimazu. Isolated and alone, I build a bunch of ports, trade, and overwhelm the map with my cut-rate No-Dachi. Never bother with another unit. No one has a chance. I've won in 11 years with this faction.

* Imagawa. Train a Geisha within four years. Kill everyone with a star, have half the map suddenly become mine, vanquish rebels and ronin at my leisure.

* Mori. Piss everyone off by converting to Christianity, build a million ports, then invade with heavy cavalry backed by Jesus.

And on and on. Oda's the closest thing to a challenge, but even then, if you're really backed into a corner, you can just send an emissary to a port followed by a giant army to flank that opponent.

Still. I want to be an emperor, not a general, and that's what this game is best for. There's an up-and-coming title called "Oriental Empires" that looks to be a compelling modern alternative.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played the original Shogun: Total War often when it was a newer game, and returning to it, I can still say that it is a great purchase. Due to its limitations, I would recommend it for the following individuals:

-Those nolstalgic for it.
-Those who enjoy older Total War games, from Rome 1 backwards, or who want to experience the difference.
-Those who haven't gotten into Total War yet, and want a basic experience. You can see how later Total War titles were influenced by this game, and the basics from here will get you far in the other titles.

Potential purchasers should also be warned about compatability issues. While Shogun has been updated to work on modern operating systems, some users still experience difficulties. If you are interested in the game, I would still recommend getting it, but be aware of the possibility of spending a few minutes getting it to function.
