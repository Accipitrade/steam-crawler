\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you are at all a fan of strategy games, feudal Japan, or games that require thought, you will proabably enjoy this game, at least to an extent.
Shogun: Total War is the first game in the very expansive swath of Total War games,
I found out about this game after recently playing Civ 1991, which I also enjoyed.

This game will make you care about each individual Daimyo. You'll be upset when Takeda sends a ninja at you and fails while you're allied. You'll scream at your monitor when Hojo attacks you while allied even though you were leaving him alone and he has no chance of success.

In short, the game is split into two phases, the Strategy Map, and the Battle Phase.
During the Strategy Map, you move units around, train units, and create buildings. When two or more non affliated units meet, they battle.

In the Battle Phase, the units are at your command in real time to do battle.

If you don't wish to read any more, stop here at this Tl;dr:
This game is decent. Buy it if you are even remotely interested in the themes this game portrays, or are a big fan of the Total War series or strategy wargames in general.

Onto the specifics:
Graphics:
They hold up today, especially when played in the highest reso you can muster. They aren't flashy, and they aren't sexy, but they get the job done. The only time I had gripes with the graphics specifically is when there were 2k+ units on the field at once and I couldn't tell which unit was which from my aerial view.

Gameplay:
A mixed bag. The Planning Phase is alright. It could totally be far more informative, as you'll oftentimes be overwhelmed with information about who is doing what, and you may forget what your providences are currently up to. Moving units en masse is VERY clunky, and is one of the reasons why I haven't put more time into the game.
The Battle Phase is fun, however. There is very little which can mimic the rush of adrenaline you get when you send a 1k man squad, including your Daimyo, over a bloody river in order to take that one strategic advantage you need to wipe Shimazu off the map.

However, with the exception of the Mongols and the Mori, and even the Mori to an extent, the clans are too similar because you always end up running Geisha, Heavy Calvary, and Kensei anyways. Surround 4-5 Kensei with a few units of Calvary and Heavy Calvary and every army you attack will invariably rout. While this is going on, send your 2-3 Geisha after every Daimyo you see, and you win. This works even on Very Hard. This makes pretty much every clan the same, and destroys diplomacy and stealth because the Emissary is garbage when compared to your Geisha, and the CPU usually offer alliances anyways. The Ninja take like 3-4 Emissary kills before they can even dream of reliably taking out a worthy General, and if at any point he fails, bam, everyone knows you're a sneaky ♥♥♥♥♥ using Ninja. Geisha however, don't die when they fail, and there is nothing anyone can really do about it besides throw another Geisha at yours, or hope and pray their higher leveled Ninja can stop her. This is the second reason why I haven't put more time into this game, some of the gameplay mechanics are too broken, which renders large swathes of the game pointless. The same happened in Civ 1991. Why struggle to maintain an economy or a republic or a democracy when you can just have one or two far off settlers which constantly buy and sell barracks in a despot that sheds blood on every turn? I am no Sun Tzu, and I didn't look these strategies up for either of these games, so I'm sure others have figured this out before me.

Sound:
Mostly positive. The Muskets are nice to listen to, the screams and shouts of various units are nice, background music is non intrusive. The "Yo-hoh-ho, yo-hoh!" actually really gets you in the mood during the Battle Phase. The archer sounds are paltry, however, and the voice acting is abysmal.

Multiplayer:
Non existant.
While this isn't technically true, there are LAN battles, that is just it, only LAN BATTLES. The full campaign is not playable in any form in multiplayer, which, in my opinon, makes none of the battles have any weight.

Entertainment Potential and Replayability:
For casual players who don't wish to always run the best strategy, and just wish to watch or passively intervene in thousand man battles, this game is required reading, and will last for a long time. However, once you grasp the mechanics of the game and then realise some of the glaring flaws, the game becomes too easy, and loses most of its challenge. That combined with the very slow and clunky movement of units in the Planning Phase make subsequent playthroughs boring and sometimes even irritating.

Overall, I'd give this game somewhere around a 6.5-7.0. I like it, yes, but can't exactly reccomend it to anyone due to its glaring flaws.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game which started it all.

I still have the big, red, cardboard box this game originally came in. I remember playing a demo of it on an old PC Zone demo CD. Good times, good times.

The game runs great on newer systems, now. You can run it in your native resolution without a problem. I've experienced no glitches or bugs, apart from a strange sound glitch which cuts off the voice acting in the throne room.

The game is still as fun as I remember it. It's not a complex game. The diplomacy amounts to sending emissaries to forge alliances and nothing more. The only goal is to build up resources and then use said resources to recruit armies in order to conquer Japan. That's it. It's simple, but a lot of fun. I also love to cheesy cinematics - characters actually looking at, and talking to, you; it's also one of the main things I loved about the old C&C games.

I will say that I am speaking from nostalgia. I love this game still, because I loved it well over a decade or so ago. You may find it hard to get into if you're coming from newer TW games.

If you can overlook its simplistic design, then I highly recommend giving it a go.\t\t\t\t\t
