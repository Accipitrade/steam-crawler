\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDespite what Steam claims, I've played Shogun to death for nearly a decade. I've beaten the campaign as all available clans, and played all the historical battles. Despite knowing the basics of how it'll play out, every Shogun campaign is a thoughtful and nail-biting affair, with battles that can swing unexpectedly for or against you.

Probably the most impressive feature was (and is) AI that, while occasionally a bit simple, is usually surprisingly good to taking advantage of what it has. Shogun was the first game I played, outside the Close Combat series, that modelled morale in battle, and still does it startlingly well. Graphically simple, it's mechanically complex by the standards of the time, and even now, I adore its simple interface, simple graphics, and purity of gameplay. More than a few times I've finished a battle against the odds having won by intelligent use of weather, terrain, and concealment; more than once I've been beaten, from a favourable position, by the AI when it does the same.

The sounds are solid (even if the firearms sound a bit lacklustre), and the simple graphics allow for huge battles without straining even the most ancient machines. The music is minimal, but thoroughly effective in drawing players into the atmosphere the game seeks to set - that of a Japan torn apart, and a thoughtful leader trying to manouevre to best advantage among the chaos. The combination of turn-based and RTS elements sounds daunting, but works beautifully; the campaign map is perfect for a turn-based game, the battles for real-time.

Buy it, play it. It remains my favourite RTS, surpassing even its successor.\t\t\t\t\t
