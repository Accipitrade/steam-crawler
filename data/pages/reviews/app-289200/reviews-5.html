I did finish the game and it has to be said that there's just no other game out there like this, but at the end of the day this game is just barely functional at every level.

The tactical combat works, but is very exploitable. The RPG elements balloon completely out of control very quickly until you're an unstoppable badass. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo first off: If you have Windows 10, this game might not work! It works for me when I set it to use XP SP3 on compatibility settings, but the resolution isn't great with that for some reason.

Anyway. Wow, this game has surprised me. Strong RPG element, you play Alexander, King of Airath, and control him on his quest to regain his throne. Apart from travelling around the world on your quest, the other big element is turn-based strategy combat. It's fairly simple to understand and to get good at.

I thought after an hour that the game would take me about 10 hours to complete, having looked at the map. I got to what I thought would be the end of the game, and turns out there's a whole new world!

Some minor flaws in lack of attention to detail - spelling errors, occasional graphics bugs, but nothing that I feel spoils the game.

Enough difficulty that you can't just sail through, but not so difficult that you give up. For the price it's definitely worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSince 1990, when New World Computing changed the world of turn-based strategies once and for all with their legendary King’s Bounty, many people tried to make their own game of that kind. This one, for example, was made by a small Ukrainian team called DVS (if you didn’t hear about them, that’s OK, since they’re known only by this game and its standalone expansion called Ascension to the Throne: Valkyrie). Ascension to the Throne appeared a little more than a year before Katauri Interactive released their own King’s Bounty (which was a big success), and that was the only time when Ascension to the Throne had a chance to get some attention. Just because it didn’t went out that good. But I’m getting ahead of myself.

The main idea of the game is pretty much the same with the one in the original King’s Bounty. Only instead of looking for map pieces, you should conquer the number of castles and unite the land. Everything else is pretty much the same old formula. You control the lone hero, who should hire the troops and bring them into battle. Here and there you’ll find vendors, who’ll sell you some items, here and there you’ll find places to hire troops (don’t expect this game to be like Heroes of Might & Magic in this matter, you can’t just go to the castle and hire any troops you want, better troops are pretty hard to find and they’re very limited), here and there there are places that can give some bonuses to your stats… You know how it is in such games. There are ships too, only, unlike both the original King’s Bounty and the one from Katauri, you don’t have the full control on them here. Ships in Ascension to the Throne are more like instant travel points. But anyway, Ascension to the Throne is pretty much your usual King’s Bounty clone.

Of course, there are differences. Like this game is paying a bit more attention to the main character than usual. Our hero (the name’s Alexander, by the way) will be in every battle, so, his stats will matter a lot (even though it's an RTS game, it's hard not to remember SpellForce here), while the attribute called “Authority” will determine how many troops you’ll be able to take with you. All that, together with some basic non-linear dialogues, makes this game to feel a little more like an RPG game. But still, if you’ve played King’s Bounty, it’s hard not to recognize it here.

But that’s not a bad thing. I honestly don’t think that there can be enough King’s Bounty variations. That’s why this game instantly got my attention. And, I must say, it gives you a very nice first impression. The beginning feels pretty hard, since even the very first battle in the game can easily end with your defeat, but graphics are nice, the story about the king, who should reclaim his throne, is simple, but OK for such game (I mean, strategy game doesn’t need to be like a good novel, it just needs a good excuse), and, well, you can easily find yourself addicted. Unfortunately, after some hours you begin to see the problems of this game and when you’ll conquer life half of the world, the game will turn into a joke. The thing is - there are some small, but critical problems in this game that ruin the whole experience. And most of them are about broken combat system.

Unlike Katauri, who really tried to examine New World Computing’s games and learn from them, DVS didn’t try much. So, tactical combats in this game look nice, but feel absolutely terrible. They’re way too dull and way too random. For example, most of the units can cross the whole map in only one turn. Which means that if you have units that you want to hide on the back (like archers, or mages), you can forget about the whole idea from very beginning. If enemy will want to get to the certain unit, he’ll do so in the very first turn. At the beginning of the game it doesn’t look like a big problem, since you can easily adapt to the specific battle (thankfully, there’s an autosave before every combat). But later in the game you’ll have some units that’ll die quickly and whose death will mean game over for you. Now think about it - you have unit who should survive the battle no matter what, but you can’t do a single thing to save him / her, just because the enemy reaches him / her in the very first turn, long before you’ll be able to take the control on your troops. That’s it - the battle begins, enemy strikes, you’re dead. Game over. How fun is that? And I’ll tell you what, AI just loves to focus on such characters. When the battle begins, AI will strike the important characters with everything it has and, as I’ve already said, such units die very quickly. You can get some items to improve their health a little, but that’ll only mean that they’ll survive another turn. After that it’ll be over anyway. Moreover, archers and mages in this game can strike through the whole map and they never miss, while there’ll be times, when you’ll have not one, but two important units in your ranks. So, how should you survive that? The answer is... luck.

Simple like that. This game is all about luck. You try some different unit placements and hope that this time the AI will hit somebody else and you’ll have time for maneuver. And after more than ten tries you may just give up and quit. That’s the worst thing about this game - the whole thing is 100% random. It isn’t about the tactics, no sir, it’s all about luck. The game also doesn’t allow any mistakes. Make some wrong choices in your character build somewhere in the beginning of the game and somewhere in the second half of the game you’ll find out that you have almost no chance to win and can do nothing about. Just because there are limited number of battles you can fight and just enough places with bonuses to get at least a little chance to win in the end. In this game you should know what to do and how beforehand, or there’ll be a lot of troubles.

All that together means that it’s close to impossible to enjoy the gameplay. It looks really fun on screenshots, but in real life it feels like a random mess. Tactics, balance? Forget about those. It’s just a chaotic something that looks like a proper TBS. Something that lost its whole purpose right after Katauri released their game. As long as you’re not a hardcore TBS fan, who wants to try each and every game in the genre and aren’t afraid of frustration, skip this one and try Katauri’s King’s Bounty instead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPretty rough start but once you get the hang of it, a great game with a good story and 20hours of gameplay to offer to any one considering grabbing this game.The combat is turn based and the hero tho he can fight is best kept faaaaar away from the front lines until he got the hitpoints to take all the damage that will be thrown at him.

When starting this game you are a weak hero that will depend on his cannon fodder, you will die allot learning this game, once you got the hang of it i suppose the combat can get a bit repetitive but not for long depending on your pace.Units requires Authority, that you acquire from chests,statues and when you level up.The units you can hire are limited but reappear over time in case you screw up badly so no need to worry about running out of cannon fodder any time soon.

Items seems to only be usable by the hero , a real shame really but oh well, not much of a collection any ways.You can buy rings from merchants that boost your or your soldiers stats and the blacksmiths can sell/give you a weapon and armor to help with progressing from place to place.A great trick i learned in the game was to buy a ring that gives authority, wear it and buy a extra soldier or two and then swap it with a ring that boost the soldiers stats.

Time is on your side in this game as there seems to be no time limits, but there are some crash issues with the game but all in all it should not happen allot, but best to save offten to be on the safe side.My game crashed a couple times and at the end of the game as well, but luckly auto save does save the game at each battle so no need to rage over having to do all the non stop fights from scratch tho you still got to do the last fight over again.

Other then a couple crashes, no other bugs exept a one time strange bug that teleported me on the top of a mountain that surprised me as i was far away from the mountain walking down, funny and harmless as it turned out i could walk down without getting stuck.

All in all i had a blast and would rate this game at a solid 5 out 6 stars.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed this a good while ago from a CD, but it wouldn't run on W7. I'm happy that steam made a compatible version.

Don't expect anything fancy in the technical sense - graphics weren't awesome even when the game was new and there's no voice acting, but it doesn't matter. The game is pretty stable, I found just one bug: a big guy sends you to find a key, but you already have it by the time you talk. Talking to him again later will crash the game. Also, the auto-save feature is not always hepful: it's supposed to save before a battle, at the point when you can choose not to attack, but sometimes, it saved right in the first turn of the battle. And choosing your battles is essential, especially at the beginning.

You - the king od Airath - get betrayed by your court mageand hit with a spell that should kill you. It only breaks your armor, though, and deposits you somewhere far away, in a land called Oganthar, alone and unarmed. Oganthar doesn't have a king, the lands are divided between seven Lords (six, actually, plus one Lady). And since you can't return home in your current state, you decide to unite Oganthar and raise an army to take back your lands.

Easier said then done - you have a handful of hitpoints and hit about as hard as a peasant (the weakest unit in the game). You get some cash to hire an arrmy, but money is not really the limiting factor. Your Authority is. That's a stat, almost like a currency, which you need to command troops. Unlike King's bounty, here every unit takes up a bit. Also, units are not available for hire in infinite Numbers, so you need to optimize a bit. You also get XP and level up fairly often (lvl 50+ at the end), so don't worry, you'll get more than the initial six peasants. You can inprove your physical damage, but there's no point playing as a fighter. You still need to move around the battlefield to hit someone and you only hit one enemy with your sword. With a spell, you can kill a whole stack at once, so you'll end up leveling your magical power.

Stacks work differently than most turn-based fights. You have space for ten stacks in your army (one of them being you, so nine, really), each with max ten units. "Units" come in all shapes and sizes - humans (melee and shooters), animals, undead, giants. Yes, you can have giants in your army. Yes, they are as awesome as it sounds. In actual combat, you don't see stacks like in HOMAM, you see each individual unit - but you control the stacks. You fight on a hex grid and your (and enemy) units are smart enough to go for the most kills. So, if your archers do 50 damage each and the enemy monsters have 51 hitpoints, your nine archers will kill four monsters and heavily wound a fifth one, rather than wounding ten monsters and leave them standing. All units have a Speed stat, which determines hw far they can move on a turn, but also how soon they attack. Killing the faster enemies will make your life easier and if you do it right, you can kill all oposition before they get a chance to do anything.

The beginning is a bit frustrating - you are pretty much too weak for any battle, so you have to pick your fights carefully. The game ends if you are killed and since you have no armor, that can happen very easily. Some early quests give you some extra units beyond your Authority limit, so you can get something done. And when you get organized, you;ll find the game is pretty well balanced. By the time you find tougher enemies, you can also hire more and tougher units. And when you finish uniting Oganthar and turn homewards with your army of Furies, Dragonslayers and Knights of the Sun, you feel pretty invincible. Don't worry, you'll be proven wrong, soon enough.

The story is nothing to write home about, but also not terrible. The whole game took me about 13 hours to finished and I think I'll come back.\t\t\t\t\t
