\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI picked this up on deep discount after hearing Austin Walker talk it up quite a bit recently. This is shocking to me; not only because he likes it (which, still) but because he was also SO bullish about Invisible, Inc.

If Invisible, Inc. is a masterclass in how to make a turn-based stealth combat game really WORK and provide the player with all the information they need to make informed choices (and it is!), this can be considered its antithesis. When in-combat tooltips mention that the projected jump arc -- your ONLY form of in-combat movement, and the only means by which you can avoid instant death to a single bullet -- is "a lie" then you know you're in for a rotten time. The number of deaths to randomly falling short of a projected landing point, stopping dead because you hit someone to knock them out (which usually lets you keep going???) or unexpectedly crossing over an enemy's line of fire are many and infuriating!

The poor signposting and bad readability don't stop there, sadly. Your special abilities, gated by consuming Limit points, are usually OFF-limits, as you'll automatically burn your entire stock upon filling up, getting a free turn that's usually much less useful than a ranged instant kill, but too bad! It's never quite clear how near you need to be to kill enemies -- which is a problem when that is what the gameplay entirely consists of -- you're sometimes able to snatch flying enemies out of the air and sometimes not, sometimes teleport short distances through floors for a killing blow, sometimes not... and the ever-present problem of not knowing where you will be standing when you are DONE killing an enemy often as not leads to your heroine helpfully standing in the line of fire of a shot that would have missed, had you only known.

There are a lot of other problems with polish, like the "humorous" tooltips that "humorously" mention they can't be disabled -- good one dude, glad you prioritized this over working jump arcs -- or the incredibly bad movement both in AND out of combat, the entirely nonfunctional "stealth" elements... just don't get this. Play Mark of the Ninja again, it's still a great game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a fan of Gunpoint I was eager to find similar experiences. RONIN takes a more combat-focused take on the concept, using an intuitive and simple turn system that is tactical and engaging. RONIN retains a stealth sytem like Gunpoint, but I feel that it can be almost abused with certain abilities (like the sword throw/retireve, check https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8faF2H5YcRc for a direct example)

RONIN also has some very annoying bugs that should really have been ironed out when it was in early access. Almost on-command you can grapple yourself through a wall into an inescapable room with literally no way out of. This can happen on every level, and sometimes even teleports you to the end of the level, particuraly the last one.

If you can look past this, and the rather high initial difficulty curve, RONIN is a great game with an amazing soundtrack too (seriously go listen to Swordpoint)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWasn't going to write a review untill i saw alot of negative ones, so i figured i'd give my two cents. Brought this game on sale as the screenshots showed what seemed to be a well paced action game. And thats exactly what i recieved. I'm just going to do some +/- points to save time.

+Levels are structured well, creating oppertunities to take on all enemies at once in new game and oppertunities to take out some enemies to even the playing field in new game plus.
+Variety of enemies in the game, including people that call in backup, machine gunners and trip mines.
+Games difficulty curve MOSTLY works well, teaching mechanics at the start while slowly adding more enemies to each level, to prepare you for "boss" levels, which lead me onto...
+Boss levels are hard missions designed to make you use all of your skills to overcome a difficult challenge, that wil take many attempts and strategies to complete.
+Skill tree unlocks if you complete the bonus objectives for each mission.
+Fun combat that challenges you to think about your enviroment.
+ Checkpoints work with a load checkpoint system.

- Horrible bug that often occours: when you grapple you go through the wall and get stuck inside with no way out, forcing you to restart.
- Machine gunners made me consider suicide in new game plus.
- Locked 30 fps, but that didn't bother me.
- Need special edition to get a story.

+/- As i said above: difficulty curve MOSTLY works, the WISEGAL and GENERAL missions pump up the difficulty a little bit too much imo, taking me ALOT more attempts to complete, but i suppose some might consider them easy or might enjoy the challenge i suppose.

Definitely get this game when it's on sale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRonin is to games what B-movies are to film - you can tell it exists because other designs inspired its look and feel, but it's an enjoyable enough diversion that the borrowing doesn't offend. Spend any time at all with the game and you'll see its heritage - titles like Gunpoint, Mark of the Ninja and Splinter Cell: Conviction. However, its stealth game heritage has been flavored with doses of Transistor and Hotline Miami, creating something which only looks like a stealth game. Stealth kills are available (and necessary at times for three starring levels), but ghosting through a building undetected by all and sundry will not happen - there are going to be several set pieces on each level when you will have to draw your katana and go to work. In combat, if you get hit once, you die, but checkpoints are plentiful and other than the last level, you never need to replay more than your current room.

Killing in Ronin is a turn-based system of taking jumps, knocking enemies down and finding windows in which to finish them off. As you level up, you'll get some extra advantages (decoys, thrown weapons, teleport stuns, etc.), but they never sidestep the core of the experience. At the end of the game, it will still be you, your sword and a bunch of enemy laser sights to avoid. And when everything plays out well, you'll come out feeling just as satisfied as you would after a particularly obnoxious level of Hotline Miami.

There are three kinds of enemies: pistol-packing security guards, armored guards with assault rifles and cybernetic samurai with blades (there are also a few bosses sprinkled in). Each is different - the pistoleros fire single shots while the riflemen put down suppressive fire that is dangerous to leap through (but are consequently slower to shift aim points). Cybernetic samurai take two hits to kill (a stun and then a quick follow up hit). There are also a few environmental hazards (long falls, laser grids and sensor activated mines) to be navigated - fortunately, all of these can be used against enemies. The enemy A.I. is adequate and has patterns which you will learn to exploit over the game.

To level up, you need to satisfy three criteria on each level - spare the civilians, kill all the guards and prevent the alarm. Sparing civilians is generally a matter of planning (how to dispatch guards without being seen, which should be old hat to any stealth pro). Killing all the guards is just a best practice. The last criteria is the most annoying - during combat, guards will sometimes try to trigger the lockdown, putting you on a clock to stun them (civilians will also trigger the alarm but are much easier to avoid). In large fights (and especially those where there are mutually supporting enemies in different buildings), this can cause a significant amount of tooth gnashing and cursing.

I thought the game controlled reasonably well, except for the grappling hook, which was a bit swingy. The code for walls and windows is a bit off and you will sometimes clip through them, which is sloppy. The soundtrack is first rate, the art design is consistent and the graphics are crisp, so good marks for presentation. The writing, however, is both sparse and weak (even the digital comic offers no real insight into why there is such an all-consuming need for vengeance), but if you go at Ronin with the mindset that it is a diversion and not a meal, you'll be fine.

Length would seem to be about 5-6 hours, I'll let you make your own judgment on what is an acceptable price for that time. There is a new game plus available after finishing the core game for those who want to go at it all again.\t\t\t\t\t
