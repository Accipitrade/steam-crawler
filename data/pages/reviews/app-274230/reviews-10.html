\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI keep returning to Ronin every few months, even though I've already exhausted all of the game's content. I greatly enjoy playing it. While some criticisms may suggest that the game has significant issues, I find its turn-based combat to be easy to learn yet strategic enough to keep me engaged. The momentum and verticality of the game's movement mechanics are particularly impressive. Propelling myself across the stage is always satisfying, and the later ability to teleport under certain circumstances adds even more excitement to combat.

According to Tomasz Wacławek, the mastermind behind Ronin, Gunpoint played a critical role in inspiring the game's development. Similarly, Mark of the Ninja has many similarities with the game's design. However, Ronin sets itself apart from these games by concentrating solely on combat and minimising puzzles or stealth components. The game's humorous hint, "This is not a stealth game. Just kill everyone." accurately encapsulates its central theme. The game's skill tree is well crafted and suitable for its size, although some abilities, such as decoy skills, are used less frequently than others, such as sword throwing and teleportation. My main critique of the gameplay is the lack of additional content (I can comfortably beat all 15 levels in around an hour), but I don't consider this a downside of the game. Additional enemy types, level designs, challenges, and new abilities would have been welcomed. Nonetheless, the game provides an exceptionally enjoyable experience with its current assets, and its core gameplay loop remains consistent throughout, which can be viewed as either a positive or negative aspect depending on each player's individual preferences. A New Game+ mode with more difficult enemy placement does allow for a greater challenge, as well as an achievement.

Many have voiced their discontent with the game's narrative, which is minimal, to say the least. The primary goal that players are tasked with is to eliminate five individuals without any clear motives. While I do not perceive the absence of a storyline as a problem, as I believe it corresponds with the developer's approach, I do believe that incorporating one could have enhanced the game. I thoroughly relish the game's art style, and the music significantly contributes to the overall ambience.

I recommend this game a lot, especially because of how well-polished it is, with glitches being rare and levels having strong design. If you're interested in playing, I suggest waiting for a discount rather than paying the full price of £10.99. Moreover, while the game recommends using a controller, I find the mouse and keyboard to be far more intuitive. Thank you Tomasz, I can't wait to check out your other work!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros:
+ the fighting mechanic is really dynamic and immerse yourself in the action
+ the story is fairly simple, but it lets you imagine the reasons of this vendetta
+ the graphics are in phase with the ambiance, the facilities and the rain reflect, with good balance, the dark atmosphere
+ the progression in your competences with the skills tree is well balanced and it forces you to not just go on a rampage, and sometimes, the best option is to analyze the level and be stealthy

Cons:
- some checkpoints are not very well placed and force you to walk 5 to 10 seconds to rejoin the fight after each death

Ronin is a solid game with a unique fight style, and which can be quite challenging. I strongly recommend it (even full price).

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn a "turn-based" game where you have to move every turn in order to not get shot (and die, and start over from the last checkpoint), and your only method of movement in combat (which is most of the game) is to jump, the arc of your jump shown by the game should be reasonably accurate, barring things visible to the player, or else the game becomes a frustrating mess of constant reloading to try to work around its bugs. Unfortunately, RONIN is the latter case. Too often I attempt to jump, the arc showing a clear path, only for the character to begin a jumping animation and abruptly stop, having moved, perhaps, one or two pixels forward.

During a jump, you can alter your movement using a grappling hook, but this only changes your arc, unless you spend a third turn in reeling yourself in. This makes for some sophisticated movement options in theory, but the changes to your arc aren't indicated by the game, and you're left guessing both where your new direction of motion will be and how far along in that direction you'll travel before the bullets reach you. What could have been a fun tactical experience is bizarrely left to guesswork, and in a game that allows for no mistakes from the player (a single bullet being all it takes to kill you), this is a more frustrating than fun experience.\t\t\t\t\t
