\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is stylish, exactly as long as it needs to be and has a unique gimmick going for it which makes it REALLY hurt that I have to not recommend it. The gameplay has numerous issues with poor signalling in a 1-shot kill game (Jumps don't go where they say they will, blurry lines about whether a shot will hit you or not, etc), while also failing to provide enough tools for the player to effectively find their own solution in combat. The unique gimick of combos giving you more tools is more often than not a hindrance as it means you rarely have the tool you need, when you need it and reaching limit break can screw you out of being able to use a useful tool as it resets your bar to 0. This wouldn't be the worst thing in the world if the AI functioned reliably or if the player had manual control over saves. Neither is true, as despite performing the same actions while in stealth, exactly when or how quickly a guard starts trying to find you is a variable known not even by god. If even a single one of these were to be fixed, even as an optional assist feature, I would recommend the game as a rough gem in a heartbeat but as is, the game just isn't fun past the midway point. The devs have long since moved on so I unfortunately doubt the issues I experienced will ever be rectified, if this game does interest you I would suggest buying Gunpoint, Deadbolt or Mark of the Ninja instead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR: The game is heavily flawed, grab 'Mark of the Ninja' for a much better stealth experience, or 'Katana Zero', if you want an action game

While the game has a great concept, and a sleek style, the controls often feel clunkier than a ps2 game. Between the frustration of a jump not going as far as the game tells you it will and dying because of it, to the odd decision to lock off walking while in combat, it just doesn't flow as well as it could. Additionally nearly every level I played felt like it was encouraging me to take a skirmisher playstyle dipping in and out of combat, in order to break line of sight with the enemies relentless attacks, but it puts a mechanical punishment for doing this by making guards begin to call for back up almost immediately after you leave their vision. And if they finish the call, more guards spawn, and you can no longer get a skill point for the level unless you die and do it again. The worst part is, that in some of the more open levels, guards will call for back up mid combat, forcing you to try and maneuver a whole screen away in order to maintain your progression, while 6 other enemies are still shooting at you. Lastly, stealth is damn near impossible if there are more than 2 guards in a room, so the tag is a bit of a stretch.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn my opinion RONIN suffers from fundamental design flaws akin to a game such as Minesweeper. I know how the mechanics are supposed to work, and they're fun when they do work, but they always eventually fail me by forcing me to make a random guess, which is where it all falls apart. Because the skills and techniques required to succeed are either obscure or nonexistent, and luck ends up playing a far greater role in my performance as a player than any real proficiency at the game.

Most of my deaths in RONIN are frustrating because they do not feel like they were my fault. Fine movement control is lost in combat as the game transitions to a turn based system, where you must rely on a jump arc that has a tendency of lying to you about the point at which the next turn will begin (which the game is apparently aware of, and alludes to in the combat tips). When there are tons of guards firing at you simultaneously, there's no way to know for sure whether executing certain attacks will move you into the line of fire, often making this core gameplay element feel like a gamble. This led to me adopting a far more defensive playstyle where I would basically dance with the enemy in the air, stumbling until I happened upon a position where I could kill one for free and repeating this until I won. But with this you don't really eliminate all the potential issues you could run into, and the experience can still disintegrate due to something as pathetic as pressing the wrong button when you're stacked over two enemies, as the two buttons are identical and there's no way to know which one corresponds to which enemy.

Rifle guards are especially irritating, continuously firing in the same direction for 2 entire turns, which severely restricts where you can safely move without dying. Which isn't bad in its own right, and they feel better integrated on certain maps, but multiple of these firing at you from different angles very often puts you in a position where death is impossible to avoid. Not just from them directly, but from other pistol guards whose line of fire you cannot escape without jumping into theirs and meeting the same fate anyway (such as when they target you while you are falling towards the ground, and they block you from jumping on the next turn).

I actually really like RONIN's soundtrack and the overall atmosphere of the game, and there is definitely a rewarding sense of satisfaction that shines through when the mechanics operate in harmony. But instead of feeling this, I generally find myself fighting against unfair situations that I have little to no control over, and I haven't been able to enjoy it very much the vast majority of the time.\t\t\t\t\t
