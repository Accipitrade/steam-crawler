\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm lucky I bought this on an -80% deal.

it's a unique concept, not exactly stealth but more like a puzzle game. Most of the time you will have to engage in "combat mode". The fight becomes turn based, and this is where the puzzle is. Problem is, not only does it take some creativity to figure out how to kill them+evade their one-hit-kill-shots , it also mostly takes trial and error. Because Jumping (evading) takes 1 turn, AND killing an enemy also takes one turn. Since even the easiest enemies almost always shoot every turn, and rooms tend to be very crowded, it's easy for players to get stuck in a dodging loop before even killing any one of them.

More often than not, it all will end up with you being trapped in multiple laser sights (indication of where they will shoot/attack) , and then die, and then wait for the level to load again. Which isn't long, but a good 5 seconds of waiting for every 30 second attempt. Although checkpoints are quite close.

I should also note that the skills are too costly. Here's what you need to know : Stunning enemies (jumping towards them) gives you 1 point/energy/whatever it is. Killing them gives you 2. Throwing a sword to "only kill 1 enemy" requires 2 energy. However, the other condition for it is that you have to be mid air to be able to throw your sword. Thanks to the turn-based style of the game, jumping takes 1 turn, and every turn, your energy/point decreases by one. So you basically need 3 energy to start a sword throw, which then kills only one enemy and you're left with probably another 2 or 3 enemies that you still have to dodge. If you're early in the game, you probably can't recover this sword, and then you have to get it. While dodging. By the time you do reach the sword, you would've lost the 2 energy you gained from the sword-throw kill, and you might as well didn't do a sword throw in the first place.

To be honest, if you are able to try this game out (maybe in another friend's computer?) , maybe you might like it. But if you bought it full price, I'm sorry for you. There are free online flash games out there that are more fun than this. Seriously.

Edit: not RELATED to this game but to that last part of my own review: flash games are rumored to die in December 2020. Chrome will stop the support on it and I think so will Adobe. I'm pretty sure this means you can no longer play online flash games for free anymore, or they will be un-runnable via websites. That is until I stumbled upon Flashpoint, a flash preservation project by BlueMaxima, which apparently aims to collect, compile and preserve as many flash-based games and animations as possible. Currently with, idk, thousands of flash-things saved in it's launcher. Just google Flashpoint by BlueMaxima and you should get to the main site, which i think is here https://bluemaxima.org/flashpoint/. You can learn more and contribute in the discord server, either to be a programmer to help optimize the launcher/porting, or just suggest a game if you spot one that you remember but can't find on the game's master list.\t\t\t\t\t

Ronin is a hugely entertaining and innovative Turn-Based, Stealth-Action-Ninja Platformer. However, it does feature some frustrating elements that makes it slightly less than perfect.

GAMEPLAY:
When you're not engage in combat, you can move, jump and climb in real time with the option of pausing time with the spacebar. Once you are spotted or have just completed a one-button execution of an enemy, combat begins. Then you and the enemies take simultaneous turns. Wash-rinse-repeat. For being turned base, combat is deliciously intense and appropriately cinematic.

PROS:
  • Novelty : It's not often that you get a turn-based stealth-action-platformer game, and the mash-up of genres might seem questionable at first. But as you play you realize this perfectly reflects the crazy, cinematic, bullet-time fight scenes that were made famous by the Matrix movies.

  • Art, Sound, and Music: These were crisp, clear and deliberate. Everything from the shading of the background (indicating shadow) to the familiar sound cues (indicating when you're spotted) were excellently executed. I particularly liked the clean edged art style.

  • RPG Abilities: While the first couple of levels were a bit more difficult because you lacked most of your abilities, towards the end of the game you become a nigh, unstoppable force. It made for a very satisfying combat experience.

  • Automatic and Frequent Checkpoints: For the mot part, Ronin does a good job of trying to keep the cinematic momentum going. When you die, you're usually not too far from the last thing you did, allowing you to get right back into the game and try again.

CONS:
  • NOT like Groundhog's Day: Combat is fast and intense. One little mistake and you're instantly dead. Which is probably why they implemented checkpoints and respawns the way they did. However, this can also mean fighting the same enemies, in the same space, over and over and over again. Which would imply that the enemy's movements were predictable. Or at least, the physical collisions would behave consistently.

    NOT SO! Many times the exact same move, in the exact same place, on the exact same enemy would result in a completely different outcome. And when you depend on the physics behaving a certain way to ensure you end your turn outside of any enemy sight lines (because ending your turn IN a sight line results in getting shot and instant death), it makes it frustrating when it randomly puts you somewhere unexpected.

  • Repetitive: Given the high stakes of combat (and the arbitrary hamstringing by the physics engine) a lot of time is spent replaying the same content. For better or worse. This is particularly frustrating later in the game. The last level has no checkpoints... you have to start from the beginning after every death!

  • Lack of Storyline: What little exposition occurs before each new stage, in about 4 or 5 lines. The entirety of the story could be written on the back of a paper towel. It is vague and very shallow. Clearly, it was not a focal point during development.

  • Lack of Instruction: While the controls are very straightforward, I would have preferred a bit more of a tutorial, as some basic maneuvers were hard to execute. Each ability had its littler quirks and inconsistencies associated with it. And it would have been handy to have been given a better understanding of the meta. Using the spacebar to pause time immediately before engaging in combat or being spotted is CRITICAL to being able to beat the last level.

Conclusion:
Despite the occasional frustrating combat scenario that you just can't seem to move past- I found Ronin to be both fun and rewarding, particularly after struggling through a difficult encounter. There is a great degree of skill and meta knowledge that goes into the game (perhaps intentionally?) and I completely recommend you try it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI liked the concept of the game quite a lot, which is doing ninja combat turn-based, the game doesn't give enough information to make decisions. As an example, there's a red laser line indicating the trajectory of enemy shots, but there's nothing showing your actual landing positions. Sometimes I dodge bullets even tho their trajectory is within my landing circle, because the character crouchs at last second, sometimes he gets shot in the foot and dies.
The controls are kinda clunky at first but you can get used to them, but you still don't have much control about where you're going land when enemies are on the way.
Then there's some weird bugs, like switchng to real time mid-fight, or going through walls when roping to them. I eneded up in a different room (and fight) from where I started, which resulted in the alarm going off because I can't deal with 10 guards in 2 rooms at once.
I tried to give the game a shot because it's from the Hotline Miami devs, but went through the first 2 targets and couldn't put up with it anymore.\t\t\t\t\t
