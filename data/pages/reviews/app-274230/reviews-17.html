\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd like to recommend Ronin but I can't. Combat is really iffy at times (controlling what needs to be fine precision moves with thumbsticks is a horrible idea, seeing how they're prone to serious wear and tear. Even without wear-n-tear they're not super precise when returning back to default position. This seriously reduced precision and induced frustration for me).

The turned based nature of combat is great, but lack of flow sucks (why can't I walk - but MUST jump around instead?). Also the targeting arrows at times feel like they're lying about my landing position (technically they're not, but it's just the way combat is designed is very flawed and doesn't feel very intuitive). And since combat is the primary aspect of this game... Meh.

The game's got some bugs (enemies getting stuck in walls), and the way enemies and civilians behave sometimes is just silly in a bad way.
I loved Gunpoint, from which this draws some inspiration, but this just feels like a big mess.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRonin's mainly just a more action-focused Gunpoint; same side-on camera, same focus on big jumps to get around big levels, same wall-climbing and zero fall damage, same...well, that's about it, but that's enough to make a subgenre. The main difference is that Gunpoint was a stealth game with a healthy dose of puzzle-solving, while Ronin is an action game with a healthy dose of stealth. This change makes it a substantially different creature, despite the aforementioned similarities, and while I like it, it has more than its fair share of issues.

The combat's turn-based, which seems like an odd choice on paper but makes a lot of sense in-game. Your movement options are so hectic and there are often so many enemies pointing guns at you at any one time that trying to balance both of those in real time would be disastrous. There's also a combo system, which unlocks special moves as you hit more and more enemies in a row. It took me a while to figure out how it was actually working, but once I did it was a lot of fun to use. Dodging five bullets at once, changing your midair trajectory with a grappling hook, then landing on and stabbing a security guard in order to get your combo high enough to teleport to his friend and knock him out a window is amazing when it works, and the game is designed to make these moments come up pleasingly often.

It doesn't always stick the landing, however. The game has a lot of small mechanical issues that often stack up to make it far too frustrating for its own good. The jumping controls can be irritatingly fiddly, as can the often-vital pause button. Additionally, sometimes action prompts get in the way of actually putting in the correct jump arc, which is an unfortunate oversight. There's also a weird thing where the most glancing of head- or leg-shots can just not hit you; however, the boundaries of where this can happen are not at all defined, so oftentimes you'll die because an enemy's shot is just a hair above the line where it'd arbitrarily miss. Compounding these annoyances is the fact that there're no manual saves, and checkpoints are really far apart. Considering that you die in one hit, this means that one ♥♥♥♥♥♥ due to malfunctioning controls or arbitrary hits can send you back by half the level (along with erasing any cool, hard-to-replicate stunts you might've done along the way). The final level is especially egregious in this regard, as it only has one checkpoint right at the start of one of the biggest, most populated levels in the game. It's frustrating how often the game gets in the way of its being fun.

There's also a strangely anemic skill tree, with about seven actual upgrades spaced out by literal placeholders that'll take a skill point and give you nothing but the promise that next time you'll get something substantive. It's also weirdly stingy with points, only giving you one per level if you kill every enemy and don't trigger any alarms, which is no mean feat. Luckily, pretty much all you need is the teleport power, so you're not really missing out if you take that and ignore the rest of the tree entirely.

Overall, I still recommend Ronin. When it's running at full steam and not ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ up the mechanics, it's a lot of fun, and definitely worth a purchase. It just feels...almost-but-not-quite-there as a game, which is a shame because the core concept has a lot of potential. If they decide to make a sequel with a bit more polish, I'll be all over that, but until then this is a perfectly decent way to pass the time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game looks very good, and it plays well...for the first 30 seconds. Once you get to an enemy however, the problems start. To attack, you must click on a red button that pops up. Simple, right? Wrong. This button's actual "hitbox," or where you can click it, can be anywhere on the screen, and it is up to you to find it by moving your cursor randomly all over the screen. This means that for every enemy you want to defeat, you must spend 1 to 3 minutes just moving your mouse around, watching for the button to enlarge, showing that you found the hitbox. This bug makes the game near unplayable in terms of enjoyment, because it kills whatever sense of pace the game tries to establish. I found myself getting increasingly frustrated when I would click, miss the invisible hitbox, and die because the clicking takes your turn away from you. I would not reccomend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wanted to love it.
And I sort of do, the pieces for a fantastic game are there. It is exactly the kind of game that I love.
I got it for 3 bucks, I'll keep it. Maybe they'll patch it.
Or maybe between cutting myself and pouring lemon juice in my eyes, I'll take a break to play this instead.

I love these psuedo-stealth-action-killy-puzzle games. Deadbolt and Mark of the Ninja are two of my favorite games.
BUT YOU HAVE TO HAVE SOLID MECHANICS. That's it. End of story. If it controls bad, it's bad.

I made it to the second level and realized that the 'helpful' lines, the ones that are actually supposed to let you know what will happen, aren't accurate. Like, at all. As in, sometimes you'll jump through the floor onto the guy who's about to shoot you, and sometimes you'll jump onto the floor above the guy who's(now going) to shoot you.

Then on top of that, even enemies aren't consistent. To test this I did the exact same thing a few times in a row, and sometimes the samurai guy made it all the way and killed me, other times he didn't.
Despite Xcom being one of my favorite games of all time, I DESPISE this kind of randomness in a high precision game that meant to be strategy.

And that's not even getting started on the grappling hook....

It's tragic. I love the skill tree idea, I love the turn based ninja gameplay and I wish I had more games like this.
But at the end of the day, I don't feel like I'm solving puzzles with this kind system, and that is the exact opposite of what I'm looking for.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf this game was a free flash game on Kongregate in 2008, I would totally recommend it.

Otherwise, absolutely not.

The game feels shabbily thrown together, where the main movement and fighting mechanics left me guessing whether my character was going to decide if they wanted to actually jump through a platform, or stand completely still, get gunned down, and force me to restart the fight I'd been spending 10 minutes on. Don't get me wrong, when the game didn't decide to screw me over, the movement was loads of fun. Any fight I've had in this game where the game worked was some of the most fun I've had in a stealth game. But those fights came less than half of the time.

The game is lacking visual clutter, which is relieving. I found it easy to have full awareness of where everything is in the level at any given time, and could quickly judge where the most opportune place to move to next would be, as the characters, platforms, hazards, etc. made their visual presence known. Even when 10 enemies were pointing their guns all over the room I was in, the right move was obvious.

The game's visuals in the other sense aren't too great though. If I could sum up the art design in one word: uninspired. The character and setting seems to be going for this neo-tokyo Samurai Jack feel, but it just looks recycled and uninteresting. It's not ugly, but still. There's not much going for it. Once again, if it was a 2008 free Kongregate game, I'd love it. Also, it's a game released in 2015 that's running at 30fps. Why.

Gameplay would be fun if it worked. The game makes you plan 3 moves in advance at all times, which is great, but in NG+ there's random elements, like which enemies spawn, that ruin the balance entirely. When it works, it's a tense, loads-of-fun strategy game.

The story is staler than the half-eaten box of crackers slowly decomposing in the back of your pantry. Honestly, if the game never attempted to tell a story, the story would be passable. But the game will present a "cutscene" before the last level of each chapter, saying 4 very generic lines about the character you're about to kill, that just add nothing but a longer loading time.

TL;DR: don't play this unless it goes on sale for like $1. Maybe $2, but even that's pushing it.\t\t\t\t\t
