\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRAD doesn't work as a rogue-like. Or rogue-lite. Or whatever adjacent word you want to use. It's an okay game you can play for a bit, but it doesn't have all that much replay value to it. It's exciting at first as you learn the game, but there's very little depth to it so it doesn't take long to learn pretty much everything. I like the idea behind RAD, it's just not very well executed. I was honestly going to tell you it was clearly some indie AA studio's brainchild with Double Fine producing it, but nah, they made the thing.

A single run takes a lot of time, as opposed to Nuclear Throne's ~10 minutes (ignoring looping) and Binding of Isaac's... 20-30 minutes? It's been a while since I actually playing BoI. In addition to this it only has three "worlds" (2 levels per world), so its stage (and by extension enemy) variety is incredibly limited. So it misses the mark on both stage variety and how fast you're able to get through those stages. Progression within a run is also pretty slow, starting each run with only your bat and having to whack everything in existence for a level and a half to actually get three "exomutations" to play with.

Which leads into the next point- RAD doesn't really do the whole "every run is different" schtick all that well either. There's not actually that many different exomutations- the powers you get from leveling up, which are actual active abilities. Endomutations you can get somewhat frequently, but they're a mixed bag of usefulness, and some are actually bad to get. But unlike Binding of Isaac, for example, you often don't know what endomutations you're getting- these skull altars just give you a random one. There are items to get rid of a single bad mutation, but you'd think as slow as the game goes it wouldn't waste your time like that.

The game offers some combos for mutations- some endomutations specifically modify others, or buff exomutations, but not really all that many. And from what I could tell what exomutations affected each other mostly just seemed like a visual twist. And some of the exomutations just don't seem very good, and with such a limited pool of them thinking "oh crap I got THIS again" doesn't feel good at all.

There's unlocks but they don't feel rewarding. You unlock new styles (characters)... somehow, but they aren't any different mechanically. Keep in mind this is a game that entirely revolves around mutating, so halfway through a run you can be nigh indistinguishable. Maybe you're wearing a different jacket or something, but that's it. There's different bats to unlock (I think by beating the game? Maybe?) but the ones I've gotten aren't very good. One reduces damage from falling off ledges, one boosts how fast you mutate (and therefore is the best conceptually since that's the point of the game) at the cost of taking more damage off of ledges, and another one... "unleashes a blast when executing skillful moves," whatever that means. (Did I mention the game doesn't do a terrific job at explaining everything?)

I will say, though, I kind of like the.. concept behind the art direction, even if the whole "neon retro" aesthetic has been run into the ground by this point. The bright purple of the Mender's tech and darker purple/blue of the wastelands being overrun with vibrant green of the Remade player character works well, I think.\t\t\t\t\t
