\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR : If you come in expecting a roguelike or even a roguelite in the vein of isaac you will be dissapointed despite what other reviews say. Very light end of rogue-lite, i would debate describing this as rogue anything. If you want a fairly simplistic hack-and-slash borderline moba this might be of interest

Judging by my playthroughs so far you really can't compare this to games like gungeon or isaac. There just isn't enough content or freeform character diversity between runs. This feels more like a MOBA where endomutations are your items and exomutations are your character abilities. You're stuck with more or less identical weapons aside from those exomutations which tend to be very fiddly and frustrating to aim/use, and often seem to promote very cheese feeling strategies (Wings + groundpound spam, or backpedalling with the skull/fire arm) Calling it a roguelike is a huge stretch, this is very much on the lite end of rogue-lites.


The fact that you're stuck with a melee weapon slows the whole thing down and makes combat very clunky. Given most enemies don't telegraph very well you'll be dodging constantly, unless you pick up a ranged mutation in which case you will be waiting for the cooldown constantly. You only really have three basic attack options, a slash, a zelda style whirlwind attack, and a groundpound, with the groundpound being perhaps the most abusable and safe option in most situations.

It is fairly fun as a game in it's own right, but it won't really scratch the itch of true rogue-lites or roguelikes, it's more like a diablo style hack and slash. Perhaps the biggest problem is the sheer amount of time you have to spend doing anything, either running through the levels or fighting very spongy bosses that often don't pose much threat. Despite my low playtime most of my deaths have come from slow chip damage missed due to visual noise or being swarmed into environmental hazards.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is an absolute trip and it's a solid match for any rogue-lite fans out there. One thing that a lot of rogue-lites lack is a sense of charm and it's nearly overwhelming in this game and I mean that in the best way possible. Others in the genre have immense amounts of it as well (gungeon, isaac, etc) but there's something to the high-def slickness of RAD's presentation that sets it apart in my mind.
The methods for clearing maps are concise and well laid out, nice mix of exploration and getting things done. Difficulty seems as fair as any of the classics, meaning if you drop your guard it won't take long to ruin your hour-long run. But the pinch harmonic guitar squeals + the neon + the soundtrack and everything adds up to something unusually special for the genre.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is a painfully fine balance that roguelikes have to strike, which other games can generally gloss over. Because of the permadeath aspect, roguelikes have to ensure that the progression and variety experienced in each run is satisfying enough to survive being run hundreds or even thousands of times. In general, the roguelikes that go on to be genre classics have openings that never get old, and progression that spins off in all sorts of crazy directions. RAD will not be joining this hallowed pantheon, and it’s not for lack of trying. No, despite the excellent aesthetic and solid gameplay systems, there’s just not that much going on between runs to make slamming through another one all that compelling.

Yeah, it’s the apocalypse, but wait! We’re actually two apocalypses in here, one the standard humanity self-capping and the other quite a bit more exotic. The result is a world being choked to death by radiation and corruption, with humanity huddled awy in decidedly-80s enclaves of car husks and makeshift arcades. The keepers of this place turn to you, one of the hardy youth, to take on the power to purify the taint and carve out a new place for humanity to thrive. Armed with only a sweet baseball bat and your newfound mystic powers, you must set off into the radioactive Fallow, battle strange creatures, and become more than a little strange yourself.

What that amounts to is a top-down brawler with an absolutely stellar presentation. Just as its name implies, RAD is entirely the product of 80s nostalgia, from the mohawks and skate pads of the characters to the heavy synth soundtrack and floppy disks that serve as keys. Even the narrator is the big, bold kind of voice you would hear on commercials or game shows, booming out “MOOLAH” and “DINERO” when you pick up the cassette tapes used as cash. There’s a bright, saturated look to everything that helps make combat pop and interesting landmarks stand out, and the underground is filled with neon to light your way. In terms of looks and feel, this one has everything going for it if you care at all for the 80s.

RAD also has an excellent hook for character progression that I want to highlight for a few reasons. Bashing enemies and finding certain items lets you absorb radiation like experience, and when you level up you get a random mutation. These can be subtle things like developing stronger leg muscles for running or an immunity to toxic sludge. Or it can be a more conspicuous change, like growing an ovipositor tail or a detachable organic boomerang for an arm. Mutations can provide dramatic changes to your capabilities, and these are reflected visually on your character so you can enjoy turning into all kinds of technicolor weirdness as you progress. You even have a few extra avenues for finding mutations, like buying them from a vendor or finding a stone idol in each level.

I know that sounds pretty amazing, but this is also where I first noticed problems with the game. Mutations are absolutely the highlight of progression, but the pacing of them is far too slow for what they are. During any given level you can expect to get one or two mutations through combat if you’re diligent about clearing the place out, plus another if you can find the stone idol. At best you’ll get three organically, but only one might be a conspicuous power boost like a snake head or bone spikes. You need to get a few levels in before your build really gets rolling, but clearing a level can be a 10-15 minute affair. And you’re forever at the whims of RNG to give you something neat, and after nearly a dozen runs I can tell you that it doesn’t happen often.

The core problem holding RAD down is pacing, and its RNG is the chief culprit. In Binding of Isaac or Enter the Gungeon, RNG can determine if your run will be a cakewalk or a nightmare, but either way can still be interesting and varied. In RAD, it determines if your run is going to be fun or not. If you get bad rolls and boring passives, you’re going to be slumming it with the same bat swings for the better part of an hour, assuming your basic bat skills are enough to get you that far. There’s pretty much no relief from the default bat early on unless you get cool powers, and the other progression systems are limited to one consumable you can carry (which is going to be a healing item) and one artifact that grants you another passive bonus. Your most important progression system is the one you have the least control over, and the one you rely on most for gameplay variety.

With that in mind, it should be no surprise that combat and exploration can get pretty tedious. But there are other factors that exacerbate this on top of the progression. Enemies tend to have clear patterns to their attacks that you’ll learn to deal with, eventually executing them with little concern. However, elite and boss versions of these enemies have more health and attack faster, and you have no guarantee of getting mutations that will keep you at parity with them. That means fights with tougher enemies are just longer and more tedious, because you have less chances to attack and whittle down their larger health pools. And again, the RNG can be absolutely vicious if you get unlucky, and not just with mutations. I had one run where literally no healing items dropped in the world until the end of the second level. None!

I know I’ve been pretty scathing of this one, but it’s out of love for something that’s not bad, just disappointing. RAD has a huge amount of potential, from its sharp aesthetic to its tight controls. But it needs some serious tuning to get the fun back in sync with the rest of the game. If you get a good run with powerful mutations and lots of healing, RAD can be an absolute blast. It’s just that those runs are rare, and death can come quick if you lose your edge for a moment, and both of those factors can make losing a good run even more heartbreaking. There is fun to be had here, but you have to be lucky to find it, and most players aren’t going to want to sit through half a dozen bad runs for the one good one.

<< Part #1 >>

I've seen a few reviews both on Steam and in gaming publications that have spoken about the belaboured overuse of the '80s aesthetic—not that RAD is entirely '80s to begin with, but we'll get back to that—and how they believe this impairs the game. This betrays a shallowness that I find disappointing.

Just to get this out of the way so I don't have to retread it—RAD is a mix of late '80s to early '90s aestehtics. What I'd say is instead of just dismissing it for its creator having chosen that aesthetic, perhaps we should ask why it was chosen? I mean, that it's cool is absolutely a valid answer and that's unquestionably part of it but to say that's even the most important element of that choice would be wrong.

The culture of the '80s and '90s was one of rebellion. A sentiment building in strength after the very fascist, white-washed '50s in America. There was this building crescendo in the background of it that culminated in an era of celebrating the outcast, the underdog, and those who aren't a part of what's commonly accepted as the normal status quo.

It was about the youth finding a voice with which to lash out at fossilised dinosaurs who clung to a world gripped in the throes of rabid racism, white picket fences, and gladhanding. Just... so much gladhanding. The message of the era was that we should always try to be more accepting of individuals regardless of their difference and that there's no nightmare scarier than becoming a unified commune with no individuality.

This is what gave us monsters like the Borg and the Cybermen. It's disappointing that some will incorrectly believe that these foes represent technophobia and why it's important to be wary of technology, though that isn't even remotely true. The warning was of the dangers of allowing ourselves to devolve into a hive-like state simply because it allowed us greater security, safety, and peace of mind. It wasn't worth the trade-off of everything that made an individual unique.

As an autistic person I can tell you how aware I am of this collectivist nightmare. There are always those who want to return to the '50s so that humanity might embrace such a faceless hive state, one where everyone is the same. I don't know how aware you are but autistic people are regularly tormented and tortured just for being different.

I know some will shrug that off but if you do I want you to open a new browser tab and do a web search for 'the judge rotenberg center autistic hoya.' I'll wait. Yes, there are kids being starved, forced to live in ♥♥♥♥, being put through all kinds of abuse (physical, mental, emotional, and sexual), forced to live in solitary confinement and worse. All because they're autistic, as if being born autistic were some kind of terrible sin. It's wrong to be different.

There is a fetishised obsession with normalcy and the status quo that many have that would actually drive them toward this collectivism, where they'd happily remove any traits they found undesirable until everyone was effectively a clone, all living identical lives. Just like the Borg.

There's a belief that the best way to "fix" the world is to rob it of all identity, to have it become a frozen moment in time of whatever the person who believes this idealised. This kind of mindset doesn't understand that the world is ever moving, changing, evolving, and mutating. That's who we are at the core of our very being, our sapience is built upon unexpected mutations. This is why nature requires diversity and why inbreeding is problematic.

I say as an autistic introvert that most of humanity has the problem where they'd rather surround themselves with those most like them rather than being able to accept differences. Yes, within a relationship, having a certain per centage of similarity is helpful to keep it stable. Opposites attract in that context is silly, it's equally silly however to believe that you would have a happier relationship with a partner who's identical to you. Hence compatibility.

Outside of the purview of relationships however, we should all be more accepting of differences and of a changing world. It's a very toxic part of the human condition that there is this fetishised, imbalanced value based upon similarity which drives the pathology of normalcy. Yes, normal is an innately pathological concept. It's saying that that which is most akin to myself is the superior, correct state whereas all that isn't like me is somehow less, broken, and wrong. It's a dizzyingly toxic binary that doesn't account for any of the myriad nuances of life.

This is the topic that RAD covers. It's entertaining to me that the '80s and '90s as a period of rebellion was chosen as the aesthetic because those acting in its favour aren't the heroes of the story. You see, this particular aesthetic belongs to a group known as the Menders. A group who caused a second apocalypse by believing in an ideal of a long bygone world, where they felt that they could build a better world by shaping the existing one to fit their ideals.

You aren't supposed to like the Menders. The Menders are taking an aesthetic of rebellion and using it in the same way that the aesthetic of the '50s was used to whitewash the American culture. It's a nice aesthetic to be sure but just as some have pointed out it's tiresome to fetishise it, which—entertainingly—is exactly what the game is saying as well.

Lee Petty, RAD's creator, is a clever sort. This sort of thing is all over his games. Stacking, Headlander, and RAD all deal with this core idea in different ways.

In Headlander the concept was more that human beings can be convinced to believe some ridiculous nonsense out of their desire to fetishise the familiar. They can even be convinced to fight passionately and sacrifice their lives for pointless causes if they're tricked in this way. Where they don't even know what they're fighting for, yet their passion for the superiority of their own kind drives them to continue fighting anyway regardless.

In Headlander, the question—above all others—was 'What are you fighting for?' Why are you causing all of this mayhem and destruction? Would you really be willing to destroy an entire planet of sapient minds to uphold the sanctity of the human condition when humans have been dead for so many millennia? Would you actually commit genocide against robotic life to prove the value of human purity?

In RAD, the question is similar. Why are you fetishising a world gone by? Isn't it more important to accept the world as it is and try to build a better world based upon what we have today rather than trying to capture perceived glories of days gone by? Aren't you just going to create another apocalypse by trying to shape the world in your image?

The Transfigured—as a group the player meets who don't stand with the Elder and his vision for the world—exist as a counterpoint to this fetishism, in opposition to it. Instead of seeing becoming a Remade One as a terrible sacrifice where one's humanity as lost, why not see it as an incredible gift that allows one to transcend their limitations? Why fetishise the "beauty" of humankind to the point where you're even willing ot die out for it? Do you really think any of that is what's best for you or this world?

Ultimately the best thing for everyone in RAD would be to become Remade, to leave the '80s/'90s-styled technology of the Menders behind and to embrace the world as it is now, joining the Transfigured in celebrating a new condition, an entirely new way of living and thriving as a part of this peculiar world rather than against it.

In long? …
It’s kind of strange to see some of the anti-80s backlash against this in the face of so much nostalgia for that time, but everything wears out its welcome, and everyone’s tolerance and memory will be unique. Not everyone loved the 80s, I guess, and personal tastes are a wholly legitimate entitlement.

That said, if you haven’t OD’ed on 80s, this little rogue-like will be a fun distraction. While post apocalyptic settings, and even specifically alternate Cold War history settings are nothing new, the big kid on the block with that is of course Fallout, with a completely different aesthetic. This changes out 50s corn for 80s cheese, and that changes everything, other than the whole, post-post apocalypse bit.

The mechanics are an interesting variant on the idea of “leveling up” and are so much more than just “ding, +str plz!” Your poor child hero is twisted and mutated in freakish, sometimes cool, but often horrifying ways. Oh, sure, it’s definitely an immense gain in power, but suddenly little Janet’s gone full Ghost Rider. Her head flesh has completely melted off and her remaining is alight with nuclear flames. Which she then launches at her enemies. Not the flames - her actual burning skull of a head.

No, the game isn’t death metal themed, but with a level up like that, it feels like Double Fine contracted Dethklok to do some of the designs.

That said the game does want for, well… more. The bestiary is limited, at best, and the biomes are static and lack the kind of diversity a concept like this could really leverage. You have a whole three main tilesets, with two extras for internal regions, and that’s it. The mutations are interesting, to be sure, but the RNG nature of it means you can easily complete the game’s entire story without seeing many of them.

Some of the mutations, while not explicitly designed as detriments (some are) are so underwhelming that getting them is functionally a penalty. You are limited to three active or exomutations at once - mutations where you hit a button and things happen, also your character is physically, visibly warped in relation to them. Getting a peashooter instead of a bazooka is going to grossly affect your ability to play. Maybe later you can reach a swap point to change it for something else (at random, so you might be exchanging one peashooter for another). Maybe not. Maybe being stuck with Home Slice means you don’t survive to reach a point where you can get rid of it.

But, some will say that that’s just the nature of rogue-likes. Not all power ups are created equal, after all.

Character movement is fairly solid, but some of the animations can get locking when you really need to get out of the way. There can be some backtracking, but the game actually boosts your speed in areas you’ve already covered, so it’s more a minor issue than it would be in any other situation. That said, there’s no temporary invulnerability once you get hit, so it’s distressingly easy to get pinned in a tight corridor and have your heath obliterated in stupid short order.

Still, it’s a fun game. The concept is great, the art is fun, and the music is good. It’s a twisted mix revolting and gratifying to watch your hapless teen hero mutate into a horrible hideous monster with phenomenal powers of destruction. Yes, my little green Mohawk boy has sprouted centipede like tail and now lays eggs which immediately hatch into insectoid horrors bearing his face. He is forever marred as a disgusting freak unto his fellow men, a monster populating the world with other monsters. But… those horrors devour and lay waste to his enemies with a ravenous fury. He (she?) is the mother of crawling destruction born from the cloaca gifted him (her?) by the fruits of the world’s destruction.

So it all balances out.

Really, the biggest problem with the game is that there just… isn’t enough of it. The levels definitely could have used another three or four biome sets to fight back the fatigue that comes from running them over and over and over and over. But if the biggest problem with a game is that it needs more of the game, and not that it needs to be fundamentally different, then you know the game itself is doing things right.

7/10 - Give it a try.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRAD is a game that offers a lot that has already been offered but that's ok because it makes it's own twist in how enjoyable it is.

This games takes a lot of stuff out of other indie games like Nuclear Throne, TBOI, Enter The Gungeon, Dead Cells does taking stuff out of other games makes it a copy cat? In this case I would have to disagree because it feels refreshing even when you played all those games listed above for example you have the progress by mutation taken from Nuclear Throne, you have the wacky look from TBOI the more powerful you are, you have the rolls, cartoonish enemies and ranged combat of Gungeon, the speed when you're on a kill streak or getting more loot when you kill multiple enemies with a single hit that Dead Cells offers.

Gameplay and progress system is weird being completly honest I don't quite like it but that's just me, one thing that you should realize is that the game seems far more shorter in runs that it really is.

You should buy this game if it's your first time in the roguelike genre because it's a mix of a lot of them and it's not too complex like TBOI or Gungeon. If you've played some in the past I would think if you liked Nuclear Throne and Dead Cells if you did then go ahead and buy it if not I think this game would not be of your enjoyment\t\t\t\t\t
