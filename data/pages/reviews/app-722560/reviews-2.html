the art, music, and setting is pretty neat. by dribbling out the lore and having some kinda sneaky progression mechanics built in it doesn't feel like the same game over and over again and your success is dictated by chance. your current run can be used to develop your next run.

it still has all the problems of being a procedurally generated roguelike... but it isn't as tedious or punishing as what i've learned to expect from the genre. if you're gonna have one of those in your library this is a good one. i haven't beaten the last boss yet, but that's coming soon and i think i'll occasionally boot this up for years to come.


this is an imdb-style 7.1 : 10 in a genre that never scores very high.

Pros:
- Colours, sounds and music are absolutely amazing! The game captures a retro vibe in a great way, creating amazing atmosphere! Strongest aspect of the game
- Good sense of humour - chilled vibe
- QoL when backtracking - walking leaves a flower trail, which gives you a small speed boost when walking on it

Cons:
- Long runs (more than 1 hour to beat the final boss). They also feel long, as the gameplay is not that fast
- The saturation of colours might be too much sometimes, which hurts my eyes
- History is "meh". No motivation to fully finish the game (need to beat the final boss 9 times for that)\t\t\t\t\t

Huge Disappointment

"Hey, it's not as bad as when I first played it!", I thought to myself. Well, half an hour went by... and it most certainly is now.

RAD is such a great little concept on paper. It's videogamey, rad and filled to the brim with variety and personality. However, it is NONE of that where it truly matters: the gameplay.

-
Okay, so what's wrong with the gameplay? And how bad is it?

Well, it's pretty bad.

It starts out interesting, mind you. The long, barren lands in the levels seem nice visually... until you're aware of just how much time you spend in each run doing nothing and how disgustingly repetitive the "many objectives" design choice here is (because remember: you're always going to be doing them. Every run. And too many mandatory fixed objectives sprawled across an empty, unsatisfactory map is a letdown.). The combat (and enemies) are ehn, at least... until they're (faster than you might expect) not anymore. Another problem present is that there isn't enough variety in each run to even warrant calling this a proper 'roguelike' much. They're more like... occasional, novel add-ons.

The whole level design really is amateurish at best coming from Double Fine, and it's not the lukewarm kind you'd expect from some 'indies' releases. It's just bad. Call it being spoiled by other better, superior roguelikes. Call it being "too harsh", I don't care. It really does suck. The presentation is fantastic too, so it pains me that the game ended the way it did.
-


Skip. I'm glad I barely paid for this game. Deserves a dollar, barely. And certainly doesn't deserve much of your time. At the very least, it's a good example of talented presentation, so go for it (in a sale) if you'd want to inspect that ruined by the game more closely.\t\t\t\t\t
