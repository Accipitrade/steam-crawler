\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's fun to hate on Destiny, and Lord knows I've done it. Viewed cynically, it's an endless, repetitive, time-wasting, soul-sucking grind that's best enjoyed with friends, and I have very few. It has robbed me of 1500+ hours of my life over the last 6 years, and I regret a good portion of those.

And yet.... while most of that has some truth to it, Destiny does some truly remarkable things as a video game that no other shooter has ever come close to matching.

For starters, this has the best-feeling movement and gunplay of any FPS I have ever played, both on console and PC. It is also the prettiest - Bungie's artists have crafted beautifully serene and alien environments that can transform into crushing psychedelic displays of violent color whilst in combat, regularly rivaling any big-budget setpiece in your typical shooter campaign. Supported by world-class audio design and a riveting orchestral score, Destiny's presentation is so good that I still haven't gotten bored of it after a paltry 62 full days of my life.

Its unrivaled raid encounters have me thinking mechanics and teamwork in a way that rewards concentration and communication, and are absolutely worth experiencing for any shooter fans. Raids require solving puzzles as a group, platforming through epic chasms, calling out symbols and debuffs, assigning roles and switching them on the fly, and improvising when s__t hits the fan. During boss fights, usually a complex sequence of mechanics leads to a damage phase, where your entire group joins together in a cathartic release of the hardest-hitting guns you have on your person, buffed by all kinds of space magic as you tear down a demon the size of a skyscraper. It's phenomenal stuff.

Destiny's world is dense with lore and story, and while each individual campaign tends towards the short side, there are now 5 of them, plus countless quests to complete that often offer additional cutscenes, locations, and content. These aren't Shakespeare, but they're more of an excuse to guide you through the gorgeous locations. Any player curious about Destiny should know that, although these campaigns have recently been made harder to find by the new free-to-play structure, they are still readily available and serve as a great 20-30 hour introduction to Destiny's systems. And since the first 3 are free, there is very little reason not to try.

Since endgame vendors are everywhere in the social space you quickly gain access to, it's easy to get overwhelmed and decide this isn't the game for you. Don't worry. You can come to grips with Destiny on your own terms, and the campaigns and their associated quests will give you more than enough to do before even considering engaging with all of the later stages of content, which, like in any MMO, is where most of the optional grind is found.

The onboarding experience for new players has already been cited by the developers as something that has problems and will improve in the near future. This may lead to beginners meandering through random activities and unsure of how to progress, which I can't imagine is fun. My best advice is for players to become well-acquainted with their quest logs - focusing on quests and bounties ensures steady progress no matter what.

While intimidating for beginners, the upside is that there is never, EVER a shortage of things to do in Destiny. While most "hardcore" players stick around for incremental stat upgrades on their gear and levels, replaying the same activities over and over to pull the slot machine lever for marginally better rolls, this MMO-style of progression is Destiny at its worst, and I can't be bothered with it. Fortunately, even players like myself (and maybe you) who prefer things to do (content) over things to chase (gear) will have their hands full. It doesn't take hundreds of hours to access some of the game's best loot, and unlike other looter-shooters such as Warframe, there is no content hidden behind an enormous time investment. Leveling in Destiny is quick, and after playing the campaigns and a handful of quests, you will have access to the vast majority of activities and content in the game. From there it's up to you to decide what interests you. In this way, Destiny 2 is fun to both casual and hardcore players, and it is one of its strongest features.

Some further details about gear here. Many of the best weapons have fixed rolls, meaning that there's only one version of the gun to obtain. This makes it easy for new players to access really strong weapons by focusing their attention on exotic quests, which often involve secrets, puzzles, and all kinds of unique mechanics. After you've experienced the storylines, these gear quests are a good way to keep the game going, letting you become more powerful without making you feel like you're at a casino. The armor, meanwhile, is now catered to nerds. The "Armor 2.0" system that Bungie has created can give invested players lots of RPG-like ways to improve their stats, which takes a lot of time and planning. But here's the thing - none of it is necessary. If you don't care about gear, you will lose a bit of efficiency perhaps, but nowhere near enough to struggle with any of Destiny's unique content.

Because they are so plentiful, you will usually work on several objectives at once, making any activity feel like it's rewarding you for your time. Some repetition is inevitable, but a careful player can bypass most of Destiny's grind and get at the heart of what makes it great, which is the approach I recommend to everybody who values a balanced lifestyle. And even when you start to see some of the same things again, Destiny's gameplay remains so tight, varied, and engaging that it hardly matters.

Bungie once famously talked about the concept of "30 seconds of fun" - that all video games can be boiled down to a 30-second gameplay loop, and the most successful ones will hide that fact in the most varied ways. Destiny's 30 seconds are excellent. It's a game that transforms shooting aliens in the face into infinite permutations of fun, from dozens of activity types to hundreds of gun stats and perks to give each weapon a different feel, supported by thousands of achievements and quests, spread out from the most casual material farming to the sweatiest PvP matches. It's a game with limitless potential, hampered only by its quiet hope of owning all of your free time and gating its more cosmetically-appealing gear behind a paywall. (Pro tip: don't bother.)

You can have fun with Destiny in a healthy way and still find plenty of time for other games and activities in life. I speak as a player who made those typical MMO mistakes before, treating Destiny as a priority and suffering through hours of grinding that felt more like a job than a fun activity. Although the game is far less grindy than it was in its 2014 origins, even today I catch myself going down rabbit holes I could safely ignore all because I wanted That Gun I Probably Won't Even Use. So although I recommend this game, I do so cautiously. If you are a completionist like myself, someone prone to becoming obsessed with projects until they're fully accomplished, this is a game you need to be careful with. If you have any doubts.... stay away.

Despite my overstuffed Steam library and embarrassing backlog of unplayed games, I still find myself coming back to Destiny over and over again. Six years on, I still can't tell if that's a testament to Destiny's quality or an indictment of its psychological design. Most likely it's a bit of both. But no matter how many faults Destiny has, its perfect gunplay, beautiful visuals, and plethora of unique mechanics easily make it one of my all-time favorite games.\t\t\t\t\t
