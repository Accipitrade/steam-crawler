Quick disclaimer: While Steam only has around 95 hours of my total playtime recorded, I had many more hours put into it through Battle.net when Bungie was partnered with Activision. Please do not let my lack of hours here be regarded as a lack of gameplay experience.

As a separate disclaimer, this review will contain spoilers, both of the story and of some of the lore. Be advised when reading.

A Review of Destiny 2

The last time I remember playing Destiny regularly was in November 2019; in mid-May I decided to pick to back up again to see if there were any changes. I was disappointed to find that the seasonal events came and went with each season, and that there really wasn’t anything of substance that was new. Regardless, it had been a while since I played all of the old content, and I perfectly fine with doing that for a bit. That was about the time when the news of Destiny’s future changes were announced, along with the game’s upcoming fall expansion. I’m specifically referring to the removal of content to the Destiny Content Vault, and the sunsetting of certain weapons in the game. Before I begin on this, I want to make it perfectly clear that I am very much aware of the rights a developer has in regards to digital licenses and the control they have over their products. I am also aware of the terms and conditions I accepted with the game’s EULA that include changes to the game and the possibility of access to certain content being revoked at any time. However, there comes a certain point where a developer, or any authority with such broad discretionary power, can cross the line into abusing their power, and Bungie has crossed this line. Allow me to explain with a legal comparison.

In Kelo v. City of New London (2005), the Supreme Court found that a legislature can take private property using its eminent domain powers under the Takings Clause of the Constitution if the reason for the property’s taking is for a “public purpose,” such as economic development. Despite having this broad discretion, legislatures tend to shy away from excessive invocations of this power; if it didn't, we would certainly call it a tyranny and an abuse of power, even though it’s a power the government legally possesses. In much the same way, while Bungie technically has the discretionary power to add or remove game content as it wishes, what it did in the case of the Beyond Light expansion was excessive, arbitrary, and capricious. It wasn’t the simple removal of some content within the game; instead, it was a removal of the vast majority of the game’s content. What’s more, is that they removed content that had been purchased by players such as myself. I paid for the Warmind and Curse of Osiris expansions and all of the content associated with them, including strikes, missions, and locations; the Annual Pass that brought the forges, Gambit Prime, the Menagerie; and they have announced other content that we have paid for will be cycled out in the coming years. All of this begs a simple question: why would I pay for content that will eventually be removed from the game? The answer is just as simple: I won’t. I cannot, in good conscience, pay into such a practice, nor can I recommend Destiny 2 as a purchase worth anyone else’s investment.

Not only has Bungie removed content that one has paid for, but the amount of content they removed makes up nearly half of the game. Beyond Light certainly didn’t replace this lost content, so we are at an even bigger conundrum: the majority of the game’s content was wiped without any kind of replacement. Most of the campaigns are gone, many strikes, raids, locations and activities associated with those locations are gone, and Beyond Light didn’t replace them. While it has been noted by the community that Destiny's pool of content hasn't substantially changed over the past several months/years, wiping away over half of that content wasn’t the right direction or a plausible solution for these issues. If anything, it makes these problems more apparent. If Bungie’s resources are indeed stretched so thin that they cannot keep up with content creation in a way that appeases the community, then I think the emaciated game we have now is a fixture of Destiny will be moving forward. Bungie has simply not left anyone with the impression the skeletal game we are left with will be padded out with vast quantities of quality content in the future. Finally, calling what is now the base game free-to-play is almost as insulting as removing content that has been paid for, as nearly all of the free-to-play content was wiped from the game in its newest expansion. All of this amounts to among the most disgusting practices I have seen in the gaming industry, and this industry is wrought to the core with disgusting practices.

If Bungie’s actions were limited to just removing content I have paid for, I might still have given Destiny a final chance to convince me that it’s worth my time. Bungie’s mistakes were twofold, however, with the sunsetting of the game’s best and most popular non-exotic weapons. It is at this point where Destiny has lost me entirely. First, Bungie is telling me what content they want me to play out all of the content that has been released and paid for in this game. Now, Bungie is telling me how to play, by artificially obsoleting most of the game’s most useful weapons and replacing them with a pool of lesser weapons, both in quantity and quality. Indeed, as with the content removal, Bungie added far fewer weapons to replace those they are sunsetting. And, these new weapons are objectively worse than the weapons they are replacing, which creates a less fun gaming experience. It also creates an arbitrary grind to replace one’s weapon library. I’ll admit it, I have certain and specific weapons I like to play with in the game. Sunsetting those weapons isn’t going to cause me to “branch out and try new things” within the game, as I've arrived at my preferred weapons out of having already tried many of the others. Instead, I’ll branch out and try a new game that doesn’t arbitrarily force me play a certain way, for no other reason than being unable to churn quality that can properly compete with those that are considered best.

Destiny 2: Conclusion

Destiny 2 is a reflection of the gaming industry as a whole. It continues to be unfinished and unpolished, and now it has an arbitrary grind (whether through the story-stopping bounties of Shadowkeep, or new weapons to replace the sunset ones). Destiny 2 is not a unique game and it is not an interesting one; it doesn’t go in new directions, it doesn’t tread new ground, and there is nothing about it that makes it watershed. Instead, it is a perfect reflection of the filth, the garbage, and the ugliness of trends within the gaming industry as a whole. I greatly enjoyed my first experiences within Destiny 2. I was induced to spend money on that good experience and I was not disappointed with it, at least, not initially. However, as time has gone on, and Bungie has introduced more and more practices that I disagree with, Destiny 2 has become a game I cannot, in good conscience, recommend to anyone. Stay away from this game, and the kind of toxic practices Bungie is espousing. If they are indeed listening, as they always say they are, then the Destiny franchise must be steered in a radically different direction- one that prioritizes the player rather than the coffer. Regardless of whether this happens, I will not be around to find out, as Bungie has burned through my last reserve of patience.\t\t\t\t\t
