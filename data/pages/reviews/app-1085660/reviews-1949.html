\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSome background: I played Destiny 1 religiously and have played Destiny 2 since the beta, with some periodic lapses. I love the franchise, but try to maintain objectivity as best I can. Take what follows for what it's worth. I'll warn you: this is a long review.

TL;DR: Core gameplay mechanics keep me coming back. The game looks and feels great. It's as complex or as straightforward as you want it to be. The grind can be difficult to stomach sometimes. Finding others to play with is not always easy for certain activities. Developers mean well, but can overreact or misunderstand fundamental issues the community raises. There's a little something for everyone and in spite of it's flaws, once it gets its hooks into you it doesn't let go.

The Good:

The audio and visuals are, in a word, wonderful. Sound design is exquisite and the music will give you goosebumps from time to time. Visually, it's a beautiful game. Weapon models are and largely unique from one another, armor is generally very attractive with a few misfires here and there. PvE environments are thoughtfully created and lovely to look at. You can stand in the game's social area and watch the sun rise over distant mountains, or travel to Jupiter's moon, Titan and see enormous waves building beneath you. There are 5 enemy races, each with their own distinctive environments and unique characteristics. If you're on a PC capable of getting close to max settings, you won't be disappointed even though the game is a few years old at this point.

The core gameplay mechanics - shooting weapons and moving around the map - are the best I've ever experienced in a shooter. Nowhere else have I found such a satisfying combination of weapon feel and fluid movement. The various weapons do truly have a unique "feel". Maybe you'll like 140rpm hand cannons. Maybe you'll prefer 450rpm pulse rifles. The game certainly has meta loadouts for both PvE and PvP, but I've found that you can be successful with whatever you like best, as long as your map awareness and movement is on-point. And oh the movement... it's really a joy. Titans feel stout. Warlocks feel graceful. Hunters feel nimble. In my view, each class's personality is felt most keenly through movement and the respective class abilities more than the skill trees/Supers available to them.

The gear is interesting and varied. Most weapons have randomly rolled perks attached to them. Maybe you'll get the perfect Dire Promise hand cannon the first time, maybe you'll never get one, but you'll always come back to keep trying. Armor benefits greatly from the modification system that allows you to tailor your gear to a certain situation. There's a lot of choice involved. The rarest ("Exotic", in-game) weapons and armor have static perk rolls and unique properties that, as the name implies, sets them apart. It's generally a good mix, though you may find yourself frustrated from time to time when you can't seem to get the drop you're looking for no matter how many times you do an activity. The grind is real, but I personally don't find it too cumbersome.

More on that: the weapon perks in particular are really interesting. Each non-exotic weapon has a static intrinsic perk dictated by the type of weapon it is, a randomized sight or barrel option, a randomized magazine perk, and a randomized set of two trait perks. There are perks that buff the damage you do or your reload speed. There are perks that allow you to do more damage with your melee attack following a shot. There are perks that deal more damage to specific types of enemies or make the weapon excellent for a particular use case. The combinations of the perks on your weapon can add up to make it outstanding when it might be just very good with a different perk set. Chasing that perfect roll is, in many ways, the endgame of Destiny 2.

There's a little something for everyone in Destiny 2. PvE-oriented? Min/max your character for success in nightfall strikes and raids. PvP sweatlord? There's a robust community of PvP-only players to scrim against and a suite of weapons/perks that are very much geared toward PvP for you to chase. Somewhere in between? Jump into PvP when the mood takes you to get some gear, then run some public PvE events and a few strikes. Completionist? Work to finish all the tasks to complete seals and earn in-game titles.

The lore in this game is phenomenal, but it doesn't beat you over the head. If you want to play this game with only shooting things in the face in mind, then you're more than welcome to do that. However, if you're inclined to look a little deeper you'll find a really fascinating set of stories behind individual weapons, events, NPCs, and enemy races, to name a few.

Destiny 2 has a dedicated and vocal community. There are people who absolutely live for this game, sinking thousands of hours into it. The developers are interactive and respond to feedback (admittedly not always choosing the best course of action), but they mean well and do the best they can to keep things interesting and engaging in-game. There's some negativity, of course, but more often than not it's born out of love for the franchise.

The bad:

I'll first expand a bit more on my comments about the developers, Bungie. Their intentions are good, but they often come across as tone-deaf, or take actions that bring to mind using a flamethrower to light a match. It's a sprawling, complex game that's difficult to balance, but often the first response to something that proves to be overpowered is to nerf it into the ground. It often feels as if Bungie - despite their statements on the subject to the contrary - have an intended way to play in mind and will disable or nerf anything that doesn't comply to that vision. That can be a good or a bad thing, depending on your perspective, but given that this is ultimately a space-fantasy game in which you assume the persona of an immortal demi-god of sorts I don't feel there's really such a thing as too overpowered.

Bugs and connection issues. This is an online game and particularly in the current season disconnects have been a major problem. Bungie lacks dedicated servers, instead going with a peer-to-peer model and it shows. I've been kicked from the game or an activity as many as six times in an hour of trying to play. Bugged quests are more of an annoyance than a real problem, but it's really frustrating when you have to google a workaround to advance or start a quest because the quest-giver won't accept an item from you or won't give you the quest in the first place.

The seasonal model that's currently being employed is simply not yielding interesting content. There are a lot of activities that feel like pointless busywork; run an errand for so-and-so, do X number of that event, kill Y number of enemies with a certain weapon. It gets tiresome quickly and some players have walked away at the time I'm writing this to wait for a big expansion that shakes things up and makes your actions feel meaningful. Really, the endgame is maximizing your potential via gear upgrades, min/maxing and becoming more skilled. That begs a question, however: What are we doing all this for? I think that's a question a lot of people are struggling with right now, but on the bright side with this franchise that feeling tends to be temporary. A big DLC is expected in the fall and should inject some new life into the game.

Matchmaking is another area where there's room for improvement. The most challenging activities do not have automated matchmaking, meaning you have to either have a ready-made group or find one. The game doesn't have a built-in system for doing so, requiring third-parties to make those connections (LFG Discord, for example). For a game of this kind that feels like an oversight.

Summary:
I haven't found another "MMO-lite" that's grabbed me like this. It has shortcomings, yes, but I think new and dedicated players alike will discover something to enjoy.\t\t\t\t\t
