_______________________________________________________________________
Completed all the year one story content (which by itself is a massive improvement over the first game's), and got through most of Shadowkeep before seasonal activities completely derailed those efforts.

Eats up my time like a Bethesda game, which is neat.

Splitting from Activision has clearly allowed Bungie to chase their dreams with this game, and boy it works. Except for when you hit a Local File Validation error in the middle of a firefight and get kicked out. This is an error that's affected the PC version since launch and it's still around, still unfixed.

The File Validation Error is annoying because it shouldn't be there by this point. We're on year 2, and going into 3, and it's still there. It costs us time because we have to rerun Local File Validation every time it happens.

And yet, despite all that... 245 hours at the time of writing this review.

Not bad.

Destiny 2 is super fun most of the time, aggravating some of the time, but I think it's worth the money for the expansions all of the time.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR: Only Rec if you know what you're getting into. See the Edit at the bottom.

Your enjoyment of Destiny 2 is tied directly to how you play games. If you're obsessed with collecting every item ever, completing every quest and triumph, and getting every achievement, you're going to have a really bad time, and you really need to take a step back from the monitor if your crowning achievements are collecting every item and leveling every class in MMO/OARPG games. And I don't mean that to be in a derogatory way; I'm very proud of some of the things I have on save files in my gaming career. But at some point you need some separation. If you can recognize the fact that getting literally everything is highly improbable, and it's a world built to enjoy instead of micromanage, you'll have a great time. Play at your own pace, and just enjoy the journey. You can get meta without having to be a die-hard-24/7 player.

_________________________

This is going to be a bit of a long one.

Destiny 2, is, in many ways, a husk of its former self. Destiny 1 had actual perk selection, not static subclasses. Fun activities that remained fun once you hit the light level cap. A good spread of exotics that were useful. A feeling of power. Hitting T12 stats for the spread you wanted felt excellent, and for some reason, much more worth it than the current Mods 2.0 system where you pretty much just want one thing, and have to farm for the element affinity to have the proper mod slot with the proper stat spread from the armor set and.... you get the idea.

But... see, that's the problem at its core that Destiny 2 suffers from. People want to play this game like a homework assignment, like a second job, like a literal new MMO. Almost every single problem that you'll see people spouting about Destiny 2, they're coming from the place of people who do pretty much nothing else but play this game. And that's fine, if that's how you choose to play the game. That's how I played Destiny 1; most every other game took a backseat to mindlessly killing enemies in some patrol zone, or doing a raid with my friends.

However, as a nearing-30 person with a lot of things on the plate and a full time job with a normal schedule and standard obligations and no longer an interest in complete and utter grind-fests (looking at you FFXIV), I appreciate the direction that Bungie has taken with the core gameplay, loot, and rewards system. After picking the game back up during Forsaken, having not played since Curse of Osiris, I had no issues casually climbing back up the power ladder, devoting time when I felt like it. And after putting the game down again and coming back during Shadowkeep, I had an even easier time hitting the baseline power cap of 750 or whatever it was at the time. Or, at the very least, getting a few points away from it.

If there's one main gripe I personally have against D2, it's the quests. The quest design leaves a lot to be desired. 90% of the time, it's more of the same old Destiny things: Kill X enemies, do X things, do X activities, rinse and repeat, which does get monotonous and really needs a good shaking up. But that's MMO quest design in a nutshell.

The Silver store is a bit obnoxious, but if you don't want to buy it, don't buy it. And I don't appreciate their recent adoption of the Blizzard "'Nam" content delivery style for the seasons (you had to be there, man!), but hey, that's really not in my control, and I have better things to worry about, especially considering, special event quests aside, the seasonal quests for catalysts, shaders, and weapons don't ever really go away, and you don't lose progress. Before switching to PC D2 I actually finally finished the strike quests for the Loaded Question and Oxygen SR-3, and those are... if memory serves, Year 1 quests.

Is it perfect? Far from it. Shadowkeep kinda... destroyed the mission structure, and doesn't even really broadcast that you can pick up the Vanilla/Curse/Warmind campaigns from Amanda Holliday, which is a huge misstep.

Is it better than D1? *OPINION* No, the writing for vanilla and Curse of Osiris is eye-rolling at its worst, and chuckle-worthy at its best; its largest disservice is the Borderlands-2-ing of several characters. Assassination through reduction, resulting in token group dynamic roles.

Is it a good time with some friends, and a good time solo? Yup. The worlds are gorgeous, the gameplay is tight, the guns feel great, and the sound design is second to none. It pains me to admit, but Bungie is taking the right steps with D2. Granted, they did the same thing in 1 with The Taken King and carpet bombed it with vanilla D2, regressing horribly, but as it sits right now, this isn't vanilla D2. The bad blood of Vanilla D2 doesn't exist anymore.

And despite all of its glaring flaws that I'll gladly recognize and rail on in group chats, I love this game. Because, despite all of the bad, it's a beautiful world with interesting weapons and armors and some of the tightest, if not the tightest, gunplay mechanics around. It's a game that was truly kept afloat by and built by the community who stuck with it in D1, when every new online game was "THE DESTINY KILLER". And yet, here it still is, and here we still are. Just like Homer, our child may be rambunctious, make mistakes, and do very stupid things sometimes, but it's still our child, and we still love it. And now that it's on PC, there's a whole new host of people who have the chance to try this truly bafflingly wonderful game.

I hate that I love you, Destiny, but I love you.

Just play it. It's free to play. What do you really have to lose?


EDIT: Review update post-"vaulting" policy.

I have extremely mixed opinions about D2 these days.

For myself, I've all but left the game behind. While I don't necessarily /mind/ the concept of vaulting content, I disagree extremely with the fact that versions of the game with DLC that is no longer accessible, or will only be accessible for a short amount of time before it's locked away in the "Content Vault", is very predatory and honestly, inexcusable.

I don't know if I can recommend Destiny with the same bit of precaution I would have when this review was written. At this point, it's somewhat become a "play at your own risk" type deal, because there's no real guarantee what you'll have and for how long you'll have it. Bungie lays down roadmaps, sure, and is decent about letting people know when content has expired. And while I understand the need to keep the file size down, and the overall bloat down... I don't know. D2 just leaves a bad taste in my mouth whenever I think about it any more. It's definitely not the D1 I fell in love with, and the D2 I loved with a chip on my shoulder.

I genuinely don't know where I fall on this game anymore. There's fun to be had and the gameplay is as tight as ever. The recent updates to core gameplay are absolutely excellent, but the overall issues still remain.

I'm going to leave this as a rec, but only if you fully know what you're getting into.\t\t\t\t\t
