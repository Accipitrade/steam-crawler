\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a tough decision, but given the totality of the game's state right now, gotta say I can't honestly recommend the game.

Note: Have played on/off since PS4 release. Game time only accounts for PC play.

TL,DR:
If you are a solo player , do not buy this game .
If you don't like PvP , do not buy this game .
If you have a full-time job or a family , do not buy this game .
If you want a fair return on investment , do not buy this game .

Main issues:

-You cannot experience the entire game without having 5+ other people who also play regularly with you.

-An increasing portion of the content is being released without matchmaking.

-The game ties some PvE progression to PvP performance.

-PvP "content" is often used as a development crutch during content droughts.

-In-game economy is often designed to drain bloated currency stacks of the few, not make the game more enjoyable.

-New "content" no longer adds to the game world, but drops off of the game each season.

-For PvP, there is a notable issue of cheaters in top-tier PvP activities.

-The battle pass analogue in D2 seems designed for failure and to double dip into the customer's wallet.

Now, more details on each of the issues:

"You cannot experience the entire game without having 5+ other people who also play regularly with you."

The Raids in the Destiny franchise are some of the best gameplay (and increasingly, lore/story) content in the game. Despite this, only ~20% (the last solid figure I've seen) of players have apparently experienced them in D2. Instead of increasing accessibility and enjoyment for these activities, the devs have created even more PvE (and PvP) activities that have no matchmaking system. There is less and less of a reason for anyone that doesn't BYOF (Bring Your Own Friends/Bring Your Own Fun) to invest time or money into the game.

"An increasing portion of the content is being released without matchmaking."

This one is an issue that feeds the prior issue. After doing D1 raids, D2 raids, and D2 dungeons, I am confident that these activities could be matchmade. One of the only hurdles, and another issue unto itself, is that the raids and some of these other activities have a nonsensical lack of in-game direction. I don't know if it is an attempt at creating illusory difficulty or something else, but it stands out to me as feeling unfinished, like alpha gameplay or an abandoned asset-flip.

The outcome of this is that people wait for streamers or clans to finish the raid (usually a short wait), watch a guide online to memorize the stages instead of playing "Guess what the developers were thinking", and pressure anyone without a stable raid group out of LFG. Instead of making the game fun, it just incentivizes playing the game more like a job.

"The game ties some PvE progression to PvP performance."

This is mainly an issue for exotic gun quests, but pops up elsewhere occasionally and is an offshoot of other issues. While you get most exotic quests from PvE activities, many of them force players to participate in both PvE and PvP content. While I have played through much of this forced PvP, it isn't something I enjoy. Funny enough, there is a better bounty system in the current season, that give branched paths to completion through either PvE or PvP. Sadly this has not been implemented on a grander scale or retroactively applied to the aforementioned forced crossover quests I was discussing.

"PvP 'content' is often used as a development crutch during content droughts."

This was an huge issue with D1 leading up to the Rise of Iron expansion, and I think the devs are trying to avoid that in D2, but I don't believe I have seen a balance of PvE and PvP content released throughout the years. As a PvE focused player, this hasn't had too great of an effect on me until the current season, which is now focused on PvP content. Looking back from this perspective, I now feel I can see that the devs have not been able to release meaningful content to players across both PvE and PvP activities evenly from release to now.

"In-game economy is often designed to drain bloated currency stacks of the few, not make the game more enjoyable."

This has been a consistent issue across the life of the franchise, and the lack of meaningful response to player feedback has often just fueled player frustration with the devs. The balance of currency costs vs. currency accrual has always seemed biased towards the anomalous players: those who can play endlessly or get paid to play the game. Instead of enabling meaningful progression for the average player, the devs seem to be desperate to balance grind for those few at the far end of the bell curve. This is not an issue unique to Destiny, but it is ever present as the devs tweak the in-game economy.

"New 'content' no longer adds to the game world, but drops off of the game each season."

This is a complex issue, but the outcome is plain to see: the purchased seasonal "content" is mostly culled from the game after each season ends. Guns and gear remain, while the actual activities are removed with no known return cycle. The main problem I have with this is that the seasonal content is generally solo friendly, unlike a lot of the other content. Since it is removed, the game world and player experience is never really growing. If the rest of the game weren't so exclusionary to ~80% of players, that might not be such an issue. Instead, it is the sliver of content that evaporates all too soon.

"For PvP, there is a notable issue of cheaters in top-tier PvP activities."

Not much to say here since I'm not a PvP focused player. The current seasonal highlight is "Trials of Osiris", a PvP activity that also lacks matchmaking. On top of that, the activity is a hard progression gate to in-game lore and gear. There is no PvE progression path to that lore or gear. To top it all off, those that are willing/able to partake in that PvP activity are often reporting frequent run-ins with cheaters that completely ruin and trivialize the game mode.

"The battle pass analogue in D2 seems designed for failure and to double dip into the customer's wallet."

This is a big gripe of mine. I have played many games that have adopted the increasingly popular battle pass model of pseudo-subscription. I think it is a good idea for D2, but their implementation is problematic. From my experience, it fosters burnout before the half-way point of progression is reached. More insidiously, the devs have added the ability to purchase levels of the pass, even if you've already paid for the pass. "Fear of missing out" has been cited as a reason others I know have left the game, often in reference to this mechanic. I find it strange that paying customers seem to have such difficulty actually getting the content they paid for without paying a second, scaling amount again. Buying the pass should allow you to progress to completion at any point. The current system seems predatory and is not worth the price to most players (unless they're being paid to play).

Conclusion:
I didn't bring up the positives mainly because I think the negatives outweigh them currently, hence the fact that I can't honestly recommend the game to the demographics I mentioned up top. That said, the game has many positives. If these issues ever are overcome, I would likely recommend it. Until significant changes, I would recommend holding off though.\t\t\t\t\t
