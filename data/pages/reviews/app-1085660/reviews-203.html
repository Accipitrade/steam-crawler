\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDestiny 2 (there shouldn't have ever been a sequel, but that's just one of the dozens of problems plaguing this franchise) is a hard game to recommend despite how satisfying its core gameplay is.

As far as I'm concerned, Destiny has some of the tightest FPS mechanics on the market. Nothing else plays like it. It is fine-tuned and for a game where the majority of your time is spent shooting things, the fact that it feels so good is a huge positive in its favor. It's everything surrounding that shooting that leaves a sour taste.

Bungie refuses to address obvious problems that have been festering in Destiny's bones since its inception. Power creep, sunsetting, player on-boarding, content removal, repetitive content, bounty integration, microtransactions, pvp, map rotation/creation, garbage loot, an unhealthy obsession with RNG, more RNG, the obscuring of basic mechanics and builds, the distribution of mods, the drip-feeding of content, seasonal frameworking, fixing features only to backtrack and break them again, and I could keep going. I can't get into the specifics of each of these because there isn't enough room in a steam review to even begin, but I have a feeling that anyone who has spent a significant amount of time with Bungie's creation will know what I'm talking about.

It's hard to reconcile a game with great core mechanics and stellar art direction, some of the best I've ever seen, with the growing pile of refuse that clutters every other facet of the experience. Destiny isn't really a game you play for fun---there is fun to be had, but it feels incidental to the design logic dictating most of the experience; there will come a point where playing Destiny feels like an obligation, hand-crafted around the idea of player retention and FOMO; in many ways it can begin to feel like a second job you're paying to work at.

I've been playing Destiny pretty much since it came out, and it's frustrating to watch a game with so much potential flounder in mediocrity and greed. Basic mechanics and features don't exist for non-sensical reasons, from inventory limitations, to shader and ornament implementation, to the removal of the Red War, champion mods dictating how you'll play a season and with what weapons, an ever-shifting and broken meta, rampant cheating and so on. What this game needs more than content are quality of life improvements focusing on the rotting foundation (barely) holding everything together, but that clearly isn't a priority for Bungie, and I don't think it ever will be.

The story is...well, it exists, I guess. You'll hear people say that it's gotten so much better (and frankly, it couldn't have gotten much worse) season over season, but honestly, it's about on par with a soap-opera. If you've ever come across a generic story where light and dark literally and metaphorically clash with all of the dramatic tension and maturity that a T for Teen rating will allow, then you've already parsed with Destiny and its unfortunate sequel. It's a game whose story forces you to commit genocide against a faction of space spiders, then humanizes them, forces you to interact with their family drama, and then continue on with the killing. There is no real story, no themes, no messages. There are pockets of dialogue and ideas which exist only as means to incentivize the shooting beyond loot, but it all falls pretty flat after the hundredth strike, season activity, or raid. The lore is surprisingly good, but it doesn't ever make its way into the story in any meaningful way, and it never will; that would be antithetical to the live service. The story of Destiny will conclude with a financial decision regarding server upkeep; nothing really matters beyond the fiscal reality that the game exists upon; not even Cayde-6 or the Traveler; they also removed most of the Cayde-6 stuff, because of course they did.

For new players, Destiny 2 is a hard sell. All paid DLC/expansions/season passes are essentially mandatory. It costs a lot of money up front to play, and that's putting aside the massive time investment and learning curve. I don't know how they expect the community to keep growing with rampant burnout spreading through the player base while simultaneously erecting a massive barrier to entry. It's doing okay for now, but nothing lasts forever, and with Destiny 2 already existing, I wonder if Bungie won't get to a point where Destiny 3 starts to look appealing, rendering everything in this title moot just as the first installment was.

I like Destiny 2, and in all likelihood, I will continue to play it until the very end; but at the same time, I have never felt compelled to recommend it to anybody. I don't think people should waste their time or money on this game; I don't think I should either, but I'm already in this deep.
