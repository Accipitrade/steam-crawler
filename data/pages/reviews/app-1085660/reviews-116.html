Nigh-Impossible To Return To, Nigh-Impenetrable to Enter: Destiny 2 and How FOMO Can Kill a Game

FOMO, as an acronym, means 'Fear of missing out'. It is what compels players to obsessively play a game. Fear of missing out, be it on stuff that affects your progression, or on stuff that affects cosmetics. It is what drives players to spend more on microtransactions, or on lootboxes, or battle-pass progression. It is what drives players to buy boosters, to buy the items themselves. A fear of missing out on items.

And Destiny 2 runs on it for almost everything.

This is not as much a critique on Destiny 2 - It's visually nice, the gunplay is among the best in the business, and the game itself is free. This is not going to be my usual objective review, but rather, a critique on FOMO, how it can harm a game, and why a new player experience is important, using a game that has utterly failed on all three concepts.

Fear of missing out is what drives Destiny 2. Fear of missing out on content, on seasons, on expansions, on lore, on weapons and missions and challenges and everything in between. This fear drives players back into the game, even if only a few times a week, so they can keep pace.

This fear is killing Destiny. The game is impossible to enter; You're not given much of a tutorial, the new player lore introduction is missing in action, in fact the holes in the lore are big enough to fly a gunship through if you haven't been playing since the opening campaign of Destiny 1, which by the by did not see a PC release, and Bungie have worsened this problem through a practice known as sunsetting.

Sunsetting refers to making older content inaccessible. This can be done for a few reasons; Maybe the content wasn't well-liked. Maybe it wasn't stable, requiring patch after patch after patch to bring into a working state with every update. Maybe it contradicted too much of the game's tone or lore. Maybe it was seasonal. Whatever the reason, Bungie sunsetted several paid expansions and the initial campaign, 'Red War'. If you paid money for those expansions, it's gone now. There's no returning to that content, and there's no getting your money back, either.

Fear of missing out on this content can drive people back into Destiny, but in so doing, Bungie have made the lore impossible to follow along. There isn't even text blurbs in the game explaining what happened, there's no 'here's what you missed' features. If you want to know why characters and places are important, you're forced to go to third-party sources, such as Youtube channels and the wiki. If you took a break, you're left trying desperately to play catch-up. If you're new to the game, you'll have no attachment, no investment, no ability to care for these characters.

Fear of missing out can hook players in, but the hold is weak. If players burn out, they fall into a spiral of having missed too much, and in trying to re-enter, they end up smacking into a brick wall of unknown stuff with no ability to catch up without having to put in more effort. They'll end up confused, lost, and ultimately just log off with no real plan to return. If they fall behind the curve, it's almost impossible to get back into it without some serious grinding.

Fear of missing out drives players away. And it's practically the rationale behind all of Destiny 2's business practices. It's why we have 'Seasons', it's why sunsetting has gone beyond just the year one game, it's why we have timed cosmetic shops, it's why players who hate the game can't truly put it down.

It's even worse for new players; As much as I've given Warframe's new-player experience flack, and justifiably so as it barely covers the bare minimum, if I burn out on Warframe and come back in six months, I'm able to catch back up. If I burn out as a new player and come back, I can still catch up, just as well as a player who's hit the endgame. Systems don't rotate in and out. Story content remains accessible, and while there might be some gameplay tweaks, they are small. Destiny 2, by contrast, is nigh-impenetrable. The lore is a major unknown for new players, who are bombarded with pestering reminders to do all this stuff before they miss out on the content gated behind it.

Destiny 2 might have great gunplay, and it might be visually appealing, but while the core gameplay loop is decent, everything surrounding that gameplay loop is allowed to rot in service to peddling more content, to remind players on what they're missing out on, to try and force players into doing more, more, more, because if they don't do enough, they'll miss out on the content.

I cannot recommend Destiny 2. It's a game, but one that's impossible for new players to get into and become invested in, and impossible for players to take a break from without missing out on significant portions of the game as it cycles out of existence. It's a game, but it's a game that chains you to it and drags you along by the neck. It's a game, but one that has allowed FOMO to dominate it, entirely to it's detriment.\t\t\t\t\t
