\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo preface, I've been playing Destiny games since December 2014 with the release of Destiny: The Dark Below. Since then, I have played every major expansion and content release in both Destiny and Destiny 2. Over three years, I put roughly 1,474 hours in Destiny and, as of writing this review, about 1,142 hours in Destiny 2 across Xbox and PC. This review is from the perspective of a hardcore player that is very invested in the Destiny franchise. I will include a rank for core aspects of the game as well as a written explanation for said rank afterward. To reduce clutter, I sometimes refer to Destiny 2 by D2.

NOTE: This review is from the perspective of a HARDCORE PLAYER at the ENDGAME

The gameplay of Destiny 2 is almost certainly its strongest aspect. Obviously, being developed and supported by Bungie Studios has led to D2 having a lot of Halo DNA coursing through it. The quality of gunplay and movement is immediately apparent as soon as you pick up your first weapon and begin moving through the world. In my PERSONAL OPINION, D2 has the best feeling combat and gunplay of any FPS ever made. Whether it's animation, particle effects, audio, visuals, or tuning, when you're in a flow with the game it just feels effortless and incredible to play.

D2 hosts a wide variety of weapons and weapon types. These weapons are broken down into classes: primary, special, and heavy weapons. These fill out your loadout, each taking a different type of ammo. Combat involves using the weapons at your disposal and abilities granted to you by your class (Hunter, Titan, Warlock) and subclass (Solar, Void, Arc, Stasis) to defeat groups of enemies and bosses. Being a descendant of the Halo franchise, encounters with enemies in D2 often feel like a puzzle of which threats to prioritize -- which weapon or ability to use and in what combination.

Destiny 2 is a looter-shooter, and as such it boasts a wide variety of loot to earn. Loot rarity is fairly standard (Common, Uncommon, Rare, Legendary, Exotic). As opposed to a game like Borderlands, D2's loot pool is small but more curated. Each content release bring only a couple dozen new weapons and a dozen or two new armor sets. However, within those dozens of weapons and armor are random rolls; meaning, each new weapon or piece of armor can roll with a different set of scopes, barrels, magazine perks, and two different weapon perks from a larger pool.

The game also features exotic weapons and armor, which are unique pieces of gear that are special and unique among all other weapons in the game. This is because of their capacity for unique gameplay moments and kit building potential.

In my opinion, Destiny 2 comes very close to being perfect in this regard. Arenas and open-world areas -- particularly sky boxes -- are graphically impressive while still managing to cram in a lot of detail. This attention to detail is really clear in areas that show the ruins of humanity, whether its a faded advertisement or an abandoned bar.

When it comes to audio, D2 is very pleasant. Audio cues from enemies during combat are clear and readable, in addition to the satisfying sounds of operating weapons and killing enemies.

I feel like I would be doing the composers of D2 a disservice if I didn't mention their contributions. I would highly recommend going on Youtube and checking out the OST and boss themes, either on Bungie's Youtube page, or from a game-rip video.

A lot of the endgame past hitting the end of the seasonal battle pass and power cap is about tinkering with your build, completing personal PVP or PVE goals, or collecting gear and lore.

There exists in the Destiny franchise a dichotomy that I've always found surprising. Almost without exception, the big-budget storylines in Destiny games have always been fairly bland and somewhat poorly written. But the smaller, lower budget lore has always been so much more interesting and better written.

If I were to seperate the story and lore sections, the rating would look quite different. In terms of in-game, cutscene driven story, the game is mediocre at best. Bungie does seem to be getting better about implementing deep lore into the larger story and varying the ways they present it. But, Bungie still has not fully capitalized on the lore they have at their disposal. If you're looking for a great in-game story that you play through and don't have to think about too much, this game isn't for you.

Speaking of lore, this game has a lot of it. It's also the primary method of storytelling in D2 outside of the main storyline content. Like a certain game that I refuse to name in this review, D2's lore is spread across the game in various forms. Lore in D2 manifests as collectable lore books, item descriptions, quests, and the open world itself.

If you're interested in Destiny Lore and want to know more or at least have someone piece it together for you, 'My name is Byf' and 'Myelin Games' are two great Youtubers that have dedicated their channels to covering the lore of Destiny.

The game features large DLC expansions every fall on top of three month seasons that bring new content and features in small burst for the hardcore playerbase. The seasons can be bought individually or in a bundle with the yearly expansion. The seasons are not required to play any of the DLCs and vice versa. I would not recommend buying the bundle with every season unless you're sure that you're going to play D2 throughout the entire year. At the start of every year, everyone's power level is equalized so you're not penalized or disadvantaged if you want to skip one or more of the seasons in between DLCs.

D2 also features a real money store in-game called the Eververse Store. You can pay for 'silver', D2's premium currency with real money. The Eververse Store offers purely cosmetic items for your character. In my 1,142 hours with D2, I have not felt obligated to spend any real money on Eververse items.
