\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR version: Leader pool cap and completely game-breaking leader mechanics change the core game in a way that makes progressing into later stages of the game not fun.

In-depth version: This is one of those expansions that I have to seriously question what Paradox was trying to achieve here. Your leader pool cap is a very general, broad-stroke approach to this system that seriously hinders your ability to be able to do pretty much anything, both from a micro and macro perspective. In the civ I made, I start off with 5/6 leader positions filled (leader, heir, admiral, scientist, governor). The good news is that your chief scientist heads up all research being conducted in your empire instead of needing one for all three categories AND simultaneously flies around in a science vessel doing science vessel things. The bad news, though, is that this means that I can have ONE more staffed science vessel and that's it. Only two science vessels off the rip able to actually do science vessel things. Want to have two fleets flying around? Well, there goes your second science vessel unless you want to forego having a second admiral to lead your troops. Oh, you're involved in a first-contact war? Gonna need a general to lead your troops to victory? Don't have enough space in your leader pool? Sounds like a skill issue to Paradox, my gamer. And the Tradition Trees? To say that they feel useless is an understatement. Leader XP boosts? Cool, glad all SIX OF MY LEADERS ARE GONNA LEARN FASTER. There ARE good elements to this expansion, don't get me wrong, but they're completely overshadowed by the negatives. This particular expansion doesn't leave a good taste in my mouth and I can honestly say this is the first time I've had buyer's remorse with a Stellaris product.

Here's my recommendation to Paradox: either trash the leader pool cap (making this expansion completely broken and unnecessary) or remove the need to have science vessels staffed to be able to carry out their duties. Having to pick and choose when and what type of leader to recruit at any given time is frustrating enough without knowing that there's a cap to being able to manage a staff effectively. Salary/Staff caps are for sports; let the MLB worry about salary caps, ffs.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tQuick reference version: big mechanics changes that change gameplay in big ways. Lots of the complaints are from people misunderstanding the new mechanics, or are very early takes on balance changes we've yet to see pan out. Others are about changes to the UI (new nested menus clear up a lot of old clutter, but you have to re-learn menus again), or about how the patch changes the central game so majorly. (to be clear, a lot of these changes came with the patch, so if you updated your game you already have most of them)

Now, for my own review: I like it, with no reservations.

The new content brings a HUGE one-two punch with council seats and leader caps. You start out with 3 council seats and 6 leader cap, which grows to 6 council seats and ~10 leader cap by the end of the game. Your leaders can be assigned as councilors, and being on the council DOES NOT stop them from doing their normal leader duties.

Translation: you now need to focus your leadership on tech, economy, or military in the same way you focus your empire as a whole on tech, economy, or military.

Every civic in the game gives you an associated (unlockable) council seat, most of which are limited to certain leader types. MAKE SURE the civics you have are giving you seats for the leaders you're hiring, and also MAKE SURE the leaders you're hiring have a start to advance into on your council, otherwise you'll end up needlessly inflating your leader count and eating harsh exp/upkeep penalties. Example, If you want 2-6 scientists exploring the galaxy from the start of the game, then you also want civics which give those scientists council seats, which in turn is going to shift your government and overall leadership into more of a tech focus.

In other words, if you make a Technocratic government and spend most of your leadership on scientists, then you're going to end up with a government that looks and acts like it's being run by scientists. This means HUGE research bonuses across the board, plus lots of galactic exploration, but few to no bonuses in military or economy.

Conversely, if you want a more military-minded government, then you should instead skip on hiring those early scientists in favor of early admirals/generals, with the civics to match. A council stuffed with 4 high-ranking admirals will have skull-smashingly huge wartime bonuses, though it'll be lacking in research or economy bonuses compared to other government types.

An economy-focused empire will instead want its early leaders to be governors, and to pick economy-focused civics which likewise give council seats to those governors. Just like the other examples, this will give your government huge productivity bonuses with fewer comparative tech or fleet bonuses. (you can also assign governors to individual planets now, even if that sector already has a governor, so even small/tall empires can benefit from a bunch of governors)

Your need for individual scientists, admirals, and governors is eased up in small, easily missed ways: your singular Head of Research (default council seat) oversees all research instead of needing to assign a separate scientist to each department, while simultaneously also sitting in a science ship to explore the galaxy (i.e. you only need 1 scientist at start where previously you needed 4); Admirals now boost fleet cap by +10 per level (which is further boosted by traits), so slapping one onto a fleet can let you squeeze a whole second fleet into it; Governor skill level still applies to all planets in their sector as long as they're assigned to a sector capital, however their traits only apply to the planet they're personally overseeing, so "tall" empires that don't plan to expand beyond one sector will want to focus on building up their capital while using other colonies for support.

Where it gets weird is that a planet or fleet can still function without a leader assigned to it, but a science ship can't do jack without a scientist. So unless you plan to go really hard on tech from the start of the game, you're only going to have 1-3 science ships out exploring the galaxy even by end game. This is similar to, but not necessarily the same as, governor (non-council) traits now only applying to the planet they're assigned to, except a single governor can still apply their *council* traits across your whole empire as long as that one is also on your council.

Overall, I have to say I really love these changes, and I think some of the early criticism is inaccurate. I love how you can design an empire around some neat or silly idea, like knight crusaders or environmental protection agencies, and have that be more than just a mouseover description for your (previously only flavorful) government type. I love how the leaders you choose have real, meaningful effects on your empire at large, and how you can tailor your government to work in just about any way you want. I feel like the low leader cap is a necessary part of this, forcing you to be more choosy about them and making those choices significantly more meaningful. Leaders are now a major part of your empire and gameplay decisions, and I love so much about it.\t\t\t\t\t
