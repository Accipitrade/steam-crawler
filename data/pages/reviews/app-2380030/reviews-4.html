A quick metaphor before we get into the meat and potatoes of the review:
What Paradox wanted the DLC to be like:
You're hungry, and decide to stop by a nearby restaurant. You take a look at the menu (leader pool) and all the food (leaders) looks fantastic! However, you only have enough cash in your pocket to get a couple items, so you need to choose wisely for what you really want to get!
How the DLC actually went:
You've had your teeth (leaders) your whole life (the game's lifespan). They're just kinda there and you don't think about them much, but you know you'd certainly miss them if they're gone. One day, you see a local dentist (Paradox) offering a procedure to make your teeth a brilliant white and extremely strong, all for just $15! So you go into the clinic, pay the money, and the dentist immediately straps you into a chair, pulls out some pliers and gets ready to rip out most of your teeth. You object, saying you want to keep all your teeth, offending the dentist, who replies that you'll still get to choose a few teeth to keep, won't that make them more valuable for the scarcity? He'll even throw in hundreds of teeth whitening strips (new traits, many of which are useless) along with the procedure!

Still with me? Excellent.
So, in brief the goal of the Galactic Paragons DLC was to make the leaders in Stellaris, a crucial but old and oft overlooked feature, much more interesting and powerful. Did this DLC succeed in this?
In a way, yes. Leaders have a vastly expanded trait list, veteran classes to specialize in a specific field, and a much larger pool of traits that you can select from rather than the old system where traits were randomly assigned.
However, this DLC has two major flaws (ignoring the incredibly buggy release, now mostly patched): Many of the new leader traits are next to worthless, and—by far the worse issue—an extremely strict leader cap.
Let's go over the traits before addressing the elephant in the room almost all reviews are talking about. Even if the leader cap was never added, I would still be disappointed with many of the new traits. Don't get me wrong, some, especially some of the Councilor traits, are quite good. However, they are vastly drowned out by truly dogwater traits such as +2 Unity or +2 Alloys a month. No, not percentage affecting your empire, a flat +X number. Maybe +6 Consumer Goods is a nice shot in the arm for your very first hired leader when you're in the very early game, but it becomes negligible in the extreme once you truly get the ball rolling with +200 consumer goods produced by your planets, effectively making the trait a wasted slot that could've been spent on something better on one of your ever-so-limited leaders. The ability to choose your new traits helps mitigate the issue, but you're still largely at the mercy of the RNG, and it often becomes an issue of just choosing the least bad option.
The leader limit. Where to even begin? If the leader limit had been implemented differently, I genuinely think that the response to this DLC would have been rather good, or at least lukewarm. As it stands, however, the cap didn't just shoot this DLC in the foot, it amputated the entire leg.
You get 6 leaders for 'free' as your base, which can be increased by 3 by two separate tradition trees (eating into the already precious few traditions you get in vanilla Stellaris). Any hired beyond that face an increase of unity upkeep and absolutely plummeting experience gain for all leaders. If the only cost was an increase in upkeep I think it'd be more than tolerable; after all, empire sprawl increasing the cost of traditions and research is a widely accepted part of the game, why shouldn't keeping a large retinue of the best and brightest of your empire take a significant amount of willpower and, well, unity to keep on track?
The experience gain is, I believe, what is mostly objected to. Galactic Paragons was meant to vastly improve your leaders, but instead you're faced with the choice of a handful of mostly-better-than before leaders or a swarm of absolutely abysmal losers who are unlikely to make it higher than level 4 by the time they die. This single feature, by itself, nullifies almost all benefits this DLC offers, as you'll never truly be able to support all the leaders you had before this patch unless you're playing an ultra-tall empire like some megacorporations or perhaps an origin like Shattered Ringworld which keeps you close to home.
So is the leader cap optional, like the L-Gates, Marauder Empires, when the Crisis happen and so much else in Stellaris? Nope. It's Paradox's way or the highway. Or, of course, mods, which are bound to break in patches. Take it or leave it.
Oh, and if you don't buy the DLC, you still get the leader cap. All the drawbacks with almost none of the benefits—stay classy, Paradox.

OK, but how do the new leaders actually perform?

Governors: Hit hard, but more by the patch than the DLC itself. As of 3.8 Governors only give their traits to the single planet they're based on, rather than the entire sector, which merely gets a bit of crime reduction and other tiny boosts. While wide empires are clearly hit the hardest by this (like most aspects of 3.8), tall empires also suffer if you planned to create a lot of Habitats, Ringworlds, terraform worlds, etc. in a single concentrated sector. You'd have to put a governor on every single one to get to use their traits, so say goodbye to staying below the cap.
Scientists: Right there with Generals in terms of how much they've suffered. All research positions are now handled by a single scientist, the Head of Research on your council, who can also command a science ship! Sounds good for keeping your leader cap down, right? And it is! But when it comes to actually using them, well...
Paradox explicitly stated that they disliked the early game science ship rush to explore the galaxy in earlier versions and I can only imagine it played a large part in the way the leader cap is now. If you want to explore the galaxy quickly now, you'll be doing it with a team of level 1 plebs who utterly ruin the experience gain for each other and all your other classes of leaders. If you play by Paradox's rules and stick to just a couple science ships, they can certainly survey fast once they get their veteran class in, but it's still slower than having a dedicated team of science ships who work together. In addition, anomalies must either be ignored or slow your entire exploration endeavors to a crawl, to say nothing of archaeological sites which are almost always a complete waste of a scientist unless you're completely done exploring. The loss of a single science ship, whether you accidentally jump into a system right next to a leviathan or trigger an anomaly which spawns enemies is crippling, putting you back many years in game time as you have to level a new replacement (as having one in reserve running errands in your space would count against your cap) and get them back to the frontier.
For me and I imagine many other players, the early game exploration of the galaxy, seeing the playing field slowly unravel before you as you carefully shepherd your teams across the galaxy was one of the best parts of Stellaris, now severely limited—why?
Admirals: Probably the only class of leader that's truly seen a net gain. Many of their new traits are quite good, though the leader limit promotes doomstacking rather than smaller quick reaction forces spread throughout your empire. No real complaints, but nothing that has wowed me thus far.
Generals: Not just worthless, but actually detrimental. There is no reason to ever, ever recruit a general, such a niche leader before, if they're going to count against your cap.

TL;DR: Patch 3.8 and Galactic Paragons promise better leaders and meaningful choices, introduces half-baked traits, tedium and completely artificial scarcity to force decisions.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Galactic Paragons DLC and it's respective 3.8 Gemini update are such a welcome addition, especially for Democratic empires. In the past, you had to stop whatever plans you had to fulfill your ruler's wants by the next election. Wash, rinse, repeat. Which was frustrating to say the least when you're busy trying to maintain resources.

In the base game, you're basically cycling through buffs in the form of agendas, instead, while your leaders developer new traits and flaws as the game progresses. Prior to the update, the former was only found in less friendly empires, while the latter was a constant. In the DLC, the Council itself basically functions like SimCity 3000's Ordinance system, but with additional character development.

This update FINALLY delegates all research to a single head of the department. I love this game, I really do, but holy crap they're some questionable design choices here. In addition, there is now a cap on how many leaders you can have. However, it will alert when they're idle.

Overall, Galactic Paragons has got to be the most immersive update in a while, and this is coming from someone who has sunk tones of hours into this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't know what people are talking about not having enough leaders. Either they don't know how the new sector system works, or they're literally trying to put a leader on every single world like how it used to be.

Or they're making like 50 fleets and trying to rush (personally in most large galaxies, I only have like 4-5 mega fleets)

This expansion is fantastic, not only does it bring a lot to biological empires but also the internal workings of them with new events and fleshed out leaders.

Your leaders aren't on par with like CK3 characters, but they do have a bit more character to them and especially your council rulers. Instead of seeing them as just a pretty picture with some stats tied to them, they're now pretty pictures with AMAZING stats tied to them and said stats level up over time and get better.

This new system makes playing tall extremely powerful, and those who play wide (with the right empire sprawl traits from leaders) can also be a totally viable option now.

If you're an old Stellaris player who stepped away for a bit and just came back, yes you'll probably have to re learn the game a bit.

is it annoying that some menus and buried beneath other menus? Yes actually, that's one of my complaints, the UI is getting a bit cluttered. Is it a game killer though? Absolutely not, something like this can easily be fixed in a patch (I hope so at least, I'm no programmer)

My only major complaint is for Hive Minds and Machine Consciences, when it comes to flavor and such this expansion offers little for them. Personally I never liked playing as those kind of races, it always just felt boring and samey to me "Devour all sapient life" or "Destroy all bio-organics" some folk like that, though it's not for me. Yet I can still see why those players would be upset by this.

Overall, this expansion is great. It changes A LOT about how things are handled and it's great. Just make sure to adjust your notifications though, cause some times leader notifications can be a bit spammy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis dlc makes leaders feel unique, allows character control over their abilities, and makes the game much more difficult by forcing an almost painful opportunity cost with every leader assignment.

I absolutely love it.

I don't understand what all the bellyaching is about here and on the paradox forums. Apparently everyone likes the boring paint-the-galaxy simulator this game has turned into where you can do everything, all the time, every play through.

I find the profound limitations enforced by this dlc a welcome breath of fresh air after 3k+ hours with the old leader system.

If you like difficult strategic decisions and molding unique leaders, this dlc is great.

If you enjoyed the old easy-mode, where crime was irrelevant, stability was always almost-maxed on every planet without trying, and expansion was easy to manage and inevitable, maybe stay away. This dlc actually increases the strategic challenge of playing Stellaris, which apparently many people do not enjoy.

IMHO, this is the best dlc Stellaris has gotten in years.\t\t\t\t\t
