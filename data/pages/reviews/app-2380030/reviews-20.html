\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStellaris boomer here, cool DLC idea, but it changes the game too drastically, and honestly, in a way I don't think Stellaris needed. See in Stellaris vs. another PDX game like HoI4, leaders are kind of a slap on bonus to your empire that you want, rather than a major gameplay feature. This changes that, almost every civic impacts your leaders now, and most of your game (especially for democratic empires) is now managing your leaders. Where you could do that before, now it shoves it in your face. Literally.

Leaders are now crazy strong, and I personally am not a fan, as they give bonuses equal if not greater to a lot of civics, ethics, and traits. The downside being they'll die.

My other gripe is how the game gives you those "galactic paragons," those unique legendary people who can single-handedly shift your empire. There's no anomaly chain. No event. They just pop up in your task bar at the top and you can recruit them. You only need to research one easy tech and build a starbase building. It's pretty lame for the main focus of the DLC.

My final gripe is how the DLC has changed the UI. Lots of nesting. The policies and edicts tab have now been merged in one nest under the new "Government" tab. And the ship designer has been nested under the fleet manager (I actually dig that one a lot.) The edicts one is annoying, because it makes accessing your edicts more difficult, especially bad for wartime edicts. Additionally, because it's now the same menu as the policies, it takes up basically your whole screen.

Cool idea, not needed. Nice try though.

(Also most of the positive comments are talking about the imperium of man. The origin in question isn't even available to imperial types.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGalactic Paragons is a massive game-changing expansion that revamps leaders and civics while also supporting numerous playstyles and makes tall gameplay extremely viable with a leader focused build.
Galactic Paragons is a DLC I would recommend to almost anyone and the more DLCs you already own, the better this one gets. That’s because Galactic Paragons is a dlc that makes every other dlc released for Stellaris perform better by enhancing all their civics and special event leaders.

Civics and Council:
Getting Galactic Paragons makes every single civic released (including the Sovereign civics that don’t take a slot) in the game do more. This is done through the new Council available to all empires (the 3 member council is included in base game as part of a free update, the additional 3 unlockable slots that are based on your current civics is what you get with the dlc). In addition, Galactic Paragons itself adds several new civics that are focused on getting more out of leaders, plus some more very thematic ones like Crusaders Spirit, Oppressive Autocracy, and others.

Lastly, you can now set what your starting ruler trait without having to reload the game if you’re looking for a specific one. It’s good for early game setup and flavor if you’re into that. The Under One Rule origin gives you a lot more choices and even negative traits to set for your ruler. That origin is a semi-story driven one with a lot of leader mechanics and some scripted authority changing.

Normal/Megacorp Civics & Council:
To start: All normal and megacorp councils start with 3 positions. The ruler (same role as before but expanded), the Minister of Defense, and the Head of Research. The ruler gets more bonuses and is the only leader that can’t pull double duty of other tasks while being on the council. Imperial rulers get a free (90% off unity upkeep until they patch an old bug that’s from years back when they switched leaders from energy upkeep and costs to Unity instead) ruler that’s randomly selected from one of the four classes (Scientist, Governor, Admiral and General).
The Minister of Defense is a new council role that allows either an admiral or general and reduces your army, ship and starbase upkeep per leader level (up to max 20% at level 10 and not including additional leader traits that could reduce upkeep even further).
The Head of Research is the previous 3 leaders for Physics, Society and Engineering research combined into one and with a few twists. First, Head of Research gives 2% research speed per skill level like the previous system except with 2 less leaders to have to pay upkeep and replace when they all die around the same time. Second, the HoR can do double-duty and use a science ship at the same time as being head of research. Be careful, if they die while exploring, that’s a BIG hit to your science output. Third, if that sounds really scary and you don’t want to risk losing your scientist, or spending precious early-game unity to hire a second one (they’re more expensive now), then you can use your starting governor instead for this slot. Or if you did the above and lost your scientist and you don’t have the unity to replace them, then you can use the governor instead.

The normal and megacorp empire civics now all have a council position attached to them. These effects scale per skill level of the leader that is in that slot. Generally speaking, most of the really good civics got decent council effects but the less used and more forgettable civics (like Mining Guilds) got a major buff through the council position and rounds the civics out and gives more viable options to play during a game. To use the Mining Guilds example, you get +1 mineral per Miner (and planetary feature Strategic Resource mining jobs like Gas Miner/Motes Miner/Crystal Miner). The council position gives 0.05 Stability per regular Miner job multiplied by the leader’s skill level (so at max level 10, that would be 0.50 Stability per Miner. A planet with 20 Miners would get +10 Stability which increases production bonuses if over 50 Stability and makes those planets a lot less likely to revolt). In addition, it opens up a lot of new synergies between civics that didn’t exist before. For example, the Relentless Industrialist civic gives +2% Miner output bonus multiplied by skill level of the leader (at max level 10, that would be +20% to all Miners in your empire). You can combine the two civics to get a ton of minerals from your Miners (which then turns those minerals to produce a ton of alloys and consumer goods from relentless industrialists. If your species is post-apocalpytic origin and/or radiotrophic Plantoids/Lithoids they also get bonuses from your planets becoming tomb worlds through the Relentless Industrialists civic). The leaders that you put in those council positions can also get leader traits that get even more minerals/alloys/consumer goods, turning you into an economic powerhouse. And that’s just one example, the DLC enables all kinds of combinations from Egalitarian Unity builds to Spiritualist Unity builds to massive Ruler producing Authoritarian empires. The amount of synergy that this DLC gives to empire building, economics, science rushing, fleet building and cost management cannot be understated.

Gestalt Civics:
Gestalts, unfortunately, have a harder time making use of council positions for those synergies. Civics instead give an experience bonus to 1 of 4 specialized nodes that gestalts have. The tradeoff is that they trade the power and flexibility of normal empires with immortal nodes that can purge their negative traits (at the cost of some experience) giving you stability (not having to worry about replacement leaders or piling negative traits) in the long run.

Normal Leaders & Traits:
Leaders are generally much more potent (and expensive) than they were before. It has a very RPG-esque feel to it now, which is a pretty fun mechanic. There is a new “soft” leader cap that can be increased through techs, edicts and other ways to raise that limit. For each leader, you’re over the cap, all leaders get a reduction to leader experience gain. If you’ve picked the transcendent learning AP or other leader civics, the experience drop may be negated by the leader exp gain bonuses you have as a buffer.
Positive traits are much more powerful than before and can have different levels for more potency. The first 4 levels have common traits which are generic and can have useful early game benefits that can help patch weaknesses in your economy. (For example, there is a trait that gives you a set amount of Alloys per month which is extremely useful super early game when you have limited pop amounts and output).
When they leveled up enough to become veterans (level 6 iirc), they can choose a specialization for their role, with all 4 classes having a council specialized role. Admirals get offensive fleet, evasive (speedy) fleet and council roles. Generals get offensive army, defensive army, and council roles. Governors get basic resource (energy, minerals, food), advanced resource (alloys, strategic resources, etc) and council role. Scientists get exploration/archaeology/anomaly role, planet assist research role and council role.
Lastly, there is a destiny trait (level 8 iirc) that grants an incredibly powerful bonus to your leader.
Consequently, the negative traits also hurt a bit more than before. Some leaders (randomly, you won’t know as the numbers are different per individual leader) have a higher chance of generating negative traits (essentially corrupt/incompetent leaders). The negative traits appear more often the higher up the levels you go, so it essentially seems like a randomized semi-organic balancing mechanic to prevent you from just fielding insanely overpowered leaders with no drawbacks. Usually, the positive traits will outweigh the drawbacks of the negative traits.

I highly recommend this DLC. It's loads of fun.\t\t\t\t\t
