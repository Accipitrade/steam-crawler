\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn physics, there's a particularly infamous experiment known as the "double-slit experiment." In this experiment, electrons were fired through a "wall," with one vertical slit opening in the middle. After the electrons were fired towards the wall, the ones that made it through the slit would hit a flat instrument that would record the location the electron would hit if there were another wall without any openings. The instrument would display these "hits" much like how the paint of a paintball would leave a mark upon any surface it hit. By firing the electrons through a wall with a single slit, the resulting recording of electron "hits" would solely be a vertical band. This vertical band indicates that the electrons act as a particle, like a paintball on a real-world scale, as opposed to a wave. Were the electrons to act like a wave, the vertical band would gradient out horizontally as if the band was brushed in those directions. From the slit, a wave would radiate outward in a semicircle, where the electrons act as a wave. Because this gradient was not the result, this would indicate that the electrons act as individual particles and not a wave. When a second slit is added to the wall, next to the original, it would be expected, with the electrons acting like particles, that two vertical bands of hits would be recorded. When this is put to the test, the result is an "interference pattern," a pattern of many different bands that would be produced by waves as the crests (peaks) of one wave gets canceled by the trough (lowest point) of another as two waves radiate from the two slits. This means that the electrons, which were at first acting like particles, now act like waves. Even altering the rate at which electrons were fired, to eliminate the chance of electrons interacting with each other, did not affect the result. Electrons, which are particles, acted as waves until hitting the wall with the slit or the wall measuring the hits. When physicists placed a measuring instrument by one of the slits to see how the electrons acted and which slit an electron went through, the electrons went back to behaving like particles. The very act of observing changes how the electrons act.

Changed is much like this. It is, at first, observed as a cheap and trashy furry game. It then appears as the opposite of a furry game when the goal is to prevent the protagonist, a human, from becoming a furry. However, once you observe the game more closely, it goes back to being a nasty furry game as originally observed.

The plot of this game is like something out of the mind of an elementary school student, middle school at best: You gotta escape spooky science building tower place and there's this DISEASE it MAKES YOU DIE and the world is EMPTY (scary) and you DON'T KNOW WHAT'S GOING ON but miraculously you find your way around and escape the MONSTERS in the spooky science building tower place but one of them is FRIENDLY and and... ...it drags on for a few inches more.

This game is as visually appealing as the Verizon logo. I mean, there are certainly worse things to look at, but generic, frequently bright, and mildly pixelated graphics go only so far. There is one human character in this whole game, and it is abundantly clear that he was drawn by a furry. His skin shade is straight-up #ffffff white, like many of the backgrounds. The character design for many of the enemies is on the level with either generic or bizarre furry OCs. There is no particular "in-between" for the level of ridiculousness for the enemies' designs. Please tell me I was hallucinating and did not witness an enemy that was a giant squid that was also a dog or something.

Speaking of awful bosses, the gameplay is of a similar stripe. It's like climbing a skyscraper and chewing on crayons to spit out the crayon goo off the ledge onto a piece of paper to try to recreate the Verizon logo. You have to go all the way down to see how bad you missed and messed up, then go all the way back up to try again. I'm serious. From a "point A to point B perspective," the game is not very long. You could probably easily map out the entire game in your head. Playing through the whole game is another story. Some of the enemies, segments, hazards, and bosses are very annoying to play through/against. It can range from requiring exact movement and timing with extreme trial and error, a hefty deal of luck, and/or knowledge of some of the game mechanics beforehand. The gameplay is awful and strenuous, but half the people playing are probably too horny to care.

Speaking of horny people, if you are anything like me before I knew about this thing, and you are having a pleasant time playing Team Fortress 2 (or any other online game with custom profile pictures and voice chat) and you happen to see someone with a profile picture of that stupid black furry with the skull face, do not dare compel them to try as hard as possible to convince you to buy this or any other game by simply asking them what their profile picture is and why you've seen it other places. I will not give any hardcore fans of this game the satisfaction of saying the character's name, so I will refer to it as "Peter Griffin" from now on. I don't what kind of cement mix you were fed as a kid, but I don't get how Peter Griffin activates the "goo goo ga ga mode" in your brain. Please obsess over and revolve your life around something way cooler and far more respectable, like the noble gas radon, adélie penguins, or American war correspondent James Wheeler Davidson. Oh, and thank you for leading me to the point in my life where I learned about the existence of the "transformation fetish." That era of my life before I had any knowledge about it is over now, I guess. If you have a transformation fetish, check this out and have your knocks socked off: https://www.mathsisfun.com/geometry/transformations.html

If you're looking at the six achievements and thinking to yourself that this is probably a breezy game to get a 100%, you aren't wildly off, but the difficulty to achievement count ratio is rather yucky. Half of the achievements are literally at the final point of the game and achieved by going to the last save, sitting through a lengthy cutscene of you skipping text, and picking one of three options. The others require a bit of save-scumming at certain points and picking certain dialogue options and interacting with Peter Griffin in certain ways. It took me about six and a half hours to 100% it, but it didn't feel particularly worth it. You can't smoke weed in this game. I doubt that would ever be a question, but I just felt like clarifying.

If you put a gun to my head and ask me to say one thing I like about this game, there's a good chance I will just tell you to pull the trigger, but otherwise, I will say the market. This game has trading cards, which means badges, profile backgrounds, emoticons, and booster packs. There is a rare profile background you can get by crafting a badge that sells for about $3, the regular Peter Griffin card goes for $0.30, and the booster pack, which takes 750 gems to make, can sell for $0.50. If you manage to get a foil from the set, then pop some champagne because the prices for these are ridiculous. If you want a foil badge for this game, you practically have to take out a second mortgage. At the time of writing this, one of the foil cards is nearly $30. You could make more money by just investing in a decent stock or good crypto, but this comes close.
