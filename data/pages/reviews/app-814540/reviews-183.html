\r\n\t\t\t\t\tChances are you already know why you're here, but I’m going to try and write this review for everyone else, on the offchance you somehow found this game and you thought it was... ... Someone help me out here, I'm in too deep. What is it even possible for a non-furry to mistake this for? A low-budget sci-fi reimagining of Ovid's Metamorphoses? I mean, it being based on Ancient Greek literature would track with it featuring a boy in his underwear trying to avoid getting buggered by hairy bipeds.

The plot is, you are aforementioned boy in tight shorts waking up from a suspended animation pod in an abandoned facility; you learn quickly that there appears to have been some kind of pandemic - strangely relevant - and the facility’s response to this pandemic, in what was clearly a much more cute-friendly version of the Umbrella Corporation school of pandemic management, was to create a race of gooey bipedal animals that merge with humans or turn humans into more animals, and let’s just say I want to see how the people who ran this facility would have responded to COVID. These animals all seem hungry to smear you in themselves until you’re just like them, and your job is to get out of the facility, possibly to find a shirt.

To actually answer the question "What kind of game is Changed:" There's a kind of RPGMaker horror/puzzle game exemplified by games like Witch’s House or Pocket Mirror, where you have no defense mechanisms besides a sprint key and must maneuver precisely around the monsters and traps on the grid-based system or die instantly, the kind wherein each method of failure treats you to some elaborate death sequence, and seeing all the novel ways of dying seems to be the reason the game exists. Same deal, but replace 'elaborate death sequence' with 'furry envelopment/transformation sequence' and 'novel ways of dying' with 'suggestive gooey furry pics.'

And I do mean pretty suggestive. Strictly speaking it’s not NSFW, as there are no genitalia on display, but there are several sequences that are basically furry erotica in all aspects but that; I’m thinking in particular of one sequence where a masked wolf turns you into another masked wolf with his pelvis to your rear beneath your new wagging tail, to say nothing of the puddles of white goo everywhere.

Now, this is not the judgement it might initially sound like - frankly, if you were to ask me if it’s more sane to prefer to hang around someone who wants to see a variety of suggestive gooey furry pics versus someone who wants to see a protagonist get brutally murdered a lot, I would say, by all means, hit random slideshow on that furry porn folder. What is a judgement is that the game itself is more than a little jank.

The game frequently requires exacting precision fleeing with little wiggle room, which isn't a problem by itself; the big problem is that the game seems to use some RNG-based RPGMaker pathing for its monsters to allow them to circumnavigate obstacles to get to you, so that a monster - such as, oh, I don't know, just to pull an example out from under my tail, a predatory latex lioness suit - does not get stuck forever staring at a tree, the juicy human on the other side forever lost to them behind two horizontal feet of wood. But if you have RNG pathing, you can't have precision avoidance. If you're not going down a narrow corridor, there's no consistent 'solution' to the chase sequences, and it can become incredibly frustrating when even Ms. Trial and Mr. Error can’t necessarily save you from literally being made into someone's b*tch.

The other contradictory design problem is that there are a number of lore notes and findable gooey furry pics sprinkled through the zones - but some of these exist in zones that you can only be in *during* chase sequences or other active areas of danger, which means stopping to look at some of the gooey furry pics - which, I will remind, is the reason to play this game - basically ensures you'll be goo-wolfed back to the last checkpoint. And there's yet more RPGMaker jank on offer.

At this point you might be thinking, "Fyren, you incredibly attractive nutjob pretending to be a cybernetic catboy, what are you doing lambasting the mechanics of a $7 furry fetish game by a two-man development team?" And that's a reasonable point, because frankly, I’m not sure why I’m ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ writing this; I think I’m starting to go and review furry fetish games as some kind of creative exercise rather than imagining I’m necessarily providing vital consumer information. I would nonetheless point out in my defense that A) Tribal Hunter has more recently set the bar for what a small team can do with fetish mechanics relating to gameplay, and B) the developers seem to agree with these criticisms; There's a remake under construction, a preview of which is included with the game, that addresses most of these problems. The "Changed-Special" version adds consistent monster behavior, new puzzles, more plot, new even-more-suggestive gooey furry pics... although also adds, for some reason, a substantially more garbled English translation.

This is all a long rambling way of saying there’s not an incredible amount to recommend the current game from a mechanical standpoint unless - possibly even if - you really like your hidey chasey RPGMaker horror. So why is there a blue thumb next to this review?

Steam’s hard yea or nay makes this difficult, and I’m honestly on the fence, but I went blue because it’s $7, frequently goes on sale, appears to be being remade for free to solve a lot of my mechanical quibbles, I might just kind of enjoy suggestive gooey furry pics, and the one sapient monster you meet who doesn’t immediately attempt to bend you over and stuff you into his wolfpack, as it were, is kind of a sweetheart if you get to know him, though in the remake he does sometimes come across as trying too hard. It’s a game made with heart if not skill, and it’s more fun than trying to slog through a furry dating sim. Ultimately, I felt motivated enough to finish it, despite its frustrations, and and I came through the true end pretty satisfied - if not blatantly manipulated by the end of my furry heartstrings, which are the only ones I have left after the accident.

Just understand that that thumbs up comes with *a lot* of caveats, and the game is going to work a lot better if you have furry heartstrings to tug - whether you knew about said heartstrings or not.\t\t\t\t\t

TLDR: Being the dev's first game it is not without it's flaws. There are some instances I felt where it was heavily reliant upon luck to pass, and one instance of poor post boss save. While it can be difficult at times, it's not impossible to beat, but will take time. If you are up for a challenge and an intriguing story with depth I would say give this a try. This isn't the kind of game I play but it's one I'm glad I did.

In-depth:

Story:
The overall story shows the care put into the game and is what really drew me into the story. The branching bad ends are objectively more difficult to get imo than the true ends, simple due to the nature of the final save before the approach to the specified ends. The story of this game is definitely one of it's selling points.

Art:
I found the art to be rather cute. I personally really like this creators style and would be happy to see more of their work in the future. The design of creatures in-game were all based around a central idea and yet so vastly different at times that I didn't feel I was looking at the same creations repeatedly. Some may find the artwork style a bit "dated" but I've always been a fan of pixel games and this style is imo a more high class stylization of those.

Music:
All the music within the game is originally made and in the old 8-bit style that I love. If you love retro game music, you'll like this. The only song I can think of that isn't 100% original would be the remixed happy birthday simply because it's happy birthday. The soundtrack for this game is about 30 minutes long and I will be going back and listening to it time and again for the nostalgia.

Achievements:
All the achievements are gained from starting the game and endings. The only thing that would prevent you from gaining any of them would be frustration of gameplay/balancing at some points but there are multiple resources for players put together in guides. I personally used one simply because I did not want to have to redo specific sections of the game over again due to the difficulty to pass some of them.

Misc:
This game is difficult and many of the issues presented in this version of the game were touched upon in the updated version, which also includes updated art, more transformation sequences, and as I mentioned updates to balancing. The special version does not grant the achievements to my knowledge, so keep in mind when deciding what version you decide to play.

Overall, I really enjoyed this game despite the frustrations it caused me at times. The story was intriguing and my favorite ending was by far probably the hidden ending simply because I like happy conclusions. If you are willing or are intrigued I would recommend playing at least one version of the game as it still stands out as a great game despite the few flaws that make it more difficult to beat but not impossible.\t\t\t\t\t
