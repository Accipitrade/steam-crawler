\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAh, a forgotten classic of a now nearly dead genre. This game had a large amount of game mechanics that you'd think would eventually become very popular in the RTS genre, and they probably would... If this game didn't happen to come out at a time where a number of other absolutely amazing RTS games came out, such as Homeworld.

The game is most similar to Warzone 2100, another game I'd recommend. Both games have a persistent campaign and modular units, with Earth 2150 even having a better version of Warzone's main/mission base mechanics where you can send units and resources back and forth between the persistent main base and the mission area. However it also has other features such as being able to dig underground like in Metal Fatigue (a game I don't recommend), being able to paint units to look like the enemy, and being able to turn your units lights off to make them harder to spot. The latter two features only matter in multiplayer, which for a game from 2000, is obviously not a big thing anymore and honestly never really was. But they are still cool features. Given multiplayer is dead, I'll focus on talking about the single player campaigns.

So the concept around the campaigns is pretty cool. You have a main base which has a spaceport that can send resources to the construction of an evacuation ship as earth is about to be destroyed. The campaign doesn't actually require you to complete every mission. As soon as you send all the needed resources to space, you win, regardless of how many missions are left. The time limit is meant to make sure you don't just faff about, but in 2 of the campaigns, it's super generous, and in the ED one, you should still have plenty of time as long as you aren't actively wasting it. Your main base remains persistent for the entire game, letting you slowly work your way through the research tree and carry over experienced units from mission to mission.

However each campaign also has a degree of replay value individually despite each taking around 10-30 hours to complete. You see there are different branching paths through the campaign, with some missions even letting you fail them to open up new possible missions you couldn't see otherwise, in a few cases without even any indication that there was a branch to begin with. The different branches could involve allying with different factions, or potentially giving you a shot at some high resource missions. There's even secret missions you find by obtaining artifacts hidden somewhere on the map of some missions.

However the game is very difficult compared to other games released around this time. The AI often very aggressively attacks you, and in the ED campaign they are often several technologies ahead of you. You are sometimes forced to either wait for the AI to run out of resources, or if you are bold, to aggressively rush the enemy with several transport loads of units you brought in from your main base. You also need to make sure you save enough resources to send to space, although the last few missions give you a lot just in case.

The story is told through emails, and early on they might really engage in a lot of world building, but later on they tend to get shorter, more succinct, and overall feel a bit phoned in. It's obvious they didn't put much effort into the story of late game missions, and it shows.

The music is also very good, with each faction having different tracks and cycling through a few depending on if you are in combat or not. However the long length of the campaign will eventually cause you to have heard each track way too many times and you'll likely lose appreciation for it. Same with the unit voice acting. It's generally quite good, but around 7 hours into an LC campaign I eventually got tired hearing "SHOTS FLYING ALL AROUND!" for the zillionth time. The game also has a lot of cinematics, but they are in the style of national propaganda videos, so your seeing what the nations want the general public to see rather than anything that moves the story forward in most cases.

Each faction also plays very different. With ED using cannons , lasers that overheat the enemy instead of doing damage, helicopters, and a resource harvesting system involving having units carry crates of resources to be refined. UCS uses walking robots armed with grenade launchers, and lots and lots of plasma weapons, as well as having the ability to build transmitters that extend their power plants range, and having effectively the same resource gathering system as in Command and Conquer. LC plays completely different than the others, having only hover units and using weapons like sonic cannons and a short range electric cannon. They construct buildings in space and drop them down already constructed, and rely on solar power that needs battery's to continue offering power in the day, but they have global power coverage and automated resource harvesting.

As for campaigns, I recommend playing them in this order:
LC
UCS
ED

Because the difficulty of the campaigns is noticeably different and that's pretty much the order from least to most difficult.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a review based 95% on the single player experience over quite a lot of years.

The game is tricky to get running on modern day systems and equipment,
but there is a community patch by InsideEARTH Operations out there which at least for me fixed all the issues.

Short version: Go get the game and definetly enjoy the main part. Earth 2150 has a very unique "HQ-to-Mission-and-back"-system in it's campaign which I have never seen elsewhere. It also lets you design your own units a bit.

------------

Earth 2150 is a quite old game set in a sci-fi-ish apocalyptic setting. Earth was thrown out of its own orbit due to extreme use of nuclear weapons and
is now drifting towards the sun. Time for the three factions of the game to gather resources and construct space ships to escape to Mars.


good points

- Price: It is cheap - and basicly always on sale if there is a big one happening. Looking at the game hours you can sink into the game its worth it big times.

- Unit designer: Earth 2150 lets you design your unit roster partly yourself. You get some base chassis and get a selection of weaponry you get add to each unit and also energy shields to defend against energy weapons. You can save money by equipping only small weapons or you can go all the way and buy the most expensive but also most hard hitting ones. Only put lasers on your tank, or just guns, or both. It is really up to you. While it is somewhat limited and there
are probably "meta"-setups which outclass other designs completely, I really enjoy this part of the game and I havent seen it in many RTS so far. The energy-weapons are unique for their respective factions as well. e.g. You have the brute-force plasma-weapons; you have the ion cannon which deactivates vehicles and buildings to make them easy targets for either killing or capturing them and you have lasers which basicly insta-kill units if they run out of energy-shields.
There are also super-weapons like tactical nukes or weather-controll stations.
There is however this one point in the game where energy weapons get so strong that building a heavy unit actually means having a worse trade off than if you would build the medium counterpart; the energy shields just can't compete with the firepower which makes all your heavy vehicles big,slow,expensive glass cannons. That is of no concern for the main game and probably not for the first expansion though.

- Veterancy: Units can level up. I always like to keep my people alive in strategy games wherever possible and it is even better if you get rewarded for that.
Veterancy is also like 8 levels I believe. So getting a unit completely maxed out during the campaign takes quite a while. Though you can upgrade your units a little bit while they are on the field,
a maxed out veterancy light tank from the start of the game will still be send to oblivion if it encounters late game weapons, but as soon as you have your late game unit
maxed out it becomes a force to be reckoned with. There is also a "Banner" which you can put on units and buildings
which acts like an aura of 2 fake-veterancy-lvls. You get the attack and range increase but not the HP increase on your units with those.

- the Campaign HQ-system: I looooooove this one. So all the veterans you got during one mission? You can keep them and bring them to the next mission. But you dont just spawn them in. Instead you fly them in bit by bit. So with a growing army you have to think about what units to send in as help first. Military to protect object XYZ? Harvesters to speed up resource gathering to get your base going faster? Or you decide not to use some of them because you will not need e.g. a construction unit for mission A. And when you are done with Mission A, you fly your people - veterans and new ones alike - back to your HQ and prepare for the next mission B.

- terra-forming, bridges and tunnel-systems: To make it short - it opens up many tactical possibilities.

- There are three factions and they all feel different to play as. E.g. The firing lines of 3-tower-row-defenses for the ED; the vulnerable power-supply-systems of the UCS
and the global orbit-construction and deploy-system for buildings as the LC.

- music: It has a really nice soundtrack with some awesome atmospheric parts. Also I think the victory music during the stat-screen is taken from classical
music pieces - and it fits quite well - lol.

- the main game: Escape from the Blue Planet - while it does not have all the units you get in the expansions, the main game has the best cutscenes (weather- and research-reports as you progress
through the campaign), it has the best dialogue - looking at you NEO - and the whole premiss of Earth slowly dying due to its inevitable voyage of doom is shown so well with the map layouts changing throughout the levels. You also have to think about how to spend your money. Do I put it into research? Do I put it into more units? Or do I put it into the main goal of the campaign: gathering enough money for the escape ship?
During the campaign-gameplay you gradually unlock more and more technologies and some of them have their own little mission-lines. While I would not like to fail and give myself a handycap for the rest of the campaign, it is still possible: if you e.g. mess up to save the prototyp of your laser-weapon as one of the factions you will have to play the rest of the campaign without the laser-weapon because development could not be finished.

neutral points
- graphics: Well the game is old. I cannot deny that. However, I do think though that the day-night-cycle with extra weather effects is really neat. Also: Everything is recognizeable and good enough to destinguish from eachother.

- Moon Project expansion: The Moon Project adds a bunch of extra units to each faction's roster. I guess it also balances out the raw factions differences to make it more fair. (looking at you tunnel-digger for the LC)You also get a recycling station to sort out old units to make space for the latest once while also getting some refund.
2 of the 3 campaigns are mostly just escort missions or build and destroy, which is rather unfortunate but if you like the game it is
OK and I guess still better than the average skirmish-experience. My favorite part is actually the 3rd campaign where you take command over some special
forces. The dialogue is fun and especially the little videos they made for the medal-ceremony are really heartwarming. I completed Moon Project on hard
difficulty but I have to say it is really punishing with path-finding-issues and absurd "gotcha"-triggers during some missions like a random meteor hitting
your VIP-unit making you fail the mission on pure chance and the meteors just keep on falling.

-path-finding: It works....When you have to navigate through mine-fields in the campaign it is your worst nightmare, but otherwise units get from A to B. Putting airplanes into your transport ship is a bit iffy though and it definetly does not keep up with modern day standards.

- ammunition-system: Energy weapons cant keep on firing on max power forever and need breaks now and then between fights. Conventional weapons need to be resupplied by plane. I like that a lot. However, said planes tend to get shot down because they automaticly come to resupply units that are low or out of ammunition which tends to be in the middle of a fight. It feels a bit off.

- game difficulty might not be for everyone


bad points

- Lost Souls Expansion: AI cheats big times - which is the worst really - and the dialogues are too bland. There is more but
those are the main points.

- multiplayer: was a rollercoaster to get working and "building up and research" just takes really long. It just feels made for singleplayer. Counterplay is maybe not really given - if you research something your opponent hard-counters you won't have time to catch up and change unit production in time.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played the main game and its 1st expansion back when it originally came out and was mind blown how advanced it was. The amount of possibilities, ideas and for the time great graphics were simply remarkable. Everyone who likes RTS games or is a RTS developer should take a look and try out this (very) rough diamond. I made a full play-through during the last weeks and did not regret it for the most part.

However, before you do the same be aware that this game is hard, even on average difficulty settings. If you don’t know what to do and e. g. spend to much resources or lose too many troops you might not be able to see the end of a campaign. On the other hand this is one of the unique strengths of Earth 2150 - troops, resources and your research carry over through missions.

To make your life a bit easier consider these few tips:
1.) You can pause the game at any time with the „Num-lock“ key. In pause mode you can still give orders, build units, switch between bases etc. I can’t stress this enough => Use it!
2.) By pressing “Alt + G” on your keyboard a movement grid shows up which helps finding passage ways across the map easier - very useful, can also be used in pause mode
3.) There is a very well made mission guide by „axeil“ available here: https://lparchive.org/Earth-2150-trilogy/ check it if you don’t know how to progress. Or read it after you have done a mission as his descriptions are very entertaining.

If you buy this trilogy you get three games which build on each other, story-wise and I recommend to play them in order, if you are interested in the campaign. More details below.


Escape From The Blue Planet (Main Game):
The original game and in my opinion the best part. You play one of three factions where you are tasked to gain enough resources to escape earth from certain destruction. The kicker here is that you decide how much resources you donate to your escape and when. You practically can donate everything each mission but than you have nothing left for your army and new research. You also can’t take too much time as there is a time limit throughout the whole campaign.

The three factions you can play are very different from each other including their campaign missions. Worth trying them all out.


The Moon Project (1st Expansion):
This game was released close to the original game studio’s closure and therefore some of it feels rushed. A couple of missions and new units needed a bit more polish and balancing in my opinion, but it is still worth playing.

You play again as one of three factions in a side-story in parallel to the original plot of the main game. The campaigns are very entertaining and diverse, but lack any time pressure - however this might be a plus for you. New units and buildings were introduced which enable new tactics but on the flip side all factions feel more samey now...


Lost Souls (2nd Expansion):
Hardest and worst part of this trilogy. This game was made by a different studio and I only can imagine the development hell it went through. It needed a couple of more months of play testing.

The missions are super hard, as your enemies always start in a much better position with huge bases and insane defenses. On top of that 90% of all missions the enemy gets regular unlimited spawning high-end reinforcements and you can’t do anything about it. That’s simply cheating and sucks all the fun out of most missions… It is quite a shame as the campaign this time is split into three parts, one for each faction and they directly build on each other… unfortunately you might never experience it and I can’t blame you… seriously you will have more fun reading the guide I linked above by “axeil” instead of playing Lost Souls...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo this is a step up from the first at least, I'll give it that. There's actually a tutorial so I wasn't confused like I was on the first game. This game is kinda unique, you construct your units the same way you do in Warzone2100 and the unique part of this game is being able to switch to a main base while you're out doing other missions and send units and stuff of the like. First time I've seen that in an RTS.

Unfortunately, with it being older, there is quite a few problems in this game. The campaign is hard of course, the building of units is ridiculously slow for some reason, the camera angle is weird I don't like to be that zoomed in on everything. I wish I could zoom out more but I couldn't. Also in the first mission when I found enemies to slay, my guys eventually just stopped shooting them. My army would have taken out theirs but I lost the mission because of this (bug?) and for that I'm not recommending this game.

There is patches and mods that probably fix whatever issue I was having and to make this game a bit easier on the eyes but meh, I don't feel like tinkering with it. Its a unique RTS just not worth the trouble.\t\t\t\t\t

Except for graphics and animations this is better than it's sequel, Earth 2160, in nearly every way. The campaign for each faction having an overarching objective to collect enough resources to complete the game before the campaign finishes is a novel feature, and added a tension to the campaign you didn't get with other RTS's: sure you could build a huge army and steamroll your enemies but you're wasting those valuable resources in doing so.

That said, I can't recommend this version. It simply doesn't work. This worked under Windows 7 without issue, in Windows 10 it won't even launch, just saying the game isn't properly installed and to reinstall it. No matter how many time's I've tried, it just doesn't work. Apparently it's a bug with the installer not making registry entries correctly, that the game can then not find on boot. Attempting to make the registry entries for the game manually doesn't seem to work either.

Beyond that, if you do get it working, the game will crash whenever it attempts to play an in-game video. The game used to play short news and update clips using bink video, and would play them through a player that was integrated in to the game itself. It seems for whatever reason this player just doesn't work anymore so if a video gets called to play, it crashes the entire game.

There are community patches and workarounds available, but if you're willing to go this route save yourself some headaches and get the GOG version instead, which actually works, and you won't have to do as much messing around to play it.\t\t\t\t\t
