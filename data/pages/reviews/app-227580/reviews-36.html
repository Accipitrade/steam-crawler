\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUltimately nothing more than a grind
Rather than asking players to simply match icons on a board, the game is centered around a minimalistic dungeon-crawl shown at the top of the screen. Players aim to match sword and wand icons to attack monsters and keys to clear locked chests and doors. The variable value of tiles makes board management incredibly important. Players must constantly clear the shield, pack, wood, and stone icons that are of lesser use during the run in order to seed the board with useful tiles-- especially weapons as monsters can attack the player, costing more precious time. Defeating monsters quickly is both necessary and frustrating as it adds a layer of luck to the game: it is possible to happen upon a long string of monsters without the compartively easier chests and doors, effectively dooming a run since the player doesn't have time to manage the board.
Between dungeon runs, the game gives gold, stone, wood, and experience, all of which are used to level-up your strength and defenses. However, none of the upgrades ultimately change the way the game is played, neither increasing the complexity or even effecting the difficulty due to the increasing strength of monsters. Instead, upgrades are a brick wall that serve to force a particular rate of progression upon the player. Players cannot progress into the deeper levels of the dungeon until they grind out enough resources to upgrade their stats. Instead of barring players based upon skill, players are barred based upon time investment.
The way the value of tiles changes based upon the scenario presented in the dungeon makes the game decent, but the title ultimately turns out to be more of a grind than anything else due to its simple gameplay and leveling system.\t\t\t\t\t
