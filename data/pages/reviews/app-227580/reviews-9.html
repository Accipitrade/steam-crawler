10,000,000 is a modern classic among match-3 games. it might not look like much, as graphics are just about acceptable, but the music's pretty good and it definitely has that 'one more run' feel to it, can be very addictive. it also has windowed mode (just the one fixed size) and separate volume settings.

you start out as a rookie with a goal to get 10 million points in a single run, so it's not cumulative, but not as impossible as it may seem initially either. gameplay involves matching all sorts of tiles for the desired effect by moving entire rows or columns, so not the more typical flip the neighbors situation.

match wood and stone to get resources, keys to open doors and chests for items and gold, swords and wands to attack monsters, shields to defend, and if you beat baddies, you'll get xp as well. when (and not if) you die, it's back to the beginning, but resources, gold, xp and even items are kept between runs, and eventually you'll get the chance to rank up by reaching a specified score. unfortunately, reaching said score before it becomes an objective doesn't make it unlock retroactively.

higher ranks mean tougher monsters and trickier objectives, but also bigger multipliers and rewards, so it's best to play on the highest available rank. worst case, you'll end up with some resources to spend. objectives include creating a number of specific matches (5-in-a-row, etc.), opening a number of chests, defeating numbers, often in specific ways, using a number of items, etc. and they're worth doing for the rewards.

items can be direct damage weapons, scrolls to turn random tiles into swords, wands or keys, skeleton keys that open two locks at a time, and food, which lets you move forward a bit. the game is real-time, quick matching required, and every enemy attack pushes you back a little. if they push you off the screen, game over, but if you reach the other end, you can keep playing, though it's not always easy to make out what you're up against off-screen.

technically, there are some tactics to use, like clearing wood and stone between encounters to have more weapons and shields, or not wasting keys when you can see a door or chest behind the current enemy (or vice versa, not wasting weapons while trying to open a chest), plus of course trying to align everything for bigger matches, but I found that random matching often works just as well and can create quite a cascade or chain reaction.

as I said above, getting millions of points to rank up and eventually escape the dungeon might seem impossible at the beginning, that's where permanent upgrades come into the picture. besides the bed you rest in between runs, the dungeon has 5 rooms to unlock and upgrade with wood and stone. the trainer gives useful permanent upgrades for xp (cheaper upgrade costs, more effective items, more damage, the all-important 4th inventory slot, etc.), there's one room to upgrade your sword, staff, shield and armor respectively, all with gold, and the alchemist offers modifiers.

the alchemist is the only room that doesn't need upgrading, instead further potions are unlocked by ranking up. e.g. once you have all the rooms upgraded, you won't need more wood and stone, so you can turn 20% of them into xp and/or gold, or get a higher score but also make enemies tougher, so it's always a bit of a balancing act.

it was a surprisingly short game, I managed to complete it in less than 5 hours, but of course rng can make it shorter or longer, plus I only reached 10 million on hero rank with the highest multiplier and you can try it with every potion enabled or starting at lower ranks, and keep playing even after the exit opens if you want. I enjoyed my time with it, it was a bit of mindless fun for a few evenings, but wouldn't recommend paying full price for it, probably not even half.\t\t\t\t\t

10,000,000 is a match-three game in a dungeon crawler skin, and also a sort of endless runner. The gameplay consists in matching different pieces as your character runs through a randomly generated dungeon. If you face an enemy, match swords; if you find chests, match keys, and so on. It's not really endless per se, as sooner or later you'll hit a wall and won't be able to progress until you grind for upgrades.

Grind that axe, Eugene

First off, I'm a weird gamer. I'm one of those who enjoy grinding (for real!), but the grinding in this game is so forced, unfun and disgusting that I gave up. A regular run will take you about two minutes (or five minutes if you're lucky and end up getting a good board). Now, you may think that six hours played is too little, but think about how many two minutes runs I had. Now let me tell you: I feel like I'm not even near the endgame. I tried to enjoy 10,000,000 as a podcast game, but honestly, it feels like work. There are many other interesting podcast games out there.

If you want to give it a try, wait for a huge sale

I got this game for 90% off, so I got my money's worth. While I feel bad giving a negative review for an indie developer, on the other hand I feel bad for those who might waste their time and patience with this game. Even if you intend to play on mobile, there are much better match-three games out there, including free ones. Sorry dev, but you missed the mark here.

Positives:

* Easy to pick up and play
* Nice soundtrack and sound effects

Negatives:

* Skill won't matter too much
* So grindy that even a grindy gamer can't approve it
* The gameplay is too chaotic for a match-three game
* You will achieve little by little, but in the end it isn't fun\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm normally a bit of a snob when it comes to match-3 type games. They're always categorised as 'puzzles', but really they just test your ability to recognise patterns rather than presenting any kind of brain teaser. Despite this, I really enjoyed 10,000,000...

10,000,000 is a bit different. Depending on your tile-matching performance, your sprite will advance through a dungeon facing off against enemies or unlocking doors & chests. You can't blindly match either; locks will need key tiles to be matched, you may need to use items to get out of situations if you can't match the right tiles - you have to pay attention both to the tile board and the dungeon.

The RPG elements and wide variety of goals kept the experience well above tedium. There is some significant grinding to do at certain points, but its so satisfying to either hit a new high-score or kill a boss you haven't before that I found myself happily grinding to afford upgrades.

The music is also very cool. There's only really 2 pieces, one for the safety of home, another for the dungeon, but the dungeon theme is a Muse-esque chiptune rocker that makes matching tiles feel weirdly high octane. The game also has a great sense of humour, particularly in the tips which include 'If you make a game, don't call it 10,000,000'.

I liked it! Plus I think I got it for 79p, so, why not?\t\t\t\t\t
