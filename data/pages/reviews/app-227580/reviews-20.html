\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a puzzle and strategy enthusiast, I've played my fair share of games and it's always a treat when a game brings something new to the table. Deviating from the typical 'Match 3' formula, you may find snapshots of the game misleading--the tiled board looks just like any other matching game--but 10000000 has a surprise for you: You're not going to move the tiles. You're going to move the BOARD.

'Hah!', I hear you scoff. 'That doesn't seem like much of a difference.' That's what I thought, too, until I played it. Manipulating the board this way bends your brain in a completely different direction, as you cannot simply adjust one tile at a time. Every move you make will affect the entire playing field in ways you may not be able to predict at first and it adds new complexity as you struggle to speed through each encounter, which brings me to the other half of the gameplay:

You will encounter various monsters with different resistances and weaknesses, locks to pick, chests to open, and all the while the clock is working against you to clear the precise tiles you need to beat each challenge in a matter of seconds. It comes with an RPG element that rewards your accomplishments and encourages you even after a defeat and, on the whole, it makes for a very engaging experience. As for flaws, the music can become a bit droning after awhile and it's likely to become stuck in your head very quickly, and a few cracks show in the few scattered misspellings (you're going to be 'poisioned' quite a bit), but on the whole, it's a good game.

I feel like the developers missed an opportunity by not including more for the player to do. Sure, you can keep playing it as long as you like, trying to raise your high score, but since the game revolves around meeting certain score or endurance goals, once you have completed all of said goals and related achievements, it feels like there's no further challenge. It's just about right for the price and should provide several hours of entertainment.\t\t\t\t\t
