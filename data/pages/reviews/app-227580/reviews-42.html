\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me just say that I only BARELY recommend this game, and that you would really do yourself a service getting this game while it's on sale or something.

Main premise:
The point of this game is to reach a score of 10,000,000. This is done by playing a Chuzzle-esque tile match game wherein the tiles you match has an outcome on a dungeon battle. If you by example matches 3 tiles of a sword the guy attacks, 3 keys and you can open chests and doors, 3 wood or stone and you get resources to upgrade later in the game. The game include upgrades, shops, potions, bosses, and a suprisingly big number of valuables (experience, wood, stone, points, and money).

Graphics/Sound:
The game originated on phones and tablets and you can definitely tell that by a lot of factors. First of all the graphics are in a very heavy and sometimes grating 8-bit style and the sounds and music are entirely in chiptunes. The chiptunes are not bad by any means, but they're put on a short loop and they can get rather repetitive and annoying. It's not a game that needs to be great looking or sound amazing. It does its job, with average success.

Gameplay:
This is a grinding puzzle game. The main point is to get as far as possible and then retry again and again, until you can buy more upgrades and get a little further into the game until you reach the target score. This is a slow moving process. There really isn't much progress in terms of mechanics. All mechanics of the game is presented in the first 30-45 minutes and then the question is if you actually want to continue doing the same thing for a few hours.

The main problem here, is that the game does a very poor job at spacing apart and adding game mechanics as you go along so everything feels fresh. This makes the game stagnate pretty quickly, and you get bored by it easily. You can only play this game in short bursts.

This game has two parts to it; the action-puzzle part, and the management/upgrade/RPG-ish part. The first part, is mostly pretty well executed. The tiles do what they do, and you can most of the time tell that a bad run was mostly because that you didn't do well. The game can be a bit unfair in the distribution of different tiles, giving you 3 key tiles in total, when you really need it, but other than that, the nature of the game makes it difficult enough to be challenging, but forgiving enough to not be frustrated.

The problems with the game originates from the second part, which is poorly executed. It has the growing of character RPG elements. You know, you start out as a weakling, but then you become stronger and stronger the more you play. Except that this game makes you not feel like progressing at all. It's hard to describe, but I got the same feeling out of playing Defiance. Despite constantly leveling up and progressing to the next level, and fighting tougher enemies and buying more upgrades and improving your various weaponry, it doesn't really feel like you're making a difference. Well, maybe a little, but 2 hours in the game, you don't feel that your character has gotten that much stronger since last. I speculate, that this is because that the core game (the tile game) does not become easier or harder, only the outcome becomes greater.

This, together with repetitious gameplay, makes the game stale after longer amounts of play.

Story:
This game does not really have a story, and for a game like this, it's fine without.

Replayability/Concept:
I really like the concept, and I feel that with a bit more tweaking and tuning this game could have been a lot more fun and a lot more addictive, which it isn't now, because of the repetitive nature of the game. There is also a bit of originality by this concept, mixing two genres together only really done well in PuzzleQuest.
Other than achievements, there is no real reason to start over and try again.

Length and fun factor:
To be honest, I haven't beaten it yet, but judging by where I am at right now, I estimate a total play time of 3-6 hours.
I find the core game (the tile puzzle game) to be very entertaining. It's hectic and it's stimulating, however it's hard finding joy in having to do this, let's be honest here, primitive game over and over again just to feel a bit of evolution and progress.

Final Thoughts and judgement:
If you are a big puzzle fan, especially of things like Chuzzle, PuzzleQuest, Bejeweled or even Candy Crush, and looking for a simple tile game with even simpler RPG elements over it, then this is recommendable. The puzzle aspect is fun, and if you're into that, the RPG part will not totally ruin it for you. For anyone else this is hard to recommend. For what it's worth I have found entertainment in this game, but some of the core aspects are flawed.

If you like puzzle games and you see this on a sale it couldn't hurt to get this (and therefore earning my thumbs up). There are just much better alternatives.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t10,000,000 is one of those rare games that tries to mix RPGs, autorunners, and puzzles.

It's one of the many that completely fails to be any good at all 3.

First, the RPG elements are clunky. They explain it once, but then give you little to no way to actually improve. You'll always fight monsters the same way with the same spells. And considering the barebones graphics, music, and the looming end, it adds virtually nothing to the gameplay other than an annoying distraction and an indicator of what you now need to match.

Secondly, the autorunning nature feels more like an arbitrary way to make it difficult without ever having to change anything on the field or in the game. A vague "end" keeps chasing you. When it reaches you or passes you or some other vague idea of "you reach it" that seems to change from game to game, you lose. Except you can keep matching for a few seconds.

And then there's the puzzle elements. They're, at best, clumsy. The game will not simply suggest a certain chain, it all but enforces it. See a skeleton? Get those staff and sword blocks. None of those? Fine, I GUESS get some resources. Oh there's a sword. Ignore that double/chained set, just get that sword or the looming end will get you! But that's not the worst of it. Oh no. The WORST of it, my friends, is that when you scroll it around the screen...it makes no difference if there are 3 blocks. They must be blocks that were initially placed in a way to complete a chain. If you scroll it around and there's 4 swords and you need four swords? Sorry, only the wood resource that had a 3 piece chain will count. Nothing else. This seems like a drastic oversight. Even the likes of Tetris Attack on the SNES would let you change blocks with no outcome, thus allowing a deeper gameplay. This offers no depth in that sense. It has to be already on screen, already apparent, and already set up.

All of this combines to create a tedious but inoffensive puzzle experience. But what makes it truly heinous is how many times you repeat a level to complete challenges to get resources to unlock do-Hold on, champ! You flawlessly performed all those tasks, but you need 500 wood and 500 stone for the next door! You only have 300 wood and 120 stones! Nope, get back to the time sink.


All in all, I cannot recommend 10,000,000. It is a clunky, rushed, and overall ugly game that seems to hope to garner more love off the Atari-era graphics than any actual quality or effort.\t\t\t\t\t

In-Depth:
10,000,000 is a match three game mixed with a lot of RPG elements. By collecting materials you are able to build and repair your base allowing you to buff your character and slowly make your way to the end of the game.

Unfortunately this is where the game falls short in my opinions as it then becomes a grind just so you can finish the game. The graphics are quirky and the music is pretty nice, it even has a mobile version. Definitely a very casual game to pick up from time to time and play while waiting for something else.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t6/10

This game earns an "A" for being a clever blend of genres, but a "D" by poorly balancing them it makes the game repetitive, unrewarding, and leaving the player wanting for more.

It is a really great concept and fun to play at first. If it was play tested to adjust the poor "grinding" curves, it could be an addictive smash hit.

Problems and improvements:

1. Resorces... The game has a great concept with harvesting materials to unlock new "shops" to upgrade your character, but wood/stone become obsolete as soon as these shops are all open (which can be done in the first couple hours of game play). Even worse are "Experience Points", which are only spendable at one store. You quickly buy all these upgrades in the first couple hours of gameplay, leaving you a lot of game play earning useless points with nothing to spend them on. Gold is the last to go. After you spend gold upgrading everything to its max, you find that you can't upgrade any farther, and you're still only in the first half of the game.
- To fix this, the game needs more options for upgrades with a better price curve to make sure there is always something more to buy or upgrade. The game should be complete or close to completion before you run out of upgrade options in any rpg.

2. Effectiveness... Upgrades, even when all are purchased in the first half of the game, do not do enough to make your character feel more powerful or effective. Grinding for upgrades is hard enough, but these upgrades should make even the worst player more effective at the game.
Items, if used too early, will be wasted (The same goes for matches that are lined up too early). This is counter-intuitive, since every second counts. When approaching an enemy or chest, it would be great if your continuing multi-combo or clicked item was aimed at them instead of simply lost. Also, while just 4 keys in a row will take care of 2 locks, 4, 5, or more staff/sword tiles do little more than 3 against enemies.

3. The Clock... The clock is also your health bar, which is fine and the shield idea makes it work well enough. However, there is far too much working against the clock and not enough helping it. You only gain a small amount of time back after defeating an enemy or unlocking a door/chest (or matching wood/stone with an upgrade), there is a lot that wastes time like combos, which continue to fire off leaving you helpless to act until they are done. These combos, while sometimes helpful, usually just eat time and give little back outside score (which is really only helpful in reaching the overall 10,000,000 to beat the game. Keep in mind, score is NOT cumulative, and is only there to let you know how close you are to getting to the end of the game). Chain combos and 4+ matches should offer a small time bonus as well.

4. Luck factor. I have no problem with the Critical hit probability, but the luck of having the right combination of tiles in anything close to the right order to get what you need to match up in time is very random. I've had great runs where I've just had great luck, and blaze through every obstacle, and I've had luck where I lose on the first enemy due to this.

5. Gear... There are unlockable potions you an use to trade one thing for another (i.e. 20% of wood/stone earned becomes gold but you earn 0 wood/stone ). These can be turned on and off. The rest of the gear, although upgradable, has no options like many RPGs do. It would be nice if you could buy upgrades for gear, but also swap them out for different effects (i.e. a staff that uses Freeze when it crits, or armor that has a lower effects against close range enemies but blocks ranged attacks, etc.).

6. Curve... It could use a difficulty setting. Even if that meant just adjusting time limit, damage effects, etc.
Not just that, the overall "Grinding" takes the fun out of the game.

Final Verdict:
- It's not worth $5. I got my copy on sale for $1 and had cash in my steam wallet from selling trading cards, so... it was basically "free". I wouldn't suggest it for retail price.
- If it had the above adjustments made, It would be worth the $5, maybe even $10.
- While it has great roots, and is technically a finished game, it really lacks the polish to make it feel like more than a "free to play" you'd find on NewGrounds.

6/10\t\t\t\t\t
