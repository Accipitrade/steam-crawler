\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI got this game on a whim during it's 0.99 cent sale. At the time, I was suffering from a very painful toothache, which was almost dehibilitating. I wanted something I could do that didn't require focus, so I figured I'd try this game. Two things of note; one, my estimate for not requiring focus was completely off-base. Two, I played it from install to near completion in one 5 hour sitting. My toothache was an afterthought. I have no more praiseworthy sentence than that.

The concept is simple; it's a match-3 game. Even worse, it's my least favorite kind of match-3 game; a move has to end with 3 or more matching. Nothing akin to, say, Tetris Attack, where you could meticulously plot out an amazing chain, one block move at a time. So why would I recommend this game, even when the first two paragraphs of this review are faintly condemning it?

Because it's amazing, that's why. It takes cue from other match-3 games with the simple but addictive gameplay, and then adds it's own finess to the mix. You're exploring a dungeon, and your actions are based on matching tiles. Clear 3 Key tiles to open a lock on a treasure chest, match 3 Sword tiles to swing a sword at an enemy, you get the idea. Run the dungeon as long as you can, slaying enemies, collecting loot, and get as far as you can. Strive for that 10,000,000-point mark.

What makes this work so well is it's so frantic. At any given time, you're matching in the grid, while watching your inventory of special, one-shot items, while at the same time plotting for the next encounter. Fighting a dragon? Forget those keys, those wood, you need lots and lots of attacks. At a chest? Those swords do you no good, you're waisting them as only keys will open the locks. The most stressful moments for me were the time between encounters; "I have a five-chain of attacks lined up, I hope it's not a chest or door next..."

The game rewards you for each attempt, too. Clearing wood tiles and stone tiles give you those in currency form, you collect gold and EXP from felled monsters and opened chests, and you can refurbish and upgrade your impromptu-home with facilities. Not got enough 'umph' behind your swings? Upgrade your sword at the smithy.

It's a repetitive cycle to be sure, but there's no greater joy than building up a horde of gold from your failed runs, pumping up your sword's destructive might, and then laying waste to enemy after enemy with your newfound strength. However, the cycle doesn't continue forever, and once you hit max, that's it. The game will get no easier from there, and all that's left is you and your 10m goal.

It's certainly not without it's own flaws though. Early on, the runs are frantic and short. Very, very short. Even in the tutorial run, you won't completely grasp what's going on or even how to plan things out because it's ALREADY OVER. It takes running after running to finally get a feel for the game, and then it goes out of it's way to make you attempt sometimes rediculous maneuvers to advance. These goals include simple things (get a certain score, get a certain amount of EXP), to difficult without planning things (defeat a ranged mob in less than 5 seconds) to downright infuriating (use a scroll to get a triple clear in one shot, seriously? No amount of planning will get you that). These shortcomings I don't nessicarily say are the game's fault, rather, the intencely random nature of this brand of match-3 makes them far harder than need be.

When all is said and done, it's welcoming enough for beginners (given some time to adjust), and has enough depth and breadth for hardcore gamers to get a good kick out of it. At 0.99 cents, this was a steal of a game. The full-fledged pricetag of 4.99 is still low for the enjoyment you'll get out of this game. Expect to clock in at least 5 hours of good, solid gameplay before completing, and based on how much the random number god loves (or hates) you, another 3 hours until 100% completion. Even then, it's just fun to do a quick dungeon tromp to burn some time and some dragons.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t10,000,000 is a primitive gauntlet running match 3 puzzle game. Your mission is to ultimately score 10,000,000 points to free yourself from the dungeon, but you have to purchase a bunch of upgrades using gold and experience points before you will be able to achieve that goal. Upgrades are available for your sword, staff, armor, and shield. Plus you can buy additional training attributes through earned experience points. Then there are the potions which have both positive and negative effects that you must weigh to determine whether to activate them or not.

The gameplay consists of frantically matching gems to attack or unlock doors and chests in an effort to keep your character from hitting the left edge of the screen. Your character continues running to the right as long as you match the right gems to vanquish your enemies or open doors and chests.

There is no polish on this game whatsoever. The concept is nice, but the game itself is pretty cheesy. However, for a buck, is does provide reasonable entertainment value.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is just sliding tiles. It tries to seem like more with the RPG elements but that is little more than a skin. The game rewards you for making as many matches as possible, as fast as possible, regardless of what is happening in the top section. Sure, you have to match the right tiles to proceed past each obstacle, but that happens naturally through frantic matching. If you try to play the board strategically you'll find things simply don't happen fast enough and you'll fail the level because time is always ticking down and you HP usually is too. The whole experience feels like it was designed by a gambling cartel trying to get me addicted to slots.

The upgrade system was compelling at first but quite quickly became a chore and no longer enhances the experience after a few hours. There is a little strategic freedom in how you spend your resources on upgrades and perks but it really doesn't affect gameplay, it just shifts difficulty from one element to another. There is also an arbitrary wait while upgrades are occurring in which you can't do anything but watch the progress bar. This wait increases with each upgrade for no reason. It isn't long but it is completely unnecessary. The investment type perks are pointless because they effectively just adjust how much grinding is necessary and players won't know how much grinding they want to do until they've missed the opportunity for the investment to be economically viable. I honestly think they game would be better served by automating or removing the entire upgrade system and just implementing a few sliders so players can adjust how good they have to be at tile matching to not lose.

The only thing which is still interesting after a few hours is the challenges. There are at most 3 challenges active at any time and new challenges aren't made available until space is made for them by completing an existing challenge. They are things like 'Kill an armored monster with only staves' and 'Open a chest within 5 seconds'. Challenges provide a reason to adjust strategy which helps stop the game becoming stale, however I'm fairly frequently stuck on (a) challenge(s) and the game stops being fun at all. Completing an overly difficult challenge is a relief, not a reward, because a combination of grinding and luck is what gets me there. An option to skip challenges would fix this but might reduce the worth of the challenge system.

To put it simply, if you're expecting anything more from the game than a pretty good tile matching time-waster you might be disappointed. I think there is potential in the format but a lot more weight needs to be given to the RPG elements for me to enjoy this as a hybrid puzzle RPG and not just a puzzle game with a guy running at the top of the screen.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWell well well, where to start with this game?

Awhile back I heard about 10,000,000 and I was looking forward to getting my hands on it to play, after I got it, I spent 2 or 3 hours playing it nonstop, soon after my play session ended I relised I felt really weird, so I tryed to stand up, and I felt so disorentated dizzy to almost the point where I felt intoxicated, I couldnt understand it.

That aside, the time spent playing 10,000,000 has had its frustraitons, but it was very fun, what ever it may be, getting huge combos going, to the silly hints, everything about it brings such a retro feel, the music and audio compliments the game.

In a nutshell, the top side scroller looking area of the screen is a cleverly disguised timer and health bar. Your character runs through a dungeon, and all of his actions are controlled by the blocks you match. Need to unlock a door or a chest? Match some keys. A monster blocking your path? Physical attacks are executed by matching swords while magical attacks fire off by matching staves. Standing still by not matching causes the timer to tick down, pushing your character to the left, and getting hit by monsters also pushes you to the left. The damage aspect can be mitigated by matching shields, which are absorbed instead of pushing your character. Fall off the side of the screen and it's game over.

To supplement these mechanics are a set of shockingly deep loot, item, upgrade, and experience systems. Monsters can drop loot, and you can also get loot by matching backpack tiles or unlocking chests. Loot can either be gold to buy upgrades or actual usable items. A few items you'll come across are a red orb that'll damage whatever monster is in front of you, a key to instantly unlock a chest or doorway blocking your path, scrolls to spawn more tiles of a certain type on the board, and much more.

Every time you start up your game and get ready to explore, your character lierally Jumps out of bed and is ready to rock, I first noticed the score and the goal, which reminded me of one of those Awful motivation posters that were en vogue in the 90's and acts like one too.

You really need to think about the games mechanics, because 10000000 doesn't tell you much beyond the qwerky loading hits, and leave you on your own to explore, I find that this works perfecly with short playt sessions, and adds a sense of discovery that I found fun.

If you have one of your 10,000,000 runs that lasts more than a minute or two you are doing considerably well, it gives you a chance to catch your breath and break down what you learned during your last attempt to reach your goal of 10,000,000.

This is a game with a protestant work, and you have to Aceept failure as part of the Journey, and try your best to do a little better every time.

I have really enjoyed my time so far playing 10,000,000 and I hope everyone else can have some fun with it too.

I would rate 10,000,000 a:
4 out of 5
As it does what its intended too exactly as it should.\t\t\t\t\t
