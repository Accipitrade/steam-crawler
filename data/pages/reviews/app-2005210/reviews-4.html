Indie developers are, for the most part, the silent center of attention behind big AAA developers. They say little, but flex their muscles and keep their eyes alert, and sooner or later, a few of them will unleash a game that will surprise the gaming public.

That's why it's interesting and even a little unsettling to find a game that's so addictive that I have to wonder why this idea hasn't really caught on before. To be quite honest, I didn't expect the Void Scrappers to sweep those who've played it off their feet, but that's what the gameplay does. And it does it with a certain rugged grace.

I wrote my initial impressions of the game on a piece of paper, noting the following, "it is a simple game, wrapped in addiction, inside a simple premise." The gameplay fascinates you at the moment and getting from one level to the next is tricky, but it does it so that you're almost oblivious to time.

One Man's Trash...

The story in Void Scrappers is hardly original, but I do know that in a parallel universe the galaxy as a whole has gone to the garbage and that scraps of metal are the most prized commodities. The only problem is that in order to obtain scraps, you'll need to blast them out of the enemy ships.

In short, the more enemy ships you destroy, the more scraps you can collect. Like all games of this genre, Void Scrappers' premise is for the player to survive for as long as possible against wave after wave of relentless enemy attacks.

Of course, with every level, there's the obligatory end-level boss who's a bit faster and stronger than the rest and can take much more damage than most enemy ships combined.

Fortunately, you can upgrade your ships in two ways: collect enough scraps to power up your basic traits or defeat bosses to level up your weapons. Either way, you'll need every upgradeable path to take on the most hardened of bosses later on in the game.

Space Jockey

Character selection is robust and you have a choice of different pilots to choose from, who in turn, possess different skills and attributes, as well as personalities. There's not much information revealed about these hired guns which adds a deeper sense of mystery and intrigue.

In their younger days, I presume, they may have been law enforcement officers, happily spending their time handing out tickets and enforcing the rule of law upon the galaxy.

Now, they are millions of miles from home, casually sitting in a grungy-filled bar waiting for their next job with their looks indicating that they've seen better days.

To the developer's credit, Void Scrappers is a more entertaining game, to me at least, than Vampire Survivors, because it takes place in space which provides a deeper challenge since you can incorporate other variables to make the game more challenging, such as asteroids, meteors, and space debris.

Even though none of these are present in the game, there's the possibility that they can be added later via a sequel or DLC.

Don't Avoid the Void

The weight of the game obviously falls on the shoulders of gameplay. Here, I feel Void Scrapper has better combat strategies because it paces itself slower and more deliberately than other rogue-like games out there.

While other titles will try to overwhelm you from the start, Void Scrappers eases you ever so slowly until you've leveled up enough to stand a fighting chance.

Good movies also mimic this approach and it should be no surprise that good games follow. It's this type of pacing that provides good-honest fun because doing it in reverse would just spoil and totally ruin the build-up.

Overall, Void Scrappers is definitely a game worth getting, especially if you're a fan of fast-paced shooters. And at just under $5, you'll certainly be getting much more value here than with most games that cost considerably more.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've played this for little under 12 hours, got all the achievements, and unlocked most of the upgrades/weapons, so I think I can give you an idea of whether this is worth your time and money.

I'll clear the obvious out of the way first- this game isn't Vampire Survivors. They look similar, but there are some key differences that really do make them entirely different playing experience. At some point in Vampire Survivors you'll have unlocked so many powerful items and so many ways to build the exact build you want, that you can basically go AFK; you can't really do that in this game. Void Scrappers is more akin to the old Asteroid title in that you are pushed to keep moving due to the constant influx of stuff flying at you. You either dodge or die. This breeds a very interesting playing experience at the start where people in the next room over might hear the audible clenching of your b-hole as you just barely scrapped out of a dogpile of enemy ships.

It's exciting! At first...

The problem is that as you continue playing, the game really does get bogged down by the same-ness of each run. You'll quickly discover that orbital weapons are mostly trash, unless you're using a specific character- the same can be said of lasers and most projectile weapons as well. Explosive weapons are the best, but that's mostly because they require the least amount of upgrades to be effective. For example, with projectile weapons you'll want to prioritize damage, but you'll also need projectile range and cooldown on top of special upgrades like %pierce, boomerang, projectiles on dash, and enemies explode into projectiles on death. Explosives require three upgrades to be effective- damage, size, and cooldown.

Seeing this, the easiest way to guarantee a satisfying win each run is to go explosives. There are a lot of other choices, really interesting choices like projectiles exploding on hit or the various dash abilities, but they require such diverse and heavy investment in mods (mods that you aren't guaranteed to get) that you're more than likely to end up with a build that just isn't that satisfying- although, you'll still probably "win" as long as you've upgraded your permanent damage bonus.

All of that said, the game is still good. It's not Vampire Survivors good, but it's still good. For $3, it's worth a buy. The controls feel snappy and, in my experience, you'll never feel like damage you took wasn't deserved. Every character has an invincibility dash, that you can upgrade, to get you out of danger, as well as unique quirks that change the way you may want to play. The graphics are... Fine. They're not amazing, but they fit the theme. And it's $3.

Overall, I recommend it. If you've finished Vampire Survivors or looking for a more interactive version of the Survivor genre, this is something to check out.

Side note, there is the option to turn off enemy bullets- a great accessibility option for those that have a hard time discerning enemy fire in a sea of flashing stuffs... Or those that are just trying to grind out the last few achievements without swearing too much.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA fun shooter with a sense of progression, with an auto-targeting system that works well at prioritizing enemies and includes a lot of satisfyingly screen-clearing attacks, once the right upgrades are applied. The characters and ships feel different enough to be worth trying out, and the game doesn't require the player to beat the game with each and every one, only progress to the highest threat level where the "final boss" appears. With a really good build, it is, of course, possible to go endless, beyond this boss fight.

No game is perfect. Okay, maybe Portal 2, but... no, no, I'm sticking to my guns here. No game is perfect. Some of the upgrades on offer, for collecting scrap from destroyed enemies, are more impressive than others, with explosives by far seeming like the easiest strategy to pursue. So even with the unlockable ability to banish certain upgrades and reroll weapon drops, there's some reliance on RNG. A run where certain powerful cards appear very early will be vastly easier for it. And some of the character unlock conditions may take a dedicated run to fulfill.

But by the time any of this really becomes an issue, the player can be several hours in, with the benefit of improved skill at dodging and a host of permanent upgrades. So at a minimum, reaching the endgame with all the available ships and pilots, and eventually beating the "final boss" with one or more, is a good time, and quite addicting.

Verdict: 9 interventions out of 10, would get hooked again\t\t\t\t\t

It's. so. boring. and. easy.

Too easy. Too much random shots in your projectiles removing your sense of control. Bosses just auto-follow you and are bullet sponges with no unique mechanics.

Just boring. Almost fell asleep playing it on my 2nd run. After 20 minutes on my first run I asked, why am I still playing this round? I hate to refund a $4 game, but this is just too boring and doesn't hit the reverse bullet hell.\t\t\t\t\t
