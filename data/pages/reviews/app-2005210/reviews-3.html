\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVoid Scrappers is a "Bullet Heaven"-type action shoot'em'up rogue-lite that might remind one of a souped-up game of Asteroids. While limited content-wise compared to other games I have played like Vampire Survivors and Army of Ruin, Scrappers is fun, intense, and like a number of other such titles, very cheaply priced.

As one of a number of quirky spacefaring mercenaries, take on increasingly large hordes of enemy spacecraft, collect their scrap leftovers, level up, gain new abilities and passive bonuses, and periodically fight a boss that will drop a new weapon for your ship. Along the way, fulfill specific challenges to unlock new characters, weapons and upgrades.

The first thing you may notice about Scrappers is that you are far more fragile then in Vampire Survivors. Your ship has three pips of shield, and after losing those, the next hit will kill you. One character, Remington "Double" Dagger, has no shields at all- but gains double damage for all attacks in exchange for being a one-hit-point-wonder. While he is the character most suited for defeating the final boss, getting there will take some practice and upgrades.

Unlike Vampire Survivors, there is seemingly no limit to the number of different (or similar, as you can take a given weapon more then once) weapons and passive upgrades, called "Scrap Ups," you can obtain in a given run, and evolving a weapon only requires that you obtain three of the same type of weapon. Weapons come in bullet, laser, explosive, and orbital drone varieties, with some playable characters having bonuses to a certain weapon type. You can make an effective build of any type on any character however, provided you know which Scrap Ups are more useful and make use of weapon rerolls and "banishing" Scrap Ups to narrow the pool of options in your favor. According to the one available steam guide at this time of writing, explosive weapons are generally the best option and my experience suggests the same.

Like Vampire Survivors, powerups occasionally appear somewhere in the level, with an indicator showing you where they are. However, the only two such items in Scrappers are the usualmagnet that attracts all existing scrap XP drops to you, and a shield item that restores one point of shield to you. A certain Scrap Up changes all shield drops to instead be additional weapon drops, which can be highly effective if you can manage without the healing.

You recieve a certain number of credits at the end of a run which can be used to purchase permanent upgrades for future runs. There are fewer types of these then in other titles but they are useful nonetheless. The guide recommends focusing on XP gain and weapon cooldown upgrades first and I concur.

If you're like me and have essentially hundred-percented other games like this and are jonesing for more survivors action, Void Scrappers may assist in tiding you over.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe top positive view is.. just flat out fabricated. I started playing, and.. how the actual hell can they claim this has more build variety than vampire survivors? Void Scrappers literally only has four weapon types (projectile, laser, orbital, and eventually explosive), with varying aiming and attack speed the only difference between different individual weapons within the type. In a given run, you get copies of each weapon until it becomes an "ultimate" and then.. you keep collecting the same exact weapon. Even if you think VS only has a handful of builds, which is a bit of a stretch, void scrappers doesn't exactly have anything approaching variety in weapons, and its passives are similarly uninspired: just boring number upgrades to your weapons, dash or your XP & loot chances.

Even if you consider the build variety in the two games to be comparable (you're wrong, but whatever), there's only one map here, and it's somehow even less interesting than the default maps in Vamp - a flat, uninterrupted space background forever. The enemies are also extremely indistinguishable from one another - to the point that you will see bosses right next to regular enemies and they will be completely indistinguishable but for a health bar. The only area that you could reasonably argue there's better variety is in the character selection, as Vamp characters are really samey, and at least some of the characters in Void appear to have unique mechanics.

So no, it's not even close to the variety of Vampire Survivors. But is it a good game? Like.. I mean, it's four bucks. It's a servicable clone. But it's nothing special - there are a lot of other clones out there for the same price that at least try to do something interesting, but this isn't one of them. It's not BAD - the gameplay is clean for the genre, it doesn't suffer from the unreadable visuals that so many clones have, or bugs, or any real problems exactly. But would I recommend it? Not over any number of other more interesting vampire survivor clones. This is a space skin on a straightforward, low effort Vampire Survivor clone with nothing new to offer. After playing for 90 minutes, I don't feel compelled to play it over any other genre entry.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was originally going to give Void Scrappers a positive review, confident that the 30-50% of a game on display here was clearly in Early Access. Imagine my surprise when double checking the store page revealed that this was a full release, last updated a month ago with a capstone "mastered the game" character. I can't see the future so I'll have to review what we actually have.

There's less story here than bone reading a single sentence item description in a Souls-like, so moving on. Pick a pilot, dodge enemies, level up and get your damage checked by the frankly awful last boss. Pilots seem to have major gimmick differences, some thoughtful, some busted, others absurdly bad and not balanced in the least. Since the game is so simplistic and the enemy roster is lame even for a Vamp Survivors-alike there's ultimately no difference. Stack damage, sometimes you have a useless bonus and sometimes a busted +damage one. A shame post-release support has mostly been roster additions.

When you spawn you see the affiliation of the enemy sprite set but all enemies are 100% identical across factions, all enemies are 99% identical in function and behavior aside from a few lackluster projectiles identical in function to spawning tiny enemies, all bosses except the last are 100% identical in being meat blocks that chase you but move slower than you do. I mean the Dinosaur in a UFO does it too but he also spawns the only new enemies in the game, who are slightly more aggressive than the rest.

Yes, most Vampire Survivor games do the same in enemy design of identical blobs that approach you but they work in more enemy variation, boss variation, and most importantly even if the enemy pool has limited behaviors they go overboard on interesting and varied things the player themselves can do. The first two stages of early access Vamp has more surprises than the Void roster. I'd say the resolution is far too small as more than 75% of the hits I take playing the game come from an enemy spawning from off-screen directly in front of me at full speed. This seems to be the only danger in the game.

There are positives. The player ships control very well, weaving between enemies feels good and the Dash mechanic is a pretty innovative addition that helps differentiate flying a ship vs the other Vamp Survivor clone set-ups, a strength of the sci fi setting. Part distance creating dash, i-frame applying dodge roll and offensive or pick-up grabbing super dive, it really nails a feel of hitting the boosters. I wish VS had a lot more touches and things to do with mobility, it'd hugely separate it from the crowd in a good way. The OST has a few bangers though they repeat quickly and as dull as things are I do find the graphics appealing, my heart always warming to a chunky pixels 16 bit space ship sprite. Some of the weapon designs and upgrade paths are pretty clever but they're not really worth exploring since there are very obvious meta options. Since things aren't balanced you often make your upgrade pool weaker with every non-damage/meta option you unlock. VS has so little game to offer but manages to commit most of the classic roguelite design errors.

The negatives outweigh the positives and the absence of content outweighs everything. The idea that VS released in 1.0 seems like a joke. One "stage" as an infinite spanning space background with no features, terrain, obstacles, interactables, etc... no planets, asteroids(they added asteroids, now do that 10+ more times for content!), black holes, stars/suns, comets, things to orbit around, space stations, random occurrences. Just a flat void. The graphics are decent but they're not great, especially when you see details like weapons badly clipping when fired from some ships or the bit where the enemy space ship blob getting big enough makes them jostle in place like traffic jammed bumper cars, makes me laugh every time. The game seems barely able to contain the last boss on the screen and the devs give up if you defeat him, simply spamming more aggressive over-statted ships and virtually freezing scrap gain so you'll eventually die. Of course the ships started blocking each other and I had to stop moving to die. At this level of laziness it would've looked better to throw up A WINNER IS YOU and end the run.

I don't think it helps devs to criticize a game and give the "helpful" advice of "could you make more stuff?" but sadly it's 100% spot on here. It feels like 8bitskull got tired of this game before I did. Maybe if they came back to VS with an ambitious update schedule they could put out the other half of the game but as things are now Scrappers is a hard pass and frankly a throwback to the days of rushed SNES shovelware. $4 isn't a big ask to have a few hours of repetitive fun but the Vampire Survivors clone genre is already huge and I've already played three other heavy hitters out of dozens offered. Why settle for the bare minimum?\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat a blast! Void Scrappers is one of multiple titles that bring the bullet heaven formula into a spaceship-based shoot 'em up, in this case with a classic top-down view and complete 360 degrees freedom to boot. The handling feels snappy and precise, with even some momentum-based drifting that adds to the good feeling of movement, and it doesn't take long before the true "reverse bullet hell shooter" scenario emerges, with players spamming lasers, explosions, rockets and more against increasingly tough enemies, including wild bosses. The upgrade path could be a bit more varied, but the game is getting updated quite frequently, so be sure to keep an eye out on this one if you're a fan of bullet heaven and shmups and like.
