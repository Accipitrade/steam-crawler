\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an excellent adaptation of the Lords of Waterdeep boardgame. It has none of the major issues which usually plague most tabletop boardgame adaptations on PC or mobile platforms: The playfield is not horribly compressed or cluttered, there are no significant UI/control issues (which usually stem from a poor port from a mobile version), and the boardgame's sole expansion is present here in full.

The AI is fairly decent too, although as with most boardgame adaptations it won't trouble an experienced player that knows the rules, but it is a fun way to learn the game and practice for playing with others - and this is where this adaptation excels, when playing it with friends. In fact one of my few complaints with this adaptation is that you cannot add in Bot/AI players when playing with other players - the also-excellent adaptation of Raiders of the North Sea on Steam does allow you to put in AI players of different difficulty levels when playing with other human players, and it is appreciated as it adds in an unpredictable element to keep you on your toes, but sadly Lords of Waterdeep does not offer this feature.

One nice option in this version is that you can set a really long timer on a game (45 days maximum I think) so that you can play a game turn-by-turn over a long period inbetween doing other things, a bit like a play-by-mail or play-by-email games. Not many boardgame adaptations offer this option and it can be appreciated when playing with friends abroad who do not have enough time to finish a whole game in one sitting, or who you might have trouble synchronising your timetable with - as you can save the game state and both shut down the game, and play your turns alternately, independent of each other, even if you both cannot get online together at the same time.

My only other complaint with this adaptation is that if the player(s) build more than 5 buildings, you have to scroll to see the additional buildings, and cannot see them all without scrolling, even though there is enough space on a 1080p or higher PC monitor that they could easily have changed the layout to show all buildings on the same screen (max limit is 12 I think, no more than 9 per player). I suspect this is a limitation from the Android/iOS versions, where a smaller screen would have been more limiting to the layout.

Even if you don't own or know how to play the boardgame, if you have friends to play with then this is good fun if you like turn-based games. It's not too difficult to understand for those new to boardgames either, as the tutorial in this adaptation is good, and I have used the tabletop version as a "gateway game" for non-gamers before. It is not super-complex, but the decisions and strategy can be much deeper when you are familiar with the game and playing other human players, as there are take-that elements to interfere with each other's strategies, plus a little bit of deduction play in trying to work out which Lord your opponent has (randomly given to each player before a game) which will likely dictate which quests/cubes they want most and their endgame scoring.

I play a lot of boardgames, both digitally on PC and Android, and on tabletop, and this is one of the best tabletop-to-digital boardgame adaptation of the many I own (vying for top spot with the aforementioned Raiders of the North Sea, Ticket to Ride, Isle of Skye and Terraforming Mars on PC, and Castles of Mad King Ludwig which I only have on Android, of the digital boardgame adaptations I own and would recommend - all excellent adaptations).\t\t\t\t\t
