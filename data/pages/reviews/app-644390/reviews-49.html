\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's all good...almost.

LOVE the game, it's a tremendous worker placement game, and the fact that most of the real world games that you could argue are equal or better than LoW do NOT have a digital version, it makes this game even better.

When you can't find other people to play the game with you, the AI is challenging enough to make you work for victory, yet not so hard you feel the game is playing you.

Really, the ONLY knock I have against the game, is the hotseat. I love playing hotseat games, playing different sides, each trying to beat the other...my brain loves this stuff, been doing this for 20+ years...but the hotseat version of this game is sooooo frustrating. Okay, it works fine, the actual gameplay...but the REPLAY of everything that happened since the last turn...wow, that's annoying. Play a six-player game for 8 rounds, where you have to sit through a replay of everything that happened since the last turn, for EVERY player...makes the game last six times longer than it should.

I understand that if you were actually playing a hot seat game with multiple people sharing the same computer, this function lets you see what you missed since you last sat down...that's awesome. BUT, why not give the player the option to turn it off in the options or setting menu?

Just a suggestion.

GREAT GAME NONETHELESS!!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn a Nutshell
Lords of Waterdeep may turn off players looking for more strategic breadth as well as those who prefer confrontational gameplay. For those who enjoy a quick game of resource management with opportunities to mess up their opponents' plans, this game may be right up their alley. Recommended with reservations.

Gameplay
Lords of Waterdeep was created and published in America but is actually a "Eurogame." This means that unlike its "Ameritrash" counterparts, actions are not resolved with randomizers, and players cannot attack each other directly. Instead, players gather the resources they need to earn the most victory points by the end of the game.

Players take on the role of one of the masked lords of Waterdeep, a huge city in the Forgotten Realms setting of Dungeons & Dragons. The players take turns deploying a limited number of agents to gather various resources -- quest cards, gold, heroes, buildings, and intrigue cards. When completed, each quest may earn the player a number of victory points and possibly additional resources or even bonus effects that persist for the remainder of the game. Completing quests requires expending the requisite number and type of heroes from your pool as well as gold in some cases. After 8 rounds, victory points are totaled, and the player with the most points wins the game.

Players cannot attack each other directly, but they can mess up their opponents' plans by grabbing certain resources before any other player can get them. They can also play an intrigue card from their hand, which may give resources to whoever played that card, take away resources from other players, or both. Certain intrigue cards can also be used to impose a mandatory quest on an opponent. Mandatory quests must be completed before any other quest and are generally a waste of time and resources. Since all players can see what quests they are trying to complete as well as what resources they have already gathered, it isn't difficult to figure out the possible ways to mess up each other's plans.

Ultimately, there is only one basic strategy for this game: Grab as many high-yielding quests as you can reasonably fulfill, gather the resources you need as efficiently as possible, and perhaps try to prevent opponents from fulfilling their high-yielding quests. It may be worthwhile to complete quests that give few or no victory points but give you certain bonus effects, but those are best completed during the first half of the game. If you prefer the kind of game that can be won with a variety of strategies, you won't find it in Lords of Waterdeep. If, on the other hand, you like to exercise your resource management skills in a quick game, then you've come to the right place.

Pros
  • Fans of the Forgotten Realms may enjoy the game's theme.
  • With only 8 rounds, each game session can be completed fast.
  • Those who enjoy planning ahead to manage their resources may get a kick out of playing this game.
  • Knowing what quests your opponents are trying to complete, it can be fun to throw a monkey wrench into the works.

Cons
  • Players who enjoy attacking enemies directly and using dice to resolve their actions should play Ameritrash games instead.
  • Players who want a game that can be won with multiple strategies won't find it here.
