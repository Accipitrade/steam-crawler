\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn amazing game! The physics driven envrionments, combat, and exploration means that there is much more testing/discovery than can be found in any one play-through. As the devs have stated, a new user’s experience in a 30 minute level ends up being 90 minutes (or more in my case). Upon writing this review, the current lack of a saving system/checkpoint system means that I cannot fully explore a level and complete it in the same play session (as my game sessions are often short or interrupted for the time being). I am reluctant to play it right now knowing that I will not have enough time to explore a level thoroughly and complete it. The same can be said for those that need to take frequent breaks to avoid nausea. If you leave a level early or need to quit the game for some reason, you’ll find that you have to (frustratingly) replay the level from the beginning; secrets and collectibles are not kept. I recognise that the devs have already responded with their solution with this issue and I know that it will be fixed in a month at most.

TL;DR: if you have enough time to stop and smell the roses (depending on how thoroughly you explore or how much you just like to screw around in places), then you can expect to have the smoothest experience in everything the game has to offer (A LOT). Otherwise, consider waiting until the save patch at least.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is so much cool tech in this game, I just wished the story and level design had some real polish. In several parts of the game, the levels are literally made of every 3D object they made, it seems. One room is littered with batteries, elevatorboxes, crates and...shops? Bars? Next to huge generators and other technical looking objects. Might sound whiny - but unlike Valve, Boneworks isn’t telling a story through leveldesign. It might seem like it, with grafitti everywhere, but most of the grafitti just seem like internal jokes from the devs. Maybe it all has something to do with the story, but the storytelling is lacking. You might come across a TV with someone talking to you, but I never understood what they were talking about. Sometimes, you're even fighting while they try to relay story information to you. They needed much more time fleshing this out.

But the physics are awesome, right? Yeah, unless its part of a puzzle. Holding anything heavier than a key is a mess, your noodle arms bend in every possible direction when trying to lift puzzle-specific objects. Be it a huge hook, a box or a loading cart, it all feels so wonky. Sure, the tech is fairly impressive, it's just not FUN. Jumping on a box feels exactly like it did in Trespasser from 1998. Your simulated, wonky legs have a hard time finding their footing on anything other than solid ground. And climbing means that your arms move while your legs hang underneath you like two blocks of wood, making the act of lifting yourself over a ledge or getting off a low ladder pretty much impossible. Maybe you could do it if your real arms were longer than your legs.

Let's dip into level design for a bit. It seems like the "design"-part is rooted in two things mainly: making huge environments and huge puzzles. You will be tasked to do several grueling parts of platforming and physics puzzles. None of them are enjoyable, mostly because of said wonky physics. The first or second guess at how you should solve them often result in annoying, glitchy physics. When you realize what you probably ought to do, you are fed up. The "joyous reveal" - the big AHA-moments, are sorely lacking. It's replaced with "ugh, I guess this might work, somewhat...uuughh...yes, it did. Barely".
The levels are also often HUGE, like the devs just made several enormous boxes and started planting them with containers. The details are often lacking. How about a bar compromised of just...grey blocks? No shelves, no cash machine, no glasses to break while swinging around a pipe. Grey blocks and a neon sign. System Shock 2 had better details than this, and that game is 20 years old. In one level, I ran around looking for a key in an enormous stage for 30 solid minutes, before finding the key on top of a fountain. WHY would the key be there? It was a blue key, on top of a blue fountain top. Night impossible to see if you are trying to find ACTUAL places a key would be located. Level design like this is unforgivable. And what do you need the blue key for? To open a room where you look for a red key, and then some batteries, and then some more keys. The trailer made this out to be an actiongame, not finding keys and stacking boxes.

The action is pretty good though, mostly because of the guns. They handle well and have some satisfying recoil. You will end up grabbing a new clip from your belt by accident a million times, often while trying to pick up a clip you lost on the ground. If you play in a seated position, you will have to dig the controller into your waist if you want ammo. You have a slowmo option, but it is linked to the button next to your left joystick, meaning there is no way to move and hold slowmo at the same time. Since you have unlimited amounts of slowmo, there is no reason why the slowmo button isn’t a damn TOGGLE. You can cheese it though, by holding slowmo and the oculus-button at the same time, putting you into an oculus menu. When exiting, you will be stuck in slowmo. Haxx, I guess.


There are 3 enemies. Zombies, soldiers and headcrabs (probably called something else, but we all know what they are). Zombies come at you and usually trip over any object they interact with, making them hilariously inept. Soldiers are basically a rolling ball with a few robot parts and a hologram of a person ABOVE all that. While this is the first time I’ve seen an enemy like that, it makes more sense that the developers simply didn’t know how to animate and code a proper human body with legs based on physics that wouldn’t constantly stumble like the zombies. It makes no sense storywise either (you are trapped in virtual reality, but enemies are robots who fake being humans?). So you mostly end up trying to shoot their robot face behind the hologram, or just shoot them in the ball. You might hit the hologram head, and get nothing out of it, because...you know...you only hit the hologram. So headshots don't really feel good.

Headcrabs are somewhat fun and freaky though, although they mostly just attach to your face (and then you pull them off and beat them).

To be honest, I’m only playing this game still because of the sandbox mode I have been promised at the end of Story mode. So far, Story has been a huge drag, and I really hope I am nearing the end. The most fun I’ve had was in the museum at the start, where you get to play John Wick with loads of guns, swords and dummies. Seriously, holding a dummy (or zombie) in one arm, while twisting it and firing your gun at its legs, stomach and head makes me feel like I’m right in the middle of that film. Shame that the tech is the only way the game emulates this cool style. I really hope they take all this hard work and put the tech in a proper storydriven game with focus on action and style, not weird platforming that feels like developers yelling “LOOK! PHYSICS PUZZLE! BECAUSE PHYSICS!” Right now, I’m fairly sure they finally released Boneworks because they were fed up with coding such an ambitious physics driven game.

I recommend trying it, but I can't recommend it as a fully realized game. It's not. There is fun to be had here, it just feels like a techdemo with a halfassed storymode that should be called levelmode. If you consider this a masterpiece, I'm not sure you've played the games that inspired this.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere's definitely a lot of jank with the physics, and the controls are very different from what other VR titles have gotten me accustomed to. But once you get past that and get used to how the physics work, it's insane how well it all works.

This game spoils you with the physics. I'm never going to stop trying to poke doors open with the end of a barrel or blade. I'm always going to be wishing the doors opened just by pushing on them with anything, your hand or any physics object. I'm always going to hope other games had buttons and switches that you could press by just poking them with your hand, instead of pressing/holding an interact button on the controller. I'm always going to be annoyed when a game doesn't let you climb 2 feet to reach loot.

Just yesterday I was taking something out of the fridge. It caught on the edge of the fridge for a second and all I could think was "♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥, my wrist made the same kind of movement as it does in Boneworks when something you're holding catches an edge".

The weapons could use more variety, right now there's basically 2 different assault rifles, 2 different SMG's and 2 different pistols. If there are more, I haven't seen them yet. But it does feel a bit repetitive when that's all the variety you get in an average campaign playthrough. There's a lot of different melee weapons available, but most of them you only see once during the campaign in the middle of the levels, and their main purpose seems like they're just meant to be reclaimed and unlocked for the sandbox mode.

Overall, it's a game that needs patience in learning and getting used to the mechanics, but it's also a game that has a lot to give. I can't wait for extra content, and I hope they release official mod tools so people can start creating.\t\t\t\t\t

This is going to be one of my only serious reviews as I waited for this game to be released for over 6 months. Stress Level Zero in the past has brought some great additions to the VR community but they made a huge mistake releasing this game in December. The game feels unfinished and like it's not adding anything to the community where it resides. Feel free to discuss with me in the comments but I'm giving this a negative review because of a few main reasons as listed below:

Level Design: The levels are extremely boxy and linear. They are all pretty unoriginal and boring and I don't see anything unique going on. A lot of these levels have red herrings that are seen to be extremely frustrating. Some levels like throne room are very awkward in there format and nothing is given to you as a hint which leads me to my next point. They seem to have spent 2 years on the engine and about 4 days on the levels. I am very disappointed.

Story is confusing, and boring: There is no comprehensible story in this game. Stress Level Zero hyped up this so-called narrative but has refused to give one in the bulk of the game. Cutscenes are all that give you exposition other than random drawings on the wall. A reddit post which I cannot link in steam reviews basically summarized it and my only thoughts were 'really?' I won't go into it in case others who have no bought the game yet or finished the story are reading this but have you know, without that post, I could not give you a damn shred of the story.

Saving is utter ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ in this game: You can't manually save and each level is at least 30 minutes long if you don't know what you're doing (you won't). Checkpoints are few and far between making every single your eyes start to shut after being killed, all you think is how hard you want to punch the ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ wall.

This isn't a game: This game is obviously a showcase on the physics but instead of doing that in a sandbox or arena mode (which you have to BEAT the game to unlock) you are forced to complete tedious and mind-numbing puzzles out of a 10 year-old's pop-up book. If you are going to focus so hard on the world's rules, why cover that great work with terrible, boring puzzles. Shooting levels and fun action are usually very quick and take forever to get to. Enemies seem to be randomly placed which coincides with terrible level design.

Final statements: This "game" has a boring, untangeable story with terrible levels and even worse puzzles. Like the flop that Death Stranding was, this game is 2 hours of fun and 10 hours of monotony. Physics are great to mess around with and are suprisingly great for a VR game but everything else is unfinished, unoriginal and worst of all, un-fun. I am EXTREMELY disappointed in Stress Level Zero and I'm afraid that a full rehaul of the story/levels will be the only real way to fix these problems.\t\t\t\t\t

If you are not well situated with VR this game will make you feel sick.

This game is fantastic, and i know for a fact ive been playing it wrong the entire way through, but at the same time there isnt a wrong way to play. I would end up finishing a section and realize that I had taken a completely different path than what was intended, I guess thats the joy of this game, Theres almost an infinite amount of ways to solve stuff, whether that be to climb up a small archway of poles and pillars, or to pull levers and solve physics puzzles.

Handling the weapons feels clunky but also very satisfying, Pulling back the slide of a pistol contorts your hands like rubber to then let go with a sharp snap, im not too sure how to describe it, but i love it.

Level design feels very early dev type, like a half and half of devblocks and well thought out geometry, urban exploration meets retro city 17. Alot of the levels are huge, and you cant even really see the scale of it unless you have the tools to explore it with. Cities, factories, and even something out of a DnD session, it has a nice variety.

Enemies are somewhat lackluster, having huminoid zombies in different forms squeeze through doors and boxes to try and swipe at you whilst VR headsets on legs will try to hop on your head.

Speaking of the VR headsets on legs, if one ends up landing on your head you can see another world through it, like peering into your own VR headset but in game, its insane, The amount of detail they added is insane, Fake glass that peers into a farmland with amazing detail is insane.

I feel like there is just too much to even say about this that i cant really put it into words, its just good.
