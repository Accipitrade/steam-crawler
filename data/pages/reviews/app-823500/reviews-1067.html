\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is a mixed bag. I'm glad it was made and I'm glad that I played it because I'm interested in game design and it serves as a demonstration of what works and what doesn't work in VR.

Some of the things that don't work:
- The full-body physics simulation is extremely janky and wobbly. For example, trying to climb on top of things (especially other physics objects) is a nightmare.
- The way that your in-game hands' positions can become so significantly dissociated from your real-life hands' positions feels terrible.
- Anything heavy is painfully annoying to manipulate due to the wobbly physics and hand position dissociation.

Things that are good:
- One-handed guns feel great and are fun to use.
- There are some nice subtle systems like the way your in-game hand posture adapts while holding two-handed weapons.

I felt that most areas of the game that involved heavy use of the experimental physics system were a chore to complete. I don't recommend playing Boneworks unless you're interested in experiencing for yourself how frustrating the physics can be at times.

I'm somewhat concerned that, with all the hype around Boneworks, that other game developers will feel like they should introduce a Boneworks-style physics system into their VR games in order for them to be popular. I would be very annoyed if this happened and this negative review serves as my veto against janky physics simulations in VR games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWith the recent patch which added checkpoints, I'm changing my review to positive.

Boneworks is a complete and refreshing game, but not perfect. The story is decent (so far) and the physical interactions are gratifying when they work. You have to move the way the game wants you to move, and it can sometimes feel like a hinderance. The guns handle nicely and melee weapons are decent enough but I often times find my hands getting stuck on things, and some motions, like mounting ledges and jumping, are extremely awkward. I also feel that the weapons arsenal could use some expansion (no revolvers?! no shotguns?!)

Level design is decent but amateur at times. I got stuck at the first locked door in "Streets" because i couldn't find the key. **SPOILER** It's in a random box in a stack of 20 boxes in the corner of the map with no audio or visual cues to go over there and break boxes. EDIT: so ive been told there is a sign with a key icon above the boxes. I still think this couldve been handled better. I am generally good at puzzle solving and dont get stuck in games. I've only had one minor sticking point since then but I can't say I've been blown away by the level design.

Boneworks will go down as a landmark VR game. Despite some issues, SLZ has taken some risks and elevated the level of interaction we can expect in a VR game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ttl:dr cool tech demo, and a game that doesnt really work with its tech

The VR related physics, body movement, hands, and controls are all very good.
The actual gameplay tries to emulate a half life 2 style physics puzzle/shooter but fails pretty hard. Somehow the physics are getting in the way of the game. Half life was built around and from its physics engine, whereas Boneworks seems more like a bunch of unrelated puzzles that were created and ported into the physics engine. I am almost constantly frustrated by the physics just not behaving in a way that makes sense for objects that are placed in puzzle areas with obvious uses. Need to put these long planks across a gap to make a bridge and walk over it? the planks bounce like they are entirely made from the same rubber as superballs, and about half the weight you would expect, and the whole thing slides backwards as you walk forwards, and falls into the gap because it slid too far. Why is it so slippery? Why are giant metal crates so light and bouncy? Is it possible to pick up a trash can without one of my arms clipping into it and it getting stuck and forcing me to run jump and spin in circles to get it off?
and the melee combat
oh god, how have you done this
The weapons and your arms respond as if they have realistic and accurate weight and the impacts and physics of hitting objects is great. I have also never been less satisfied from hitting an enemy with a hammer. The enemies barely react, if at all. Smash an enemy in the shoulder with a sledgehammerand he might fall and die instantly, or just keep lurching forwards unfazed with an orange hole in his shoulder, or maybe fall flat on his face as if he tripped without dying. Sometimes the hits dont even register. I once had to swing a lead pipe, like full force home run swings, at least two DOZEN times but the lead pipe mostly just slid or bounced off harmlessly.
The sandbox mode might be fun, but I wouldnt know because you have to unlock every single item by exhaustively searching the campaign maps and bringing the items to a hole in the ground at the end of the map. And the maps are rather labyrinthine. And theres no checkpoints or autosaves or manual saves, when you clear one map you unlock the next. So if you spend 2 hrs exploring a map and then quit because its getting late or youre getting tired, you get to restart the map or you can quit without saving any progress. Bafflingly, if you die, you dont spawn at the beginning, you respawn at certain checkpoints. It just doesnt save the checkpoints if you quit.
The gunplay works great, and the recoil works the way the makes sense.
Ive heard that they are going to implement saves later, Im really debating refunding and maybe buying it again later if it gets better or just hanging on to it and waiting\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really wanted to recommend this game, as I thought it represented a technical leap in VR. I picked it up super excited, but this game simply could not get me to continue. Most of my very limited hours have been spent on sandbox, where I enjoy the game best, but it just seems to have so much holding it back. Honestly that techincal leap was already made by Blade and Sorcery. Unfortunately, Boneworks is committed to physics to the point of it bogging down the experience. Swinging a weapon is incredibly clunky and you can't get the desired momentum and angle when operating on instinct. Every single object (at least on valve index) is held at an infuriating angle in the hand, which does not match the angle I hold objects in real life. When I kill an enemy, it's usually with a gun, or by thrashing a physics object around. The handicap you face by muscle and physics based gameplay removes any of the split second decision making and intentionality that makes VR so amazing. I played through like 2 or 3 levels and the lack of autosave made me retread far too much ground. This is especially true for VR since it's much more exertive and games should be designed with smaller play sessions in mind for those who can't play for hours on end. I don't want to spend too much time comparing this to Blade and Sorcery, but I can't help it because I now know all these things can be done right. In B&S, weight on the objects feel correct, enemies are light enough for you to throw them into other enemies, physics is only there to make the game more fun and rewarding! They shouldn't be there to impede you the way they do in real life, because you're there to escape it!

Now there is one aspect in which Boneworks has more to offer than genuinely any other VR game I know right now. This physics engine has an entire story-based game to go along with it. Most others like Gorn and B&S are pretty much tech demos, but that doesn't make this game more fun for me. I do enjoy story-based games, but this never gripped me in the short time I spent on it.

While I do commend this game for making an entire story to go along with the engine and mechanics they've created, they haven't told it in an innovative way. The other games I mentioned have captivated me for hours and hours with no story at all. Why's that? Because the innovations they bring to the table are ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ mindblowing. I'm not sure why y'all decided to drop hundreds on a VR headset, but I did because I wanted to experience something new. The novelty of a head mounted display is cool for a while, but with two controllers we have for the first time ever, we are now able to use our bodies in VR games.

I respect the absolute ♥♥♥♥ out of stress level zero and I've been a fan of corridor since before stress level zero. I think what they did here was really really important. They properly marketed a VR game and proved that there is an audience/money to be made in VR. I feel this is a crucial step in motivating AAA companies to develop games for VR (and no, I don't mean ports). And while I do believe that thousands of people will enjoy the ♥♥♥♥ out of this game, it simply didn't hit the mark for me. There's so much to be taken advantage of in VR and they've certainly made a start. However there's a certain state I can reach in a few other titles where I'm sweaty and hyperventilating but the only thing I'm concerned about is the axe coming toward my head. This is the real advantage of VR. It has the ability to tap into the instincts and reflexes we've been developing our whole lives. Unfortunately here we have to relearn them. There's still so much potential here that if SLZ takes the feedback properly they can create some of the greatest VR games of this early generation.

I will recommend this game if any of the following apply to you:
You have a long attention span
you don't have astronomically high expectations (like I did)
you love story in games
