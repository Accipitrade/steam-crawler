That's where it ends for me. After 7 hours, I'm putting this on the shelf and probably won't pick it up again.

Where Boneworks pushes the boundaries on VR input, it falls flat in just about every other area. Dull combat, a vague, vapid and shallow storyline, repetitive and predictable puzzles and a mountain of chekhov's guns left me bored and frustrated.

Let's start with the input (a pro). Never have I felt so "inside" of a game before, and this is due - entirely - to the novel means of interaction with the world. At times some of the physics were frustrating but it was easily looked past. The mostly realistic gun interactions, inventory system and using the physics engine to your advantage were all immersive and enjoyable. The game wants you to cheat with physics - it even tells you to - and that's something I think many people, including myself, really appreciate. The game was built to play.

Unfortunately, beyond that there isn't a whole lot left. The storyline is intentionally vague, not that there's any reason for it. It's obvious the writers were trying to keep an air of mystery but it comes across as edgy and as though they're trying too hard - which is a bit ironic as the game lends itself to not to be taken too seriously. It's a juxtaposition that sticks out and makes everything feel fake and amateurish, and ultimately unenjoyable.

Every trope in the Valve-ian "cult classic" category of games was used. Spray paint on the walls from a mysterious unseen ally, purposeful "amnesia", a teehee so quirky AI trope (the melons are just analogs of the companion cube, and the adorable personalities of Portal turrets are the "Hi" the null bodies say before they attack), crowbars, a grungy but also high-tech cityscape, etc. It's all there - we've all seen it before.

It's clear you're trying to escape a simulation that has either broken or gone rogue, but there's very little explanation as to what the null bodies are, or why they want melons so badly, or why any of the storyline is happening to begin with. I am confident that if I kept playing this would all be revealed, but 7 hours in feeling like I've done absolutely nothing to progress is not a great motivator to reach that point. Further, the introductory (tutorial) scenes in the very beginning contained so much incoherent exposition that it was impossible to understand any of the information being presented.

Further, the puzzles - as few as there were in 7 hours - were predictable and repetitive. Upon arriving at one, it was clear there was a way the puzzle "intended" to be solved and a way that it could be solved "cleverly", either by using physics or by climbing somewhere that wasn't obvious. But that's the thing - it was obvious. Every single time. Either that, or the puzzle intentionally made you start over again and again just to piece together the final solution - not out of error, or by mistake, but because the level was designed that way. There's very little actual problem solving, and it felt like all of the puzzles existed just to artificially extend the game time.

Lastly, the combat. Not the gun interactions or how you went about combat; as I mentioned earlier, those aspects are stellar and feel very well done, if not a bit awkward at first. The combat scenes were few and far between, and what little combat occurred was so incredibly dull. There is exactly one way to kill things, and letting them line up and walk toward you is by far the easiest way to do so.

There was only one instance in which I felt as though I needed to employ any strategy or tactics, and even then it was because they flooded the level with enemies that seemingly came out of nowhere. It's as though very little thought went into the combat scenes and they just decided to spawn as much nonsense at once. This has an unintended effect, however, since the AI is so poorly written and single-minded that it becomes the same fight over and over, endlessly. Half Life 2 had unequivocally better enemy AI, and that was almost 17 years ago.

The progression system is also confusing. At the end of each "chapter" per se, you're supposed to throw away all of your weapons, but somehow you keep your ammunition. You also collect a bunch of little rubber balls with artifacts inside of them but it's not fully explained what they are or why you should keep them (you must also "reclaim" them at the end of each level). There's no permanent progression, nothing that keeps you interested in finding new things or searching around the levels, and you start out with the same weapons over and over again. In the beginning tutorial level, you are introduced to several tables of interesting and exotic weapons, but 7 hours in and I've seen maybe two or three of them.

Bundle this all together and it's just a walking and looking-around simulator. It's a single track, uninspired Portal/Half Life clone with some cool and novel aspects but a dreadfully slow pace.

Buy on sale if you really want to, but all of the positive reviews here genuinely confuse me.\t\t\t\t\t
