\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPerformance: I played slightly above the minimum specs, with a GTX970, i7-4790k and 16gb DDDR3 RAM. Everything ran fine except for a couple scenes in Tower. Headset was WMR Lenovo Explorer.

PROs:
- Every object and part of the environment is interactable and feels "solid"; they bump into everything else and have weight to them. This makes for the most "lifelike" VR experience I've played (when it works).
- The displacement of your in-game body when lifting heavy objects is really good at simulating weight and resistance.
- The headset-crabs are cool enemies
- Things overall just feel really good to do, like pushing things around, reloading, force-grabbing, getting ammo, etc.
- Mike Diva cameo
- Soundtrack has BANGERS

OK:
- The buildup for the story in Museum and Streets is amazing, I was prepared for a solid Portal-type plot. Unfortunately the story starts to matter less and less as the campaign continues. I was left a bit disappointed by the end (although the final moments are pretty cool).
- Same goes for the game's visuals. Some parts are really cool; the sewers somehow really felt damp and filthy and the museum felt like a mix of bright 90s colors and dark, empty minimalism (almost like if Half-Life was made by a bunch of guys in LA, which, well...), but other times it feels like a crossover between a generic 2013 sci-fi movie, Unity standard assets, and a ♥♥♥♥♥♥ Half-Life reskin. Not a huge complaint, but they started high with Museum and it kind of got blander from there.
- Some of the puzzles are easy but require you to fight or "cheese" the game's physics to get something done. Multiple times I've had to do the climbing cheat where you crouch as low as possible and then physically crouch to raise yourself over a ledge. There are still some puzzles where I don't know the actual solution to, and just had to get through some other way. I know the game encourages this but sometimes I feel like I'm fighting the game instead of focusing on the actual puzzles.
- Climbing feels great but bugs out when something doesn't have a grip point.
- The null-body enemies are extremely dumb, but are designed to turn that dumbness into creepy zombie-like virtual abnormalities.

CONs:
- Dr. Ford has the limpest wrists ever, and because of this melee can be frustrating. Maybe it's better on Index (using WMR) but I could only use the longsword effectively by pointing it forward and using the joystick to directly run into someone.
- Physics jank feels like Source physics jank at a VR level. It's way cleaner but occasionally you or your hand will not be where you want it to be, leading to some unexpected results. For example, climbing a ladder can be frustrating when your hand can't get past a lower rung, forcing you to make a goofy wide reach-around each time you want to climb up. Multiple times my arm has gotten trapped in a dumpster wall or a fence and started flailing around.
- If you have gun aiming problems in other VR games, it's worse here. The stock of the ARs bumps against parts of your body and my headset spergs out when the controllers are too close to my face (this also sometimes results in whatever I'm holding flying out of my hand). Just get really good at hip-firing.

SUMMARY/ETC.:
If you like that sub-genre of games that go for realism and end up having some clunky, jank parts, Boneworks is a lot of fun. This is the first singleplayer VR game I can seriously recommend, and the first where the campaign kept me playing for hours straight without feeling fatigued. I think that a lot of other VR games can be a bit overwhelming but Boneworks' setting, music, and puzzle gameplay make it comfortable to play. This in turn makes the action sequences a lot more enjoyable as well.

One of my favorite games is Portal 1 because of that feeling of isolation it gives, and Boneworks offers plenty of that. You're alone, nothing else is real except your need to keep moving. I just really wish the story had expanded a bit more.

7/10 PLEASE MAKE A SEQUEL\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tANYONE SUFFERING HEADSET DRIFT PLEASE READ THIS

I've gone from refunding after 25 mins of nausea to 4 hours of blissful play.

The drift can best be described as if the camera is pivoting at the front of your nose, so when you look up, you feel shorter, and when you look down, you feel taller.

After working out how the skeleton/camera rigging works with the dev in their bugchat forum on discord, I realized that setting the in-game height to TALLER than your real world height totally fixes this issue some people have been having!

Climbing ladders is still a little weird, but I'm totally fine with it compared to how much it was drifting or shifting / felt like tracking issues.

Again: set your in-game height to TALLER than your real world height.

I'm 5'10" or so but I have it set to 6'0" with 0" floor off-set and I am totally enjoying the game now. My character's knees aren't bent and he's not on tippy-toe, and I'm having no problem climbing/pulling myself up over stuff.

Also, for the plot, I had to realize that this is NOT a half-life game. Rather, it's a satire of VR development in general, and I'm really enjoying it now that I see it as a comedy rather than a serious attempt at narrative.

There is plenty of plot to pick up on, but you might need to take off your art-critic glasses like I did and just enjoy this for what it is. A brilliant, fun, full game experience for VR by an indie team for $30.

With no headset drift, this game is stellar.\t\t\t\t\t
