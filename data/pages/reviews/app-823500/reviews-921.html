\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is really awesome.. Maybe a bit intense for some people because of all the movement, but amazing if you don't get motion sickness from VR. This was built for people who are comfortable with running, jumping, grabbing climbing, swinging, falling, and being launched through the air without getting the barfs.

There are no teleport controls here for the squeamish, no training wheels, but the benefit of not being built for those things is a really immersive experience with nice melee and gunplay, and endless potential for open-ended gameplay.

I'd describe the gameplay as being sort of Half-Life or Portal-esque.. It has a little semi-linear guided portion near the beginning that helps to familiarize you with the game, then gives you large open-ended levels to explore and fight in, with multiple paths through. There's a balance between combat, puzzle-solving, and exploration... and the game doesn't get mad if you use physics to cheat the puzzles, or carve your own path by stacking objects until you can reach something to climb on and bypass entire sections of the levels entirely..

Your reward for at least trying things the intended way, or sometimes just for having a keep sense of exploration, is items that you can bring to one of the game's many reclaim centers, which unlocks them for use in Sandbox mode.

I'm still slowly progressing through this game, trying things out and exploring every nook and cranny I can find, but so far it's been a blast and i'm very impressed. Try it out.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me preface this by just saying Boneworks is by far the best VR game to date, and I wanted to be sure by waiting until I had 100 hours to review. It is aimed at VR veterans who can handle movement and understand your VR body and combines an excellent set of mechanics with physical interaction. The reason most people miss the story is they take the surface-level monitor videos and assume that's all there is. You will be able to make a lot more connections if you've played SLZ's previous game, Duck Season.

The main mechanics and the IK just blend in to the gameplay, and once you're done with the campaign there's still arena and sandbox to explore. More recently a lot of collectibles have been added, rewarding people who look for secrets in the main levels. Along with consistent updates, the game also have a growing modding scene to help push it even further.

If you own a VR headset, you should own Boneworks. Play it after you're comfortable and take the time to understand things like weight and the game just clicks perfectly.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is a game that seemingly wants to be VR's version of Half-Life, which isn't to say it's similar to Half-Life Alyx. The two are very different games, and while I'd grade both on a mostly equal level, Boneworks does have some issues.

What it lacks in overall polish, it makes up for in depth. While it may feel clunky and inaccurate at times, it makes up for that in the sheer amount of interaction you have with the environment. Climbing, jumping, and smacking objects until they break are all viable, and in many cases your creativity will ultimately decide if you win a battle or not. In this sense, it allows a freedom that has been seldom seen in VR games, but it doesn't come without a cost.

Whereas other games have dedicated slots for inventory (i.e, Half-Life Alyx and its gun system) Boneworks allows you to pick your weapons and assign them personal slots. This system is mostly fantastic; it allows for any player to customize their challenge level and general accessibility through each level, which is important as each level resets your arsenal. Weapons can be bought before each stage starts using ammunition, much like METRO with a big weapon vending machine, though the biggest complaint here is the recognition of slots. Side-loading pistols and SMGs works fine, but anything holstered on your back feels as if it's hit-or-miss whether you'll grab it or not.

The level design is passable; it attempts to recreate the world-building of the Half-Life series, and while it starts off well with a small neighborhood that lets one see the devastation of this strange world, it unfortunately only gets worse as it goes along. That's not to say that the gameplay ever gets bad, but the story basically becomes non-essential after six hours of play.

While the majority of the game is still fun from a strategic point of view, the ending is pretty weak. It leaves you wanting more, and in the worst way. You don't feel like you've learned much, and the final moments only open more questions than answers.

While the gameplay has a lot of fun to be had, the story is not a reason that you should purchase this. Furthermore, I do recommend this to people who have had experience with VR first and foremost. It's not a horribly exhausting game, but as someone who does daily strength exercises, there are a few occasions in which you'll be swinging, climbing, and bashing a lot. Furthermore, locomotion is recommended for this game as many of the enemies respond in a very traditional FPS fashion, so twitch response and aiming could only help through the experience.

While most definitely flawed in areas, the ambition that Boneworks shows is commendable, especially when they've achieved so much freedom within the gameplay. Finding a safe vantage point by just barely climbing upon an elevated surface is a satisfying feeling, as is acting like an action hero during combat and finding that it sometimes does work. Overall, I'd recommend this to those who have some experience in VR. Alyx may have the story and detail that some are looking for, but for those who crave raw gameplay and combat, Boneworks is a great alternative.\t\t\t\t\t
