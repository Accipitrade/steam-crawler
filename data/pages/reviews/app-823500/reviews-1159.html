\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSummary: A must-buy if you're developing a VR game yourself or if you're interested in VR technology. Boneworks has a lot of cool, interesting technology up its sleeve, but unfortunately most of it gets in the way of a satisfying gameplay experience, resulting in an awkward, frustrating game to play. Boneworks has a bunch of cool ideas and surprisingly neat presentation, but sadly none of that make it worth its current price point when other, better implemented games exist.

(Note: I played on an Oculus Rift CV1 with the Oculus Touch controllers)

From the moment I booted up Boneworks, I was both enthralled and appalled. The opening map beautifully introduces a well-crafted universe and several cool interactions between your body, the environment, and the weapons you can wield. The presentation is genuinely well done and there's a bunch of really cool effects in play. Unfortunately, though, the museum also introduces Boneworks' most major flaw: though it's cool to be able to interact with objects to stack them, jump on top of them, and then use that newfound position to climb into an air duct, actually _achieving_ it is a massive frustrating chore that takes minutes to achieve without either getting your arms stuck inside of the physics engine (prompting a restart of the game), causing the objects to fly all over the place (prompting a careful restacking of the objects), or failing to get your legs inside of the air duct correctly (prompting an exasperated sigh or - more commonly - some frustrated swearing).

Sadly, the levels following the first lack the same type of visual creativity and cool factor as the introductionary section has, and generally feel quite boring and uninspired outside of a VR context, repeating the same patterns and environments as we've seen in Half-Life 2. These levels introduce a few enemy types (walkers, zombies, headcrabs and riflemen) and puzzles (object stacking, blocking things, key hunting and pressing buttons), and then sadly keep repeating them, merely changing the environment as you go from puzzle to combat section to puzzle. The combat and exploration can be genuinely fun in these, but the physics engine often gets in the way, making it a frustrating experience.

Thankfully, the last few levels in the game have the same quality as the introduction section. One in particular - the best level of the game, in my opinion - is both beautiful, mostly free from annoying physics-based puzzles, and heavy on fast-paced combat. Unfortunately, after this particular level it goes downhill pretty quickly, and the number of annoyingly simple puzzles goes up way too much. One physics "puzzle" in particular, which needs to be solved in order to progress, took me 20 seconds to figure out and 30 insanely frustrating minutes to complete. Imagine that you're playing Flappy Bird, except drunk, using the controls of QWOP and without being able to control your legs. By far my most terrible experience with the game.

Gunplay is fairly satisfying, though not as satisfying as the gunplay from other titles like H3VR, Onward and Contractors. There is some weird weapon smoothing and virtual gunstock going on, meaning that you can't rely on your muscle memory from other games, and aiming anything other than a pistol is genuinely difficult as a result. Sometimes, the weapon's angle shifts by a few degrees when you move your hand a millimeter to the right. Sometimes you remove the magazine from your pistol instead of holding it with two hands when your hand position is slightly off. Sometimes your weapon gets stuck on geometry. Sometimes you thought you holstered it correctly but it actually ended up falling to the floor. Though I expect the unholstering action to work fine on the Valve Index, it's a bit awkward on the Rift with its Touch controllers as you have to press the trigger and the palm grip to grab your weapon, then depress the trigger and finally press it again to fire a round.

As you might've guessed by now, frustration was a key experience when I played Boneworks. I can't possibly describe how exhausting it can be to want to grab a rifle off of a table, and instead grabbing the table, making the rifle scurry off in a random direction. Or how fun it is that the game makes you "purchase" weapons at the start of a level using an exhausting purchase system which locks off most interesting things behind an unreasonable, impossible price (those cool weapons/objects you saw in the launch trailer? Don't expect to be able to use them outside of a few key moments in the game).

..and yet... despite being a frustrating mess, it's hard to elaborate on how _cool_ it is to hold a pistol, use it to slap open a door, then within a second shoot an enemy in the head and watching its body become limp, then fall down a staircase. Or using your crowbar to hook an enemy behind the head, pulling its head towards you, then finishing it off with a stab to the head using the knife you held in your other hand. It's really these small combat interactions in a weird, interesting universe that make the game worth its while, and though the game has a sandbox mode and an arena where you can practice your combat skills, these environments feel a little too sterile to be as fun as when you get to do it in the campaign.

Did I enjoy Boneworks? All in all, yes. Did I find it frustrating? Yes; more so than any other VR game I have played. Would I recommend it to people who haven't been involved with VR for almost a decade...? No. Not in its current state. Maybe when they tone down the physics puzzles in the campaign and polish some of the usability issues. There's a lot of cool stuff in Boneworks that's waiting to be polished up and turned into a must-have product, and I can't wait to get to experience it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHoly crap. Just finished the game, and I NEED to write this review.

If you're the kind of gamer who:

- Owns a VR
- Is looking for an insanely fun single player campaign
- Wants to be immersed in a setting not unlike Half-Life or Portal (no WAY those games weren't inspiration)
- Wants a mysterious plot that isn't too in-depth but is always there, just out of reach of understanding
- Likes shooters (These gun and melee mechanics make fighting too damn cool)
- Likes physics (Smash crates, push enemies over, scale buildings)
- Needs lots of playtime (The campaign, if you're like me and not rushing, takes about 20 hours, and even then there are plenty of secrets to unlock)

And doesn't mind:

- Some tedious puzzles: the physics does make the puzzle-solving fun and unique but at times it feels like it gets in the way of gameplay, making you stuck for a bit figuring things out when you just got down mowing down AIs
- Needing a good computer to run; if you're running VR you probably already have this
- (SLIGHT SPOILERS!!!) A plotline that can leave you confused and at the end sad

Then this is an EASY $30 to spend. In my opinion, one of the best VR titles out there right now, and soon to be on every 'must have VR' list. Most other games I've played in VR have been fun, but feel more like arcade games that are fun to chill with every now and then. Boneworks feels more tangible, like I got an actual GAME game that I can sit down and sink way too much time into. It's got an amazing campaign that uses the idea of being inside a VR game to it's advantage, intense fighting that is easy to learn and, over the course of the game, you will master, and immense replayability.

Though I'm certain this game is the final form, I can't wait to see if SL0 decides to add DLC, a sequel or just make another game. This game has shown me what the future of VR is probably going to look like, and boy am I happy. So I'd say the price tag is worth it, this game will give you hours of fun.

But one last complaint I would give is... too many rigid bodies. :)\t\t\t\t\t
