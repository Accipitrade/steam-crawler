\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBasically, this game is extremely impressive. The reviews for this game are true: it's currently the best VR experience with a physics engine to match (plus or minus some odd clipping). I was a bit worried that the game would have no purpose after the ending, but I was wrong. After beating the game, I realized that I missed quite a bit. One big example is the vast array of physics weapons. I'd only used of of them in my first playthrough. In addition, there are secrets and pathways hidden throughout the campaign. The guns and weapons meet a perfect balance of realism and video game requirements. Nothing really feels overpowered, except maybe breaking boxes with the knives ; ). The one negative thing I will say is that some of the puzzles don't really seem difficult. Within the first 30 seconds, you can solve 95% of the puzzles. Where this game shines the most is its combat. There are just so many ways to deal with different things. Sure you can just shoot that headcrab, but you could also slow down time, grab it well it's jumping, and throw it into the nearest create. The physical weapons feel unique to each other. The hammers are heavy, but can literally throw an enemy away with a well-aimed hit. Oh the other hand, the throwing knives are fast and fun. If you've got $30.00 and a VR headset of any sort, GET THIS GAME. If you don't have a headset, nag a friend that has one and prepare to spend the next 10 or so hours at their place!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game sucks. Quite honestly. I have no idea why people like it so much.

TL;DR - Everything this game does, has been done better in other places long before it's release. It is in no way innovative and is simply a cobbled together pile of half-assed levels and game-play mechanics that come together to form a totally disjointed and uncomfortable experience.

Boneworks. This game was one of the games that was selling me on VR. This was one of the first games I bought when I started playing VR games. I jumped into it with about 5 hours of VR time under my belt. I played about three chapters, got stuck on a physics puzzle, remembered there was no saving, closed the game, and refunded it.

Now save files are in the game, so I bought it again to beat it. And beat it, I did. I have completed a full, any% run of the game. I did not collect all of the sandbox items nor did I spend any time in the sandbox mode. I did not spend any time there because the unlock method for that mode seemed confusing and after watching gameplay of it, I wasn't too interested in it.

Now onto why I believe that the game is bad. I'll mention the (few) things I did like after.

Level Design - The entire game feels like it was an indev level from Hammer. Ever see a HL2 indev level? Look that up on Google Images. Kinda looks like Boneworks, doesn't it? It's almost like the devs modeled in all the basic geometry, got the main "focus" points of the level down a little more solid, and then shipped. Over half of the game just feel empty, and unfinished. There was only one section that was good - I don't remember the name but it's a section where you're fighting Rolly-Soldiers in an open plaza, and then it goes into some corridor areas where you do some CQB against the same type of enemies. That was literally the only area in the game that I felt like the devs polished enough to be passable.

Enemies - The game has a total of 4 enemy types. Turret, Headcrab, Wobbly-Zombie-Guy, and Rolly-Soldier. The only enemy that has variations is Wobbly-Zombie-Guy, but these are so slight that there might as well be no variations at all. They only differ in appearance. The turret only appears a handful of times, the headcrab only shows up closer to the end of the game, Rolly-Soldiers are the second most common, and Wobbly-Zombie-Guy you see constantly.

Turrets are a bore. There is nothing special about them. They have a laser pointer and when they see you, they fire. If you get behind them or knock them over, they de-activate. Nothing special.

Headcrabs - called Headsets in game. Ever play Half Life? You get the deal. These were decent to fight but only one variation. And sometimes the game just throws like 10 at you because it doesn't know what else to do.

Wobbly-Zombie-Guy - When you first encounter these guys they're cute because they're like these low poly "bodies" you fight off. After you kill the 178,879'th Wobbly-Zombie-Guy. You're a bit bored. Because you've killed the same guy way too many times. All they do is slowly walk towards you, fling their arms about, and fall over physics objects. That's it.

Rolly-Soldiers - These are soldiers that are holograms projected out of rolling balls. These guys are actually fun to fight, especially in the only good environment in the game (that plaza I mentioned earlier). but again, no variation at all so you're shooting the same moving orange guy throughout the entire game. They also have laser pointers on their guns.

Melee Combat - Never have I seen such an awful excuse for melee combat in any VR game. There is never, at any point, any clear indication of how much your melee attack is going to do. Sometimes I'm putting my all into swinging a sledgehammer into the eight millionth Wobbly Buddy, and I barely scratch them. Sometimes I go limp and let gravity do the work for me, and I one hit kill them. And if you get any pointer objects (think swords and daggers), you litteraly just run into whatever you want to kill with your pointy stick pointing towards them. They do nothing to counter. They do nothing to defer your attack. They just let it happen. You run into them, pointy sword thing goes in, you back up, they die. There is an arena sequence in this game and that's literally all you do for about 15 minutes.

Physics Puzzles - 95% of the "puzzles" in this game aren't even puzzles. They're usually involving something along the lines of dragging a platform into place, or pressing some buttons. The only decent physics puzzle in this game is one where you pick up a giant hook to move a platform into place, that you then weigh down with a heavy cube with a massive hook on it. But even then, this is just a variation on "move the platform into place". But all of these were caused by a massive hinderance to the entire game which is the:

Physics - this entire game is a wobbly mess at every conceivable point. Climbing a ladder? you're wobbling all over the place. Scaling a wall? Wobbling. Picking up a crate? You're grooving. Trying to shove a wobbly man into a wall? Better close your eyes to avoid the motion sickness. Seriously, the amount of wobble for almost any action you do in this game is insane. And don't even get me started on trying to jump on top of a crate. I think every time I tried to do that, it took me about 5 tries. and I think for most of the crates i tried to jump on top of I was usually either rage quitting while trying to do it or very close to doing so. But the climbing and jumping are really just a problem caused by:

VRIK Body - Why the hell do you need a full body in this game. Your legs get in the way of so. much. ♥♥♥♥. Every time I need to scale something, move something, crouch, reload, perform melee, anything - my torso and legs are in the way. This wouldn't be so bad if they weren't constantly glitching into geometry, but they do all the time. Good luck trying to scale a complicated section in any conceivable way. I hate the VRIK body in this game so much. It never played nice. I personally really enjoy how The Walking Dead: Saints and Sinners does it - you have IK arms, but no torso or legs. That solves so many issues.

but the biggest issue of all that this game has against it is:

Vacuum Development - it is abundantly clear that the developers did not playtest this game with regular people. Any regular person would have pointed all of the above issues out to them, but they were too concerned with "pushing boundaries" they they were totally unaware had already been pushed. They spent too much time telling themselves the game they're making is the next Half Life (Which they did try to get valves permission for) that they ended up making the jankiest VR Tech Demo that ever existed, and forgot to build a game around it.

Stress Level Zero; if you guys read this - this game should have not been a game at all. You should have developed the engine, and sold it. This game should have been far more than it is, and you should have not been the hands to develop it. Maybe next time instead of telling yourselves your game is a masterpiece, play some other VR games with mechanics you're trying to replicate, think to yourself why they work in other games, and get some normal ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ people to playtest for you.

I hate this game. I still don't know why I bought it again and beat it.

Don't buy this game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a good game, first and foremost. Its controls are mostly tight despite an initial learning curve, combat fun and puzzles decent. What strikes me about this game is, despite its melee combat being a bit less fluid than Blade and Sorcery and gun-play less detailed than H3VR, the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

B&S is not yet a complete game with any form of mission progression, H3VR is mostly made as a shooting range and a lot of other VR games feel less like fully-fledged video games and more like an arcade game or a tech demo. Boneworks stands out as one of the first complete-package experiences. Many make comparisons between this game and Half-Life, and it is an apt comparison in more ways than one. Of course, there are the immediately obvious homages to HL such as the design and prevalence of the crowbar and headset crabs, but where it truly deserves comparison is its importance for the state of gaming. Where half-life proved the viability of physics in standard video-games, Boneworks proves its viability in VR. This is a huge feat and one worth celebrating.

One unfortunate parallel to Half-Life is the fact that this game does not run well on most people's PCs. Even with and I5 9600k, 16GB of RAM and a GTX 1060 6GB, I personally face pretty substantial lag in some of the game's more open areas which I either have to endure or watch the world turn to pixelated mush upon checking the adaptive resolution box. My 1060 is definitely the limiting factor here, but I digress.

My prediction for the future is that Boneworks will act as a basis for what's to come. From SLS we can expect another game to push this engine's limits and most likely a push from other devs to have as much in-depth physical interaction. This game is both an exciting glimpse into whats to come and a grim reminder that peak VR comes with unfortunately steep demands from a PC.

Overall, I recommend this game both from the perspective that its just a good time and from the perspective that this game offers a fascinating glimpse at what VR can uniquely offer as a means of play. If you want an arbitrary number then I will rate this game an 8/10. The game itself IS fun but I know that this engine can be pushed further and a better game produced. SLS, congratulations on your momentous accomplishment! I cannot wait to see your next project. I know you're capable of a true masterpiece.\t\t\t\t\t
