\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEasily one of the greatest VR games ever made.
Amazing graphics with exception of obscure places no one would normally go to.
Physics at time can be jankey but overall it's very well done. You can learn to do 'parkour' if you play around with the physics enough
I've never felt motion sickness in the game despite the only way to move is via controllers. (You can't teleport) There's no real comfort options but if you play the game for a little bit over time you'll become immune to VR motionsickness (Except rollercoasters - probably)

The story-line seems non-existent but that is not the case. There are TVs around the map that fill you about the world outside around you. Some cutscenes also play in certain points. Similar to Half-life the game mostly uses subtle storytelling using the world around you that you're in. Why are Omni's shooting at you? Because you are considered an anomaly due to the circumstances you put yourself through. Writings are all over the walls and floor, the most obvious is just before the break room your character (Presumably) wrote on the wall about ideas of immortality. I won't spoil anymore of the lore, it's deep and crosses over to other games (Other Stress Level Zero titles).

Playing the game I've never felt like it was very linear. Even in one of the chapters 'Sewers' (Which obviously is meant to be a bit cramped) there's many detour places you can visit and many wide open spaces.
There's always a second solution to every puzzle in the game. As long as you have a decent understanding of the physics and can scale things you can 'parkour' your way out of all of them.

Many fixes have been made toward the combat. It's incredibly well made and polished. (Enemies won't get caught on your arms anymore) This despite Half-Life Alyx has a TON of melee weapons (Shovel, Bat, Barbed Wire Bat, Crowbar, Katana, Pickaxe, etc.) And there's no shortage in guns as well. The only gun you can't find in the vanilla game are shotguns and snipers (There's sighted guns however)

The soundtrack 'Bonetones' is amazing and is perfect for this game. I suggest giving the soundtrack a listen sometime.
Ammo is used as money in the game and it is 100% possible to beat the game without using a single bullet

Lots of things to do after you beat the main story-mode such as
- Finding Collectables hidden around the chapters which can vary in Tools, Weapons, NPC/Enemies (, And Dev weapons/tools)
- Playing minigames such as Zombie Warehouse, [REDACTED], Sandbox, And Fantasy Arena
- Fun achievements to grind for.
- Replaying the game to understand the lore
- Collecting all the ammo to unlock the Grand Prizes
- Finding the batteries so you can fly around in 'Hover Junkers' vehicle

TL;DR
The game is 100% worth the price and is a MUST have. This game, in my opinion, is better than Half-Life Alyx (Which says alot since I love that series)
I can see from this game Stress Level Zero has a bright future.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUnique mechanics that, while not perfect, help push VR forwards by trying something new -- surrounded by boring level design, janky physics puzzles that we've seen a million times before, and a less than intriguing story; make for a lackluster and disappointing overall experience.


There's no real story to keep moving you forwards, and the levels are often just big open voids with enemies randomly scattered around, occasionally containing physics puzzles that I found to be very unclear, frustrating, janky, and easy to break. Enemies variety is very low, consisting of both "dudes who shoot at you" and "dudes who run in a straight line at you and slap you".

The sandbox is the most fun part of the game, but everything (levels, weapons, props, enemies) needs to be unlocked first through playing the story campaign. This is annoying on its own, but the mechanism to do this is even more frustrating. Instead of simply walking over a collectible to unlock it, you have to physically pick it up, bring it with you to the end of the level, and put it in a bin that's extremely possible to accidentally miss before proceeding to the next level (where the unlockables will disappear from your inventory.)

Often this bin is a long distance away from where you find the unlockable, so you'll need to put it in one of your storage pouches to safely bring it with you -- which means dropping one of your weapons to make room. Sometimes you'll find multiple unlockables per level, which furthers the struggle between "weapon or unlockable?" and makes playing through the campaign a lot less fun with limited weaponry.


The gameplay mechanics are definitely something new and different in VR, going against the grain and providing an experience for those with good 'VR legs' as opposed to something more introductory -- but a lot more time could've been put into polishing. Your entire body is a physics based ragdoll, which should make for some interesting interactions with the environment, however this doesn't always work as intended:

  • I could never get climbing to work properly, and had to do this weird gorilla stance 'walking' on my knuckles to pull myself over ledges. Very game breaking.

  • Your hands don't instantly go where you want them to, but rather slowly interpolate position towards where your controllers are in 3D space -- this makes for a very unresponsive and sluggish feel.

  • Snap turning doesn't instantly rotate your model, but rather rotates the desired position of where your model should interpolate to. This means every time you snap turn, your body turns into jello and slaps around a little bit as it interpolates into position.

  • There's no toggle grip button, so if you don't want to drop your gun, you have to constantly be holding down your grip button. Perhaps this is easier with the Index controllers, but on WMR (as its grip buttons are a bit mushy and will often spontaneously un-grip if you don't apply enough force) this is quite tiring, and will result in dropping objects (and your gun) extremely often, and extreme hand cramping.

  • Your hands will clip into things while not in view, resulting in your hands often getting stuck behind objects or under the map with no real way to fix it.

  • The game expects you to have a lot of space, both horizontally and vertically. I can't count the amount of times this game has had me stick my hands into my ceiling fan blades, and this is the only game that's ever been able to do that.

  • Melee weapons have inertia, which seems like a cool idea at first, but results in melee weapons feeling extremely uncontrollable and very pool-noodle-like. I'll agree that there needs to be some kind of solution for lack of physical feedback for melee weapons in VR, but this definitely isn't it.


Overall the game just feels like a chore to play, both physically and mentally. There are definitely some cool ideas in there, but I ultimately couldn't force myself to keep playing to experience them. Comparisons to Half Life: Alyx are deeply misguided.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTldr: Half life Alyx meets portal!

This game is everything I could have wanted from a Vr game, everything has a hit box so you cant clip through things and the world paired with the story feels extremely immersive. The combat feels fluid and the areas are all visually satisfying. Melee combat feels great as it simulates the physics and weight of objects, so it's no longer a game of wiggling a controller.

The only downside is the movement. Too many times have I been at a full sprint and almost fell over irl because the camera stopped too quickly, and don't get me started on the nauseating feeling of sliding around on slick surfaces or falling from heights. Like the tutorial says, don't be afraid to take frequent breaks when you need them.

Bonus points to the soundtrack, I've had "pick it up" stuck in my head for days now.\t\t\t\t\t
