\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is what all VR games should try to achieve. The level of interaction at your literal fingertips (Assuming you have Index controllers) is amazing. Shooting, climbing, pushing, pulling, and throwing all feel natural and play out exactly how you would expect your actions to.

The only place this game falls flat is the hand to hand combat and partly the story and melee weapon combat. Punching enemies doesn't feel as satisfying as it should and a lot of the time feels pretty bad. I'd expect when I put all my force and wind up a punch just as hard as I can for it to deck the enemy in front of me and transfer the force from my punch into the enemy, but most of the time its a very weak slap noise followed by them falling over very stiff. The same can be said for the melee weapons although not as much. It isn't as bad as the hand to hand combat and can be very satisfying to fling a sledgehammer at an enemy and watch them fall to the ground, but many times I found it kind of weak and it was more satisfying to just shoot the enemy in front of me.

The big thing that is a mixed bag for me is the story. I love the story, or at least the potential story. The game does an amazing job of showing you the story instead of flat out telling you everything, leaving a lot up to your imagination. Walking around seeing this virtual world slowly crumbling around you as you work your way through some fun environments is amazing, the visual storytelling is great. But where it falls flat is in the spoken story telling. There are two characters who follow you every now and then through the story through monitors talking to you from outside the world. But these don't really go anywhere, they do give you some information about whats going on but are spread far apart from each other and I found myself not being into the story during the first half of the game. But after that first half it really does start to pick up.

There is a difference between the first and second half of the game, and I mean big. It goes from visual to more spoken while also mixing in the visual as well. This section of the game really started to grab me and held me all the way to the end of the game where I found myself on the edge of my seat wanting to know more, then it ended. The story in the second half of the game is what I wanted the entire game to be, it really felt like Stress Level Zero finally figured out what they wanted to do and where they wanted the story to go.

Overall, should you buy this game? Hell. Flippin. Yes.
This game is an amazing experience to have if you own a VR headset, and even if you don't have one its worth getting one to play this game. It may have its faults but in the end it has provided me with hours of enjoyment and many more to come with the collectibles, hidden areas and future content to be added down the line. This game should be on every VR players game list.

Oh and I let a bunch of people play this at a party recently and they all spent forever just throwing things around and doing whatever they wanted to, just in the first room of the game so I'd also recommend this for parties as well.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks isn't flawless. It can get very janky, some of the puzzles/mazes can be confusing and frustrating, and the graphics are pretty flat. That said, it's also by far the most immersive VR game available right now, offering experiences unlike anything else with a level of interactive fidelity that Half-Life: Alyx can only hope to aspire to.

When you shatter a crate, sometimes you need to brush the ruined bits of wood around to reach the ammo underneath, and you do that by just using your hand to push it aside. You can flip switches and open doors by shoving them with the hand axe you're holding. You can climb tall structures by hooking them with a crowbar and climbing it to reach them. Interactions are natural and tactile. Sometimes they get janky, as noted, but nothing else even comes close. The only downside is that other games feel worse after playing Boneworks. Going back to Pavlov, there's more gun variety and they feel a bit better but then the environments are entirely static and consist of invisible walls blocking movement and shoving your body around when you try to lean over crates. You're just sort of bobbing around a cardboard cutout world. Every game is that now. My time in VR is substantially worse after playing Boneworks.

Luckily, we've already seen the result. Half-Life: Alyx is purported to have physical interaction only because of Boneworks and I imagine every major game will do the same from here on out. You don't need full-body puppets with inverse kinematics (although that is fun) but without being able to interact with and shove around anything that isn't nailed down, I just don't feel as immersed anymore. It's game-changing.

Edit: Update over time. I had said that climbing could be sickening because of head bounce but that issue has been mitigated a lot, if not completely, so that form of movement is now more comfortable. Deleted that comment.

Additionally, Alyx has now come out, and it very much feels like Alyx took a lot from Boneworks and we owe Boneworks a debt for pushing the envelope and encouraging Alyx to follow. Even then, while Alyx has very little jank by comparison, it also still isn't as immersive, the weapons are all one-handed and weightless, there's no melee combat, and ironically the Half-Life game has fewer physics puzzles. Alyx is the better game but Boneworks is the better VR game, and a lot more aspirational, even now.\t\t\t\t\t
