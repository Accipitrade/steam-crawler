\r\n\t\t\t\t\tImagine a Half-Life VR title utilizing the core mechanics that made HL2 the game that it ended up being. Now rewrite the story and concepts to take place totally out of the world of Half-Life. Add in some side modes outside of the main campaign and give the campaign a solid length to it.

That's quite literally what happened during this game's development.

The story mode has a tremendous Half-Life influence in the best ways. Physics are obviously at the forefront of the game and how it works. Puzzles feel (for the most part) natural and seamless within each level's structure and design. Every now and then a puzzle comes in that feels shoehorned or feels blatantly like a puzzle that exists just as an inconvenience or stop gap, but for the most part, the puzzles are interesting and fun to work with. I also really appreciate the devs' focus on flexible problem solving in every scenario be it a puzzle, combat, or navigation. I love that things don't feel like there's just one way to go about them. It dramatically increases the amount of unique experiences you can get out of the story.

The basic combat and navigation in the game can feel awkward at first, but once you get your physics-based VR legs in check, everything starts to feel extremely natural and you understand why your shots are going all over the place or why your crowbar isn't following the path of which you physically swing it. People who have played Pavlov/Onward and Blade & Sorcery should have a good head start with getting used to the basic physical interactions.

I've replayed several levels in the game multiple times in different ways to find different collectibles/weapons/areas that I didn't find in a previous playthrough. Replayability is huge in this game. You can take your time and sift through every avenue each level opens for you (and even break outside of level bounds on some of them) and you'll still find a reason to go back to a previous level to see what you may have missed. Nine times out of ten, something ended up being overlooked.

The tutorial section is probably the best tutorial area I've ever experienced in a video game. Hands down. The flexibility and open-ended nature of the tutorial level (a museum showcasing the basics of gameplay while simultaneously setting up the story and universe within the game) makes it so fun to learn how to play the game. You can spend hours in the tutorial level alone and it all flies by. Take your time or jump right through, you play it at your own pace. Though as thorough as the tutorial section may feel, there are some points that could use some better (or deeper) explanations.

For instance, the progression and unlock system is frustrating at first only because I had no idea how levels worked or were structured until I finished the first actual level. That may sound silly but in the tutorial section, a concept called the reclamation bin is introduced. The way it's described seems to imply you can carry weapons and items with you between levels. That isn't the case. The reclamation bin is where you unlock items/weapons/npcs you find throughout playing in story mode for use in the sandbox or arena modes. No items cross with you between levels. You definitely wanna get involved with the community and ask questions so you can understand the game a little bit more clearly.

Lastly, the save system is severely lacking right now but SLZ has already talked about how they plan to address that problem. The fix should be implemented by the end of January apparently.

So in summary: The game is an amazing experience that could use a little better explanation at some points but does a solid job of being a fulfilling, clever, and super enjoyable VR title that finally feels like a full and proper video game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\ti wanna go in depth but I'll get to the point first. It's a decent game that although some what short, doesnt feel insignificant and to me was well worth it's price. it took about 9-10 hours to finish at a leisurely pace while spending time exploring for the games many hidden secrets. once you're done with the story there's both a sandbox and arena mode to play in but id be happy to replay the story levels as well. which you can select individually from the main menu once completed.

so for a bit more detail and constructive criticism. story wise the game felt a little lacking. they've obviously gone for a fair bit of mystery and want to leave you with questions at the end. the only problem is they took it to far. the world felt empty with little to no backstory or lore and after everything i still have no idea what happened. i dont know why i was doing what i was doing and what's worse i dont actually know what it was i did. there seemed to be about 3 factions that all attack each other but why? feels like maybe story elements got forgotten or cut during development and were never reintroduced.

sadly level design was a little lacking as well. no major problems but felt dated and a bit like Doom or others. go get the blue keycard for the blue door or push this button or pull that lever to progress. puzzles were underwhelming and felt a bit contrived in most cases. there was a good mix of puzzles to fighting though and the pacing stopped things from feeling stale at any point. the game did feel quite easy to me even at later stages but the difficulty curve felt pretty much perfect. i imagine most ppl would find the difficulty about right.
id say ammo crates were a little to prolific as well. it was nice to have a lot of ammo but constantly having to smash boxes got old fast. maybe tone that down with less ammo boxes but just have more in each one. there were a lot of hidden or difficult to reach boxes as well but it never felt like it was worth the effort to get them given their abundance. fewer boxes would make those challenges more worthwhile

physics were good if very slightly janky from time to time. the melee weapons didnt feel as impactful as i would have liked though. hitting someone in the face with an axe it felt more like rubber than heavy metal as it just bounces off. this could possibly be fixed if more energy from the swing was transferred to the target knocking him off his feet and such. gun play however was excellent. both single and two handed weapons felt spot on with nice variety as well. the lack of a button for mag and/or bolt release made me sad but obviously the devs are limited by the number of inputs available on the controllers so totally understandable.

in conclusion an enjoyable game worth your time if you have VR but with a little room for improvement. really looking forward to see what comes next from these Devs.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe good:

- Gun play is solid, a good medium between physics-based systems like H3 and push to reload like fallout and Doom. The sound, visuals and haptics all reinforce the power behind each shot and it feels awesome.

- The physics-based enemies are a ton of fun, especially when you catch one of the headcrabs and smash it against the wall or empty a clip into its weak soft underbelly.

- Theres a series of levels with a story linking them together and a feeling of moving forward, something quite rare for a VR game right now. Wave defense or sandbox roguelike this is not. This is a proper adventure.

- The soundtrack and visual presentation is really, really good. From 80s synth to monk hymns, it all works to evoke the feeling the developers are going for in a specific moment, and complements the varied environments you travel through.

- Bypassing a puzzle or obstacle by clever use of the systems makes you feel amazing accomplishment.

- The moment to moment gameplay is solid and fun, and I crave more.

- It feels like a solid base to build from Im expecting either hefty DLCs or mods to create more content for this game in the future.

The bad:

- Your arms are not where you think they are. There are times when this is a good thing, but more often than not your character will get stuck on some part of the geometry while climbing and you wont realize, leading to frustration

- The story is barebones, and more implied than outright stated. I didnt feel an emotional connection to any of the characters and the ending left me thinking now what? Theres also a weird transition two thirds through and the game kind of goes off the rails in the third act.

- Pacing is kind of all over the place. On a moment to moment level, puzzles take significantly longer than the action set pieces theyre supposed to be giving you a break from and range in frustration from moving boxes around to playing hide and seek with your older brother who left the house a while back to go on a date with his girlfriend. There were multiple times when all progress stopped in its tracks as I had no idea what the developers wanted me to do next. I honestly spent an hour of my 9 hour playthrough looking through an empty hangar for a key thats hidden at the beginning of the level, and theres a section where what was previously useless background geometry is now the only way of moving forward through the level. On a story level, the climax comes at the end of act 2, and then the game just sort of goes on for another 2 or 3 levels.

- Its definitely an indie game pretending to be triple A. Theres no saving and loading in a level so if you cant reach the end of the level for whatever reason youre SoL. I got a few bugs with the haptics that wouldnt turn off after I picked something up, which was annoying. It seems the game wasnt play tested with anything other than the knuckles controllers for any length of time because the Vive wand controls are awful for melee weapons and movement, and the lack of options is shocking.

- Theres not a lot of variety in both weapons and enemies, with only 4 guns and 4 enemy types, and your final boss is a giant ladder. The game is also pretty short, and has mechanics they teach you in the beginning just to use once and never touch again. Feels more like a long form tech demo than holistic puzzle in those sections.

The missed opportunity:

- The core concept (VR inside VR) is underused in this game, and feels more like an excuse for some game mechanics than a wholistic setting. You spend 70% of the game in city streets, sewers, warehouses, and 29% in a medieval castle if not for some props theres not much indicating that you are in a virtual world. The small bit that is there is awesome, like the security corps chasing you using smooth locomotion by having a giant rolling ball and the headcrabs being a VR helmet but after playing virtual-virtual reality I was hoping for some crazier environments and level designs, from floating worlds to escaping the level to fighting inside a loading screen, theres many ways they could have taken this concept. As it is it feels more like window dressing. Reminded me of inception you see all this crazy stuff they could do in the beginning but the whole time its just normal offices/hotels/bunkers.

Id rate it 4/5 and recommend checking it out.\t\t\t\t\t
