\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game was a pleasant surprise. I'd held off for a while because it was reportedly fairly janky, which it...kind of is, but in a mostly-tolerable way that reminds me of early builds of Half Life 2. Boneworks is very immersive, it reacts to your actions surprisingly well, and never really feels like it's taking shortcuts in its interaction models.

The campaign is of average length, but the narrative being told feels inconsistent. The environmental narrative has that same "you are exploring the back alleys of a system you are not meant to peer behind the curtains of" vibe that Portal did so well, but the live action cinematics that put words to your progression felt like they came out of nowhere, describing actions and intentions the story's protagonist had that I was completely unaware of.

The biggest downside to the campaign is that there's a plethora of delightful tools and systems to play with, but for the most part the combat is just "here's your guns, shoot things". It's just a missed opportunity when impaling enemies with swords, daggers, shovels, or throwing random crap around feels so good.

Ever since playing Alyx, every other VR game has been soured due to how high the bar had now been set on interactivity and immersion, so I was delighted to find that, while Boneworks lacks polish in some areas and is pretty janky at times, it actually manages to raise the bar even HIGHER in a lot of ways. The ability to jump higher by physically jumping in the real world, while quite dangerous, is a fantastic touch. Scraping meat cleavers together produces satisfying sparks and sounds. The collectable system is strangely appealing, emphasized by the number of secret items and areas sprinkled through the campaign.

After having played through the game's story and checking out a number of the optional post-game content, I've gotten my money's worth out of it but I'm still left wanting more, simply because of how enjoyable being in this engine has been.\t\t\t\t\t
