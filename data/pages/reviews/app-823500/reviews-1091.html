\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe good:

It's an incredible VR physics based shooter with platforming and melee elements. There are a wide variety of both ranged and melee weapons to be found, and the enemies are straightforward but varied enough to keep the gameplay interesting. Crablets are downright scary, and definitely a favourite enemy of mine. The audio cues enemies emit are really well designed, and you can always tell something is coming just by listening for the positional audio, which is really neat.

All weapons and several other items are "collectable" throughout the game. By bringing them to special locations within each level, items can be "reclaimed" for use in the Arena and Sandbox modes. This adds a huge amount of replayibility to the game, as you'll want to go through and find all the weapons and other items for later use.

The physics element of this game is unlike anything else yet attempted in VR. It's akin to Half-Life 2's leap in physics based game design. Walls can be climbed, ammo boxes can be thrown (and broken), enemies can be pushed, shoved, grabbed, smacked, and crushed. Weapons can be adjusted in the hands. You can run-jump-grab onto ledges, climb walls, swing on bars, or use a crow-bar to climb a distant ladder. It really is amazing what SLZ have accomplished with their unified physics solution.

In additional to the full roomscale mode to the game, there's also a seated mode, and *it's great*. Being able to play the game in while seated is a great alternative to walking around for hours, and it works in a much smaller space. I'm really glad they took the time to tune this gameplay mode.

The game also has an unlimited slo-mo button. It's awesome. Shooting, stabbing, or bludgeoning enemies in super slow-mo while diving off a ledge is one of the most Neo meets John Wick feelings in a video game, even beating out F.E.A.R. Tossing your left-hand's Glock up in the air while you reload your right hand's Uzi, in the middle of a firefight and all in slow-mo, and then catching the Glock again to empty it into a null-bodies face is a completely unbeatable feeling.

The story is also interesting if not light (more on that later) and the lore and world-building is great. The attention to detail in the environments and assets is really top notch.

Overall the game is engrossing, stupidly so. I took ~18 hours to beat the main story, and my average playtime was 2-5 hours *per session*. I've played a lot of VR games, including many shooters, and few have the kind of gameplay mechanics, environments, and story required to hold my attention for 2-5 hours at a time. Now all I think about is Boneworks, and when I'm not playing Boneworks I think about playing Boneworks. It's Breath of the Wild all over again.

The bad:

The physics based movement can be very motion-sickness inducing if you're not already used to this kind of game. This is the biggest barrier to entry for new VR users. Even the act of grabbing a heavy object *while standing on the ground* can cause the players entire body to move slightly, which will make the head move and the view shake. This "bobbing" effect is subtle, but will cause bad things to happen if you're not used to it. This is before you factor in the additional linear locomotion, jumping, and climbing.

Speaking of climbing, the ability to climb up walls just by grabbing onto them is great. However, it can be very cumbersome since any obstacle on the wall will interact with your virtual body, potentially blocking your hands from moving to your intended location. Learning to climb up onto ledges without accidentally falling, getting stuck, or jumping off is also a real challenge, and I wish they'd added a little bit of assistance for this action. Climbing is also a little *too* common in some of the levels (*cough* right at the end of the game *cough*), and it's not a very fun mechanic to have to do for a long amount of time, or do twice.

Doing things twice is an unfortunately common theme in Boneworks too. Some of the checkpoints are just a little *too* far apart, making the player redo large, easy sections just to return to a challenging fight. At the time of review, checkpoints are also not saved. If you exit the game in the middle of a level, you will have to completely restart the level over. This is apparently being fixed in an upcoming update, so YMMV.

The story. While the world-building is top notch, the story is quite peripheral, so while the game gives you a narrative and some hints towards a bigger picture, it never explicitly lays out exactly what is going on. Without spoiling too much, this game also has ties to Duck Season, which may or may not help the story to click. The ending is a little weird and ambiguous too. If you're not the type of person that enjoys hunting through every last detail, reading theories online, playing ARGs etc, then this ending might leave you feeling unsatisfied as to what it all means. On the other hand, if you're not the kind of person that needs concrete story closure and likes things left up to interpretation (i.e. thought Twin Peaks ended fantastically), you'll probably like this.

The ugly:

The physics engine, although amazing, still suffers from some rough edges. Your hands can get stuck inside objects, objects can get stuck inside objects, and things can just get occasionally jank. If SLZ allowed the player's hands to move through certain objects like ladder rungs, maybe the situation could be helped, although I understand why they've gone for the more realistic (yet sometimes cumbersome) route.

Hardware compatibility:

I played through the Boneworks story mode with an OG Vive (with Vive wands), on a NVIDIA 980 Ti.

Playing through this game with the Vive-wands is clearly not the first-class experience for the game. While the wands work well enough, constantly holding down the grab button to grab melee weapons and other objects can become very tiring, and even cause hand cramps. Guns fare a lot better since they don't require the grab button to be held constantly. Playing this game on a Vive *will* make you yearn for the Index even more than you probably already are.

The 980 Ti holds up mostly okay for the Vive's resolution and refresh rate. Playing with the default settings, and without dynamic resolution enabled, the game is mostly hitting 90fps. It did dip into re-projection territory towards the end of the game where the levels are much larger. Still, you can get away with a 980 Ti on a Vive, although I'd recommend a 1080 Ti or faster to really make it buttery smooth.

Conclusion:

Overall, Boneworks is solid 4/5 stars. It's not perfect, but at the same time it's the best VR game I've played to date. I'm incredibly excited to see what SLZ do next, although I really hope it's Boneworks DLC.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t---A stepping stone for Half-Life Alyx?---
First off, kudos to the devs for attempting to bring VR physics to the next level. Interactions are amazing as you can use items in different ways as tools to accomplish your task. A crowbar can be used to not only break a box, but also as a hook to pull yourself up ledges. It definitely is one of the better VR physics demos.

However, this is mostly that. A physics demo.

The game itself is extremely lacking in the design department. How so? Here are some examples that are repeat offenders throughout the title:
-Lots of padding corridors to extend the level. Long stretches of empty tunnels with literally nothing in them are scattered throughout the map. Long ramps and stairs are thrown into the mix and it makes the levels just feel so poorly designed.
-In some levels they intentionally designed you to have to walk back 5 minutes of empty corridors if you fail a jump. In one level, the devs put a 'sorry' on a door, and made it a one-way locking door which required you to place a brick to jam it or else it shuts behind you and you have to re walk an entire section you just cleared. This is very bad use of the puzzle logic.
-While the zombie-like enemies and robo-headcrabs are pretty fun to fight, the human enemies with guns are terribly designed. The way they do gunfights is often spamming a whole lot of these gunmen that charge you. They take a lot of bullets to kill even with headshots. You will find yourself mostly just peeking a corner with a rifle and edging as you shoot one down at a time.
-While I appreciate that the puzzles require some thinking and not handed to you on a platter, the use of confusing assets on the maps waste a lot of time because these assets look like they are important when they are not. An example would be a guardhouse-looking structure. It is blue in color, contrasting to the level which is mostly grey, and you can clearly see an opening into it which is inaccessible by normal jumps. With the knowledge that this is a physics puzzle game you bring crates and stack your way up, and finally walk into the doorway into the structure. Empty. The structure itself was supposed to be an ambient prop and not part of the puzzle at all, yet it was given a high level of importance and emphasis.
-On a touch controller cocking your weapon is frustrating. A lot of times your character uses to finger to pinch the handle and you have to jerk the controller around randomly just to ♥♥♥♥ your weapon. Guns feel very clunky to use, far from NODE's claim of 'the best vr guns'.
-Puzzles feel very weird. They aren't exactly difficult, but they just very janky to complete. I'd admit its in part due to VR being janky in itself, however, some of the puzzles leave the spot just high enough that you can't get with a normal jump, and you have to climb a pole and drop yourself just nice on the next spot.

There are many more on the list that I could ramble on, but the main point is that it feels like the devs spent so much effort into making great physics and tech systems that they had to skimp and quickly throw together a game without polishing it properly with good design. Overall I think Boneworks is a nice title to buy if you want to experience next-gen physics and interactions. its a great tech demo, just not a good game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR - Overall experience was bad or annoying, combat was bad, and the physics puzzles were nightmares when the physics conked out. I had to refund.

I don't know what to think of this game. Everyone has raved on and on about how good it is, and yes, while I had it, boneworks had some level of fun to it, however the main marketing point (the physics) was quite broken. I should probably specify that my computer is more or less minimum specs, with a 6GB GTX1060, a good ol' AMD fx8320 running a CV1 Oculus, which might have caused some issues (such as objects moving fast enough phasing through scenery or enemies).

That aside, onto the combat. I found that trying to punch or hit enemies with blunt objects yielded next to no reaction from them (hitting one of the AI slaves with a hammer was effectively rubbing a pillow on the poor guy's face). So melee combat was incredibly unsatisfying. Unfortunately, the main thing I was looking forward to (the gun combat) was equally unsatisfying. There isn't any haptics when firing or reloading, and the guns feel... stiff. It's hard to explain, but i feel that Pavlov does a better job. My opinion on the guns would certainly change if they added haptics when firing, but until then, it's a hard no from me.

The physics, like I mentioned earlier, were quite often really, r e a l l y wonky. But if the system worked, it worked nicely. But that's one hard if. While I played, my arms got stuck through the sides of at least 3 barrels, 2 buckets, and one really annoyed AI. For how much everyone is talking about the immersion the engine gives the player, I've only been constantly reminded I'm playing a game and that my computer still needs another $500 put into it.

If I could, I'd rate this as a meh, but I can't, so I gotta rate this down.\t\t\t\t\t
