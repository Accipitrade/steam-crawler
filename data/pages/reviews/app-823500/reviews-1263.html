And I love how your hands can't just pass through objects like other VR titles do. The elastic feeling you get from the visual disconnect is a great substitute for the sensation of touch actually. So putting your hand through a table and how the brain interprets the fact that the hand stays in place while your real hand goes through. That makes you feel the table.

This game makes me feel like a kid in a candy store. It's a constant "I wonder if I can do this" and then "Wow, I can! Cool!"

I have a great stomach for VR, so I fully recommend smooth turning if you can handle it.

If Half-Life:Alyx is anything like this we're heading towards better times for VR.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game, to be very blunt, is a low budget Half Life 1 fan game without the genius game and level design that Half Life has to back it up. It is, however, a very innovative and brave VR title and one of the first of its kind, and I think in its many flaws, gets a pass for being what I think will be considered a very ok game in the long run.

If you're okay with the occasional (or frequent) fighting of the physics engine, it's a pretty neat experience and currently one of the only of it's kind on VR until Alyx comes out.

PROS:
Innovative VR title with great tech and physics
Emergent gameplay and lots of mechanical depth

CONS:
Mediocre level design with many unrewarding dead ends.
Occasional puzzle that feels impossible or straight up poorly designed (platforming puzzles especially).
No saving during levels so be prepared to start a potentially 1 hour experience from the beginning if you didn't get to the end.

Additionally, don't buy this game expecting anything more than a thin story, or great graphics, though I don't consider these cons. The game is about being physically present in the environment as you play and that's enough for me.
