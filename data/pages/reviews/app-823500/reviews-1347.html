\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is largely what was promised if you were keeping up with its development. As of writing, I've just made it through the tutorial and into the first area afterwards, but I may have spent a good 30 minutes just at the 'menu screen' alone. Interactions with objects in this game feel more natural than any other VR game I've come across yet, and just grabbing things, tossing them around, climbing, dragging, jumping - all with a real body presence - feels like a game changer.

Some interactions can get a little weird. There are times you'll just pull yourself off the ground when trying to move something heavy. Your hand will probably get stuck in some doors and cabinets. Lockers are frustrating to open sometimes as the door will hit your hand and immediately close after you've pulled it open. Such are the frustrations of a physics based game I guess, but I'm honestly willing to deal with all the goofiness of that to feel like I have a real presence in this virtual world instead of an intangible pair of floating hands.

If you're old enough to remember what Half Life 2 felt like when you were first told to throw a can away with the physics engine, this game is the equivalent of that moment for VR.

A word of warning though - If you haven't gotten your VR legs yet, play this sitting down. There's been many a moment where I've had to just pause the game for a moment to ease the nausea that came with climbing, running, or floating and falling. I also had a brief sensation of detachment from my arms after taking the headset off. From experience with other games, these symptoms will pass, but it's definitely a little disorienting at first.

With that all said, I wholeheartedly recommend Boneworks. This is the gold standard for what VR games should aspire to and I hope it sends a ripple through the industry for developers to take note.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been anticipating this game for a long long time now. When I first tried Boneworks I was having a lot of issues moving around and was getting really frustrated, even after following Node and SLZ and seeing a ton of gameplay throughout the development of the game. Today I went back in with a fresh mind and gave it another shot, and had a much better time. I already had my "VR Legs", but I had to retrain my mind to account for having a full physics body, and now that I'm able to easily navigate around, I'm having a blast! Pulling yourself up on a ledge and standing up for the first time is super satisfying. It's like the VR equivalent to learning how to crouch-jump in the first Half-Life. I still have a long way to go to finish the game, but I'm having so much fun just exploring and seeing what I can do. Great job SLZ!\t\t\t\t\t

Ridiculous amount of loading screens.
Controls are the worst of any VR game I've played.
Constantly clipping into geometry and getting stuck.
Looks awful.
Doesn't really use the index finger tracking for anything despite how much the marketing played it up. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tI used my Valve Index for this game, while in roomscale

EDIT: Took another go at it, redid my height settings, and let auto floor detection do its thing. This is indeed an extremely well done VR adventure game, the best in a long time. The game still suffers from awkward inventory management typical of FPS shooters that use body harness inventory, but the issue is mitigated by a sub menu system.
Pacing between puzzles and exploration are done very well, puzzles are on the simpler side I think, but makes great use of intuitive physics based puzzles. These guys reeaallllly love Half Life 2 it seems...
FPS portions are more on the arcade side, so don't expect release mechanisms, fire selection, or safeties, but I think this was done in order to bring the shooting portions to focus. Personally I think safety would be nice, I've shot myself on occasion, but I might be stupid.
Some areas require scaling certain objects or terrain, and it works well, most of the time. I found my hands to spaz uncontrollably should I try moving them "the wrong way".

Overall, this is a fantastic entry for anyone into VR and would like a fun adventure. I still believe the museum segment is the absolute best for anyone getting acclimated to VR, and the devs should look into expanding it as well, maybe make it's own thing.

EDIT End

I need to stress that this game is an otherwise very well done demonstration environment interaction coupled with basic gunplay and exploration.

At the same it's the environment interactions that brings the game to a grinding halt. Parkour sections are awkward, object stacking and climbing doesn't work all that well, and the hands are prone to bugging out. Many sections require using boxes to climb onto platforms, except the boxes themselves are prone to being whisked away by questionable physics as soon as you try to walk on them.

There's no vaulting over walls and ledges either, I ended up switching over to "Seated" mode, adjusted floor and height settings, and gave myself a massive jump boost just so I didn't have to deal with climbing anymore. I've gotten motion sickness twice, and it was all from trying to do puzzles while standing.

Gunplay is somewhat basic, but serviceable. You take a gun, load the clip, figure out where the cocking handle is and go to town. There's not much in the way of fire selection or having a magazine release, but that's not important. What's odd is that you can accidentally eject an unspent round, but you can't pick it back up to reload a magazine with.

There's some emphasis on saving ammo, as you're given melee weapons, but then it's a moot point when it only takes a single bullet to the head. Still melee weapons CAN be fun, but it ends up being more clunky. Think near literally any VR game that features melee weapon, but ultimately leads to frantic swinging. There is a swing mechanic in place, favoring longer and heavier swings for more damage, but that's still finicky in itself, as there is fake weight placed on heavier objects so you can't move them as fast, which leads back into questionable handling of world objects.


I'll end it with this:

It's a great exploration type game, with good locomotion and enough feeling for combat. Much of the game feels as though it takes ques from the age old Half-Life games. It's certainly a breath of fresh air, since no one else has a done a good job without resorting to wave based shooting and/or using out dated teleporting mechanics for locomotion. I would absolutely love and will look forward to more projects like this from Stress Level Zero.

However, more work could be had with the "physics" side of things, especially anything involving a puzzle. That being said, I'm not sure if it could be improved, as the entire game was designed around the current limits of VR technology, which isn't good enough to emulate our hands, let alone solve the issue for tangible virtual items and weight.

TL;DR

A worthy try at making the best possible experience with the current tech VR has to offer, but not without shortcomings from the same tech used. The intro/museum segment is arguably the best VR has to offer right now, even for anyone just getting into VR.

Tidbits: For the museum segment, there needs to be a function to reset items for the Advanced Topics area, I softlocked myself after discovering that TWO(2) balloon guns existed, prompting me to run back through the item barrier, thus permanently losing both balloon guns.\t\t\t\t\t

WMR Support is spotty, now it's playable through a comunity made binding. This game is Jenky, but still really engaging.
