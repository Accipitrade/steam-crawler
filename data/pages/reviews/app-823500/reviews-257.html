\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHot take: Physics and VR don't go well together. You'd think they do, but there's this problem: your avatar's movement and your body's movement are not in alignment if your avatar carries anything heavier or hits a wall. Worst case it can cause you to feel sick, best case - it's just imprecise and not fun. And physics are BONEWORKS' main "thing" - so if you don't enjoy that part, the rest of the game simply isn't gripping enough to keep going.

In a nutshell: It's the original Half-Life, except lesser in every way you can imagine, and in VR. If that sounds great to you, and you don't mind the physics problem, then this is not half bad. I'll try to give a more detailed description, spoiler free - both the good and the bad. Note: I played on Valve Index.

The Combat
Ah, it's so right and yet so wrong. It's very physically correct: enemies trip, it's possible to push them, sweep their legs using heavier weapons (at least ones that have legs), or knock them back with a well placed blow. But sadly all that is weighted down by clunky weapons handling. The only weapon which feels good is the handgun (Glock), and perhaps the hatchet. Overall, you have many, many kinds of weapons: one handed, two handed, bladed, blunt, pistols, rifles, SMGs. Everything is also physically correct: there's weight, recoil, proper reloading, different grab points; every weapon can be thrown or used as a bludgeon (more or less).

And it's just so clunky and unfun.

Case in point: I got pretty good with the hatchet, striking the enemies' heads and killing them in one shot. I wanted to step up my game and try throwing the hatchet. And that's where it fails: you can throw, but the detection of you letting go of whatever you're holding and how you're holding the controller simply don't match. It is possible to somewhat train yourself to take that mismatch into consideration - I ended up being able to at least somewhat aim with throwables - but it feels incredibly unnatural.

Then there's the two-handed hammer. A relatively common weapon, a natural choice for breaking skulls as well as crates. Sadly, because you have to hold it with both hands, there's definitely going to be this mismatch between A(vatar)-hand and H(uman)-hand. A-Hands will follow the weapon due to the fact that you're holding it, while H-Hands will stay where you keep them. So to make things match up, you have to move the H-Hands to match the A-Hands. This creates this nonsensical situation in which it's the avatar that controls you - not the other way around. So unless some new crazy haptics solution shows up that properly simulates weight and is able to move your H-Hands, this is just not fun at all. And besides, smashing enemies' heads doesn't actually work as well as shooting them or splitting their skulls with a hatchet.

Finally, guns. The pistol is pure joy. It's accurate: you have to properly align the sights to make it shoot where you want. Taking a crowd of enemies with badass headshots one by one is a thrill. Then there are the rifles. My experience with firearms IRL is low - but not zero - and aiming down the sights does not work the way you expect it to because the stock gets in the way of your A-Body, it does not clip through and obviously you can't use it to stabilise the gun. Again, it's possible to learn to take the virtual stock into account; but the more natural solution is to find a gun with a laser sight and shoot from the hip like a cowboy. Getting a sniper scope also helps out.

The Enemies
I played only 6 hours, and during that time I only saw three kinds of enemies, two of which were mechanically identical; so in the end it's just two kinds. Let's call them the "zombie" and the "soldier". They act the way you expect them to. Zombies walk towards you and engage in melee combat, while soldiers use cover, spread out and try to shoot you. They're alright. But that's the problem here: it's just two kinds. For a game that is essentially an FPS (I know, it's not, but it's the closest analogue) this is a little low. And that makes things rather boring.

Wait, I actually lied. There were two more types of enemies - each of which I encountered only once. One was a "headcrab" - exactly what you expect (killing one with a carpenter's hammer was... an experience), and one was an immobile turret. I wish I could have seen them more often, both of them were dangerous in the situation they were in. For the most part, I was only fighting "zombies" and "soldiers". Mostly "zombies" in fact.

Did I feel threatened? I never died, but I came close twice. Once a zombie got a few good hits at me when I was unarmed - gave him a piece of my fist. And the second was in an extended firefight against the soldiers; I was scrambling for cover while trying to reload my rifle. I ended up dropping it and using my handgun from behind a barrier. The game uses regenerating HP formula, by the way. That probably sounds cool, but it was not very cool to actually do all that stuff.

The Physics and Puzzles
Early on, the game tells you that the puzzle sections are meant to be accomplished any way you can, including "cheating". That was quite refreshing and set the tone for the puzzles rather well. That said, I think I solved every single puzzle by "cheating" - which usually involved sprinting, jumping, climbing, rather than thinking. And it's not that I didn't try; I just didn't really see the purpose of many elements. Were they red herrings? Or was I too stupid? I can't say. Overall, the puzzles involved moving large objects to create pathways, clearing paths for objects to be moved, and ultimately some minor platforming.

The platforming and climbing in particular deserve a special mention. If you can grab a ledge with your hands, then you can probably climb it. It's a little hard to do and quite confusing - again, due to the mismatch between your A-Body and H-Body - but once you learn how to do it, you can do it almost every time. It involves a variation of doing the crouch jump maneuver with your body. Actually lots of effort. That's probably my favourite part of the game, actually. Then, there's climbing: certain pipes and some surfaces can be grabbed, and even if something can't be grabbed you can probably wrap your arms around it a little, and hold on like that. I once fell from a bridge and managed to climb back on it using those techniques. That also was quite a moment - there's nothing like that anywhere else in the world of gaming. Even Climbey - another VR game which is just about climbing - doesn't do this as well.

The World and The Lore
In my review of Black Mesa, I wrote that Half-Life's main draw is it's environment design. This game clearly tries to emulate that, but simply doesn't do it as well. It tries. But overall it's just nowhere near as fun to explore. There does seem to be some lore, which I never really got to explore due to, well, not liking the other parts.

All in all, this game takes place in a VR environment - yes, really, this is actually really interesting - which seems to be a simulated... city? Facility? Hard to say. Because of this, there are places which are f. ex. garbage processing, but instead it's "geometry processing". Also, your bullets are the currency, like in Metro - but they're very plentiful. There were some interesting parts, such as a museum which explains the alternative timeline of BONEWORKS' world, but overall it was just very meh and not exciting to explore.

Conclusion
It's a very middling game. I think the reason why it's so highly rated is because there aren't too many "real" games on VR, but VR itself is incredibly fun. So people are enjoying themselves due to VR itself, not the game. BONEWORKS isn't bad; it's average. I think there is fun to be had here, if you look for it. I, however, realised I'm forcing myself to play instead of enjoying it. Hope that helps. Enjoy!\t\t\t\t\t
