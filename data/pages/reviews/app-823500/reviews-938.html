\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is a strange game to me.

The shooting and gun mechanics are good, not the best, but the physicality is what sells it above other types of VR shooters. The fact that you can pistol whip and enemy, or rifle butt someone does change things; that cannot be undersold actually. Even Half Life Alyx misses some of these key interactions, and this is where Boneworks shines.

It's a fantastic sandbox, that gives the player an amazing amount of control over the world. You rarely feel limited by anything other than your aim and skill. Almost every enemy dies from a swift one tap to the head, so much so that rifles feel almost redundant to a swift pistol shot. It remains satisfying, but it is very easy if your VR gun aim is good.

This is where the praise of this game should end; it's worthwhile to play, in my opinion, the fully physical world deserves high praise. BUT, it also lead to some complacency in the creation of this game from my perspective.

The "campaign" is not carried by this, in fact, often it just felt boring and somewhat uninspired; that the lead up and completion of each level had no connected feeling, no story, no drive or motivation. In fact, it felt like people just kinda made a start and end level and decided to just make things to tick boxes. Some levels feel more like this than others. A few have some clever design, like the warehouse and the sewer levels. But outside of a few small examples, most of the campaign feels uninspired and pretty dull outside of an excuse to exhaust the one part of the game that shines. Because the enemies in combat are either stupid zombies that act more as toys than enemies, or enemies that take pot shots from any range, which does end up taking away from the main selling point of the game; the freedom.

This game benefits from that freedom immensely; like in the first level, you're put in the path of a turret with very few options to deal with it and players are forced to get creative. Picking up a trash can lid to circumvent it's gaze was something I won't readily forget, but I honestly cannot think of a single other moment in the whole game like this. I guess it's a fundamental issue with the enemy design and level design. A good compromise would've been stealth mixed with a more limited resource set. Save the open gunfights for the late game, and force the players to avoid the roller ball guards. Forcing every element of a sandbox to be relevant instead of just popping a headshot would make the game immensely more immersive.

Perhaps ammo not being currency, but a resource that is far more limited to give-way to the much more impressive physics system to combat the nullbodies; like picking up cinderblocks to save ammo for enemies with guns. This solution solves two problems, the first was the issue of nullbodies being complete pushovers (literally) and makes each encounter more meaningful and improvised.

As it is now; the options are spend 3 of my 2000 bullets to kill 3 nullbodies, or put myself in harms way to force myself into a more engaging experience but risk taking damage while dealing with the games previously abysmal checkpoint system. (now fixed I think, I haven't tried it myself)

This is the inverse problem of HL Alyx, because the game limits your resource and options to kill an enemy even without those resources. The only option is to conserve ammo but still spend some to kill enemies, which is engaging in a different way, but still far more meaningful of a choice than most anything present in Boneworks.

All this to say, that despite the fundamental design flaws that keep this game from being a true VR masterpiece, it's still one of the best bases for a VR game that currently exists; the only close contenders are Blade and Sorcery (Which has masterfully done melee combat and far far better AI) and HL Alyx (purely because of how well polished the game is as opposed to it's core gameplay loop).\t\t\t\t\t
