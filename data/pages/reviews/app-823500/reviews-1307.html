\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of the best VR games to date. I say this not yet having played any of the Oculus exclusives.

If you like Half-Life 2, you'll love this. The story is a lot more lowkey, and of the "excuse for the game scenario" variety as opposed to the "what a great narrative" kind.

Fun gunplay, physics puzzles (with multiple solutions, so whatever you do to figure it out, you'll feel smart), and just the right kind of ability to use your own basic logic to get things done. You can hold things how you want, use them how you want, and feel confident that your ideas will work (for example, using a crowbar to help with climbing).

The levels are somewhat non-linear. I say somewhat because there's one exit (as far as I know), but you might take one of multiple paths to get there. You're often rewarded for exploring off the beaten path. The music is solid.

The levels can run more than an hour long your first time through, but if you die or quit, you have to start from the beginning, as the game only saves when you finish a level. There are no checkpoints or anything, even if a given level requires a good deal of legwork, or has you fighting a bunch of enemies.

Performance is okay on my 2080 Super and 4790k, although I do get some issues in certain scenes and situations. The reprojection isn't enough to significantly impact my experience, though I haven't played with fpsVR, so I can't say exactly how "bad" it is.

Note that the game doesn't have any kind of comfort options you might be used to from other games, so if you don't have strong VR legs, well, this game will help you earn them quickly.

Boneworks, an indie game, is setting a standard for presence in VR that I think the vast majority of even AAA titles won't reach for quite some time. Blade and Sorcery is the next best thing in terms of how weight is represented, and even that game does so in a fairly simplistic way; Boneworks items actually feel like the weight is where it actually is, rather than spread evenly over the whole object. What's clever is how when something is heavy, your character's movement is restricted realistically while holding it.

I used a Vive with Index controllers. If you're still using wands, then know that this game expects you to hold down those irritating grip buttons to carry things, so you might wanna hold out for an update that switches that to a toggle. Other than that, this game is a great buy, even when it isn't on sale.

It lives up to the hype.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEdit: Nothing has changed save for my perception. Same issues still stand but touching on the positives, the game offers enough new and interesting gameplay in the VR space that I cannot tell enthusiasts to stay away from it.

Major pain points: No proper save functionality, hands easily get stuck in objects since they have always have collision (and no haptic feedback for contact).
Minor pain points: Visuals can be summed up as "dev environment" plus a few interesting bits, sound design varies with most other sounds being alright but voice acting being poor.

As it stands it's fun to mess around with the physics interactions, but too much is missing or flawed for me to give a pure recommendation. I'm really enjoying moments of it, but some design choices make me forget all the fun I'm having.

Dreams and wishes:
1) A better save/checkpoint system to avoid excessive "commuting".
2) The ability to free your hands so that the solid wall that's got your arm in a vise will hold you no longer.
3) Better cutting blades (they pierce very well (too well), but mostly act like blunt instruments when trying to cut).
4) A little more control over objects' rotation when holding them and little less noodle arms. Even when holding large objects (e.g. a bench) with one hand, you can use your fingers to stabilize the object in question, and your brain automatically fine tunes your motions to keep the object still. Neither of these can really be done by the user, so it would have to be taken into account in the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn the game's current state, I can't recommend it. For a physics-based game, the physics are horrible. I spent most of my time playing the game getting stuck in objects and having to fight the game to be able to play. Enemies aren't a threat, levels aren't exactly well-designed, there's no save option, art direction could really use some improvement, and the game NEEDS comfort options.

The way that the game is marketed as being for "advanced VR players" and calls teleport locomotion "primitive" just comes off as elitist, and shows that the devs are in kind of a bubble where they don't realize that the majority of players are going to feel nauseous playing this game.

They need to allow hands to pass through objects when you're not looking at them (or at least have that as an option), and hand actions CAN NOT affect head position unless climbing. That's a big one. There are so many points where I was opening a locker or pulling a lever and I just got hoisted into the air and immediately felt like I was going to puke. More than anything, Boneworks is a great example of why having a full body in VR is a bad idea. Seeing your legs and elbows be in entirely the wrong place just wrecks it.

Also, the way that your hands drag behind their real-life position when you're swinging something kills immersion. I won't say that the game isn't immersive though, every time I took the headset off to take a break (which they recommend, despite having no save & quit option) I would have some really weird disassociation that I've never had with other VR games. My hands felt fake when I used my phone. Entirely anecdotal, but I kinda thought it was cool that a VR game could make me feel that immersed.

I want to like the game, but it really needs to be fixed. Please add comfort and physics options, SL0. I've refunded for now mainly because I needed the money and I wasn't feeling it in the game's current state, but if these things are fixed, I'll absolutely buy it again because it's a great concept that's just not well-implemented right now.\t\t\t\t\t
