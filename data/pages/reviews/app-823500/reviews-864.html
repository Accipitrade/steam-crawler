\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAwesome game! I use the Oculus Quest with a Link cable, and it works great! I've probably spent over 10 hours playing the Story Mode, so there is a LOT of content. There are many different weapons to choose from, including a AR-15, an M4 (with laser, flashlight, vert. grip, or scope), an UZI, a few different "standard" pistols, a couple SMGs, baseball bats, wrenches, battle axes, bricks, and literally everything else that you can find. Boneworks is a physics demo/simulation where, if it is in your play area, you can climb, pick up, or smash it. There are several different enemies, all with different characteristics and models/textures. The AI for them works really well, although there are a few moments where I realize they are morons, and I laugh. It was definitely worth the money (I bought it during the Steam Summer Sale), and would have paid more than full price for it. The graphics are pretty good, better than I have seen in most other VR games. Also, other than Story Mode, there is Arena, where you can just fight guys that come after you in waves, and Sandbox, where you can mess around with everything in the game (assuming you threw your items into Reclamation Bins in Story Mode).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's an understatement to say that [mechanically], Boneworks is the true successor to the style of Half-Life's physics. I could even go to an extent that Boneworks is more worth your time (and money) than Half-Life Alyx, which is the VR game that recently launched.

It brings me great joy to say this is what raises the bar in advancements with VR gaming. Despite some gripes with the body system which janks out most of the time, having an issue with aiming down the sights and a few other tidbits with some of it's level design (which is a BIG issue at the last portion of the game), the game holds up pretty well. Also noteworthy, you have multiple options to tackle some of the stages presented, which is great for replayability along with it's extra modes. Story-wise, I couldn't bother much with it, but it does have a lot of different nuances, ranging from light-hearted and action packed, to ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ insane and horror clenching with it's segments. The best part is that the game has a very immersive ambience, not only from it's great music and interactivity with the enviroment, but also by ususual puzzles and progression.

Just be careful when playing, this game might exert you to more strainous activity than usual on VR and it's not great for a First Intro into VR. Give this to someone used to it and willing to experiment with new stuff. This will definently spoil you for other current VR games in the market.\t\t\t\t\t

Fantastic tech demo, sub-par game.
Great fun for a few hours if you get it very cheap, otherwise over-priced for the experience.

- Amazing VR physics and combat experience.
- Enjoyable tools to play around in physical VR space.
- Disconnected and completely un-interesting "story" & boring world.
- Frequent struggles with various physics and climbing puzzles.
- Collect-a-thon required to enjoy sandbox, with strong limitations on collecting ability.
- So much potential as a tool, sloppily presented as a game.

Additionally note; the game uses the Joysticks for locomotion and body movement. This is crucial to the game, and while I enjoy this mode of VR movement I know some people find this method dizzying.

- - - - -
"What if this is just a tech demo?"
If I could, I would Neutral rate this game, but because it is so highly rated already and costs so much (~US$30 right now), along with my overall experience of the game I can't in good conscience write a positive review. If you're on the fence about buying this game, give this a read.

Firstly, the mechanics in this game are incredible, almost incomparable. It is still a janky mess, which can get frustrating at times particularly during certain physics or climbing puzzles, but even then the physics mechanics are still fantastic; generally objects behave amazingly realistically and it can be very satisfying at times. It was one of the most realistic "trash the room" games I've played where you can pick-up anything and it feels like it weighs as much as it should, and as you flail about or throw things around all the objects behave appropriately and impressively.

Additionally the AI body mechanics are deceptively impressive; shoot a leg and they will limp, damage their arm and they can't punch. You can skewer limbs together and the AI will still try to find a way to carry out their goal (usually to kill you). This depth of AI using rigid-body coding to behave feels like a notably more realistic combat variant of Gorn, and really shines during melee combat sections such as the Arena, but rarely was even noticeable during the story as I just found myself quickly shooting enemies and moving on.

With climbing mechanics and puzzles; I was frequently disappointed or just frustrated. As someone who loves committing too-much time to attempting to explore in most games, I was either always annoyed or just bored in this game. Too many times I would scale a pipe and swing across a beam, only to have my body thrown off the platform I was attempting to land on. I "solved" many climbing puzzles by getting up as high as possible and shaking around until I forced my body inside the pipe so when I let go I would clip on-top of it. It isn't fun, and relies on boring trial-and-error and even breaking the game a bit (the tutorial even encourages breaking the game). The extra problem with this? The collect-a-thon aspect of the game relies heavily on solving these puzzles.

I am a sucker for collect-a-thons, I want to get all the "things" a game can offer, and throughout the story of this game there are many things to collect. As I already explained, a lot of these are hidden behind climbing puzzles, while a few are tucked away in usually clever secret areas (some are genuinely well done and interesting!). Unlike other collect-a-thons however, if you want to "collect" that item, you need to carry it to the end of the level and put it in a bin. If you don't, that puzzle you solved was for nothing. Ideally, you would simply "collect" the item by discovering it, but instead you have to slot it into your limited inventory space, preventing you from carrying fun weapons, and haul it all the way to the end. The number of times I got to the end of a level with a single weapon with every other slot being occupied by a collectable was... well every level. And this is because I refused to replay the story just to go back and get these items. A quick personal note on this, the final "battle" (if you could call it that) you get the opportunity to spend ALL your in-game money on super expensive super weapons, which you can of-course unlock, but only if before the battle ends and forces you away, you stopped and disposed of your powerful items in the bin. There is no prompt you will get whisked away, and as such I spent my entire story's collected funds on an item I didn't even get a chance to unlock. :(

So the 'story'. The world itself started off feeling relatively well constructed, particularly in the tutorial, but as you continue it feels more and more like the designers bought a few asset packs and smashed together a repetitive and uninteresting series of levels and then crammed in a few enemies and weapons (they even self-parodied and had signs up in one of the final levels saying it was "Asset Plaza"). Occasionally there was a cool non-linearity to a level, but because of the aforementioned collect-a-thon, you still go back and explore everything anyway. The combat aspect of the story was painfully easy; even when I would forget I could slow time. Often I just blitzed through, killed everything, then back-tracked to look for collectables. While some puzzles were an interesting display of the capabilities of the engine, I never felt an "I did it!" moment the entire game, more of a "let's keep going." The story itself? God... I love complex and meta stories, but they introduced a handful of interesting story points, occasionally had a "real" character talk to us via video, and then never expanding or addressing anything. Empty plot-points just to pretend there was a story. I had zero engagement in the story what-so-ever, and when it ended I was just happy to be done.

Beyond the story there are two other modes; Arena and Sandbox. Within Arena essentially there are two internal modes; Arena Combat and Zombie Survival. The Combat provides you with with both a handful of (pretty easy) challenges and trials as well as tools for a wave-based survival mode. You get to use weapons you unlocked in your story to fight, so added incentive to replay the story... The Zombie mode was also relatively fun and a solid showcase of some more of the combat as you either survived waves or completed mini-missions in an urban arena, but I still felt it was very easy and I quickly got bored.

The Sandbox mode was... eh? Firstly, it is a fantastic way to test and play with everything the engine has to offer, but at the end of the day this mode is just a tool, and one that relies on you having completed the story and gone out of your way to collect everything fun. There are a few sandboxes to play in but personally I was so done with the game by this stage I genuinely didn't find it much fun.

So, what do I think?
I genuinely believe the work that went into building the engine and mechanics for this game should be highly praised (fantastic work Stress Level Zero!), but the story mode is useless. I would have just had the Arcade and Sandbox modes (putting more effort into them), and collect items through challenges, or at least be able to instantly unlock collectables by just interacting with them. I am firm this game is absolutely just a tech-demo for Alyx, and while it has some additional features Alyx doesn't, Alyx does have an open Workshop which honestly means anything is possible at this point. I was disappointed, but possibly only because it was so hyped up.

Alternatives:
- Buy Half-Life: Alyx for a WAY better version of this game or just want this with a solid story.
- Buy Hotdogs, Horseshoes & Handgrenades for better gun mechanics and more weapon features.
- Buy Gorn for dedicated melee combat in an arcade mode with enjoyable unlocks and modifiers.
- Buy this tech-demo if it's super cheap, because it is definitely worth some money and the devs should still be applauded~\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere are few VR games that don't feel like a novelty. Most are arcade-like or can be experienced just as well outside of the big face box you plug into your pc. These games can definitely be fun, but in the end, they're not too remarkable or anything.
That being said, this game is one of the very few that could never be experienced outside of VR. Its a ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ masterpiece.
The story, the sandbox features, the puzzles, the guns. Its all is so fun. I don't even know where to begin. It is truly such a creative and fun experience.

I'm not going to give too much away, simply because it is such a unique experience. It's truly something you have to play for yourself in order to understand.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tImagine you want to make a game, so you get yourself a really good game engine. The trouble is that it's complicated and has quite a high learning curve. You mess around with it and get some ideas. Next you start to make levels. They're not great to look at (you don't both to add textures or lighting) or to play, but they're helping you learn and gain the skills to make something better. Eventually you have the ability to make a game - only you look back at the work you've done already and you think 'that's a lot of stuff and I should sell that. I know it's not really any good and it doesn't even look finished, but the engine is cool and I like money, so why not?' Congratulations, you have just made Boneworks.

Boneworks has a really cool physics engine. It also has very little else good about it. The levels are poorly designed, seemingly with little thought. It looks like it will be interesting to explore, but there's really only one way to go.

For a game with 'Stress Level Zero' above its name, it's also intensely frustrating. The physics puzzles are a boring mess and I felt like I was just using the borderline-broken climbing system to get around them more than anything else. Sometimes you'll climb perfectly fine, but then try to do the same thing again in exactly the same place and it won't work, seemingly for no reason. You'll just end up waving your arms all over the place in hope of getting somewhere.

It's also really ugly. Even with the graphics settings on maximum it looks really bad, both technically and on as aesthetic level. It looks like some boring generic models bought cheaply and thrown together, with extremely flat and bland lighting and really bad, basically unfinished texture work. Seriously, so much of it looks like those 'developer textures' they use to test the game before, you know, they finish making it.

Gameplay is: look for weapons and attack stupid enemies. The weapons can be fun to use, but you can't take them between levels (which you could do in Doom 27 years ago) so don't get too attached. Most enemies stand around and then walk at you swinging their arms. Enemies with weapons walk towards you and shoot. There are no tactics. I fought seven armed enemies by standing around a corner and waiting for them to come to me.

Sounds effects are okay, but special mention has to go to the radios that can be found around the levels. They all play the same song and it is absolutely atrocious, easily one of the worst songs I've ever heard in my life. Destroying those radios is about the most satisfying thing this game can offer.

I'd look at another game from the same developers, but this? This is the most 'meh' game I've ever played in 35 years of gaming. It looks a lot of fun, but fun is hard to find.\t\t\t\t\t
