\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was so hyped for this game. Hyped enough to buy a headset on Black Friday in prep for this game. I have a whole bunch of other games but really was looking forward to this one. This is not a negative review, simply one describing why this game didn't work for me.

Hardware:
Intel i7-4770
NVIDIA GTX 970
MSI MPowerMax Motherboard
Oculus Rift S

Pros:
-Set Height and floor offset: This feature is amazing and something any VR dev should implement, I hate when I can't grab something because the floor is lower than the game says it is
-Physics: Obviously this is a plus, almost everything has physics in the game, with the exception of certain broken objects pieces

Cons:
-Sprinting: Sprinting is awkward in this game as it feels more like a fast walk. This feeling is furthered when looking at the model run below you.
-Overdone Physics- I get it this game is about how everything interacts with everything in a realistic way, however, this is VR. Or at least VR as we know it today. If I touch a wall in VR my hand in the game stays on the wall but my real hand keeps going. In this specific example that doesn't really break immersion, especially if there's some haptic feedback. When it does break immersion is when I'm opening a cabinet and reach in to grab an item and then the cabinet melds with my arm and follows me across the map. Or when I touch a shelf and the simplest movement of my arm forces the thing to go flying when I just wanted to grab the can on its shelf. While I understand how complex game development is and the time it would take, there needs to be balance in the system. If I put my hand in a cabinet it should move around the object unless I'm grabbing it. A shelf should weigh enough that in combination with the previous mechanic I should not be able to send it to space or have it topple on me unless I'm grabbing it and chucking it.
-Guns: This one saddened me the most. I was really hyped for the prospect of guns in an ultra realistic physics environment. It just didn't work for me. I mean of course I had to do the slow mo gun flip reload and that work amizingly well, but I ran into a major hardware issue. On the Oculus Rift S the controllers have inverted rings compared to their predecessor. This doesn't affect me in games like Pavlov, but in Boneworks I was barely able to reload as I had to slowly touch my controllers together and wait for the white circle to appear for my hand to pull the action back. I know they made this game mainly for the Index but it feels like I can't enjoy the gun mechanics at all due to this. In Pavlov there is nothing more satisfying than dropping a mag from a pistol, slamming a new mag in it, pulling the slide back, and unloading. Didn't even get in the same ballpark with this game.
-Ambiance(Highly Subjective): The physical look, sound, and feel of the game was more meh than I expected. The world looks like it was overlooked in major areas such as the distant backgrounds and areas that the player doesn't interact with. Whether it was low res or odd color schemes I didn't vibe with it. In certain areas it felt like an old cartoon in the way that areas that were meant to be interacted with had more detail than their surroundings. There was a "secret" door in the museum of technical movement or whatever that had the same texture as the wall but had a barely noticeable gap that indicated a hinge on its opposite side. Not like it was purposefully done, more like I could see through the wall at certain points. Then the soundtrack. I didn't like it, it frustrated me even especially after accidentally falling off the first puzzle twice cause my player model kept running after I stopped moving the joystick. I ended up muting it.
-Climbing: Another game mechanic I wanted to love. It worked decently well on climbable surfaces, but I could never get over a 90 degree ledge. I would run up the warp wall or jump to a ledge and stick it, pull myself halfway up, and fall. i couldn't grip the floor, I couldn't yoink myself up, I couldn't jump up, I couldn't tiptoe up, nada.
-The Force: This game mechanic worked real well most of the time, but when I broke an object and a brick, for example, was laying underneath it but very visible, it wouldn't let me force-grab it. This happened too frequently and I ended up bending over to pick up items more than I wanted to.

Overall a good game, just not for my tastes or my hardware. It needs polish and Brandon acknowledged this in Node's most recent video with D. Maybe I'll buy it again when it's been polished.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was looking forward to this game, I really was. The physics are cool, a game changer for sure. All the cool new ways to interact with the world around you. I would recommend this to people just getting into VR or people looking at getting the most realistic VR experience available currently, as I don't actually like the game and I wouldn't recommend it anyone looking for something else.

My big problem is the downsides to having full physics in VR at the moment. My arms getting caught on everything and I gotta wiggle my real arms to figure out where they went. Climbing, I feel like my arms are about half as long as they actually are even though in game they look and feel right everywhere else. I also feel like its a bit too slow, but that might be because my arms are getting caught and slowing me down.

When it comes to weapons, the melee weapons cannot be beat. I don't think that there is a game that can beat how well melee weapons feel in this game. Blade and sorcery comes close but it has some issues. Just the way it interacts with not only enemies, but the world around you is great! Little things like the way you grab it changes how much damage it will do or how fast you can swing.

Guns, oh boy... It's hard to explain, but guns feel off, like everything is right but when you actually shoot it feels like you're compensating for something that doesn't exist? It's almost like the recoil is kicking the gun down then blasting back up past the dot to make it seem like recoil. I will say reloading is cool, it's very satisfying pulling the mag out and shoving a new one in.

Weapon/ Inventory slots are sometimes a nightmare. They don't seem large enough. Wheres my pistol? Dropped my crowbar again... Happened too many times and it upset me (maybe I'm just dumb). Dropped my stuff without noticing and walked away leaving important stuff in who knows where land. I'm sure however after putting a few more hours in it would have been fine and I'd be able to nail the Weapon/ Inventory slots no problem.

I think that this game has done a lot of good for the VR community and really shows what VR can do, hence the recommendation. It's just not the game for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving finished the game and done a bit of postgame content, I can say with confidence that SL0, against all odds, mostly lived up to the VR world's honestly inflated expectations for the game, and their brags on their YouTube channel about being "VR physics and combat realized".

Most stuff feels pretty good! The weightiness of certain weapons and items and how to properly leverage that weight takes a little getting used to, but it works pretty well once that's figured out. I will say climbing generally feels really crappy and sluggish, and grabbing and throwing enemies to the ground is way harder than it looks.

Gunplay is pretty clean. It's a bit too easy to grab the mag out of the gun instead of two-handing it, which is a problem. Otherwise, the guns shoot where you point, the NPCs are a bit simplistic in terms of behavior but very fun to fight regardless. The ammo system is a unique take that I think works out very well for that "action movie" feel - you have an ammo pool which decreases by 1 every time you shoot which is independent of how many rounds are actually left in your mag. This means that while you have to reload, dumping a full mag never means you lose those rounds, which makes reloading more of a "no spraying" measure.

An important point on gunplay - two-handing unstocked firearms is UNBELIEVABLY stable for aiming. Whatever they did with the position filtering for this class of weapon needs to be open sourced and used everywhere RIGHT NOW, because no other game does it better than Boneworks right now. I can't stress how happy it makes me to not have to viscerally hate an MP5K the instant I see it.

The game world they've set up is also super intriguing - a virtual city abandoned and quarantined, with you against anything that moves - and their method of storytelling and guiding you (mostly environmentally) is usually fairly effective. It gives off Half-Life 2 vibes; abandoned cities overrun by zombies and security forces, with you alone tasked with getting through it. I found myself completely lost in the world of Boneworks around the back half of my playthrough, to the point where I was unintentionally dropping snappy oneliners and dramatically drawing my guns at slowly approaching enemies like an overweight bootleg Neo.

All of that praise aside, let's not kid ourselves here; this game is *jank*, though this isn't often a dealbreaker for the overall gameplay. My melee weapons will get stuck in my neck as I try to pull them out of my shoulder holsters, crouching too much makes it next to imposslble to grab items from your pouches, the game puts you through a few platforming sections that feel like crap with the innovative but still unruly jump mechanics... it's not often worse than other VR games, but fully physically-interactable "presence system" game environments and mechanics still need a lot of tweaking before they're 100%. I suspect if SL0 releases another VR game of this kind they will be able to pin down and make these improvements.

Physics puzzles were pretty grating on my patience, too; many require an awful lot of working with weighty objects, which are sluggish to grab and move at best. At times, it got to the point where I just said "screw it," and used climbing, stacking and other methods for doing what I had to do. I got used to things around the end of the game, but it was honestly a bit too late, as they weren't as heavy-handed with these janky puzzles in the back half.

Accessibility also leaves something to be desired. As a lefty, a left-handed hip pouch would have been nice. Subtitles would be great for some of the sections involving dialog, as I completely missed what certain voice lines were saying outright. And, of course, this is to say nothing of the lack of comfort options, which I'm sure lots of negative reviews from people that got severe sim sickness from just the earlier parts of the game can tell you all about.

Overall, I think this game serves as a pretty important stepping stone for VR as a lot of games move towards more environmental interactions. It's not perfect by any means, but the experience is definitely worth the 30USD I paid for it. If you can stomach it, and don't mind putting up with some VR jankiness, then I highly recommend picking it up.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTarget Audience: VR Enthusiasts
Satisfactory? Yes.

Marketed Audience: General VR
Satisfactory? Somewhat, the game can be very rough on people who don't have their VR
legs yet. There are many points where the player completely loses control of the camera
and it is impressive that the game doesn't cause more motion sickness.

Story: 6/10
Graphics: 7/10
Physics: 10/10
Combat: 7/10
Controls:8/10
Comfort: 7/10
Avg: 7.5 ---------------- Pure average of all categories
Overall: 8.5 ----------- What I think the game deserves

Story: There are some PC monitors that occasionally come up with live action video to "talk" to the character. This gives you a basic understanding of what you are trying to accomplish but there isn't enough to really say that the game has a "story". I mean it's there but it's honestly rather irrelevant. The main focus of the game isn't story though so I decided not to weigh it down too much.

Graphics: The game looks old. This is more of a side effect of VR than anything else. Until we can get more complex scenes to run beautiful graphics at a constant 90fps VR games like this will have this problem. Especially with a game so physics heavy. Overall I never noticed significant drop of frames and the graphics are passable.

Physics: Everything is interactive, a coffee mug on a table, a gun, even the enemies you will be fighting. Knives will get stuck in enemies and require enough 'force' to pull it out. Puzzles require you to lift objects and drag them across rooms to put in the correct position. The AI will interact with other physics objects in the game as well. They do it in a way that reminds me of videos of machine learning stick figures figuring out how to walk. Physics is awesome and is definitely the reason for purchasing the game.

Combat: Almost mind numbingly easy. Don't get me wrong the gun play is fun but isn't anything new to VR. The melee combat feels great, and I love how anything can become a weapon. Everything is there to make an excellent combat experience, except the enemies. There are nullbodies, zombies, zombies who shoot lightening, rolling shooters, acid null bodies, and headsets with legs. Ultimately this comes to 3 enemy types, ranged, zombies, scary. The zombie type makes up most of the game and rarely pose a threat. Even when they reach you they are just as likely to follow over as they are to damage you. The zombies who shoot lightening shoot these orbs at you but they are easy to dodge because they move so slowly. The walking headsets will jump at you and put themselves on your head but you can catch them making them rather easy to deal with, though will give you a few jump scares. They can be fun to fight but aren't difficult at all. The rolling shooters roll around on these balls with guns and shoot at you. They are the most difficult enemy but because they are always rolling around on these balls the AI doesn't really effectively take cover and, ironically, the physics gets in the way of them being a difficult foe. I died a couple of times to them but they were decently rare so most of the game didn't have the one enemy that could put up a fight.

Controls: The menu system is brilliant. Perfect for VR and should definitely be taken as inspiration for future VR games.The game works perfectly with Index Controllers and finger tracking is shown really well. There is a two negative things. One, it doesn't seem possible to choose what ammo you bring out, so you can use your ammo to purchase things, and your hands get stuck on way too many things. It's full smooth locomotion with snapping that allows you to jump and even crouch, the crouching is really innovative.

Comfort: Considering everything that is going on in the game from your camera being moved when you're not expecting it to jumping mechanics and so on it's surprising that I encountered as little motion sickness as I did. This is certainly not the first VR game that you should try but it is one you should be fine with after getting used to VR for a few months.

Overall Boneworks is a VR game that has a baseline of interaction and immersion that all AAA VR games going forward should have. There are certainly some things that aren't necessary, for example the hands often get stuck on objects is more annoying if you're not making a puzzle game. The numerical value came out to a 7.5/10. In honesty this would be a fair score if the game wasn't so innovative. But because it is so innovative and long enough to not take off points, I think it should receive a higher score at 8.5/10.

One final complaint, add checkpoints within the levels. This is 2019 not having these is ridiculously tedious.\t\t\t\t\t
