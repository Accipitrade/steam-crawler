\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBONEWORKS was up there on my wishlist for a very long time. Since NODE's videos previewing the game, I was incredibly enthusiastic for the idea of a VR physics game, The physicality that was being demonstrated in the original videos was, and still is, in my mind the next step for VR. Tactile interaction is one of the sorely lacking experiences in most VR titles, with a few exceptions that, similar to BONEWORKS, pride themselves on physics.

This game sadly misses the mark.

The physics suffers from notorious bouts of "rubber-band arms" where it feels like I'm manipulating elastic more than solid muscle, making climbing far more difficult (and nauseating) than I was comfortable with. It has a distinctly Garry's Mod feel, where the physics itself almost rest in a tactile uncanny valley. Everything works juuust close enough to be engaging, but immersion is not in the cards. The fun, in my mind, rests in the fun interactions you can have when breaking the game, but it's those broken parts that usually get in the way while actually attempting to complete puzzles. I regularly found myself clipped onto something, usually out of my line of sight, or fighting with a heavy object that I couldn't necessarily drag around with any reliability, often with my own legs clipping into the object while I attempted to move it.

This only compounded as the game progressed, with the last few puzzles highlighting exactly what I described above. Anyone that would have played this game's finale section would have noticed wobbly arms while climbing or poor simulation of moving heavy objects, which (surprise!) are the two main components of the endgame section.

That's not to say there's not things this game does well: the gunplay is, as expected from NODE folks, punchy, well designed, and satisfying. There's just enough Quality of Life shortcuts that make the guns feel like an enabling tool rather than yet another obstacle, and I found the gunplay scenes were the points where I was the most engaged. I would be remiss if I didn't mention that most of my time spent with the M16, M4 Carbine, or Glock had me channeling reload drill techniques I learned with their IRL counterparts. Unfortunately this is does nothing to advance the game further among its peers: there's plenty of examples in the same realm, and I imagine anyone who spends a significant amount of time in VR would not see anything too novel.

And of course, the live action narration portions, cut-scenes, and other slides back to reality, are a nice touch. (Though not unlike early Command & Conquer titles, nobody's gonna win an Oscar from their performances.) The breakup of pace between sequences adds a layer of story and intrigue to an otherwise fairly self-governing experience. Unfortunately they're so sparse I find myself wanting more, desperate for more context that would allow myself further investment into the stylized world. Going the extra step to include any level of interaction with these moments would have pushed a rather forgettable portion of the experience over the edge into groundbreaking. (VR Stanley Parable anyone?)

At the end of the day, this is still a feat of development. Stress Level Zero have managed to create a physics game with a sparse, but engaging story, delivering the expected level of punchy gun play, and some novel and challenging puzzles. It evokes the same kind of wonderment and challenge I felt as a child with Portal 1 & 2, but manages to put its greatest selling point, the physics, in-between the character and its puzzles far more often than not. While I would love to be able to recommend this title to others, there's a lack of polish and deeper thinking to some of the core parts of the gameplay loop that leave me frustrated. The game feels like a rocket with just enough fuel to feel weightless for a short period of time, before being brought back down to the ground. A title that just manages to scrape the surface of the next frontier in VR experiences, but falls short just as it nears something profound.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is an amazing tech demo but bare bones in terms of being a video game. The physics while occasionally janky are for the most part superb and responsive, but i do have a few gripes I need to tell potential buyers....

climbing is great, until you reach the top and find you cant get on top of what you're climbing. Seriously WTF? I can climb anything a can get a grip on but cant pull myself on to a ledge because i cant lift my legs!

there is NO SAVE SYSTEM! you have to finish the level before a checkpoint is saved and that can take hours, need a break? F*** you go back to the start of the level and all your inventory is gone.

The HTC vive is an after thought, this game is unashamed of its Valve ass kissing. If you dont have the index knuckles you will get searing pain in your forearms after 20 mins because you have to hold the grip buttons to properly wield a melee weapon. There is no f****** reason why i have to hold the grip buttons when the triggers would do just fine, the firearms stick in my hand why cant the melee? because buy an Index that's why.

Its been 2 months since release why havent they been fixed? these problems shouldnt even be present in the first place. If these issues are addressed I would highly recommend this game to people who are looking for a more involved VR game but right now its great moments are over shadowed constantly by niggling, annoying and unnecessary design choices that as ive already said just scream buy the Index knuckles.\t\t\t\t\t
