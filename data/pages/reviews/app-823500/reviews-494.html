I've tried fix upon fix to no avail and now I am past the refund period. I have spent more time trying to fix the game than I have bloody playing it.

It's a real shame, because I was really impressed by the physics and unique ideas within the game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR- Boneworks is an ambitious but ultimately kind of unenjoyable game and I'm not entirely certain it wasn't made specifically to evoke the same sorts of feelings you get during a dissociative episode or a depression dream.

Boneworks is really into its physics. You have constant physics puzzles, enemies stumble around and have a full inverse kinematic model where they actually physically respond to the world around them rather than just playing canned animations, and even your own player character is physics based. This last one is where a huge part of the game's problems lie. I want to use a box for platforming- to get across a feeling of weight, your character's hands lag well behind where your real world hands are, you're slow, the box drags you around with its inertia... but when I put it down and jump on it just the force of my steps against it is enough to make it slide around or roll. I want to try pushing something heavy along the ground rather than lifting it up, hoping it will slide, but pushing it just pushes my character backwards. A moving bit of level geometry moves just close enough to me that my character is pushed down, forcing me into a sort of nauseating uncommanded virtual crouch. I want to pull a crowbar off of my back- I go to pull it out, but the "hook" at the top of the crowbar catches on my character's head. I reach into a trash can to grab something out of the bottom, but I bump it a bit so it tips over, leaving my hand stuck inside and my wrist clipping through part of the can. Every time something like this happens, it contributes to me feeling like your game is kind of janky. It reminds me I'm playing a video game- like, I didn't forget, but it breaks that immersion you get when you get invested in a game. Ironically, a more traditional system of movement where just moving around doesn't really affect the world around you as much, and your hands are only physics active when they need to be, would make the game feel more realistic, just because you aren't getting those moments of immersion-breaking jank.

This next bit requires a bit of vocabulary. Dissociation refers to a mental state where you feel disconnected from the world around you, sometimes even your own body. Everything feels alien and bizarre, often hostile and unpleasant. You may recognize things around you, be in a totally familiar context, be able to rationalize that you're safe and comfortable, but some wiring in your brain gets fried and hey now it's time to be constantly anxious and disconnected from everything. It's quite similar to (and sometimes it just straight up is the same thing as) the shock and grief feelings you might feel after learning someone close to you has died, or being involved in a traumatic incident like a car accident or a robbery. Boneworks ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ nails that feeling to the level that I'm almost convinced it's intentional... and uh, that's not a good thing! The environments in the game are often places obviously designed to have lots of people in them, but you're the only one there save for perhaps a few enemies who are bizarre approximations of human beings that would feel right at home in some kind of distressing fever dream, eliciting the same creepy feeling that an GMod map does. The landscapes are constantly bleak and barren- concrete and dev textures, stark lighting, a very drab color pallet. And the music... ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥, the music is ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ spot on for the audio representation of dissociation. In the Streets level, the primary ambient track is this meandering series of echoey electronic drones and tones. It is artistically interesting that they manage to convey these feelings this well. It makes the game ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ miserable. Imagine bashing your head against yet another physics puzzle while hearing really weirdly loud dissonant droning, where you get that same arms-made-of-jelly feeling you get in depression dreams because the way the game conveys weight means your real life hands are usually well ahead of your in game hands so actually performing actions is harder than it feels like it should be. This is not an experience I want.

So let's say we can excuse all that- you're a mentally typical individual so dissociation is just a fancy word for you and you just wanna shoot the cool holozombies. That's great! That part of the game is actually pretty fun! Too bad every time you start to enjoy it, you start to pick up momentum and feel yourself getting invested in the game... it's time for another ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ physics puzzle! The gameplay loop is constantly explore -> fight enemies -> physic puzzle -> repeat. Rarely are combat sections allowed to go on long enough to become interesting (most of the fights i've gotten in so far have taken me less than a full magazine to resolve), and the level design really doesn't lend itself to good exploration. Hell, the level design just kind of sucks full stop- everything is so tedious. I need to take an elevator up to a control room to open a door. To move the elevator, I have to power on some emergency brakes. Apparently the button at the bottom of the elevator- where I am- doesn't work, though, so I have to lower the elevator, engage the bottom-most brake, command the elevator back up so it gets stuck on the brake, then hop on the elevator and disengage that brake so I go up. Instead of just... like, powering the elevator on and going up. This doesn't add anything to the game, it just pads it out. There's a physics puzzle later on where a gravitational anomaly causes things inside of it to fall upwards, their gravity reversed. There is also a bridge that you need to cross that, when you step on it, falls under your weight. Said bridge is at the top of like four flights of stairs from where the bridge falls down to. You are provided with barrels to put into the gravitational anomaly to keep the bridge lifted up... and instead of having to throw maybe like five barrels into the anomaly (which isn't particularly hard, you just huck them in a general area and they float up), you have like fifteen or twenty barrels, and the bridge won't even go back up under its own weight until you have seven or eight of them in. And sure, you might be able to get away with just throwing a few more than that into the anomaly, but if the bridge falls back down you have to go back up four flights of stairs again. Tedium. I get it. I have solved the puzzle. Don't make me have to throw twenty ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ barrels into a spot to finish it. Don't make your levels huge and convoluted just for its own sake.

And then some miscellaneous gripes. Your handgun holsters are in really bizarre positions- they sit under your arms like shoulder holsters rather than a more traditional holster on your hip or maybe down on a thigh like a drop leg holster, which makes grabbing handguns kind of fiddly, especially if you're trying to draw from your left holster with your left hand or right holster with your right hand. Melee weapons, for some reason, have your character's default hold position halfway up the shaft- like, for a hammer, you're holding halfway up the shaft, which means literally any time I pull out a melee weapon I have to adjust my hold on it before using it (holding further down obviously gives you more range, and should result in more power being delivered by the strike, though I have no clue if that's the case in game or not). To pull anything out of a holster with oculus touch controllers, you have to pull the grip button as well as the trigger... which is an issue with guns because obviously the trigger fires the gun; it doesn't fire until you release the trigger and then pull it again, but it's just kind of weird, I can't think of any reason it should be like that, you can pick things up in the world just fine by using just the grip button.\t\t\t\t\t
