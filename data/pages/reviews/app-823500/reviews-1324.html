\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere are certain VR games where you just can't shake the feeling that you're puppeteering an avatar. Boneworks continuously reminds you that you're only loosely in control of a character and the game seems almost intentionally designed to cause nausea. There is a unique physics-based shooter lurking behind the janky interactions but you'll need patience to find it.

There is just far too much springiness to the player's head movement. Whenever you grab a climbable surface the camera will snap and sway in an extremely distracting manner. I don't get motion sickness but the climbing was an irritating chore compared to other VR games. I understand the developer wanting the player to fight gravity to keep them from just doing one-handed pull-ups but there were so many times during climbing where the camera was just swinging wildly and my virtual hands were separated from my real hands. Even quickly crouching reveals a bit of lag between your head and the camera as it catches up. This is not how you maintain the user's presence in VR.

Imagine your head is the ball at the end of an antenna attached to an RC car and you'll have an idea of what it feels like to move around in this game. Every time you run over a small bump or brush your hand on a wall or hold something heavy the player's collision rig will get jostled and end up moving the camera. Even if you don't easily get motion sickness the constant bobbing is presence-breaking especially when you're trying to climb or move a heavy object into place to solve a puzzle. Be advised that artificial locomotion with the thumbstick is the only way to get around and there are no comfort options like view vignettes or reference frames.

Your hands will often actually snag against things and get left behind. Sometimes I'll hold my real hand up in front of me and see nothing then realize I have to look around for my virtual hand because it got caught on something again. I also noticed the game would sometimes disregard the small object near my grabbing hand and would instead have my character grab the desk or shelf _under_ the object. There is a 'force grab' move to pull only the important items into your hand but that requires a trigger pull and is less natural than just squeezing the Index grip.

Once I got weapons into my hand I did enjoy using the pistols, they are straight-forward and responsive to handle. Some of the smaller melee weapons are also fun but the two-handed weapons feel sluggish. I know the developer wanted to convey the weight of larger swords and hammers but the effect ends up being like swinging through water or dealing with internet lag. Being able to hang from a crowbar hooked on a pipe is a neat interaction I admit though.

The performance of this game is mediocre. I ran my Index at 90Hz and at 80Hz and it would drop frames in either mode even when there was very little action going on. There are more physics-based interactions than the average VR game but after playing shooters like Contractors, Space Pirate Trainer, Compound, and Pistol Whip at a consistent 144 FPS the frequent hitching in Boneworks was a letdown.

I did like the art direction. The game features a sort of surreal mixture of polished office spaces combined with grimy urban and industrial sections. The levels are also full of humorous texture details and creepy corporate positivity. The sound design and score are well-crafted as well and fit nicely with the 'fractured reality' concept.

In its current state Boneworks feels like the Trespasser of VR. There are some fun moments when the physical interactions aren't literally tripping you up but I can't recommend it as a polished and complete game especially with the frame rate issues. I certainly don't think anyone sensitive to motion sickness should play it until it receives an update to 'de-spring' the player camera, or until it gets some comfort options.\t\t\t\t\t
