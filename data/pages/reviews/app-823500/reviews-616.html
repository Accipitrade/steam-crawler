\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt sucks to give a bad review to a game so many other people love, but there's only so many problems I can excuse.

Boneworks aims to do a lot of cool new things, but the execution and lack of polish leave me wishing the game spent a little bit more time in development.

There are three major areas where the game desperately needs love:
1. The puzzles
2. How the player's model is rigged and how it interacts with tools/weapons
3. The physics of how the player interacts with the world

There's two types of puzzles the player will typically run into: puzzles that must be solved in order for the player to progress, and puzzles that can be optionally solved to earn some kind of reward (ammo, collectables).

Puzzles that must be solved to progress in the game are relatively simple, but any pleasure that would be had in solving them is reduced because the solution to the puzzle is usually written on a wall or surface around the puzzle area. Arrows and text litter every map explaining where to go next and how each puzzle ought to be solved. There's also a lot of flavor text that develops the story which is great, but the narrative gets a bit confusing when flavor text is mixed with puzzle explanations.

Optional puzzles are much harder and rarely have much of an indicator that a puzzle is even there. These can be fun to solve, but many times I find myself fighting the game's physics engine and wondering if I'm solving the puzzle in the "right way", or if the physics associated with a given puzzle are just plain janky."

The player's model itself has some interesting animation/rigging that looks like it probably took a lot of time to figure out. The problem with this rigging is that it means your hands in the virtual space rarely match where your hands are in your physical play space. This causes a ton of problems when it comes to reloading weapons, interacting with climbable walls, and _especially_ stowing weapons in the inventory.

That last point is especially painful. Any time I stow a weapon (you do this by stashing it over your shoulder or under your arm, similar to HL:A), I have to look behind me to make sure the weapon was actually stowed instead of just being thrown behind me. To make this even more painful, sometimes a successfully stowed item will still make a "clank" noise as if it hit the ground.

The opposite problem happens when it comes to unstowing items. In tense situations surrounded by enemies, I often find myself accidentally unstowing items from my inventory instead of reloading my weapon. At the end of most battles, I usually have to go find all the weapons/items that I accidentally unstowed across the battle area.

My last issue relates to how the player interacts with the world through the physics system, specifically when handling heavy objects. When handling heavy objects in Boneworks, you must move slowly. This is to help the player "feel" the weight of the item they're interacting with, and a lot of the time, it's a great mechanic. The main issue is that this mechanic falls apart when handling physically large items that are also heavy. Physically large items almost always end up colliding with geometry around you when you try to move them. Managing these collisions is very difficult due to the size of the item you're manipulating (the collision often happens outside the player's field of view), and this difficulty is compounded by the fact that the player must manipulate the item slowly in order for it to move at all. I often find myself abandoning physics puzzles where large items are involved since moving and orienting them is such a chore.

I don't hate the game. For a tech demo, I would call it fantastic. For a full fledged game, I would call it incomplete and released far too soon. I think a few extra months of polishing out the edges would have paid off massive dividends and made this a much more enjoyable experience.\t\t\t\t\t
