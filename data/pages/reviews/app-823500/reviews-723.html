"What if this is just a tech demo?"

This shows up in many places throughout Boneworks. How meta, that an indie darling is totally aware that it grovels at the feet of the AAA darling pack-in. What becomes of a project, so originally rooted in its descendants, it supposedly existed as a forgotten Half-Life game, to become something different?

Half Life Alyx is a game, but Boneworks is the experiment. Half Life Alyx makes wise concessions to remain fun, Boneworks gives you no hand-holding. Half Life Alyx was realized with focus groups, market testing, and a design philosophy. Boneworks is fully-baked design concepts at challenge and harmony with each other. Half Life Alyx is the catapult, and Boneworks is the trebuchet, built by engineers and artists, that launches pumpkins just for fun.

The climbing brings back memories of Discovery Zone, monkeybars, and pantsing the fat kid at recess. The game rewards you for being clever about locomotion, in both experience and wealth. A stressful climb rewards you with unlockables.

The places Boneworks falls flat are mostly oriented towards narrative, gameplay shortcomings, and character animations. I can excuse the animations given the nature of IK; but having a well crafted walk cycle would add to the gravity of MythOS. Gameplay wise, I felt more akin to comparing Boneworks to a very linear Immersive Sim. Weapon choice, tactics, and environmental puzzle solving are rewarding in their own right. However, the linearity goes against the established tropes, and at times I wished for more open spaces, customizable weapons, and flexible gameplay styles outside of the tactical-styled shooting. The story is threadbare at best, but arguably its missing. There's no establishment of stakes, and my motivations don't become clear until 5 hours in. I feel unsympathetic to any cause, going through MythOS' paces. By the time its over, I feel confused, not resolute.

In closing, I ask of you to compare and contrast to Half Life Alyx and other VR FPS games and see for yourself. I argue above that the shortcomings and desires are nary enough to ruin the experience that Boneworks offers. The wake that all other VR experiences will swim behind, will indebt Boneworks as the pilot fish. It's worth your money, and your time.

80/100\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEXTREMELY disappointing ending for the campaign. Most of the campaign was very fun with interesting puzzles, and it looked like it was shaping up to be an epic story with epic encounters. In the end there wasn't very much story and really no encounters to speak of besides battling all the standard enemies they throw at you. Can't really say more than that without giving spoilers. Although there's really not much to spoil.

The way the story was presented was a little problematic. Everything was way too cryptic and vague to even understand what they were talking about. A couple times I had other things start happening while the characters were trying to talk and I completely missed it because I either couldn't hear or I was focused on keeping myself alive.

The vending machine idea was pretty cool. They decided to use ammunition as the currency though and the prices are pretty high. This means they had to put loads of ammunition everywhere to give players enough "money" to buy anything. This also means you'd often have thousands of rounds of ammunition on you. I think it would have been better to have a separate item as the currency so they could tweak the amount of ammo and currency individually.

The additional games modes do give you a lot more to do, however. Also the campaign does have some replay value if you're interested in gathering all the collectibles. Definitely worth it for $30.\t\t\t\t\t
