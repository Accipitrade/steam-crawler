\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks has definitely lived up to the hype that was built! I haven't even finished the campaign yet and I can say it feels really polished - graphics, mechanics, physics... nearly everything. Climbing is a bit funky but I'll just blame my inability to efficiently climb until I have played more and understand how climbing works. Melee combat feels really good (arguably better than Blade & Sorcery), especially bringing a hand axe down onto the head of a nullbody. Gunplay is, although super simplified compared to H3, is still decent.

Not to mention, I love the interactions with doors; as you can just shoulder charge them open, breach and clear with a gun, or just nudge it open with the barrel of an M4 or any object for that matter.

BUT, the game will most likely induce motion sickness or nausea. I have upwards of 120 hours in VR (mainly in Hotdogs, Horseshoes, & Hand Greades using Armswinger as my smooth locomotion option), I'm not very susceptible to motion sickness and I'm well accustomed to VR, but I still managed to get motion sick from playing Boneworks.

One flaw - not game breaking or anything - is the lack of save points or checkpoints in a scene. I know the game is meant to be played as 1 scene per play session, but the scenes can take a bit of time to get through if you haven't played them yet and don't know how to solve the puzzles, like upwards of an hour in a scene. If you quit mid scene you lose any/all progress made and you must restart from the absolute beginning of the scene.

Now I understand that, for game structure and coding reasons, that it probably isn't possible/ideal to do a 100% save of of everything upon exit of the game, but there could be a few alternatives to that. A thought I had was maybe making a few checkpoint rooms throughout the scene, where you can find a recycler for your gear, a small shop that has cheap mediocre gear, and a lever to activate the save. So, the process to save and log out would be: Throw gear in recycler (and get ammo or something for it), pull lever to save, and exit the game. The only thing being saved in this concept is player location; the entire scene would be reset to it's default state.

It's not a perfect solution, but an idea. At least you wouldn't have to redo the entire scene if you exit mid scene for any reason (including but not limited to: motion sickness and fatigue). To be perfectly honest I would be willing to give up my entire inventory (aside from ammo since it is already saved) mid scene and without compensation if it meant there would be a save location mid scene.

A secondary, flow/annoying aspect, is the sprinting on Index. The "double press in any direction to sprint" seems really sensitive. No option (that I've seen) in game to change it, or even to completely disable it. Probably some exterior setting I haven't found yet but it was worth mentioning imho..

Overall, I would absolutely recommend Boneworks (if you can stomach the smooth locomotion). It definitely lived up to the hype that was built for it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI just want to say that from what I have seen of the negative reviews, most are misleading, underplayed, or flat out wrong.

This game is everything it hyped itself up as and much, much more. Easily the best VR title to date and will remain the best until Half-Life: Alyx comes out, and even then it will probably be a close call, I imagine. The mechanics here are incredible. Everything feels great, operates well, and is fun to play around with. The levels are fun and I think the puzzles are maybe a bit basic but still lots of fun to solve. The combat sequences are great and really memorable with cool locations and fun enemies even though the AI seems pretty poor. For the most part the set pieces, locations, and gameplay really make this one of the most fun games I have ever player in or out of VR. The story is mediocre, but that's not really what anyone is here for. Oh, almost forgot, the sound design and music is really top notch.

If I had any issues the first would be a lack of saves. I wish there were at least check points scattered about instead of each stage needing to be taken all the way through to avoid losing progress; with that said the levels tend to only be about an hour long anyway. Also every now and then there is a wall or object with janky collision that stops me from shooting enemies or gets my arms all stuck in them.

Other than that stuff this is a near perfect title and really breaks some grounds for VR. I really can't recommend this enough.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEDIT Jan. 19, 2020: There is no quicksave feature. This really bugs me. There are times where I want to take a break but my two options are to quit the game and lose a ton of progress or power through the rest of the level (and you don't really know how long you have left). Really bad decision for a VR game.

While the mechanics are a bit janky sometimes and there is a bit of a learning curve, it's hard to fault this game for those things because this is an entirely new game with entirely new mechanics in an entirely new space.

The art style is greatI think if they made everything super detailed and photorealistic it would make players focus less on the mechanics or require more demanding hardwareand I really enjoy how they eased the player into the new mechanics with the opening "Museum" scene. They encourage the player to take their time and take breaks often. They know that they are introducing very new concepts for VR, and they want to make the transition from "traditional" VR mechanicsweightless objects, teleportation, floating handsas smooth as possible. They do a great job at tackling the learning curve.

The new mechanics can be a bit jarring at times, mostly due to how your body in-game is a physical object as opposed to a "floating hands"-type game where your hands can just phase through anything. For example, if you pick up a heavy object your virtual hands will move a lot slower than in real life. It can sometimes break immersion and confuse your brain, though it's pretty easy to get used to. That said, this is one of the few VR games where I've played for an extended session because I was entirely immersed in the game due to how natural the mechanics are after the tutorial.

If you decide to buy the game, I highly encourage you to buy Index controllers if you haven't already. I've tried the game with both my Index controllers and Vive controllersthe Index controllers are miles better (kilometers if you're not in the US).

There is much room for improvement. Specifically, the virtual body. It's really hard to move around and climb sometimes because my hand is somewhere else stuck on some object. It would be helpful if the hand could clip through things when they are not visible.

This should hold me over 'till Half-Life: Alyx.

good game 7/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI finished the game, took just under 9 hours. Really mixed feelings about it. I think for the price of 30$ I got a satisfying experience, but I am exhausted with the game at this point. I can see how many people would not get the same value out of it, and honestly 15$ seems like it would be more reasonable for the current content in the game.
The engine is amazing, and is definitely the golden goose here. It has so much potential and it feels like nothing else I have done in VR. I got motion sick the first two hours, but I would take breaks every half hour and drink water. The last 7 hours I didn't need to stop for motion sickness, as my body seemed to adjust. Not sure if others will share this adaption as quickly, but its worth it once it happens. You can pull of some cool stuff with the character physics, combat, and slow-mo. Assuming your stomach can handle it.
The story and overall structure of the game itself is bad. Really bad. Whoever thought putting live action shots for the cut scenes thought wrong. It broke immersion and every scene caused me to cringe. The entire atmosphere of the game is trying so hard to be what Valve has pulled off with Portal (Dark tongue and cheek), yet it both doesn't take itself serious enough or provide enough humor. It feels like a youtube channel trying to recreate what a movie company has made. Keeping the characters inside the game engine would have made it so much more interesting and immersive. The story really didn't even have a defined plot line, and the ending felt rushed. There was so much potential to increase the time by another hour or so with the use of the new gravity weapons they added, but instead its cut short and leaves you without any real conclusion.
I will say that the world itself is super compelling, but the level design felt odd. It's like they wanted to make every scene have two ways to get through it, but ultimately you'll find that you're forced to take the same single path anyways. There's some great level execution in here, especially with the interiors when you are fighting the headcrabs or at the very beginning. But a lot of the outside levels felt empty and without life. I spent so much time searching for a secret window that was open so that I could scale up to it, or a building that had a secret interior, but I can't remember a single instance of this happening. The scale of the worlds is very impressive for how well it runs, but I feel like the amount of 'wows' wasn't nearly as high as it could have been. There's definitely some really stunning moments when they happen though.
I think that the idea of having a bunch of physics puzzles to show the engine off is cool in theory, but in reality the designers of the game need to consider how it feels for the player. There's a lot of frustration that the developers should have removed. The engine's potential to create a smooth semi-linear adventure without frustrating puzzles is far more appealing to me than a game that forces you to spend half an hour trying to get through a single room. When working with VR design, you have to consider that the player is physically moving to solve the puzzles. This adds so much frustration because if you fall or mess up the puzzle and have to climb all the way back up you have to exert so much more energy than in a traditional game. I think this in itself is going to prevent a lot of people from seeing the entire game through. Puzzles are great, but only when they're developed to be clever and easy to pull off instead of being simple to solve but very labor intensive.
I really can't wait to see the stories that come from this engine, what others will do with it. Stress Level Zero have worked their butts off to make an engine that can facilitate some incredible experiences. Unfortunately the main campaign for this game isn't it yet. It's mostly bland and frustrating, with a story that I would have rather just been removed. I had fun though, and enjoyed it for what it is. I just don't think I have much more interest in the game because the mechanics are only so fun in a sandbox enviroment. A real story pushing you forward would be so immersive and fun that its frustrating to see that its not taken better advantage of.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUpdated review:
The game is fun to play through and mess around in, but it severely lacks a level of polish and focus. I genuinely don't think this game was playtested in a serious capacity before release. It succeeds as a sandbox with some fun environments, but often fails when it comes to actual game and level design. It's basically blade and sorcery with guns. If you have strong VR legs and are able to put up with its many frustrations, then there are some genuinely enjoyable moments to be had, just don't go in expecting this to be a good "game" in the normal sense.

the good:
- The level of physics and interaction is truly fantastic.
- Gunplay feels really good.
- Good replayability with arena mode
- Good music and sound design
- Good price

the expected:
- Checkpoints make the game much more enjoyable by allowing you to progress at your own pace.

the bad:
- Performance issues.
- No comfort options (not even vignetting).
- Climbing is better than before, but still pretty wobbly. Other games do this better.
- No Oculus SDK for people who bought the game at launch.
- Very little variety. You'll be fighting the same 5 enemies in a lot of samey looking environments for the length of the game.
- Blade and Sorcery as well as Saints and Sinners both have more satisfying melee.
- Something as simple as climbing on top of a box is infinitely frustrating and unintuitive.
- Some puzzles are just frustrating and not very fun. Most of the required ones to progress are fine, but getting some of the optional crates feels more like trying to wrangle a kite out of a tree rather than an actual satisfying puzzle. As a result, rather than feeling satisfaction when you finally get that crate, you're often just left feeling exhausted and motion sick. At least now it's possible to save once you get to that point...
- Ammo/collectible system is bad. Ammo is used as currency, which is no fun. If you want to be able to buy the special tools then you basically need to do a gun-free run of the game. Also, if you're missing just one ammo crate you can't just replay the level and go grab the one you're missing, you have to go back and grab EVERY ammo crate all in one go, resulting in a single, long, miserable run of the level. I also don't want to have to take up weapon slots holding item capsules for the whole level. You should just be able to smash them or something and unlock the thing.\t\t\t\t\t
