\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am using an Oculus Rift.

This is a good game, and I'm sure that you've seen many of its features which are executed very nicely, so I'll highlight some of its weak points. There are a few minor things which could be considered weak which I will detail in the following paragraph. Dedicated to their own individual paragraphs following the next will be the two main problems have plagued me throughout my play-through so far: imprecise motor functions and level length.

Firstly, I'll give a synopsis of a general review. This game is revolutionary for the medium of VR. That is its main appeal. For each thing that it does wrong, there are many more that it drastically improves upon or flat-out brings to VR for the first time. If you're looking for a story, there is little in such way here. What story is developed throughout the game is very infrequent and bland. You'll often go for quite some time, even through entire levels before being reminded by a thirty-second clip on a T.V. that there you do actually have a goal apart from "make it to the end of the level". The lack of a story, in my opinion, can be ignored because the game is fun without one to me. It is, however, still a valid criticism which has been leveled by many. The gun-play is fun, but not the best that I've seen, and the melee weapons are badass. Do you want to sneak around the levels and take people out with knives? Go for it. Do you want to bludgeon people to death with cinder blocks and bricks? Then use your environment. Do you want to enter a room with a katana and run it through multiple people's abdomens and then push their corpses off of the end of it? Sweet. The game really is the best physics sandbox available in VR right now. With that being said, as with most other physics sandbox games, it can be pretty janky at times. There will be moments that you get frustrated by the fact that your hand is stuck on the other side of a wall and you have to pretty much attempt to break the game's physics to get it fixed. Although I have been through some definite annoyances, none have softlocked my game or severely hindered it. As for a general statement, It's worth the price if you have been excited at any point in the past by what it has shown to offer or what it has promised us. It really does live up to the hype that surrounded it.


That's all for the general review. Now, I'll discuss two things which really annoyed me throughout this great game. It is not infallible.

Secondly, I'll discuss level length first to get that out of the way, since that seems to be the common complaint in the reviews. I'm meticulous in games and I like to explore as much of a level as I can before I leave it. In this game, you are encouraged to do so because of the many collectibles hidden in unexpected places. That means that I could easily spend a couple of hours in a level before leaving it. With no system in place to pause, let alone save to close the game and come back to it, that requires a dedicated time to play for multiple hours at a time. When you feel the desire to pause, you can simply take your headset off and set it down, but this feels like a solution which shouldn't even be called into question in the year 2020. When I take my headset off to "pause" the game, I'm never certain that I'll still be alive when I come back. I don't want to be forced to feel nauseous after a two-hour playtime out of fear that I'll lose progress if need to take a break. Please add an option to enter back into a level where you left off. I've seen many people attempting to refute this point with quips such as "you know what you're getting into when you start a level", or "I don't know what your problem is, it only took me twenty minutes to get through each level", but wouldn't you at least like the option to pause and return at a later point? Everybody's play style is different and many are just not well-accommodated by this lack of a feature. It seems feasible to add, and pretty much every other game has such a feature, so it seems logical. I won't dwell for any longer, it just irks me.

Thirdly, the gun-play is not as great for me as everybody seems to be depicting it. Specifically, it is hard to perform many fine-motor functions which several different guns demand because of the way the game is structured. With all of the guns, I find myself having to draw my left hand back, about a foot from where the gun is being held while chambering a round. This is strange and unnatural. I understand that chambering a round is a spring-loaded action, but it's a big disconnect from what I'm seeing in the game. For specific guns, I find that with the rifle, for instance, the stock constantly gets hung-up on my shoulder and impedes my ability to aim down the sight or even point it in front of myself reliably. Shouldn't the stock nestle into your shoulder rather than constantly get caught up on your side? I know that it would behave similarly in real life, but there is no physical sensation of a stock in VR to give you feedback. With the MAC (not sure if that is correct, I'm not a gun guy), the bolt (once again, not sure about the terminology) is positioned far too close to the grip. It can take several tries to grab the bolt and chamber a round before being successful, which can be very detrimental in a fight. The pistol is good. I find myself using it the most because it causes me the least of a headache. This game is not necessarily revolutionary in the way of VR guns to me, I prefer Pavlov purely as a shooter. The gun-play in Pavlov is completely unmatched, whereas the gun-play in this, although a blast, can be pretty finnicky and headache-inducing. Often times, instead of continuing to use the rifle in my hand during a gun fight, I would throw it on the ground and resort to my pistol because it was much more reliable.
