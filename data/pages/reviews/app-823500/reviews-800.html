\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI waited for Boneworks to come out with a huge amount of excitement. It's fairly rare that VR gets a title that is of AAA quality and full-length so I feel that the excitement was justified. So, having played the game very nearly (I think) to the end, how do I feel about the experience? Well, it was weird.

It's hard to describe exactly why Boneworks is weirder than any other VR game exactly, I mean it wasn't out and out bizarre like Organ Quarter there's just something deeply unsettling about the whole style and feel of the game.

The game takes place in a virtual world, a bit like Tron for the VR generation. The story suggests that you are, as a character, in a VR world yourself only in some full-body immersion variety. You work your way through the levels fighting your way through hostile Null-men, head-crab-like VR units seeking to trap you forever in an enforced alternate reality, and automated security turrets all while trying to solve physics-based puzzles and heading towards a goal that is never fully explained. Within this world are a wide variety of weapons ranging from melee to ranged. Bullets are the currency and the score: the more you have, the higher your score and the better weapons you can buy in the vending machines dotted sparsely around the world. The weapon of choice for the truly competent is the environment, dispatching Null-men without expending any ammo at all and gaining great deal of satisfaction in the process.

The weapons are OK but have a rubberiness about them that comes from trying to simulate weight and heft in a VR game where the controllers are light and move effortlessly. Hammers and axes catch on objects and come twanging free, long rifles get caught behind a body-part and do the same, and all feel just a little odd. Gun-handling requires magazines to be inserted, removed, and stowed and guns must be before they can be fired which is all good stuff.

Throughout the game there is a seriously off-beat sense of humour. Graffitti on the walls and objects lying around give an insight into the (strangely melon-obsessed) lives of the otherwise automaton-like Null-men and the same strange, slightly catchy, song plays on radios around the world. I don't know why this adds to the weird feeling but it does.

The physics are probably the main star of the show. Apart from the rubber weapons they work really well and, while frustrating, are impressive. There are usually multiple ways to solve a puzzle depending on your mindset, skills, and patience levels and these provide fun and expletives in equal measure.

Overall, I did enjoy this game although I got hacked-off with what I think was the final puzzle and stopped playing. Boneworks almost always left me with a feeling of disorientation and bewilderment bordering on depression at the end of each play-session and I can't say why. Despite that possible negative, as an experience, it is definitely worth the money.\t\t\t\t\t
