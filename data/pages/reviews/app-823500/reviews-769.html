\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI like this game a lot more now at 25 hours in than when I started, or even when I was 15 hours in. I've now played through most levels multiple times as well as the arena and sandbox modes.

When the game came out in December 2019, I played it on launch and made it through the Museum level. I was walking through the Streets level and becoming increasingly daunted by just how long the levels could be. And if this next level was going to be as long as the Museum was, then I wanted to take a break. I was also getting some mild motion sickness (which is long gone now).

I didn't play it again until the save update in February, a couple months later. I started it back up and kept playing, but I barely even used the save system- just knowing that it was there, and that I could bail on a level if something came up, was reassuring. Then I played the rest of the game.

My impressions after finishing the game, before coming back to it after playing Half-Life: Alyx (15h):
-Full body physics is kind of whack, and causes all kinds of problems with jumping on physics objects and climbing ladders (This was mostly addressed in a subsequent physics update).
-The story is surprisingly nonexistent.
-Some weapons that seem like they would be strong are either weak or take more (physical) effort to get the same result as other simpler weapons.
-Aiming is confusing (This was on me- I learned that I was right-handed and left-eye dominant, so I should be holding guns in my left hand instead),
-A feeling of "that's it?" after finishing it, in more ways that one.

My impressions since coming back to the came after playing Alyx:
-The insistence on fully physicalized interactions is much more impressive now that I've seen the hand-holdy guard-raily alternative in Alyx's theme park (I love Half-Life and Alyx was great, but it left me wanting more of what Boneworks offered). It's not perfect and brings with it its own set of problems, but its a lot more engaging.
-The story had mostly gone over my head the first time through, and now I can better appreciate the effect this kind of narrative had on the game. By having such a meta story, they actually manage to tie the gameplay into the world in a very immersive way, while making the admittedly kind of crummy-looking Garry's Mod-esque art look like it belongs.
-The melee weapons are still busted though. The combat knife is the most effective melee weapon by far in my experience, and maybe the most fun to use anyway. Spearing and stabbing crablets is a lot of fun. However, it makes it so that the only reason to use many of the more unwieldy weapons is to handicap yourself. It's frustrating when you can run a zombie through with a sword multiple times but that's more work for less damage than just stabbing them in the head with a much shorter blade.
-The post-game activities like collecting ammo and other items, playing the arena, and messing around in the sandbox are pretty fun (although having to finish the level to keep what you've reclaimed is pretty lame).
-After playing Half-Life: Alyx, I realized more acutely how unfair I had been to Boneworks: my expectations for it had been higher than they should have. This is a small team making something incredible, not a mammoth corporation. I'm much happier with what the game has now.

Final impressions for a long review no one will read:
As a lifelong fan of Half-Life, this game is a more true successor to Half-Life than Half-Life: Alyx. It has been incredible to see the influence the series has had on the game. It resembles and echoes Half-Life 2 for much of the game, but in terms of gameplay, it is much closer to the close-quarters corridor horror shooter of Half-Life 1. Not to mention to simpler, bare art style, at once like Half-Life 1 and Garry's Mod. Perhaps Half-Life 3 will take back the mantle of 'next-gen Half-Life game', but for now I see it firmly in Boneworks' hands, despite its many shortcomings.\t\t\t\t\t
