\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo be brief, I expected just a little bit more than this game delivered.

Maybe It's my fault, but when I see a game supports seated play I assume that means it's accessible for the disabled. This game is not. The final section requires too much dexterity and upper body strength for me to complete, so I watched a video of the ending.

The game is long enough for the price, but it's length is padded, like most physics puzzlers, by asking you to perform multi-step tasks, which are not incredibly well thought out, but require a certain amount of luck to accomplish.

The gunplay was acceptable, but physics based ik creates fundamental problems with loading and grasping weapons.

Given these shortcomings, I expected at least a serviceable story, but what I got was an overtly meta plot that was thin on the details. Even the ending does not explain anything, leaving the entirety of the game up to interpretation.

I saw a lot of reviews claiming that this game was worth the wait, but I found it to be a bit of a slog that I pushed myself through to see if the story would ever redeem it. It didn't, so I will not recommend this game to other buyers.

I think a lot of people here are trying to justify the purchase of the Valve Index with this game, but I can say, as a Samsung Odyssey+ owner, that Oculus exclusive singleplayer games have a lot more to offer than this. Don't be afraid to install ReVive.

Here's hoping Half Life: Alyx really is worth the wait.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery impressive! Some of the best VR and all around a great game.

However the physics based interactions, as interesting and fun as they are, don't really work that well in VR as you might think. The problem is that these are some very realistic physics systems controlled by very imprecise inputs. Perhaps one day when we have full body tracking and feedback this kind of realistic physics approach will be great, but when you only have the hand position to go off of, it just ends up being a bit clumsy. In reality you don't get your arms stuck in places because you can feel the full position of your arm or if it's touching anything, but in VR you just end up feeling like you're controlling a drunk marionette. You don't have the full precision and control that some of the tasks require, like moving objects to position them so you can step on them. It's doable but frustrating as you flail around trying to position things. I wish you could climb over ledges as well, but the level design seems to work against that idea. That said, for most of the game it doesn't really matter that much because the game itself is so good and it's so much fun that you can get past the quirky nature of controlling a physics body like this.

The only other problem is the lack of mid level saves, but sounds like the devs are working on adding that.

The game is 100% worth it for every VR fan (as long as you are comfortable with some intense VR). I hope this also leads to more games of this calibre and one day when the tech improves this is going to be a great stepping stone to full ultra immersion :) Fantastic work devs! Thank you for trying something new, taking risks and pushing the VR industry forward!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe greatest praise I can give Boneworks is also its strongest condemnation. I can't get enough of this game. I desperately want there to be more of it, and therein lies the problem. Boneworks feels like an amazing first half of a game with an ending that comes so abruptly it might give you whiplash. All throughout the experience, there's a gradual rise of tension and intrigue as events progress and levels continue. And just when things seem to be getting most interesting, it suddenly ends. For a game this good, the content available doesn't feel like nearly enough. There needs to be more of it, and it's a shame there's not.

It's telling that the greatest flaw of Boneworks is that there isn't enough of it; that alone should speak volumes of its quality. Simply put, there is no better VR game on the market at the moment. While most VR products these days still feel like bloated proof-of-concept tech demos, Boneworks feels like a full-fledged, fully realized game (albeit a short one). Setting aside some minor technical hiccups, I was amazed at how few glitches I encountered while playing. The level of polish is undeniable, and makes even simplistic actions all the more satisfying as a result. Pushing open doors, headbutting enemies, drawing weapons, climbing pipes, pushing buttons, and everything in between has a sense of oneness that's yet unmatched in the VR landscape. It doesn't just work; it works well.

It's not a perfect game by any stretch. Barring the all-too brief runtime, a common complaint among community members has been the lack of save functionality during levels. While I never personally had a problem with this, it's clear that a high degree of players did, and in hindsight it is a particularly strange feature to leave absent. Thankfully the developers have indicated that they will put into place a patchwork checkpoint system in the near future, so at least it's being addressed. The enemy AI can also be a little wonky on occasion. Nullbodies having full physics works against them more often than not, resulting in some laughably unstable pathing behaviors. Try to watch a Nullbody chase you up a flight of stairs, and you'll see what I mean.

There are some other, less noteworthy issues, but I assure you that absolutely none of them should prevent you from purchasing and playing Boneworks. There is no other VR game that plays this well, sounds this good, is as fun to play as this, and will keep you coming back like Boneworks. As long as you're willing to accept that you'll be left wanting more, I can recommend this game glowingly. If you own a VR headset and don't own Boneworks, you're doing yourself a disservice. It may be a bit too short, but it's oh so sweet.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a full game in VR and not a tech demo. That alone is worth 30 bucks.
What follows is a list of complaints. If I don't mention it here, assume it's fine or even great.

>Your character has virtually zero upper body strength. He can't hoist himself onto a ledge without something to drop down from, and if he's meant to be holding something two-handed, one-handing it will cause that arm to flop around like jello.
>The game is oddly paced. The best level, the sewers, is in the middle, and after the climactic encounter there's what feels like an entire act of stuff that makes little-to-no sense.
>Speaking of which, the story starts out making some sense but everything you learn after the first act just makes things more confusing. I honestly felt like I had skipped a vital plot segment at some point.
>Nullbodies, the main enemy type you'll be fighting, make no noise while walking. This means that you'll be minding your own business, then get the bejesus scared out of you when a Nullbody drunkenly staggers into your field of view.
>The final sequence in the game is just a long-ass ladder to put the one in Metal Gear Solid 3 to shame (What a thrill). And if you fall off, there's no way to get back up quickly. You have to start from the very bottom. And your reward at the end is just a thirty-second cutscene, a bunch of unanswered questions, and the credits.
>Building on my last complaint -- there's no checkpoints and there's no save system. If you die, you start the entire level over. This is mitigated slightly by a Fight-For-Your-Life-esque system where if you kill an enemy before you finish bleeding out, you stop dying, but that in turn is kind of screwed up by projectile-lobbing enemies who can damage you after they die, leaving nothing to get a second wind off of.
>Seriously, can someone tell me what's supposed to be happening in this game?\t\t\t\t\t
