\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI got Boneworks close to release and already had a good amount of time in VR. I tried playing for an hour and felt nauseous back then. I chalked it up as maybe being dehydrated or something. I got distracted and never really went back to trying it, leaving it in my backlog for a later date. I ended up waiting about 2 years before trying it again, that day was today. Today, I have over 2600 hours in VR, over 1600 hours in Pavlov VR alone. So I am very much a veteran to VR.

That all being said, I decided to try playing Boneworks again today. I got about an hour in, a bit further than where I remember stopping the first time I played it. And I felt... nauseous again? I don't really recall why I may have felt this way the last time I played it but this time it was a lot more evident.

Boneworks focuses very much on physics, probably a little too much so. I loved the idea of the physics based puzzles. Put a weighted object on this moving platform to lock it into position then climb it. There were also multiple solutions to the puzzle which is even more fun. I remember a puzzle where you had to build a bridge with long boards. If you didn't want to do it, you could wall climb along the other side of the room. Use whatever method you'd like.

The problem is since everything has weight, the game just feels really clunky. For that bridge puzzle I just mentioned, you had to bring these boards up an elevator if you failed on the first try. Since the boards are so long and have weight, your hands will constantly be moving away from where you physically have them in the real world, making it really hard to adjust and move properly. You had to move the boards into the elevator at the proper angle but that's really hard to do when your hands are constantly lagging behind because of the weight of the boards. That's just one example of many. You'll constantly be moving heavy boxes or any items to solve puzzles and your hands will slow down because of the in game weight of the box. It might sound immersive but it really just ends up being really disorienting. The whole point of VR is for your hands to be in the same spot in the game as they are in real life!

In addition to the weight of objects, your body also has weight. This is one of the few games where you can look down and see your feet. If you try it, you'll see how clunky the system is trying to keep up with them. I don't really care about the animations, but the big issue is once you stop moving, your in game body has to react to where your legs are so you'll still end up moving a bit even after you stop moving with your controllers. It's only small movements but it's enough to start making you feel nauseous.

The shooting mechanics are just OK in my opinion. Games like Half Life Alyx, Into the Radius, or Pavlov feel significantly better just to name a few. I feel like Boneworks was more of a tech demo that they turned into a game than a game itself. They just focused way too much on the physics that it significantly hampers the controls and after longer sessions can make you feel sick as well. If you do end up trying it, I'd recommend finding a time where you can play it for an hour and a half straight in one session. Get a feel for the game. If you don't feel nauseous, you may like the game. If you do, just refund it. As I've said at the start of this review, I have over 2600+ hours in VR, 1600+ in Pavlov alone. I don't think this is an "I need to get my VR legs going" argument at this point. It just doesn't feel right.\t\t\t\t\t
