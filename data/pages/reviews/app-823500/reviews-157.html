Before we start, the predecessor to Bonelabs
Boneworks - I dare say - is a better game (!) than Half Life Alyx. And the only reason why is the gameplay. Because honestly at every other level Half Life clearly is the better game. But if you want to push yourself to experience the future of gaming? The true VR taste-of-game we will expect in the future? Can you imagine young gamers 30 years from now laughing that we played games with characters that were basically floating arms and hands? How poor we had it we were still using controllers and trackers, and not just tracking our bodies, hands and fingers by similar technique we are using now. See... in order for VR to become the amazing thing it wants to be, we have to get to a point where reality and game intertwine nearly completely. Where if you weren't aware of a tight headset, you'd forget you are actually alive in reality for a couple of hours.

In order to understand VR you have to understand the limitations of our mainstream way of gaming. In contrast to relaxing on your couch/chair/bed/etc. VR is serious work - if you do it properly. Being someone like myself, slightly overweight and knowledgeable about it. Playing VR for multiple hours is a workout. Boneworks requires you to use your entire body to play it "properly". "Immersion" means that dream I once had as a little kid, can now be enjoyed at a quality that is acceptable, finally.

But here is the thing. I never truly appreciated Boneworks, without the right VR-device, or the right controllers. The - currently - best right now would be the Index controllers, even though the build quality of certain parts is VERY weak for the task it has to uphold (sticks). VR right now - August 2022 - is still VERY expensive for young gamers in regular families. And devices like the Quest 2, the Valve Index, HP Reverb 2 and similar devices... are great models if you want to buy your first device. But honestly? They aren't worth the money anymore. And choosing from what is available now? Our computers are still not up for the task to truly enjoy the potential of VR, as we will come to appreciate - say - 10 years from now. We talk so high and mighty about these high-end videocards worth thousands of Euro's, but they barely manage to run Flight Simulator 2020 with High graphical settings higher than 30fps (true story). And VR requires 90-120 fps to run smoothly.

Our technology is running behind on the possibilities of VR, I mean... the size of a high-end videocard is basically the size of an oldschool Playstation.

Which brings us to Boneworks
Boneworks is a great game which is best enjoyed with the most expensive hardware. Sadly. Boneworks, is currently the best VR game when it comes to VR potential, that is currently out on the market. Yes, it beats Half-Life Alyx when it comes to VR potential. Why do I dare say this, nearly as fact? We know what BoneLabs (2022) is going to bring to the table, the successor of Boneworks. It managed to show in a single trailer it could improve on an engine that allows for great VR potential.

And sadly... Boneworks is equally the example in which ways our current-gen VR systems... are greatly lacking. More immersion, better tracking, physical feedback.

Boneworks
Boneworks - finally - isn't a perfect game. It jests with the idea that it was a technical demo, and during development the developers decided to turn this tech-demo into a fully fledged game: Boneworks.

The tutorial is a perfect example of a 9/10-quality tutorial which teaches you mechanics in the game, and teaches you how to play the game with the hardware you have. Which is why my lengthy intro was needed. Boneworks doesn't "feel" right, without the right hardware. Fully playable and enjoyable, yes 100%. But not the way it is intended. On "cheaper" hardware, you notice Boneworks feels clunky and rough. The story is okay and it does pull you along slightly, but the gameplay has to sell it. It's puzzles you can skip by improvising and "abusing" the physics-engine (as intended), while most will play it in logical designed steps as a regular game. Both are equally possible. And there are levels within this game, you can finish in so many ways, after watching video's about it you can't feel like you are missing half the potential this game has to offer.

Because here is the thing. You cannot enjoy this game fully, if you play it "as intended'. If you follow the rules of the game (press button, find key, walk on path, you aren't playing it right. And even if that might be true, a lot of the progression-designed puzzles can be solved in at minimum 3-4 'obvious' ways. And I noticed with "cheaper" hardware I couldn't fully immerse myself because of the limitations of my controllers and corresponding tracking issues. Which demotivated me to do those crazy jumps, or push my control within the world to it's limits. And when you feel "physically"-limited within Boneworks, this game isn't that much fun pushing through to the end.

The Index Controllers register your fingers and have enough buttons to support all actions on both controllers. Everything is touch sensitive, even the triggers, the handstraps allow you to open up your hands entirely which translates to opening your hands in real life as well. Gripping the controller nearly grips your firsts, and so it equally translates ingame. A stupid little "trick" that makes all the difference playing Boneworks in VR. The second your hardware allows you to make some weird moves with speed, controlled? You feel safe.

Boneworks has a story, which unravels into a blend of real life and "being stuck in a VR-title" category storyline, which pushes you - the player - to fully explore the possibilities of being present in a game that responds to your presence and actions. If you push yourself to feel a little child-like crazy and completely immerserse yourself physically in this game, you will have the most fun. Finding a plush toy trying to bring it with you as far as you can. Trying to humanely kill aggressive robot-creatures, and at some point giving up and choose to brutally kill them in the most creative ways (Westworld much?).

Wearing a black hat in this game and turning this game into a 10-20 hour long tutorial into the future of VR?

Conclusion
If I had to make an assumption based on my experience with two decades of seriously playing games: Games like Boneworks, Blade and Sorcery, (and heavily modded variations of Skyrim VR: NOT vanilla), are the groundworks for the future of VR. Half Life Alyx will have set a tone of quality for it's time, but it will feel very cheap compared to what is to come. What Boneworks lacks in story and graphics, it absolutely makes up with gameplay and current VR possibilities. Equally, all of these examples are equally part of the foundation of quality we will expect 10 years from now. As a comparison: I absolutely adore a game like 'Into The Radius', but that game is a really rough f you'd compare it to the quality of modern games. There simply isn't anything better within the same genre. There isn't a market for it yet.

Boneworks sets itself aside; If you want an amazing game if you've invested in the high-end hardware, this is one of the first games to get, if you can stand the motion sickness likely included. The story pulls you along, but is absolutely secondary to what sells this game: the gameplay. Expect jumping, climbing, fighting, shooting, bullet-time, puzzels you can cheat by being smart about it. There rarely is a single sollution to a problem, and the most creative ones feel the best. Which allows for TONS of replay-ability.

Which is why I'm looking forward to BoneLabs so much: because it most likely has a MUCH better story, ánd better gameplay.

9/10 if you own high-end hardware.
7/10 if you own cheaper than a 1000 euro hardware package.

*and a 6/10 ten years from now based on my old journalistic gaming instinct (yes, an assumption).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI got this game because of the funny YouTube videos where people do trick shots with guns, but I was extremely impressed with how much more this game has to offer. The story is like no other, and for what started as a physics demo, it is a truly impressive plot that fully utilizes all of the great physics aspects of Boneworks. The NPCs are not difficult to fight, but incredibly enjoyable to fight, and have a lot of variety. I would say that gun combat is a 10/10, with incredible VFX, sounds, and feel. Blades are good, but could use a little work to be perfect. The graphics are incredible, and for a game made with Unity, the visuals are Unreal. (See what I did there) Music is top notch, and some of the best video game music I have ever heard.

In the end, there are two things that make this game so legendary. First, you play as Ford, but you are actually playing as yourself. You can play like a gun loving, kick-butt Ford, or a fearful and careful Ford, or even a wrench wielding psychopath, who deeply enjoys every kill. Everyone plays Ford how they would imagine him, often times how they themselves would act in his situation. Secondly, while the game is linear, you have complete freedom to do whatever you want in the levels. There is one level, where you have to loop around a warehouse and complete different puzzles, to eventually get inside it. But you can skip all of that, and just smash the window and get right in at the start. You can even climb onto of the warehouse, then jump over the fence to the other side.

Boneworks is an easy 10/10, and I can't recommend it enough. I am worried that the upcoming sequel, Bonelab, is going to cater to the physics trick shot crowd, and may even abandon a story altogether.\t\t\t\t\t
