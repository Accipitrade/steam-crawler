\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed on an Oculus Rift CV1 w/ GTX980 and Ryzen 5 3600X.

When I first started Boneworks, I got quite confused by the very long introductory tutorial section which takes place inside a virtual museum. I actually found myself getting a bit nauseous, then the game crashed before I had seen any combat or substantial puzzles, so.. I put it away for a month or two.

After a few updates, and some more time behind in my HMD, Boneworks didn't make me nauseous at all once I picked it back up! I got through the tutorial and once I got into the actual game, it was paced quite nicely. I got stuck once, pretty early on (where?: on the streets at the pair of garage doors that you have to go through the cafeteria to access, almost accessible from the garbage chute where the museum spits you out if it weren't for a barricaded chain-link fence.) but it turned out I had accidentally walked past a button. This taught me that the game wasn't afraid to let me miss some things and force me to backtrack, so I had to stay on my toes.

The firearm handling in Boneworks is quite good. I had consistent issues with holstering weapons in my pistol holster, and couldn't reload when I was crouched down (my body was in the way of accessing the mag pouch) but once I learned to avoid certain movements, things went quite smoothly.

Reloading was confusing to me, as I try to maintain good defensive shooting habits in VR. There is no dedicated magazine release button for the Oculus Touch controllers - I had to hold a button and point at a menu item to eject a magazine, then insert a fresh one. To boot, the 'force grab' manoeuvre would often get interrupted by accidentally grabbing a fresh mag from the pouch at my hip when trying to grab magazines I dropped at my feet. Clunky.

Melee (any form of trying to damage things with impact, really) was very difficult for me. I found that using good sledgehammer form (having off-hand at the base of the shaft, and a loose primary hand at the head, sliding it down as I swung) was very effective sometimes, but often my tool or weapon would weakly slap into the box or baddie I was trying to damage, so I relied on firearms whenever I could.

The game is very fun, and its forward reach into the future of VR gaming forgives its flaws, in my eyes. Get some VR experience before playing, and expect a level of polish appropriate for the indie studio imagination that drives the game's design.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t(Played using a vive setup) TL;DR at bottom summing all points.

Tried the game a while ago cause of the massive hype behind it. Everyone saying it would be some kind of revolutionary game and changing vr forever. When I did try it the first time, I just felt bored almost all the time and didn't find anything to really enjoy.
After playing Half-Life: Alyx recently and enjoying every second of it, I decided to read through some reviews for it. It seemed that all the negative reviews would keep bringing up the amazing "boneworks" because it would do everything that should have been in Alyx, so I decided to give this game another go.
Did my opinion on it change at all even after the updates until now? Not at all.

-Fighting-

Pretty darn disappointed with this aspect, since so many people spoke highly of it.
The gunplay was janky compared to almsot all other VR shooters I've tried. When it came to rifles, my hands didn't feel like they lined up comfortably with the foregrip, which meant they were a bigger pain to fire without slowly lining up the sights.

The melee side of it was even more jank and just didn't feel satisfying to use half the time. I like the fact that you're able to grab anything and use it as a weapon, or fight things off with your hands and push and pull them around quite naturally, but so much of those objects, even things that are specifically designed as melee weapons, all kinda feel like you're just trying to abuse a physics system to kill the ai or something. Maybe it was just the way I had to uncomfortably hold stuff with the vive wands, or just the way I did it, but it just didn't ever feel as natural or fluid as everyone would say.

-Enemies-

The enemies themselves are slow and boring both visually, and in how they fight. I still haven't played through all the game, probably wont, but I hope I haven't experienced every enemy in the game at least. So far, and by golly does it feel like a long far, it's just been wireframe men, hologram shooty bois, upgraded wireframe men, and occasional robot headcrabs.
So kind of a nitpicky thing, but how I feel the enemies could mean more, is that the enemies aren't ever really introduced in an interesting way. Besides the hologram guys there's very weak anticipation in revealing the enemies, and you become used to them being pretty pathetic too quickly. That, or new enemies just start kinda showing up and you just fight them.

-Puzzles-

When it comes to the puzzles of this game, at first I thought it would be fun, and a great idea to have physics puzzles in the vr environment. But they're such a chore. They are painful. I think the main problem is that they're mostly instantly solvable but just take forever to actually do them due to having to move stuff around.
Some of them are platformers too, and if you fall, they make you go a painfully long way just to restart it or try again.

-Story, Environment and level design-

The story is either not really there, or too boring for me to pay attention to. But it's thrown in as a video through monitors a few times throughout playing. They could be there, or they couldn't and the game wouldn't change one bit.

The game environment is one of my least favourite parts. The design of the world, though seemingly intentional, is very bare. There's nothing interesting to look at, no real reward for exploring everything around you and they continuously make you get lost, or give you multiple directions to go. All you really get rewarded with is idk, ammo, or little props for the sandbox mode that you have to carry with you in a capsule to the pools at the end of the levels.

The level design is confusing in terms of direction, which is extra painful due to it being bland. I hate comparing other games, but in this aspect, Alyx could create extra tunnels or rooms and I would still feel like it was worth every step exploring them all. They'd reward you with valuable items that you need, if you take the time to explore. Boneworks, however, is one of the games where I go into a new room and see this big puzzle yet again and just go "ugh, not again", then when I start walking in to figure it out, there's about 3 other doors leading out of that room which I have no idea if they're related to the puzzle, or something that wont even feel worth going into in the first place.

My overall experience both times was just pure pain. Looking for anything to say "hey, I'm enjoying my time here" but it just didn't seem to happen, and wherever it did, it would be ruined straight after by something else, or that good thing just wouldnt be on long enough.

--TL;DR--

Fighting aint fun, melee or gunplay, better in other games.
Enemies are boring and appear too frequently to feel special.
Puzzles feel like slave labor.
Story could just not be there due to it's lack of importance to what's going on, but it is.
Enviroment is boring but has heaps to explore, but not fun to explore.
Game is pain.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBought this at launch and waited for some updates before I left a review.
Some updates later the game has technically improved in some aspects. Hands are more stable now. But the entire rest of the game is a janky mess. Some jank is intended and somewhat acceptable, but not in this dimension.

The game performance is atrocious even with the lowest settings and undersampled resolution. The game pretends to run with 90fps and good frame timings, but the judder is unbearable. It feels like the game is internally running at 30fps and not 90.
More than three Zombish visible? Performance is tanking like crazy and dynamic resolution is very agressively making the game look worse than DOOM 1. Even the new super empty Sandbox only takes one or two enemies to tank performance.

Pretty ridiculous considering my hardware: GTX1080, i7 6700K, 16GB RAM.
If I wasn't a VR veteran with an iron stomach, I'd have puked more than ten times before even finishing the museum.

If there was native Oculus SDK integration in the Steam version it would probably run a little better. OpenComposite brings massive perfomance improvements (but offset hands), so the subpar StreamVR integration is partly to blame (again).
I regret not waiting for the version on Oculus Home because this right here is not an experience I would recommend to anyone using a non-SteamVR native HMD. And I'm not gonna spend 30 again just to get native Oculus integration.

Once the terrible perfomance and permanent judder gets fixed, I'll change my review. But I honestly doubt it's going to happen.\t\t\t\t\t
