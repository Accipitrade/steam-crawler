\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm 2 levels and about 3 hours in and this definitely delivers the sort of Half-Life-esque interactive world it promised, though as with any game that tries something for the first time, there are some rough edges and cracks.

To start with the good, this really is a no-compromises game when it comes to physics and interactivity. The levels are huge, guns feel good, and combat is interesting. Arms are an interesting addition that have good points and bad points. I do think the arms actually track pretty well, especially when you're holding a one-handed weapon at arm's length and it just matches up perfectly. Climbing on monkey bars feels good and even some of the wall scaling is fun, but the game frustratingly doesn't seem to let you pull yourself up over a ledge (though I'm told you can if you crouch, which I'll have to try), and there are several areas that feel like you should logically be able to do that only to find there's some other obscure way around.

That said, the physical arms can also be a bit detrimental, while using two-handed guns operates perfectly fine from a gameplay standpoint, they look really spazzy and trying to pull the lever on the guns, just looks hilarious. Overall I think Pavlov's gunplay just looks better (though plays about the same). Combat in general is just so much fun and there are a lot of things to be discovered with how things work. Early in the streets level I picked up a trash can lid and used it like a Captain America Shield while using my other hand to carry a hammer and hit the enemy that was coming after me. Any time the game is throwing enemies at you it's just spectacular, and definitely sets the standard for how you should be able to interact with enemies in VR.

In terms of non-combat gameplay, the game is a bit of a mixed bag. There is a huge emphasis on Valve type puzzles, and some of them are pretty good, but others are very finicky or put too much emphasis on platforming. The level I played through, The Streets felt more like exploration, puzzles, and platforming than actual combat, and while I love the exploratory downtime you get in the actual Half-Life games, Boneworks world just feels like kind of a depressing, empty void and isn't that interesting to explore (or at least not yet). There aren't really any characters and not a whole lot of life going on, which is probably a function of the game's budget and wanting to keep the focus on the game's mechanics, but it does lack a certain personality that I'd hoped for from the previews. While the puzzles are certainly clever there are times where it was like "Yes, I get it" but because it was so fiddly I had to keep retrying even though I'd figured out the solution.

My final complaint is that it simply isn't very lefty friendly. Your ammo belt is on the left side of your body, and there's no way to change that. I like being able to pull out a pistol with my left hand, yet to get ammo for it I either have to reach over to my left side with my right hand, which is awkward and only works half the time, or open the inventory menu, which works but isn't exactly seamless.

Overall, Boneworks definitely bites off a lot and mostly succeeds at it. It's weird to say that such a boundary-pushing, innovative game is just "pretty good" but there are a lot of rough edges that you have to be willing to be look past to get to the really sublime stuff. I probably sound like I disliked it more than I do since I've mostly complained about stuff, but the stuff that does work works really well. I think the game's main flaws stem more from not having much budget and also not designing the best around the systems they've created rather than any issues with the concepts here. That said, the game repeatedly, explicitly tell you "this is experimental" and I think it knows that not everything it throws at the wall will stick. If anything I think it was designed to have a big, neon sign on it that says "PLAGIARIZE ME!" because it really does want to show you the absolute extreme you can take VR concepts to.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlaying this with the Index headset/controllers.

Controls are janky and the entire time I was playing it felt like the main obstacle was the controls. Reaching for weapons directly is incredibly unreliable, and the use of the inventory interface is clunky. Specifically, to open the inventory interface, you need to take your thumbs off one of the movement control joysticks to push the menu button, and then select inventory. Doing this while something suddenly jumps at you is... not ideal. I think that if the grabbing controls didn't require the trigger to be pulled, it would vastly improve the ability to easily grab weapons. I just couldn't train myself to grab a gun from a holster by pulling its trigger.

Control mapping is awkward with very few configurable options. If you opt not to use the right thumbstick up/down to slide your body vertically from standing to crouching (ie, if you have working legs) you cannot remap the right thumbstick up/down for jumping purposes.

An hour in, I suddenly couldn't get the menu interface to appear at all, and during a panicked button pushing frenzy in combat with no weapons I somehow managed to reset my progress to the very beginning. The thought of replaying the tutorial stage was a no go.

Just overall disappointing. Hopefully this sees some patches soon to correct these issues.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlaying on Oculus Rift DK1.

pros - engaging puzzles and balanced physics and gameplay.
each gun is interesting and fun to use.
I was engaged in the overarching storyline the entire time, and never felt like exploring wasn't worth it.
Each level is long and fleshed out with many different options for you to explore. They really leave up each solution to your imagination. I never felt forced to do any specific solution.


cons- wobbly physics. The weight simulation just feels... off. Not any better or any worse than any other game that does it.
no sliding grip a la blade and sorcery - I really think that was a superior system compared to BONEWORKS 1-2 grip points on each weapon.
- gripping loose objects can be a pain in the ass, especially larger things. Some of which are essential to solving the puzzle. Large pieces will eat your hands and get your arms stuck half way through.
- Several crashes (It's launch day, let's be reasonable.)
- NO AUTOSAVE/SAVE AND QUIT. This is by far my biggest complaint about BONEWORKS. If your game crashes at the very end of an hour and a half long level, you will restart that level and do it all over again.
- Not even checkpoints. Death will restart you at your spawn location, although you will retain any weapons and ammo you've picked up. No enemies respawn on death either, so you will have to run through and empty map, and completed puzzles to get back to where you were. Some of these levels are massive.


I know I have more cons than pros, but don't misunderstand. This game will keep you in your headset until your feet and knees are on fire. They did an amazing job with the combat. I also feel like the difficulty wasn't as high as I was expecting, although each level took me 45min-1.5hours.

this is a must buy for VR enthusiasts. I would say Stress Level Zero has made the best combination of engaging combat, and unique narrative available for VR.

give me a save/quit/autosave feature, checkpoints, and sliding grip on melee/2h weapons, and you've got yourself a perfect game, SLZ.
