\r\n\t\t\t\t\tQWOP VR.

I'm going to be comparing this to Blade & Sorcery a lot, since they actually did the "physical interaction" thing well.
Melee weapons feel great - two-handing actually feels fantastic, unlike Blade & Sorcery, and throwing feels better than in any other VR game I tried.
Guns feel like an afterthough; even stuff like Space Pirate Trainer had better feeling guns, IMO. These are quite possibly some of the worst VR guns I've used so far.

Graphics are okay - the "every asset looks like a prototype" aesthetic doesn't particularly sit with me, but it's fine.
Music is cool.
Actually trying to be a functioning human is not.

The whole idea behind the game, to make a VR game where the player avatar actually physically interacts with the environment, so you can climb things and block attacks and all that good stuff, is pretty solid. Blade & Sorcery proved that it works well, but also made its shortcomings pretty obvious. Climbing in Blade & Sorcery, unlike here, feels fantastic; but two-handed weapons feel janky there, and absolutely perfect here. Melee combat also, surprisingly, works great in Boneworks - possibly even better than in B&S, maybe because the enemies there just use mostly predefined attack animations.

However, if you played Blade & Sorcery, you'll know that a big issue with this idea is that while your avatar interacts with the environment and physics objects, your arms do not, so you'll often find yourself swinging a sword IRL while it's caught on something in-game. This problem gets even worse when you try to make a game based around that interaction.

In practice, this means that you try to open a door but end up smacking yourself in the head with a desk because your hand got stuck in the drawer and you grabbed the entire desk when you went for the door handle. From there, it's a dice roll about whether you'll lift yourself up because the game thinks you're trying to climb the desk (without using your fingers; Blade & Sorcery climbing was incredible because you could hold onto any edge with your fingers, here it's just awkwardly mashing your elbows on flat surfaces), or if you'll yeet the entire desk because you grabbed onto it and swung your arm.

I'm pretty good with VR motion sickness - I never got motion sick in any games, other than making particularly sickness-inducing manoeuvres in Ultrawings - but this game has an even worse side effect; after taking the headset off, my real arms don't feel like they're mine. I don't like this. Please don't do arms in VR.

Noticeably lacks polish, too - there are A LOT of typos in the in-game text, and it's clear that nobody proofread it before release. The environments, which you already know, look like placeholder assets, even if that's the aesthetic Stress Level Zero was going for intentionally. Hell, the game starts by showing you... a flat-screen cinematic on a virtual TV screen in a cubical room. Feels a lot like all the bad Half-Life 2 custom levels every level designer made in Hammer when they were starting out.

Final opinion: very much depends. Stop bandwagoning, buy it, try it, refund if you don't like it; I didn't, maybe you will.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMy setup: Vive pro with vive wands, 1080ti, i9 9900k 16gb ram, SSD

Great game so far! It performs really smooth on my system with default settings clocking in at 0.3% reprojection! The physics makes an interesting world, there's tons of puzzles, graphics are great, there's slow motion and there's lots of enemies for you to shoot at. The biggest problem with this game is that it doesnt support workshop and mods.

Now, to understand how real the physics are, imagine holding a gun. Now, in most games you can hold it and aim it however you want, but here the gun crashes into your body, so you have to consider how you're holding it and how you are moving it. Doing like real life, pressing it towards your shoulder and moving your body to aim is a good option, but it does take time to get use to the world and physics, because your body, legs and hands all interact with the world. And that can mean knocking things over accidentally, body being in the way when you open the door, your legs hitting the edge of the table you're trying to jump up on making the table move etc.

The game is really worth trying for experienced VR players who are tired of all those demos and half finished games. Totally recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving completed it I can say it is a really good time and definetly worth your money but it is not a good game.

It's lacking enemies, there's only 3 + reskins. All enemies are very easy to defeat and pose no challenge and are more like test dummies for you. There's some jank with IK rig like two handing firearms your front hand is always under stress and you can't let go of the gun without it springing away, your virtual stock is broken meaning you can't aim well with two handed weapons. The levels start out well but what you think are the shorter, easier beginner levels at start end up being the longer and more interesting levels and it's downhill from there. There's lack of weapon variety for firearms with no shotguns, no bolt action rifles and other such weapons that would've been interesting and puzzles peak in tutorial.
They had bunch of great pieces like gravity guns and baloon guns but chose to not use them for the puzzles. It's like the game is missing half it's levels.

It's still a great sorta guided sandbox and it's saved because it's in VR, very fun and truly does push the medium forward but as a game... nah.\t\t\t\t\t
