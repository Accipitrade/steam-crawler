\r\n\t\t\t\t\tATTENTION: This game is NOT for VR newbies! Get your VR legs first before playing this title!

tl;dr, It's ruined all other non physics-based VR games for me. It's a game ahead of it's time. It would be nice to have mid-level saves though.

Boneworks is a milestone in VR game development. The physics system works well enough to be very enjoyable despite it's slight clumsiness, however the value that it brings to VR interaction is immeasurable. After going back into games like Pavlov or Onward, I find myself frustrated that I can't knock a mag out of my AK using another mag in my hand to do so, or being disappointed in Skyrim when I remember that I can't just climb a building using my body or move crates by grabbing them.

Boneworks enables you to interact with the world around in a natural way that enables the player to play the game how they want rather than learning how the game wants the player to play. In the same way Breath of the Wild opened up an "open world" to feel truly open and explorable, Boneworks allows interaction that takes VR one big step closer to that dream and expectation of what we all imagined VR would be before we tried it for the first time, what we've been told it is in movies and literature, and what it can be in the future.

Boneworks is not without its faults, however. The game feels clunky more often than one would like. This in large part due to the state of VR tech and not Boneworks however. Relying on IK rigs to predict where your body should be is often not accurate enough to make the detailed physical simulations feel as natural as they should. Full body tracking would solve this, but at this point that's both expensive and cumbersome for all but the most hardcore VR gamers. Better and cheaper FBT solutions are much needed.

The other technical issue is the lack of force-feedback and haptics. When simulating physics in such a manner, you quickly realize just how much our sense of touch and temperature dictates how we move our body. When I'm trying to climb up to the top of some rafters, I need to know where my knees are so I can prop them up while I hoist my body up. Not being able to feel the pressure on my knees means I need visual line of sight to see what my lower half is up to. Not being able to feel the shearing forces on the palms of my hand means I need to visually adjust the grip on my crowbar, rather than letting it slide freely while I focus on an enemy.

All in all Boneworks does a stellar job at ironing out most of these kinks considering the very hard problems full physics simulation in VR still faces. It's broken and disproved so many assumed VR game development rules and best-practices that it feels like everything we've learned the last 4 years is wrong and we need to start from scratch.

The only other issues I've really found with Boneworks is the annoying way of saving, which only saves the start of each level meaning if you quit half-way, you have to start a level from the beginning. Also the inventory system takes some getting used to. Fixing those two issues leaves Boneworks with issues that almost all revolve around the young state of VR technology as a whole. Boneworks is also probably the first game I've ever encountered where I'm CPU limited. The CPU demand for the physics in this game is high.

All in all this is VR history in the making. This game is going to influence many major titles in the near future, just as it already has influenced Half-Life Alyx. 20 years from now we'll be telling our kids how important this game was to the development of VR as they collectively groan and plug back into their ocular implants and nervous system manipulators.\t\t\t\t\t
