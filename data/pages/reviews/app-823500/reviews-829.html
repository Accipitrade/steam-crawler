\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFrom what I've seen in reviews for this game, there seem to be two types of players.

1 - "This game is too janky, provides not enough direction, and overall isn't enough of a 'game' to enjoy, especially when playing it requires a dedicated space, physical exertion, etc."

2 - "No VR game offers the level of freedom that BONEWORKS does, and for that reason alone I love it and can look past all it's issues."

Both views, in my opinion, are totally 100% valid. Type 1 players would probably much prefer a VR game like Half-Life: Alyx. It's more structured, has a compelling and hand-crafted story, has simpler mechanics and therefor less chance of totally janking out.

Personally, I'm Type 2. I've played a slew of other VR games, including Half-Life: Alyx, and none compare in terms of gameplay options to BONEWORKS. I'm also the type of player who loves games like ArmA, because they let me spend hours toying around while accomplishing nothing in particular, and BONEWORKS hits that mark perfectly, while also fully realising the VR medium in a way no other game has before or since (in my opinion).

What do I mean by "fully realising the medium"? Basically, most other VR games I've played have felt in some aspect like a tech demo. They may have had cool concepts and mechanics, but they felt like they were made to test those out and let you experience them for a few hours, but there isn't a long term experience to be had. The way BONEWORKS differs is that it's clear the developers had a very specific vision, and stuck to that vision from start to finish. That vision, is to give you massive levels, a detailed physics sandbox, realistic weapon mechanics, and little to no restraints on how you go from point A to B.

I've played through this game 3 times, currently on my 4th playthrough, and each time I go through a level again, I find more secrets, or figure out another way to skip certain segments. It never feels like I'm breaking the game, more like I'm discovering elements that the developers hid to allow even more variety and freedom.

I also want to touch on the soundtrack, which is easily one of my favourites of any game I've ever played. Just go look up the soundtrack, BONETONES, on your music platform of choice, you won't regret it.

Also something to touch on, the MODS. Oh my JEBUS the mods. I recommend holding off on modding too much until you've beat the game once, then going ham-egg-and-cheese for your next playthroughs (two mods that benefit from the start are the Mag Eject Button mod, and the Better Dual Wielding mod. Get them on bonetome.com ). Add new weapons, mechanics, character models, enemies, custom campaign levels, it's incredible how much the community has added to this game

Long story slightly less long, if you want a VR game that feels like it's own thing, with detailed mechanics and unrivalled player freedom, get this game. If you want a highly detailed experience that's focused on immersion vs freedom, this might not be for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is easily my favorite VR game of all time mainly due to how creative you can be with combat, puzzles, etc. The fun with this games combat doesn't come from elaborate strategy like in Half Life but instead relies more on execution to deliver it's "fun". You can go in just guns blazing and it works just as well as going around smacking people with a trash can. Combat to me feels like you have so many options and you just get to choose what seems the most fun to you in the moment. Couple that with the amazing atmosphere of MythOS' various environments and you have yourself a winner. Each level feels unique, are all paced pretty well, and the music sets the mood nicely. When "Myth OS" plays and you're hiding behind a wall with enemies just around the corner, it just tells you "fight your way through" without needing any text or voice to tell you that's how to clear the level. Another thing I loved about the main campaign was the physics puzzles. Most of the physics puzzles have you manipulating things much larger than you and looking at giant machines moving based on your actions is extremely satisfying. Once again, the soundtrack kicks in at some of these puzzle moments and gets you thinking with a slower paced track. My favorite part of all about the puzzles is that you can just "skip" them if you're skilled enough. If you find a way around a puzzle, you can take it. There's never any invisible wall or kill zone to stop you. If any of these features sounds at all interesting to you I HIGHLY recommend you buy this game, there's so much more content I could write about but that spoils even more than I already have.\t\t\t\t\t
