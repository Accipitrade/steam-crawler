\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA poorly designed game that does not respect the limits of VR. Barfworks is a more apt title.

Boneworks is a game that completely ignores the current limitations of VR hardware and instead tries to create an experience that is the "vision" of where VR can go. In doing so, it ignores all guidance around VR comfort, for "ultimate immersion". What it turned into though is a unnatural feeling physics sim, with bad encounter design, non challenging level design, and often confusing controls. The redeeming quality of the game is mainly it's interesting environments. Lack of design foresight can be seen through their overly long tutorial level, the inability to save mid-level despite being a known uncomfortable game to play, and the inability to play with the sandbox at the beginning.

They try to mask their complete aversion to established VR norms behind a "hardcore players only" tag. Comfort is not a only an issue casual players deal with. I do not recommend this game to anyone who wants to feel a sense of presence in VR, and wants to take the headset off due to feeling ill.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust finished the story last night. Overall a very good feel and an easy game to replay. There are a few things I would recommend that would make this game a lot more pleasant. First, having a "Save and Quit" option would be great. There are times where I need to exit VR and cannot just leave the game running, and having to restart the missions I had progress on can seem very tedious. The second thing is having the option to "toggle" instead of hold grip buttons and triggers to hold objects (I'm going to add the slow-motion button to this as well). I play on the HTC Vive, and it gets very tiring and wearing on my controllers to be holding the grip button and the triggers for almost everything. It makes sense for objects on the map and for climbing, but for weapons it gets to be pretty annoying. Firearms already do this, and I would presume it wouldn't be much more difficult adding this feature for melee weapons and gadgets. Lastly (I'm not entirely sure if this is possible), loading screens seem to take a lot longer than what I would think most of us are used to. If there was a way for it to speed up, that would be appreciated. Even with these gripes, this game is easily one of the best VR games I've ever played, and I will absolutely continue to play this game, even if the above is never to be addressed. I very highly recommend this game to anyone and everyone with VR wanting an experience from multiple games piled into one well crafted game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVR games tend to fall into two different categories: It's either a game, or an experience. Beat Saber is definitely a game. Skyrim is definitely a game. What I would describe as an experience would be a game that is very short, very tech-demo-esque, very "Hey we did this cool thing in VR, now let's build a game that people will play once and get bored." An experience could also be a walking simulator, such as Dear Esther. Very beautiful, might have a nice story, but you're only going to play it once and show it off to a friend maybe. Boneworks, falls under both of these categories. It's very pleasing to the eyes, it is extremely immersive, and the physics are top-notch. For the first hour of the game, you are walking through a museum that features many different neat ideas or props or weapons, that's very much like the first few test chambers in Portal. The game shows you all sorts of ways you can interact with your environment, picking up and moving objects around, platforming, a target range with a few different guns you can try, and a plethora of melee weapons you can use to smack and stab the dummies with. This is very much a game similar to Portal, mixed in with some melee brawling and guns and enemies that you can brawl or shoot said guns at, while also having elements of these short games we call experiences like Job Simulator. I don't mean to compare it to Job Simulator like it's a bad thing, I mean it's absolutely filled with so many interactive pieces and ideas. This isn't an experience that you'll drop after a single sitting or leave collecting dust in your library until you have company over. This is a game that tests new ways to play and interact with the world that hasn't been done to this degree yet. I don't think we'll see a game that outdoes this until the upcoming Half-Life VR game, and even then there will be concepts and ideas in this game that I don't think Valve will have thought of. You can grab onto pipes and climb around, you can scale certain walls. This is a game that features incredible experiences, and I think everyone who owns a VR headset should try it. It's breathtaking.

I played it with Vive wands. I don't own an Index yet, but I might at least buy the Index controllers now that I've played this. An analog stick is optional, but I would say it's preferable. 10/10: Would punch my walls by accident again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really like the physics systems, and the action-adventure half-life gameplay is what I want from a vr game.

Lots of physical doors, buttons, levers. Vehicles and machines. Everything in the environment that looks moveable is. The physical body lets you do things like blindly rummage through drawers, elbow a door open, and push objects over and out of your way. It's not perfect but it's good enough, and needed for good vr games where mechanics arise naturally and creativity is an option.

Worth it for the first half of the game, but the quality of the levels drops off continuesly after that, and the story is somewhere between not existing, and a joke.
