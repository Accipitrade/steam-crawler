Awkward Jack of All Trades

I've heard good things about Boneworks for a while but put off trying it because none of the footage I saw ever really hooked me. Despite my reluctance I finally decided to take the plunge anyway, only to be pretty disappointed with the end result.

I'll give the game credit for being ambitious. It's a VR game where you can run, jump, shoot, climb, have a full body with collision physics that interacts with the environment, a lot of which you can grab, move, and throw and some of which is destructible. Something as simple as sprinting towards a pair of double doors and throwing out your arms to fling them open dramatically is surprisingly fun, probably because it's not something I can do in other VR games that don't have this many systems in place at the same time.

But the complexity is also the source of the Bonework's biggest problem; it doesn't do any of these things very well.

Just in the starting room that's effectively a menu for choosing what mode you want, I tried grabbing some items off a shelf only for my character to REPEATEDLY grab the shelf itself. I'd get in closer to the object I wanted only for their hand to grab the SHELF again, which I then pulled over because the weight of many objects feels off, with many large objects being surprisingly light. The game does feature the traditional VR force grab of looking at something and grasping to pull it into your hand, but it feels finicky and unresponsive compared to a lot of other VR games that use that feature.

Things only got worse when I tried the story mode, which effectively starts with a prolonged tutorial through a "museum" simply showing off the game's features. Trying to break a pane of glass marked as "destructible" resulted in the entire piece of glass flopping onto the floor where it remained unbroken. Most objects aren't destructible and the majority of the ones that are fall into bog-standard gaming cliches, primarily cardboard boxes, wooden crates and the occasional board blocking a door. Later, using a gun that attaches balloons to objects to raise them I discovered no amount of balloons would raise the platform I was on until I JUMPED, which seemed to remind the platform to respond to the balloons pulling it up.

Most melee weapons feel awkward, with the game seemingly only have one set amount of force for a hit no matter how far or hard you swing. Repeatedly banging on something with a hammer over and over again made it clear why most games code their containers to be breakable in one hit. It's not like these objects dent or fragment based on how you hit them, they just poof once their HP hits 0, so why make them so resilient? And forget about using the bigger, two-handed weapons. The game slows your movement so much to simulate their weight as to render them useless.

Physics in this game feel wonky at best and having your character be affected by them just complicates things in a frustrating way. Messing around in a store room I effectively trapped myself for a while by knocking over two large shelves. In real life I could have stepped over them since they weren't that tall but not-so much in this game, where I spent a good 5 mins awkwardly dragging them into each other hoping to knock them into a position that let's me leave only for one to finally break because apparently it was destructible.

The gunplay is decently fun but really not any better then most VR games with guns I've tried. It's also hindered by the inventory system. You can store and retrieve weapons and items by moving your hands behind your back… in theory. In practice, trying to holster a pistol has a 50% of it falling onto the floor. Trying to pull a pistol is more reliable, except to do so requires you hit both the grab AND trigger buttons at the same time. It's not hard to do, but typically my mind is wired to not hold the TRIGGER when pulling a gun, and when I did do it I'd have to then release the trigger so I could actually fire it afterwards.

There is also a manual inventory menu that's more reliable but it slows down the flow of things and is annoying to use when the holster mechanic just doesn't want to cooperate half the time. I'd actually just keep my weapons out as much as possible to avoid using either, but the abundance of things you need to move or climb means eventually you have to let the go.

Climbing comes up a fair bit, but the game is weirdly inconsistent about what surfaces and ledges your character can grasp and getting confused about your character’s body blocking your movement. You could argue this is realistic, but in real life I’ll FEEL when I’m bumping into something, not so much in this game. Trying to get into a vent you MUST enter to progress during the story mode had the game constantly pushing me out while I held onto the rails inside. I also had to push a box up to the vent and jump from it to grab at the weird rope hanging from the vent cover because just grabbing the flat ledge and pulling myself up wasn’t an option (or if it was, it didn’t work on any of the attempts I tried).

Lastly, the game’s “story mode”, the bit of I did play felt very underwhelming. Now I wasn’t actually expecting a good story, I figured it’d just be an excuse for us to play around with the game’s mechanics, but I was hoping that would lead to some fun escapades. Maybe it get better as you get further in, but the impression I got was fairly empty spaces with usually only one way to progress and some occasional VR toys tossed in if you want to slow down and goof off. If not in VR, this would just feel like some sub-par Half-Life 2 mod.

There are other modes, I unlocked a zombie one and there’s an arena one as well. These are at least straight-forward and right to the action, but as I said I’ve found better gunplay in other VR titles and the melee isn’t any better. I do like you can grab opponents and smack them into walls or each-other even. But it’s not enough to overcome the many other shortcomings.

Ironically I feel like the game’s pitch, that this is game for players with “previous VR experience and understanding of common VR gameplay principles” is completely backwards. I’ve played a fair share of VR games to compare Boneworks to, which is why I find it so underwhelming. It doesn’t really excel in any one of these “VR gameplay principles” and if I just wanted the spectacle of a VR adventure, there’s numerous VR ports of games that, while not made for VR, do benefit greatly from seeing such big production values up close in virtual reality.

But if you were NEW to VR, Boneworks probably would actually be a very helpful game to start with. Because it DOES feature so many VR mechanics, it would give a new player a broad idea of the many applications and quirks that come with VR gaming. The fairly bland environments and use of common gaming cliches would be familiar to people who’ve played other non-VR games and would make it easier for them know what is and isn’t something they can interact with. And despite being sold for seasoned VR players, it has good wealth of options to accommodate different playstyles, including a seated mode.

I’m hesitant to call Boneworks a bad game. I’d concede if I was willing to play it more I’d probably get used to its quirks and enjoy it… but I can’t see myself having more fun with it then a lot of other VR games I’ve already played.

Hot Dogs, Horseshoes & Hand Grenades is still go to if I just want to play with guns in VR. Budget Cuts did the kinda Portal-esque puzzle/story/action game thing better (with actual portals no less). Job Simulator is still a great toybox for making a mess by throwing stuff around in VR. Serious Sam VR, despite being a port, is a terrific action game in VR. This is just the stuff I’ve personally played.

I got Boneworks hoping for something new and exciting in VR, but in the end is just left me wanting for other VR games I’ve already played.\t\t\t\t\t
