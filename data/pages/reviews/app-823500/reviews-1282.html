\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm having tons of fun with this game. The physics, the setting, the immersion, the story... it's so awesome.

The game world itself is pretty immersive and you really get a sense of what it's like to be in this dystopian place. All the graffiti really adds to that immersion, you feel like there were people before you as you pass by the levels. Some times you find funny jokes the devs left. Virtual reality lets you understand the scale of things in-game for some of the big objects and buildings. I don't think there's anything else like it for VR in terms of being engrossed in a world like this.

The interactions with the game world are great. I love pushing those big red buttons throughout the game and pushing boxes around. Some of the puzzles take some thinking but never too frustrating where you want to give up.

The first enemies you will encounter are pretty much zombies that slowly try to get you. Then you get soldiers that shoot back, "upgraded" zombies, and then headcrap things that are basically the devil. Getting swarmed by those things is terrifying!

The physics and player body systems are pretty great, but sometimes they can be pretty janky. Climbing up ledges or scaling walls can be pretty weird and can take some coordination, so you really have to think it through. Eventually you will get used to it and understand your virtual body and you will soon be moving around naturally.

All in all, I think it's money well spent. It's a great VR experience that scratches my itch for something more in depth. I hope they add on to this game and continue to make more games. They've got something great here they can build on.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game has the best physics out of any VR game I've experienced, once you get used to the simulated weight of objects everything else feels pretty intuitive. The enemies are pretty challenging and all interact with the physics in pretty cool ways. For example, you're able to shoot a null-body's legs to cripple it or roll a barrel at its feet to knock it over.

One totally new experience for me in VR is having the ability to grab an enemy by its shoulders and slam it into a wall or push an advancing enemy back with my hands to keep it at bay. Since your virtual body is a physics object itself, you can even headbutt enemies to death. Being able to fully interact with enemies physically makes combat much more interesting than other VR games where the extent of your interaction with enemies is just hitting or shooting them.

My only complaints so far can likely be fixed in the future such as there being no autosaves and the way that it can sometimes be difficult for me to holster and unholster items consistently from the behind the back and under shoulder slots.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've been looking forward to this game for some time, so I'm feeling a little mixed about leaving it a 'not recommended' status. It's only just barely below recommended for me, and most of my issues could be fixed.

I'm writing this pretty early in - I'm maybe just over a third of the way through at time of review - but the game doesn't seem polished enough to recommend in its current state. What's here is good, and I've no complaints content-wise for $30 so far, but there are some curious flaws and omissions. It would be accurate to say this is less a review of the game's content, and more a review of the game's feel and mechanics.

As it stands, a lot of pretty basic features aren't here:

-There's no saving or checkpoints. A death, crash, level bug, or exiting the game means restarting a (potentially very long) level.
-No official control remapping available.
-No alternative locomotion options available, just smooth motion. Things like quick-step or arm-swing help a lot of folks with motion sickness, regardless of experience level.
-Floor and height adjust don't save, and must be adjusted every boot.
-The game does not adjust tutorials/prompts for your controller, and assumes you're using an Index controller.
-There's no option to skip the longest chunk of the very lengthy tutorial.
-There's no option to enable toggle grab for melee weapons/objects (painful on the Vive controller).
-There's no option to toggle time slowdown, rather than holding it.
-There are no difficulty options for the enemies, and combat tends to be pretty easy.


There are also a number of unpolished mechanics and bugs:

-There's a lot of clipping of the body into the environment, especially while climbing grabbable walls. For a game as focused on physical interaction as Boneworks, it's odd to see the hands/arms clip into the world during lots of basic interactions. Ideally they would just slide along surfaces rather than glitch into/out of them.
-The game will sometimes forget the 'hold' state of your button with objects/melee weapons, causing you to have to click and release again to let go. This seems to happen especially often with fast motions, which is when you'd most want it not to - for throwing things or pushing an object on a slider.
-If your VR headset disconnects, the game will sometimes swap the buttons on your controllers - so your left controller's buttons control the right hand, and vice versa.
-Sometimes, bullets will stop hurting enemies until you open a new door or load a new area.
-Climbing on top of objects in the environment is recommended, but can be a nightmare of fiddling with floor calibration settings. If it's even slightly off, anything more than chest height requires using the in-game jump, slapping your arms onto the object, holding the crouch button, then crouching down manually, and hopping while walking forward. This could be fixed by just tweaking tolerances on leg collision while in the air.
-Your limbs can get stuck in things.
-Scaling objects (climbing/moving around on things like ladders and pipes) can be very slow, and often will not reflect your real arm placement.
-For me, at 6'2", the crouch button does not get me low enough to get under intended barriers.
-If your arms do not match the proportions of the body in-game, aiming down sights with full rifles is difficult due to stock collisions with your torso. This could be fixed by having an option to let the butt of the rifle clip into you a bit when pulled back.
-Jumping is wonky. Where you are in your walking animation and how much you're physically crouched in the real world changes how effective your jump is, especially on slopes.

The attention to detail in the environment is pretty great, the music is good, and the weapons feel fine. Working in VR space with a full body mapped out can be a little difficult to get used to at first, but once you get the hang of it, it really does feel substantially different from anything that came before it. The puzzles and physics interactions are interesting, and as someone who doesn't suffer from motion sickness, the willingness of the game to push me around has been a refreshing change of pace from other games.

But, until it gets some updates to polish out some of the jank and missing options, I struggle to recommend it. It just feels a little undercooked.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPlayed on Index with Index controllers.

Half-Life was great because interactive physics in games were a new thing at the time, and Valve utilized it in a way that made the game exciting and fun, but it wasn't the entire point of the game, in fact it was a very small portion of the game. If you want a V.R. game that basically only concentrates on physics puzzles then this might be for you, but so far I've found it boring.

Graphics look alright...understandably things need to be scaled back because of the limitations of hardware right now (especially using V.R.). It doesn't feel like there's any point to anything you are doing...no goal, just puzzle after puzzle. NOTE to developers: throwing stuff in a trash can does not make a fun game.

Beyond that, maybe it's just because I'm left handed, but it is pretty janky. Your body bumps into objects when it shouldn't, you grab a drawer to open it and the whole filing cabinet comes with you, at one point I grabbed a rope that was supposed to pull me up to the higher level and it shot me half way across the room randomly, and the hands get stuck in tables and other objects. Also, the shooting mechanics just plain SUCK. I understand there are only so many buttons on V.R. controllers, but whoever decided to not have a magazine eject button made a bad design choice. If you shoot guns in real life, you probably won't like the gun mechanics at all.

I also think a lot of the high scores for this are NODE fanboys who are jumping on the hype bandwagon, but I'm really not all that impressed. Maybe if there was a more engaging story right off the bat I would be more interested but right now I'm just considering refunding.

If this is your first V.R. game I can see how you might be enamored with it, but for those of us who have been using V.R. since 2012 and motion controllers since 2016, it might feel a bit disappointing.

Also be weary of the gameplay trailers, they make it look like everything is super smooth and fluid, and it's filled with things like the person playing doing a superhero landing which is completely superfluous and is more akin to roleplaying than showing actual gameplay mechanics. You're more likely to look like a bumbling idiot when you play rather than some super badass action hero.

Ah well, at least we have Half-Life: Alyx to look forward to...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tQuite fun and impressive shooter/platformer VR title. I've been following this project since it was announced and they definitely delivered everything they've promised. Interaction with objects and the environment feels more realistic than any other VR titles to date. I enjoy the subtle world-building and flair that helps set it apart from comparable VR projects.

I played this game on a VIVE headset with VIVE wands and I don't agree with other reviewers who claim there is a problem. You only have to hold the grip button to grab an object, this should not be causing you hand strain. If it is, you can change it to toggle in the options. I've had an excellent experience on VIVE.

It's pretty ridiculous some people are calling this a "tech demo." It has a plot, clear progression, and a fair bit of polish. I'm 6 hours in and not done, so I don't think categorizing it as a tech demo is just asinine.

My single complaint is the jumping mechanic. Jumping (especially crouch jumping) feels inconsistent at times. I would recommend simplifying the jump mechanic.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoneworks is a VR-only semi-linear first person shooter in the vein of Half Life and Doom. While each level contains only one entrance and one exit, the levels themselves are relatively large, with lots of hidden rooms and secrets (more on those later). The minute-to-minute gameplay involves managing where on your body you wish to store items and weapons, looting crates for ammo/money, and shooting various flavors of enemies. Every facet of the game is masterfully crafted, and it would be a steal at twice the price.

It's a shooter, and guns make for the flashiest gameplay trailers, so I'll start there. The weapons are simply phenomenal. The way the hands grab and interact with the weapons is masterfully done. Like Pavlov, each weapon has realistic reload mechanics that require the player to load the magazine and ♥♥♥♥ the weapon to use it. Magazines can be removed with rounds still chambered, slides can be racked until all the bullets fall out of your weapon, and panicking during a mid-combat reload can lead to embarassingly firing a weapon with no round chambered. The sound design for the weapons is meaty and makes them feel *very* good to use.

The combat itself is nearly perfect as well. Enemies flinch from impacts and ragdoll in a satisfying way, lending each shot a sense of real weight and heft. Gun-wielding enemy attacks are telegraphed using uniquely colored weapon laser sights. If this is a turnoff, don't worry: the AI is fairly intelligent, and they *will* flank and gank you. Remaining situationally aware is critical during fights. The combat feels dangerous in that regard, but it rarely feels cheap or unfair. I would be remiss if I did not include some nitpicks, however, and there are some things about combat that feel janky. Most of the game world is composed of physics objects, and the AI's ability to detect line-of-sight through tiny cracks and holes in prop geometry can lead to some frustrating scenarios where you'll be crouched behind cover, unable to identify where you're being shot from. This, however, is a very minor issue in a combat system that otherwise takes full advantage of the VR platform.

Levels are relatively large and open ended, filled with hidden paths, secrets, and little bits and pieces of lore. Environmental storytelling is the real name of Boneworks' game, and you'll frequently find graffiti and other things that play the story out and hint at a greater narrative. Each secret offers a tangible gameplay benefit as well. All of Boneworks' map secrets do things directly for you. Sometimes they're keys to open alternate or secret paths, sometimes they're weapons, and sometimes they're a little "plastic" bauble to hold for seemingly no reason.

Boneworks' movement mechanics are the first out of any VR title I've seen to do two major things: (1) They take full, and I mean completely full, advantage of the game's level design and (2) they trust the player to figure out and understand how they work. Don't misinterpret my second point to mean there's no tutorial - in fact, there's quite a long one at the beginning of the campaign, and it teaches basic mechanics such as climbing and object manipulation very well. Boneworks' movement is deeper than that, however, so while it does teach you the movement equivalent of, "this is a screwdriver, this is a screw," it doesn't overexplain itself and leaves a lot of things to player curiosity, which is to the game's credit. Discovering and mastering the movement mechanics offers a simple joy that not a lot of VR games get right. The movement also takes full advantage of Boneworks' semi-linear level design. Map secrets are usually only accessible by tinkering with the movement system in some way. Steam has informed me I've run out of space, or you'd get an example here. Suffice to say, you'll climb stuff.

There are good, though less noteworthy, aspects as well. The story is only borderline present, but it's just foreboding enough to lend credence to the game's general creepy atmosphere. In the same way that Half Life was always a particularly macabre action game, and not a horror game, Boneworks is a particularly horror-inspired action game. Enemies hide in corners and behind things to spring upon you if you're not careful, and getting pounced upon by the mechanical headcrabs is genuinely frightening. I had one spring on my face and, unlike Half Life, it actually attached itself and I had to shoot my own face seven or eight times to kill it so I could rip it off. I am not an easily frightened creature, but I got a shot of straight adrenaline from that. In addition to basic enemy behavior, the whole game is dripping with creepy atmosphere, from the sounds to the visuals. Speaking of sound, the soundtrack is quite good. It's got a strong synth influence, and most of it seems to be original compositions, which is pretty cool. Think Half Life 2 but with dynamic, event-based music instead of static map-triggers.

Everything I've described so far more than justifies the $30 price tag. Honestly, I'd feel like I paid a fair price for $60. I love this game, and I easily consider it one of the best shooters of all time. Half Life 2. Titanfall 2. Doom 2016. Boneworks. This game knows exactly what it's doing, and it isn't ashamed for you to see it.

Of course, being human, I've got things about the game that irk me, so here's a short list of nitpicks:
-The nature of a physics-based VR title is that your hands will get caught on things. Sometimes that means you have to fight the controller a little. Sometimes that means your reload-hand got caught on a piece of jagged world geometry and you just dropped your magazine instead of reloading, and now a very angry man has blown your head off.
-Speaking of, the way the game handles death is simply baffling. Map checkpoints are few and far between, and when you die, nothing is reset, you're simply teleported unceremoniously to the last checkpoint. Any currently living enemies will still be alive, and any killed enemies will be dead. Puzzle solutions will still be in place too, though that's really a good thing since, depending on how badly timed your death was, the last checkpoint could be a long walk back. The actual act of dying is a bit odd too, in that when your health drops to zero, there's no indicator that you've died right off the bat. Instead your vision slowly tunnels down into blackness before fading completely, and by slowly, I mean over the course of 8-12 seconds. It's a bit jarring and awkward, and has led to some situations where I've run away from combat muttering "wait, wait, am I dead? did I die?" which is probably not something your players should ever say.
-The physics simulation is *generally* great, but things are of course buggy to some degree, and getting particularly large and bulky props to move like you want them to can be a hassle. Climbing over things without getting your character's feet caught on spare clipping is a bit awkward too, but can be mitigated by crouching, which causes his legs to contract.

Don't let the game's "orangemap" art style fool you, Boneworks is the first VR title I've played that feels like a fully fledged, fully realized concept, without a hint of tech demo. The game's quite long by VR standards, I'm about nine hours in and I'm *pretty* sure I'm approaching the end, but I don't know that for a fact. Shooting and moving are possibly the most satisfying gameplay mechanics I've interacted with in any game, VR or flat, to date. Not merely a hold-over until we get Half Life: Alyx, Boneworks is itself a fully realized spiritual successor to the Half Life franchise.\t\t\t\t\t
