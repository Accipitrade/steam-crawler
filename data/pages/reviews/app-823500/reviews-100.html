\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMixed bag. The first few hours make a bad first impression, with an overreliance on banal, generic physics puzzles elevated only by the innate novelty of VR. Unfortunately the wonky object/player physics and interaction controls make most of these puzzles (and many of the climbing sections) throughout the game into mushy, frustrating, and tedious wrestling matches against the game engine. Levels are long and often confusing to navigate, and you can only save at specific points. Worse still, if you save and quit mid-level, enemies (but not ammo apparently) respawn and certain puzzles reset, so have fun replaying half the level over again if you have to backtrack. These issues added up to a fairly miserable experience, and I probably would've abandoned the game if I hadn't spent so much money on it.

Thankfully, towards the end of the third major level, the game suddenly became much more enjoyable. The environments for the remainder of the experience are significantly more compelling, with great ambiance and perfectly-matched music. The contrived, tedious physics puzzles seemed to vanish almost completely (with one particularly egregious exception towards the very end), making for a better-paced adventure and more emphasis on the pretty good combat.

Content can only be unlocked in the sandbox mode by bringing items to certain points in the campaign, usually at the end of each level. Certain particularly exciting gadgets (including one that is misleadingly shown in one of the coolest bullshots in the trailer on the Steam page) can only be used whatsoever if you play through the entire game gathering nearly all available ammo and barely using any of it. This policy of locking sandbox content behind tedious chores and time-wasting in the campaign is really counter-intuitive to the point of having a sandbox mode in the first place, and significantly hamstrings what should've been a great post-game option for less goal-oriented players.

Sandbox restrictions aside, I could almost give Boneworks a mild recommendation as a solid VR shooter campaign with an occasional bit of brilliance (usually in the form of tactile VR interaction, usually involving crowbars), but the rocky start and QWOPish physics make it hard to recommend, at least without a significant discount.\t\t\t\t\t
