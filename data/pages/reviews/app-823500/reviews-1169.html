\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've played a lot of games since I was 13 and it was something like 2002 outside. I remember HL2 release and Boneworks is the first game since then to really hit the same note as HL2.
The thing is: it's not just a game, certainly not just another cool vr demo you'll play for 30 minutes, maybe an hour tops. It's a foundation. It's a stepping stone - a very important and I can even say historic one. This game will be remembered forever and will be rightfully in the list of the most important games for VR and gaming in general.
Physics are mind-blowing. Even with its flaws Boneworks never cease to amaze me with crazy physics details it keeps throwing in you right till the very end of the game.
The story-telling is kinda meh, but the overall atmosphere Boneworks projected on me during my gameplay left me feeling for the world of the game. It's a story with a lot of questions and a sense of mystery surrounding the world you are placed in. This again reminded me of HL and HL2 - the same sinister taste in my mouth after the ending scene, the same mystery left unsolved.
And the soundtrack is really spectacular here. This song in particular is just awe-inspiring for me: Michael Wyckoff - Opera De Ossium ft. Tim Na & Hadrian McQuaig

Main Summary

The main game is about 10 hours long. This is not including any secret levels, sandbox mode, or arena mode. Those two modes themselves require you to "recycle" some weapons at the end of the levels of the main game. Everything is physics based, which is both neat and a detriment to the game. There is a decent amount of content to keep you occupied for a while, which I think is the main purpose of the game.

Story

Firstly, don't go into the game expecting too much of a story. I didn't personally, and I believe putting faith into expecting Half-Life storytelling is leading yourself to disappointment. The game does end pretty abruptly, with teasing in the game leading to no real conclusion. It seemed like there would be something there, but either it was pushed out too early or I'm looking into it too much. Story is bare-bones.

Gameplay

This type of physics isn't seen elsewhere, and there are definitely flaws. The holstering of weapons on the body is cumbersome sometimes (assisted with an in-menu inventory system). You can get your hand stuck in walls sometimes, physics can be janky in general, etc. However, a big part of this is trying to force yourself. If you aren't aware of your body and try to force it around, of course the engine is going to glitch out. If you try to pick something up that is too heavy, you'll have trouble handling it. Most problems can be avoided by knowing your limits, but I can understand where people can be upset by it.

Gunplay is fun, though there are only three types of real enemies. One is a zombie type "null bodies" with different varients, but they all act the same. Then the headsets, followed by the shooting type (not sure of the name). Getting in gun fights can be fun, although the combat overall is not challenging whatsoever and can have some issues, mainly with rifles, which collide with your head (just stick your arms out all the way). Melee combat isn't as polished as games such as Swords and Sorcery, but you get guns abundantly.

Puzzles are normally a chore. Half the time, it took me a while to get the meaning of it. There is nothing too mind-bending, but some physics puzzles are just placed on your lap and your expected to know the mechanics of the game enough to get how you piece it together. For example, you're supposed to know a platform is on rails and can move side to side to create a bridge across two platforms. It wasn't communicated very well that the platform could move, but maybe I'm a dummy :(.

No save points, which I can understand when starting the game can be frustrating. Just know that you are expected a 1-2 hour play session per level. Saving would help, but it does break the flow of the game. Having no save system basically keeps the challenge of the game in-tact, implementing a save system would ruin most challenge for the game at the end of the day.


The game in general is like a tech-demo. It has a lot of content to explore, with a bunch of mechanics to use, but the story is unexplained and rushed and there isn't much challenge. The interaction of the game is unchallenged, which I believe is the one main selling point, so if you wanna see what you can do in VR, try it out.\t\t\t\t\t
