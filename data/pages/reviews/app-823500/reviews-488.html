\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is heavily dependent on what you want out of a VR title when you go into Boneworks.
To summarize it with a comparison to Half Life Alyx; I went into HL:Alyx expecting something kind of like boneworks. Being able to whack combine over the head with a bottle and the like. I was very disappointed that the only option for dealing with enemies was shooting them and the game was very linear. HL:Alyx played VR extremely safe and avoided implementing any mechanic or detail that had potential to be confusing or break.
Then I went into Boneworks. Boneworks feels like the anti-thesis of HL:Alyx. Boneworks kind of throws as many things at the wall as it can with physics simulation and guns and lets you go nuts. Climb, run, jump, stab, bash, trip, grapple, throw, shoot, whatever, you can probably kill a nullman with it. HL:A is very polished and safe in this regard, while Boneworks is very unpolished but doesn't hold much back.
Some pros and cons:

Pros:
>As far as I have played, probably the only fully physics-simulated VR game that's out there right now. Your entire body is simulated when playing, instead of the usual floating-head-hands thing.
>This game thrives on a combination of melee and shooting combat. Being able to use weapons of opportunity or melee weapons to beat up null bodies or whatever doesn't stop being fun for quite a while. Shooting enemies and transitioning to using your rifle as a melee weapon in whatever way you want (rifle butting, stabbing with the barrel, throwing the whole thing) always lets you approach situations in different ways if you want. You'll find more creative ways to take enemies out.
>Climbing and grappling with your bare hands is a big role in this game. Its a little disorienting and you need a decent amount of room space to do it in if you want to do it standing up, but it is interesting to say the least.
>They were nice enough to add a time-slowdown effect you can use freely inside the campaign, sandbox, and arenas, that can really let you do some cool stuff you wouldn't ever have the reaction time to do at 1x speed.
>They took a small leaf from HL:A and have a force-grab system that lets you pick nearby interactables off the ground from a distance, instead of having to physically grab them from the floor.
>The Zombie Warehouse is ironically probably the most fun in the game you'll have. Just you and endless humanoid targets to kill. ONly complaint is it quickly gets pretty laggy with the number of enemies on screen, and its really more about kiting then holding out if you're alone.

Cons:
>It is abundantly clear they didn't have any idea what to do with the story, and I quickly found myself not really caring about the story in the slightest in search for more things to kill. They do some world building here and there but at some point it all just becomes white noise and you really just want to go bash someone's head in with a new weapon you found instead.
>Levels are EXTREMELY long for the uninitiated to VR and for first-time if you want to be thorough like I was. There are distinct save points throughout the level, so you can't just drop the game and stop playing whenever if you don't want to lose progress. Levels can take 30-45 minutes to go through if you take your time and are looking for secrets or having difficulty passing a puzzle or climbing section, and that's not really a good thing when you can't drop the headset and take a break whenever you want.
>The vanilla physical strength of your character feels pretty anemic and doesn't really let you do a lot in terms of exerting force on your surroundings, or your enemies. You can't really pick up or throw around null men or zombies, and trying to push them while they're targeting you won't really get you anywhere. The other problem with this is your vanilla strength is very delicately balanced so you can climb things without breaking physics, so if you raise this with mods most puzzle sections become nigh impossible.
>In a similar vein to the strength issue, a lot of the different weapons don't feel that powerful due to swing speed limitations (the game dampening the force you move a sledgehammer at, for instance) or due to odd damage calculations. Trying to stab someone with a claymore is akward because the blade is so big, and chopping with it has a lot less effect then you would expect form a full force sword swing. Alternatively, using a combat knife or dagger and stabbing enemies in the face is extremely effective because its light, easy to control, and tends to down enemies instantly. A sizable number of weapons that have a piercing component like the bat with nails also tend to stick inside enemies which instantly makes them worthless.
>Game performance tends to tank in certain sections or with more than 6-7 enemies on screen, which I found disorienting. I'm not really using a state of the art PC for my Index, but framerate gets a lot lower when you get more than a handful of nulls on the screen or in particularly large areas. Certain areas of some levels also had pretty big performance drops.
>Physics puzzles. Way too many. I spent more time just figuring out ways to avoid physics puzzles then trying to solve them. It felt like I was playing Half-Life 2 again, where after every combat encounter there's a neat physics puzzle you have to solve, except in this case these puzzles just detracted from what I actually wanted to experience which was combat.
>Enemies and Enemy AI. For a game with so much potential for combat, they REALLY dropped the ball in enemy variety and enemy AI. There are virtually 4 types of enemies in the game each with a very specific and predictable AI: Nullmen and Zombies - They shamble to you and try to slap you in the face when they get close enough. Zombie Throwers - Like regular zombies, except if you're outside arm's length they'll throw a slow moving energy bolt and they frequently kill their own troops 99% of the time. Crablets - They're headcrabs, but they're VR headsets. They're entertaining to grab out of the air and stab to death with your off-hand, but they quickly become extremely repetetive and annoying. Clean Up Crew - They glide around and shoot at you if you're not behind cover. They're annoying and not really much of a challenge to beat. Outside of a few very minor variations, those are essentially the four enemy types you can expect to find throughout the ENTIRETY of the game. None of the enemies use actual melee weapons, nor do any of them actually respond to really anything you do unless you hit them in the head hard enough to stun them. Zombies will continue to try and flail at you even if you've got their arms pinned, and they won't react to any damage that isn't enough to completely stun them. You can come up with a hundered different ways to kill a nullman, but eventually you're gonna want to fight something a bit smarter then a shambling guy trying to slap you.
>The method to unlock and collect things is obtuse and takes way too long to do. You have to collect weapons/items you want to unlock and bring them to a reclamation bin at the end of the level and destroy them to unlock them in sandbox/arena. There's several areas which are usually hidden behind puzzles or obscure climbing/jumping sections which contain collectables. Given the amount you can carry, you have to play the same level several times over to get everything and its really just a chore.
>All of the fun items like the Power Puncher and the like are hidden behind exorbitant price requirements (ammunition is money in the game), and by the way it takes you several minutes to fish out 28000 bullets in 30 round magazines and shove them into the machine to get it.
>they really could have added settings or items that makes the player more of a physical powerhouse, but beyond the power puncher, you need to turn to modding. Modding isn't natively supported on the game, so there's not really a lot of mods to chose from besides item reskins.\t\t\t\t\t
