\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have a fairly good tolerance for janky games so that wont be factoring into this review. If you do not tolerate jank in games I would suggest not picking up boneworks as its various systems can mesh together in messy ways. With that said:

Boneworks is a game that is often hailed and revered for its physics, guns and all of the crazy ways that you can mess around with them in the sandbox but for me the star of the show has always been the singleplayer story mode. I love half life and it's very clear that stress level zero took some inspiration when it comes to the overall design of the game as the levels follow the half life formula of being on a linear track but with mini sandboxes to explore. Boneworks elaborates on the sandbox concept by having it often be the main focus of the level and by increasing their scope. Not in a way that all levels are completely open and non linear but in a way that lets you take different and varied routes on different playthroughs. What this often means is that the level has a clear sequence that the player can take to get through it like finding a key or completing a puzzle to get to an area but will alternatively allow you to bypass said key hunt or potentially slow puzzle by means that can seem purposefully done and others that are left up to your own creativity. That is the true joy of boneworks for me, the evolution of games that came before it so that despite its occasionally clunky design it is up to the player what of that design they want to see and how they wish to see it. This is not however to discount the design present in boneworks as it is still very good in parts. On a mechanical level you have standouts like the third level "Runoff" which starts off slow in a puzzle room and escalates to larger fights outside in the concrete runoff. You weave in and out of buildings which allows the pace of the level to slow down from time to time for more relaxed puzzle and traversal segments. Not all levels meet this high watermark like "The sewers" which are well.... a sewer level, not a terrible one with a few interesting set pieces but on the whole acts as a much more of a setup for the dramatic payoff after the sewer level that utilizes the amazing soundtrack to its best (more on the ost later). In more abstract terms the design of boneworks can be described as having a large "You are not supposed to be here" sign slapped on it which echoes the unfinished nature of mythOS itself. Levels and areas feel vast and unfriendly and the strange places and architecture end up raising many questions about their functions in the virtual world as a whole. This, like many things in boneworks, is never fully explained and can only be started to understood by piecing together vague ramblings on clipboards and graffiti splashed along the concrete walls. Going back to the subject of the soundtrack, it's amazing and lends a great deal to the world and atmosphere of the game. Feelings of discovery in "analog sunrise" as you first discover your new found freedoms in a museum punctuated with the more whimsical "bone rhapsody". A tense beat repeats after the enemies see you and start to encroach on your position and then the music kicks into gear and you're crouched behind a barrier taking pot shots over the lip of your shelter and reaching down for a new magazine as your gun clicks empty. As you climb higher into mythOS the synths grow in intensity and bombast as you feel the weight of your travels behind you.

Boneworks is not a perfect game but their is a true gem of creativity in the unfinished corporate halls, backrooms, and storage areas that is undeniable.
