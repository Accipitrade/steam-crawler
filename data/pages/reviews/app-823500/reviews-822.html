\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGunplay: Basic, but decent. It feels good to shoot and reload, and the slow-mo ability can be fun in gunfights. The physics can sometimes get in the way of the gunplay, though. The MP5K is a bit finicky when you're trying to reload. More often than not I found myself grabbing the foregrip rather than the charging handle. One of the AR-15s -- I think it was the one with the angled foregrip -- was a bit finicky in terms of trying to grab the actual foregrip, and it would think I was trying to grab the magazine well. I'm surprised at how basic the gun handling is. Why can't I select between semi-auto and full-auto? Why do I have to physically grab the magazine rather than just hitting the mag release button? Why do I have to manually charge the bolt/rack the slide instead of just hitting the bolt/slide release? Why are the guns below the level of Pavlov VR?

Melee: Terrible, at least with the Vive Wands. Unlike guns, where you press the grip button once and your grip is locked in, you have to constantly be holding your grip buttons in order to hold on to your melee weapon. I don't know if this applies to everyone, but my hands were quite sore after holding on to a weapon for too long, to the point where I really never used melee weapons unless the game 100% forced me to. Actually swinging and stuff feels alright, but having to constantly hold the grip buttons sucks, and I wasn't able to find an option in-game to switch to a toggle grip like most other games use with the wands. I might have to do a playthrough with the index controllers to see how much of a difference they make. Aside from that, the most numerous enemy types just walk into you, meaning you only get a chance for one good swing before you have to re-position and make some distance, meaning guns are just generally more effective.

Level Design: It varies. Any level where you're forced to use melee weapons automatically sucks due to the aforementioned issue. Most are good or at least fine, but there's one instance that really soured my mood. At the end of each level is a reclamation bin that you throw your guns and stuff into because you're not allowed to take items with you between levels. In every other level before, the reclamation bin was always at the end of the level, either right next to or just a short walk away from the exit. On one level, however, after throwing all my good guns into the bin, I was hit with a firefight. The devs decided to put a mid-level reclamation bin in for seemingly no reason, and I now had to go back to the level start and buy a crap gun in order to continue. Not fun. After a certain point where you have to use vaccum cannons to shoot balls into a bigger ball (it makes sense in context), things take a dramatic downturn and the levels become actively unfun, going from weakness to weakness to Metal Gear Solid Ladder Climb Section In VR. Also, there's one point right at the end of the game which almost seems purposely designed to cause motion sickness. I had no issues with motion sickness in this game (or VR in general, usually), but that very last room at the end got me.

Level Design 2 - Atmosphere: The atmosphere present in the game is quite nice, especially the sewer level. The programmer graphics style works for the setting, and overall the world itself is interesting enough to be in. The biggest complaint is that there are elements of certain levels that don't serve the atmosphere or level design at all and are just there for the sake of having a physics puzzle.

Physics: pretty good, but with a fair amount of low points that amount to the physics getting in the way of the fun rather than enhancing it. Climbing feels shaky and unpolished, and I've lost progress once or twice because I fell when I thought I had a good grip. Moving heavy or large objects is clumsy. Movement can feel off at times. There's a constant problem of your character kicking things you meant to walk over, or your body getting in the way more than it would in real life. Overall, meh, which turns to bad when you take into account the lack of toggle-grip for the Vive Wands.

Story: Nonsensical, I have absolutely no idea what's going on or why I'm doing any of this, and I really don't care about anything that's happening. This also takes a dramatic dip at the same time as the level design.

Music: Nothing but positives here. Michael Wyckoff killed it with the soundtrack. The cheery songs sound like soulless corporate music (which fits perfectly with the world), and the more somber tones enhance the atmosphere. It's a damn shame I don't remember enough places where the music was used (probably due to the playthrough being split in half over a period of months) because it's probably the best part of this game for me. Seriously, just go buy the OST.

I recognise and appreciate the effort put into this, but overall I have absolutely no desire to play this again after finishing it once. They have some extra stuff you can find to play with once the main story's done, but I really don't want to bother with it. The only thing that would keep me coming back is the gunplay, and there's other games with better gunplay (H3VR, Onward, Pavlov, etc.)

Edit: Didn't even get nominated for the Steam Awards...Oof

Edit (2022-11-13): After obtaining Index controllers a while ago and finally getting around to playing this again, my official verdict is... yeah, still not great. The Index controllers help with the hand cramping immensely, making grabbing stuff and using melee weapons bearable, but that still doesn't fix the rest of the issues I had with this game, which I was freshly reminded of when I played again. Climbing still sucks, even without the hand cramps. Considering this game came out when the Index was just 6 months old or so, with Vive and Oculus being the more common headsets at the time, I'd have figured they would have supported that controller better.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPutting it shortly: bones are beautiful, especially when they work.As close as 2 years ago the concept of putting on a vr headset, and becoming so immersed in a game that it would become my favorite of all time, beating portal 2 in just a single week: to put it lightly I didn’t expect it.But now I sit here after countless days spent pulling off my headset to reveal the famous “oculus face” among with sweat dripping down my forehead, and yeahBoneworks is my new favorite video game.I had to go through several hurdles to get this thing working: making sure my oculus quest can play pcvr, getting a shadow subscription to play said pcvr, but it was all worth it.Let’s start with the world and way you interact with an environment.Everything feels what I would call “the perfect level of real” Just about EVERYTHING you see in Boneworks you can interact with.Wanna use that hammer on the shelf? You can pick it up. wanna smash a computer with said hammer? Go ahead! Wanna literally grab the back of a table with food on it and proceed to flip it over? Yup, you can do that too.I understand interactive environments to some players might not be the most “WOW LOOK AT THAT” thing nowadays, but for a vr game THIS expansive, it’s baffling, just about everything works how it should, and that’s great.But that’s barley scratching the surface of Boneworks, let’s talk about movement in VR.Most VR games tend to see you simply walking around a space that shifts occasionally, lending itself to more small and intimate experiences, which is great! But when it’s all you have, it can get boring.Even with an oculus quest, and a full room, sometimes real life walking isn’t enough to tell a story, so then we have movement.One of my BIGGEST gripes with VR games nowadays is the way movement is done, the philosophy always seems to be comfort over quality. However, Boneworks is the very rare case where if anything, it’s the other way around.When you first boot up the game you get a big pop up telling you this is for “expert VR users only”...and they aren’t lying.In Boneworks, movement is controlled by an analog stick, meaning your character’s legs move and you don’t, of course for some this causes motion sickness, but trust me when I say to have an experience like as Boneworks, it’s a near requirement. The game goes as far as to make fun of previous vr games that used teleportation based movement, calling them “primitive” with an image of a caveman.The movement in Boneworks just feels right, it’s not so realistic you’ll be panting and need a shower after 15 minutes, but it’s realistic enough that if you play into it, you feel like you’re there.but that’s just how the game is put together, how is the design and story? It’s amazing....mostly.The level and puzzle design is the perfect type of design that I’m finally seeing come back and I’ve missed for a while, that being “here’s a puzzle, solve it your own way”. 90% of the puzzles in this game have at LEAST more than one solution, which to me adds true replay value, meaning I actually WANT to replay the game, not “there’s things I unlock if I replay the game” but we’ll get to that later.The story is one of those that seems simple until you look into it, it’s a great example of “show don’t tell” and there’s a lot going on if you observe, however it’s not simply fed to you, and I think this has caused many players to feel as if it’s basic and surface level.That’s understandable, but I think it’s just enough to keep the average player going throughout the game.Now let’s talk about the issues.Although it’s been vastly improved since launch, there’s no denying it: Boneworks has some jank.It’s (usually) not enough to ruin the experience, but I’d be lying if I said I didn’t get my virtual hands stuck in a wall a couple times.However due to the amount of innovations this game makes, for the majority of the jank it’s easy to just brush off.However, there is one issue that does hurt the game pretty badlyThe unlock system.The way you unlock stuff in Boneworks is kinda cool at first, but the more you play, the more it gets worse.First up, there’s ammo, your ammo from level to level stays consistent which for gameplay made for a really interesting feeling. Each level after the introduction starts with an ammo vending machine, complete with a nice jingle, in which you dispense ammo, and get a cool weapon out of it, seems like a nice trade off, but we’ll touch on that in a second.The game is full of “reclamation bins” at the end of each level, if you throw a weapon into these you get to unlock it for sandbox mode, simple enough, but that’s not the issue, the issue is the way the game has you unlock dev tools:SPOILERS: for endgame weapons....At what appears to be the final level of the game you are given ammo vending machines with OBSCENE amounts of ammo required to unlock them, meaning you have to play through the game again, only use melee weapons, and collect just about EVERY ammo crate to unlock the fun weapons to screw around with, like a gravity gun, a gun that can spawn anything reclaimed, or a gun that is essentially noclip. I don’t hate the idea of giving the player something for doing a melee only run, but I think this is just dumb, I don’t like the idea of “dumb weapons to screw around with” being locked behind the games hardest challenge, but that’s still a forgivable offense in my eyes.Overall Boneworks is really great, the one issue I have with it is really a post endgame thing, and won’t effect the vast majority of playersIf you can, pick this up, it’s worth a few bones.\t\t\t\t\t
