\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter a couple hours with it, and getting past the tutorial, I'm pretty confident I got my money's worth. But I had a few gripes still.

-Movements strangely unintuitive a lot of the time. Your "feet" get caught on not only everything else but your own other foot when you're trying to strafe. It makes a weird disconnect between what you're trying to do with your controller and what's happening in game. and apparently my stomach doesn't like that because it's one of VERY few games that actually managed to make me nauseous after a while of playing

-Your hands and arms get caught on. F******. Everything. I totally get the realism but at the same time I totally understand why Valve decided on floating hands.

-The game tries to marry role play with action, similarly to how Blade and Sorcery does (by that I mean by how slow things have a lag when you swing them and you're supposed to pretend you're actually swinging it how you would in real life). I don't think it works that well. In either this or the other combat arena games.

But there's a lot to like to. Shooting is great, puzzles aren't too bad, interactivity with the world is *really* interesting. But I think 30 dollars is the perfect price. I would have a problem with this game at 60 bucks. I think the devs may have realized that early on. The thing is...I'm not sure I'd recommend it to everyone. There's plenty of not great in the game alongside the interesting steps forward.

But I can only vote yes or no so...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI normally wouldn't review a game with this little playtime, but I felt compelled to after seeing a lot of negative reviews that felt unearned. If after playing more I change my mind (I'm sure that I won't), I will update the review.

What is boneworks? Basically it's a conglomeration of the humor and styling of portal with the gameplay and story elements of half-life. It was apparently pitched to valve as a half-life game originally, and they said no, so the devs had to remove all of the half-life elements... but yeah, it's half-life.

It appears to be a full-length story (around 10 hours), though I imagine it will take me far longer to complete it with my playstyle. Why? Because the real selling point of this game is the physics engine and ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ is it good. If you played Half-Life 2 when it first came out and found yourself just playing with the physics stuff and enjoying it... this is like that on crack. It's VR... so everything feels like you are there, and your hands give you a 1:1 presence to do whatever it is that you're thinking of doing. I think I spent like 6 minutes just climbing around in a jungle-gym-like thing in the pseudo-tutorial section. I've played a lot of VR games that have similar things and never really felt the need to do that, I think the full inverse-kinematics body, and the fact that EVERYTHING is physics based changes it for me.

One of my index controllers (knuckles) is back with valve for a RMA, so I have been playing with one of the vive-motes and one of the knuckles controllers. The game was very clearly built for knuckles, playing with them feels absolutely amazing. It's easily the best proof of concept for the controllers out of any game that I have tried. But, I have seen a lot of people complaining about how the vive-motes work with it. Honestly, I don't think that criticism is fair at all. The only criticisms that I could really level at the game have to do with the design of the controllers themselves, I.e, they don't have finger-tracking, they don't have a joystick for movement, the grip is a button, rather than analogue, etc. None of these things are the fault of Boneworks, and the end all be all point is that if you are enjoying other games with the vivemotes like pavlov or H3VR or Onward, the handling is basically no different in this game. With that said, I will probably wait until I get the other index controller back to play more simply because the upgrade is huge with this game in particular.

I've seen other people complaining that the game makes them sick. The game specifically says about 4 times that it is only meant for VR veterans. You may have thought that they were kidding, they were not. If you are prone to VR sickness you NEED to ease yourself into it. You can't just go in and start jumping off of things. A lot of people can get over VR sickness by slowly acclimating themselves, stopping immediately after they start feeling off and grabbing some ginger, then coming back to it when they are feeling better. I used to get VR sickness pretty much immediately when playing desktop games in vr (like skyrim using a mouse and keyboard with a vr headset), doing this method worked for me. Anyways, if you don't want to have to do that, probably this game is not for you. With the physics engine that they use there just wouldn't be a good way to make it work for everyone.

Things that I like:

- The physics engine is a playground... always... EVERYTHING feels fun
- The gun mechanics are pretty fun and fluid, you can treat them like a real gun (except I haven't found out how to screw with the safety / fire mode), or you can let the game do some of the work for you. For instance, you can grab a clip from your belt and knock the now empty clip from the gun just by hitting it, then put the new clip in. You can then rack the gun either by physically doing it, or by pulling the trigger once. I think these small 'concessions' to further gameplay are nice to help people get into it, and it's especially nice that they made it an OPTION, so that you can play realistically if you want.
- The humor is spot on
- It looks like a portal game and feels like a half-life game
- The Melee Mechanics are the best that I have tried, easily up there or maybe even better than blade & sorcery.
- A full-length story is something that has been greatly lacking in a built for VR game

Things I don't like:

- It seems like the angle of the guns might be very slightly off, it's weird that there is no setting to change that
- Sometimes when grabbing a door handle, the character seems like they are trying to lift themselves up by it
- The music felt a little repetitive... but honestly that might be because I was in one place ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ around far longer than the game expected.
.....

that's basically it.

The game is pretty easily the best "Made from the ground up for VR game", I don't know whether it will ultimately give me more playtime than Skyrim, No Man's Sky, or Vivecraft, but it definitely feels better to play.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tVery early in my experience with the game, but after a little over an hour of running around the first couple of areas, I can say with confidence, this game is exactly what I've been looking for in VR. After getting a taste for how truly awesome fully physics based games can be in VR with Blade and Sorcery, Boneworks (which I've admittedly been feverishly awaiting since the early Node videos) is exactly the type of fully realized game that I, and I think the VR community as a whole, have been desperately waiting for. The setting and tone are pitch perfect, the graphics and general aesthetic of the game is is very reminiscent of Portal or Half Life while still treading unique new ground, and again, I've only played for about 70 mins, but the music seems like another very high point. I literally just came back to the store page to grab the OST when I felt the need to post this review as well.

Seriously, with all of the "Cool idea, but I wish this was more than a tech demo" VR games out there these days, it's truly refreshing to see Boneworks release as such a fully fleshed out game! Again, I'm posting this after having played for a little over an hour, so my experience and overall opinion could change in the future, but honestly, even if it does, as of today, December 10th, 2019, if you're looking for a VR game to buy and you ask me, my response will be Boneworks 10 times out of 10! Bravo to Stress Level Zero, you guys deserve all of the accolades and hyperbole, the hype is real!\t\t\t\t\t

That all said, Boneworks is a first person puzzler (akin to portal) with shooting mechanics and an emphisis on physics (seriously, everything felt like it had mass including the player). The story and lore hang tight (pun unintended) on VR technology which helped with immersion with this quirky world.
My advice to first time players is to "pinch" as much as you can (this is mostly for those with Vive controllers, you'll thank me later), and make sure you throw your hands up when jumping (the first puzzle was a #%$ if you don't know how to jump).
All in all, worth the wait " )\t\t\t\t\t
