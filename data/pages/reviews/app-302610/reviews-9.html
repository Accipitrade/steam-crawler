\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI started playing this game a little over two years ago after somebody mentioned it in the comments section of a Super Hexagon video on YouTube, saying something to the effect of "If you think this game is hard, then you should try Boson X!" Having been looking for a new game to try out, I decided I might as well buy it and see what the game was all about.

Boson X quickly became my favorite game to play during downtime, such as before leaving for school or waiting to go someplace. I could play it while I was talking to friends online, while listening to music, or if I just needed to get my mind off of something, I could open the game and collide particles until I felt better.

My favorite part of Boson X is that there is no inherent end to how difficult it can become. As your score increases in a level, so does the speed of incoming tiles; the first level becomes just as difficult as the last level at a certain point. Each level has completely unique patterns, so it doesn't feel monotonous switching between levels like it can be with Super Hexagon. Levels at higher percentages become thrilling to play, and the adrenaline rush that comes upon reaching a new high score on a level cannot easily be explained in words; it is something that must be experienced to be understood.

I was not good at Boson X to start with; in the beginning I found myself struggling to beat many of the earlier levels and often times I would close the game out of frustration. I'd lose my motivation to play because I wasn't sure I'd be able to beat a certain level. But I'd inevitably want to come back to it and try again and again until I overcame those daunting hurdles. And I just kept going, and eventually I finally beat the final level, and from then I was satisfied.

Or so I thought.

Back at that time I had been leaderboard climbing on Super Hexagon, having scored at least five minutes in every stage. I considered that Boson X had leaderboards as well, and soon enough I began working on improving my scores there too. As time progressed, my high scores improved further and further, and on April 1, 2018, I achieved my first world record score, on the Dark X Boson stage. I did not stop there. At the time of writing this review, I hold seven world record scores among the eighteen stages, and I currently don't have any plans on stopping.

I recommend this game to anybody who enjoys challenging video games, and especially those who enjoy leaderboard climbing. If you fall under either category (or possibly both), then Boson X is for you. However, if you're easily aggravated by games that don't let things go the way you want them to, then avoid this title.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBoson X is a third person rotational runner game developed by Ian Maclarty for the Lotech Engine. It follows the story of a scientist inside the large hadron collider performing very very fast feats of science.

Boson X is a really cheap bitesized idea with a slick artsyle and a beautifully optimized engine. I'd be surprised if your fridge couldn't run this game at 60 fps or higher, which is great because the reflex based gameplay totally suits that. Playing Boson X is akin to playing a bullet hell shooter game in the way it sort of sends you off into a meditative state as you find yourself running along a stream of your own unconscious, dodging walls and trying to land on the blue platforms. I spent over and hour and a half on just the last stage, never getting any higher than 40%, and then I started getting into a zone while playing it and talking to my sister...whereupon I beat the stage without even realizing it until it'd already happened.

It's an endless runner game but stages are gated until you can make a certain amount of progress in each previous stage -- progress being denoted by blue platforms which raise a percentage bar whenever you run on them. What this mandates is a level flow of obvious, impossible to miss blue platforms, blue platforms where you're required to notice some special detail in the level to land on consistently, blue platforms spliced into obstacle courses, and regular old obstacle courses. Cycling between different predetermined setpieces randomly each time you reset a stage creates a reactionary familiarity with the level design and lends itself towards that 'zoning out' state i mentioned earlier.

The soundtrack as well sucks you into a nearly primal, meditative zone with amelodic, bass heavy synthetic drones that amp up the intensity of the experience in a subtle way. The soundscape is sparse but it feels driving and focused, as your little dude breaths and jumps, and his footsteps get faster and faster the longer you progress.

Overall I really liked this game. It's hard to not recommend a game that's only 3 dollars especially on the adage that 'you may as well buy it' but I genuinely recommend this game. It looks great, sounds great and feels great, and its not wanting for content overall.\t\t\t\t\t

#Discussions_QuoteBlock_Author
Hello! I'm here to tell you about our Lord and Savior, RNGesus.
Have you accepted RNGesus as the way, the truth, and the light?
...no? Oh, honey, you're going to have a rough time.

Boson X is simple on the surface. It's an endless runner with three controls, all of which are some variation on jumping. Race down the interior of a particle collider and gather energy as you go. Collect a full charge to complete the level and unlock the next. Its low-poly models look fine for what they are and the low graphical fidelity lets the game run on just about anything.

Take a deeper look, though, and the appeal falls apart for all but a select audience.

There are 18 levels, around half of which are difficult and a quarter of which are absolute . Each level is generated from a series of randomly-selected segments chained one onto another as you play, ensuring no two runs are ever wholly identical. However, some levels feature segments that don't have many, or any, of the blue tiles required to build a full charge. I had one run careen to a halt at 99.90% because the RNG refused to give me level segments that included accessible blue tiles. The RNG refusing to cooperate will happen often. Get used to it.

The more difficult levels have the player moving so fast that they often require frame-perfect maneuvers to get around obstacles if your reflexes aren't stellar and you're entering a hazard even a few milliseconds behind, which was usually the case with me. It feels great to pull that kind of stunt work off, but it's really frustrating to feel like you must be absolutely perfect just to play when the RNG is allowed to be as sloppy as it pleases. It felt like a big middle finger from the game every time. A middle finger that I am now happy to reciprocate.

The trippy background designs and I do not get along. If you're visually impaired, like I am, you're going to have a nightmare of a time picking out hazards and platforms from background elements in time to react to them. More often than not, the backgrounds interfered with what remains of my depth perception and made it impossible to focus on where the runner was positioned in space. This was especially noticeable on the levels that featured some kind of elevation shift inside the tunnel, or on any level with animated textures on the platforms. I found it difficult to tell where I was unless I was running directly along a platform in those levels.

The music was fine. It would have been nicer if it had been permitted to loop endlessly instead of restarting with every failure, so I'm kicking Boson X's soundtrack to the curb here: the tracks are already brief and repetitive and having them restart over and over only makes them more repetitive. Playing without sound became preferable.

The difficulty of each level didn't seem consistent. Often, the second-to-last level in a given set was the hardest. Some of those took me upwards of 500 attempts to complete, even. However, one final level took me about 20 tries. I don't know if I just got lucky with the RNG spitting out segments that screwed me over in one level followed by segments that benefited me in the next, or if that level really was that comparatively easy, but the overwhelmingly difficult penultimate level followed immediately by the underwhelmingly easy ultimate level in that set left me feeling cheated. I'd much rather have had a better difficulty progression than the variable and unfair mess that I got.

Pros:
  • Simple to learn.
  • Stylistic graphics.
  • Runs on a toaster.
  • Challenging.
Cons:
  • Fixed resolution.
  • Fixed refresh rate.
  • Unfair randomness.
  • Visually confusing.
  • Droning music.
  • Variable difficulty.

A game for masochists that I wish I hadn't purchased or completed. If that's your thing, you'll probably still get a kick out of it. As for me... into the "never install again" pile you go, Boson X.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA good "rage" game.

For what little plot most of the games have, it's got a decent premise. There is a lot more atmosphere than one would expect from a game like this. It's somewhat of a spacey, glitchy, surreal experience.

Gameplay in these types of games needs to be fast paced, difficult, and quick to hop in an out of. I think the game works for being difficult and quick to hop in and out of, but it can be a bit frustrating to die near 100% completion of a level and then revert back to going at an agonizingly slow speed. I also feel that RNG, while never FORCING you into a situation that you can't get out of, can really screw you over and place you into situations that you'd likely not get out of without tons of practice being put into that EXACT same situation again. This is an issue that I don't feel that similar rage games, like Super Hexagon or Velocibox, place the player in. It will pretty much hit you over the head with the same difficult changes over and over again, and you'll be forced to adapt. In this game, I often times just killed myself if a difficult obstacle presented itself early on, and to an extent I think that you can softly "cheat" your victory in this game.

The difficulty curve works rather well, but the last level makes things very frustrating, and messes with perspective to make it seem like platforms are further/closer than they really are. Regardless of how much practice you get before playing the last level, it tests a completely different skill and... I'm not quite sure how I feel about that. Perspective and timing when you jumped or stopped your jump arc were in the game, but not to the horrid extent that the final level is. Though, when all is said and done, the game is satisfying to beat, like many of its kind.\t\t\t\t\t
