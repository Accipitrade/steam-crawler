\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDefense Grid 2 is the sequel to the much loved Defense Grid, a tower defence game that slipped under the radar in the late 2000's, but became a cult-classic after. At the time, the genre was somewhat quiet, having effectively disappeared again from popular culture after Warcraft 3's custom maps popularised it. DG2 continues (sort of) the story from the first game, but it's relatively standalone, as most of the character motivations are fairly obvious, and despite it being nearly 10 years since i played the first game, I didn't feel entirely lost (though I am interested in a replay of the original game now).

The gameplay loop consists (as ever in this genre) of facing multiple waves (in the campaign, typically between 20 and 30) of enemies of varying types. Some are fast, some are slow, some are shielded, and some are cloaked. In exchange, you have a series of tower types, and a limited number of build locations. The towers are built using money which is earned over time, and for killing waves quickly (unlike the classic model where it is based on kills). What isn't always obvious is the balance that the game makes you play between enemy types, and available towers. In practice, certainly by the end of the game, most missions can be completed with a small selection of fully upgraded towers, but it's not always clear what the best tool is for the job. I'm still not sure which tower is supposed to break the cloaked enemies, though gun turrets always seem to attack them just fine!
As with most tower-defence games, it's possible for the early to mid game to become a little dull, due to the pauses between waves, so the game provides a fast-forward button to speed the action up to 3 times. Without this, most missions can last over 20 minutes, so this can make some of the easier missions more bearable (especially while waiting for more money. During the game, a series of AI helpers (who are the characters that tell the story) are unlocked, who provide special weapons for use during missions, as well as a series of weapon upgrades (e.g. 15% extra damage to shields for gun turrets; or a 60% increased damage, with lower area of effect for inferno turrets). These can be selected before playing the missions, adding an extra level of nuance of the gameplay - do you want cannons to do more damage, or slow down the enemies they hit, and do you want your super-weapon to overcharge the turrets for a few moments to devastate the enemies, or need a insta-kill blast on a small area?

A healthy variety is given in the game maps too, with each map presenting various options. For instance, are the enemy routes in and out the same or different; does this map allow the player to place turrets in the path of the enemies (and create bottlenecks and kill-zones), or must all the turrets be placed at the side of the defined path. This is strengthened with some excellent visual variety: a lake-shore city, fighting around a crashed spaceship etc.

In addition to the high quality of the gameplay, the music, sound and speech are all equally excellent. The music is motivational and uplifting, with brassy fanfares pushing the player onwards. The FX of the weapons, although it can quickly fade into white-noise, is punchy and instantly indicative, as too are the alien death sounds - though again this can fade into a mess during the more furious moments. And the speech, with the story being mostly told by conversations between the talking head AI's during the missions, is well performed, often amusing, and suitably dramatic. Luckily the Fast-forward option doesn't impact this, so the story is told just as effectively whether you're truly speed running the missions or not.

Once the campaign is over, there are a surprising number of variant modes to play through. Multiple difficulties, two grinder modes (more waves, up to 100 for Super-Grinder), challenge modes (e.g. only gun turrets, or no top-upgrade tier). In addition, while there's no DLC (the special edition DLC provides 'about the development' and backstory material), there is custom map support. This get's a little interesting: There is an editor in game, and support for sharing those with others. However, the developers took a method of monetising those fan made maps for sale in the game - meaning there's a mini-shop for 8 of them. Fortunately, there's a fan site with hundreds of maps to be played available here: https://www.dg2freemaps.com/. I've really only played the campaign through once (with a few repeats to get gold medals on all of them) at the time of writing the review (12.3hrs), but I am certain I'll be coming back to DG2 for more fun in the future.

From what I've read, neither Defense Grid game did as well as Hidden-Path would have wanted. The first apparently being a victim of some XBOX-Arcade related shenanigans forcing delays and lower prices, and the second being funded by a partly unsuccessful kickstarter and an investor saving the day at the last minute. As a result it's unlikely that we'll get a third game, which is a real shame. But with the original game (20 maps), with 6 DLC packs (18 more maps) and the sequel (21 maps), with the hundreds of maps available for free, I am happy with what we have for now.

I played the first Defense Grid in 2013, and it stuck with me over the years, so much so that I picked up DG2 when it was released, but (due to the backlog) didn't get around to playing it until 2023. That was my mistake, and I encourage you to not waste any more time in picking this up - even at full price ($17.50 CAD) this is a bargain of a game, and a masterpiece that everyone should enjoy.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDefense Grid 2 can be recommended to fans of tower defense games, though I didn't enjoy it nearly as much as the original (Defense Grid: The Awakening). DG:TA is a fantastic game that still holds-up today, so go play that one if you missed it. If you already played DG:TA to death and are looking for something new to play, then give DG2 a try.

If I hadn't played DG:TA, then I would think DG2 is a pretty good tower defense game. There is plenty to like, though I don't have anything new to add to the discussion. There is plenty of info out there about what DG2 is, so let me just mention the few things that frustrated me about it, as that might be useful to someone considering this game.

- There are no aerial enemies, so that element from the first game is missing.
- I disliked several of the game modes (especially Focal Point), and many of the different ways to play the game aren't that different, so don't buy in to everything said about near-infinite replayability.

Despite my criticisms, I think DG2 is still a pretty good tower defense game that is worth playing if you enjoyed the first one.\t\t\t\t\t
