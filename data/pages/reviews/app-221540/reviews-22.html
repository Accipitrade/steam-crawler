\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTLDR - This is the Windows Vista version of Defence Grid. They took a solid concept and threw a TON of pretty art at it, so it certainly looks nice, and it's still a fun game, but you've got a mess of poor design choices to wade through and familiarise yourself with.

I might as well start with my biggest grievance - the menus. This game is essentially about navigating menus. Literally the only two things that don't use a menu are selecting *where* to place a tower, and firing the orbital laser. Why then am I left feeling like the menus in this game are clunky and unrefined? A great example is the options menu. I spin the scroll wheel, expecting the menu to scroll. Nope. It just moves the selection down the menu, and then jumps back the moment my mouse moves a single pixel. Then there's upgrading. In DG1 I could press U, tower upgraded. Now there are *two* keybindings for "Build/Upgrade" (yes, called the same thing) and both of them seem to do exactly the same thing as clicking on the tower.

Another example, you can essentially double-click a location to build a gun tower there. So when you double-click the raised platform you built (that allows a tower to be built on top), you might understandably be confused when you sell the raised platform, because for some reason that's the first option in the list instead of being at the bottom like it is everywhere else.

Then there's the main menu. It's a mess, I'm always looking for the button to proceed. Okay so I've selected a world, then a mission, and, oh I now need to select what mode to do on this mission, and then the resource gathering type (why is this even an option? At the very least it should be unlocked later) and the difficulty. The Next button is at the bottom right, it takes you to a summary screen, and the Launch button is now... above the menu? This is all exacerbated by the horrible menu design. You see, you can't just click an option in a menu. You have to click a menu, and *then* you can click an option in it, despite that every menu is fully visible. Were that not annoying enough, it actually scrolls the menus across and off the screen, so you can't even just click back. I get a very strong sense this was designed for a controller and then ported to mouse/keyboard as an afterthought.

I'm also disappointed with the towers themselves. I naively just played on Hard, despite not having unlocked any of the perks (except a "very helpful" upgrade for a tower I haven't unlocked yet), and found one of the earlier missions to be basically impossible. I got the furthest when I just spammed gun towers.

Oh, but surely a mix of towers is better? Nope, pure guns all the way got me to near the end. Any time I built anything else, I very quickly lost a bunch of cores. This is most likely smoothed out as the game progresses, but aren't they just teaching bad habits here at the start? Before anyone starts accusing of skill, I have gold-ranked every single mission and challenge of DG1, over 150 of them, with the exception of the latest DLC.

In terms of 'feel', they've also lost a lot of their impact. As I'm colourblind and can't see the colour of the bases, I was constantly checking them to see if they were upgraded because they looked and sounded so tinny. I really liked how in DG1 they progressed from tinny to heavy sounds as you upgraded them, particularly the cannon with its deep triple barrel, and even the designs clearly communicated the level of a tower, but there's very little of that here. The Inferno tower is the worst, its flames move slower than the aliens it's shooting at, so that it doesn't look like it's hitting them at all.

What I also don't understand is why the game has this great pathing system, where you're given a map and limited but impactful choices to path them through it, but then forces you into a fixed path with zero choices so frequently. More than anything, the tower placements are usually not directly next to the path, which badly impacts short-range towers. Like the Tesla tower, on literally the first mission you unlock it.

It's not all bad. I was extremely impressed when I saw I could plan a path for the aliens and actually see what path they would take without having to just let them in and see which way they went. The new visuals are very pretty, and while they may not be as intuitive as I would like, the menus do look much nicer.

My problems with it are all minor things, DG1 had its quirks as well, but here I'm finding myself thinking more about the design of the game (and getting annoyed at it) than the game itself. When I think on DG1, I think of finding the optimal path around the final level. When I think of the time I've spent in DG2, all that comes to mind is the menus.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDefense Grid 2 is the sequel to the original one, which in my opinion was one of the best tower defense games.
For the new one I have mixed feelings. From one side the addiction is high, the strategy is there but as a sequel it plays way too safe.

Pros
+ Good strategy, excellent implementation of tower defence concept.
+ Great variety of towers, upgrades, boosts and special weapons.
+ Addiction as you keep trying one level after the other without stopping.
+ Nice sound and adequate graphics for such kind of game.
+ Many modes to play the campaign.
+ possibility of co-op.

Cons
- I feel that the game is more like an expansion to the original game rather than something new: repetition of towers,
enemies etc from the first game. In some points some old elements are missing (if I recall well there were flying enemies in the first one).
- Single campaign was rather short (20 missions in total) and very similar to the DS1. I would expect surprises in the form of new enemies and challenges but no...
- While promising as of 04/2020 I could not find a co-op match to play on-line.
- The commercial concept of having such a short campaign and asking money for extra maps is not ok.

All-in-all DS2 is a mixed bug. I would call it a disappointment given how much I enjoyed the first one. I had very high expectations but the realisation is rather underwhelming. For someone who has not played the first one the DS2 experience can be great. But someone who comes from DS1 will find little to no improvement here.
