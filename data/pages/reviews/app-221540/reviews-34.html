\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe basic premise of DG2 is pretty good, and the setup is mostly good... but I find I can't really recommend the game unless you're really into tower defenses and want to play virtually every one of them that exists.

The biggest problem in a tower defense game is having your towers do what you want. A variety of tower options usually gives the player some control over what to do and how they want to go about doing it. Due to a few factors coinciding, I found DG2 to be very lacking in this regard.

While I have no issues with tower defense games where you get fixed locations you can build, or where you can't build mazes very well, DG2 takes these to a bit of an extreme length, and then pairs it up with most towers having far too low of a range to be of any real value. Most of the specialist towers meant to be good at specific tasks were just flat out incapable of doing what they were supposed to do on most of the maps due to the limited placement of the possible build sites. Mazing doesn't really help all that much, and there's no real method of bunching up enemies intentionally together to make them properly suitable for getting hit with AoE. Even the swarm levels where you're supposed to use AoE don't really work right because the enemies spawn in single file one after another, usually at a distance too far apart for them to ever overlap close enough for AoE to hit them.

As such, what I found was that a frustrating amount of the time my towers were shooting at the wrong targets, only able to hit for so short of a period of time that they were practically useless, or flat out bad at their assigned role. For almost the entire campaign, you're simply better off having long range AoE towers against big, single targets, than actual single-target specialized towers. The support towers and randomized upgrades were a nuisance as well, as there's no real way to know if you'll get the upgrades you need to actually make a tower useful before you get to a level which requires such.

Another issue I had was how the cores worked - not a bad idea in theory, but it basically meant whether you won a level with no losses or lost the level entirely, came down pretty much to luck and timing you couldn't really predict. Basically you have items the enemies run over to, pick up, and then carry off. If you kill the enemy carrying one of these items, it slowly travels back to base. If another enemy bumps into it on its way back, that enemy picks it up as well.

Unfortunately, this means that sometimes a group will run right past a tough enemy, you'll kill the big scary one and its core hovers back to base without issue. Other times you kill a big enemy, and just as you do, another one runs right up behind it to pick up the item and runs off with it at full life because your towers decided to ignore that one guy. There's zero prioritization for targeting the enemies with these vital items, so you can facepalm as you watch every enemy in a group get taken out except the one carrying the item and the one right behind it at full life. Building more towers doesn't really help in alleviating this issue.

Sooo yeah. The overall concept isn't bad, but sadly the implementation leaves a lot to be desired. If you're really into tower defense games, you'll probably still find it fun, but frustrating in some ways due to a lack of capacity to make your towers do what you want them to. This isn't an issue with the game being "hard" but rather a lack of control paired with iffy level design, oddly timed and spaced waves, and bad AI that stack up to create frustrating situations. If you haven't played a tower defense game before, this is a horrible choice for the first one to begin with, because it suffers from a lot of issues that would probably turn someone off from the genre. I wouldn't call it a BAD game, it's one of those "a neutral rating or one with a qualifier would be nice" games that I don't feel bad for having bought and I did have fun with, but... I can't really recommend it due to some seriously fundamental flaws in the game where your solutions on how to solve problems don't really matter, and there's no way to know in advance how to solve certain problems that will show up on a level, and sometimes it literally just comes down to moving a single tower over one space will completely alter everything, and there's no reason to think such would happen, so it's kind of blind trial-and-error sometimes.

Anyway, not an awful game, grab it if it's on sale and you're okay with what has been mentioned, but keep in mind that most of the difficulty of the game stems not from it presenting an actual challenging opponent to fight, but from the player being blindfolded with one arm tied behind their back and being force-fed hallucinogenics then having to play russian roulette to see if something works or not, leading sometimes to 20-30 minute delays before a problem shows up at all and potentially having to start over because of such. It's not challenging in a "that was a tough thing to beat!" sort of way, but in a "WTF was that supposed to be?" sort of way.\t\t\t\t\t
