\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDropped in a time where your only real ways to spend influence were foreign territory claims or building habitats/megastructures, Federations adds it's namesake and the Galactic Community as alternative ways to spend influence, as well as some additional diplomacy to the game. While largely a success, time hasn't been kind to all elements of this DLC, as newer additions directly compete for the spotlight and the player's influence, Overlord's vassalization contracts chief among them.

Federations:

Base Overview - In times past, defensive alliances were your primary way to circumvent being eaten by a larger empire. Federations act as a natural progressor to this end, giving more efficient influence costs from multiple alliances and add additional bonuses depending on federation type and cohesion/federation level. For the most part, these tend to come in the form of small bonuses that serve to augment the base abilities of your federation, but some of the later ones can be decently powerful

With that said, diplomacy is a fickle thing, and Stellaris is well known for having schizophrenic AI who's loyalty flips at the drop of a pin. A "-1000, will not associate with" diplomatic acceptance modifier for empires they dislike ensures that most alliances stay small, and with the addition of Overlords, the AI now seems wholly intolerant of others it doesn't like and will immediately leave a federation if a disagreeable party ends up joining, instead of tolerating them as in previous versions.

Mechanics:

Cohesion & Level - Add diplomats to increase federation cohesion & monthly XP, enough lets you level up and gain new perks after a while. Levelling is VERY slow and going from level 1 to 5 takes roughly 150 - 200 years. Adding new members resets your current XP. AI pretty much never adds more than 1 diplomat and sometimes they put in none

Laws - Mostly apply to who can join & how much diplomatic weight each member gets. Some political dogfighting concerning how long the leader can rule for, and who gets chosen next, are elements that I quite like. Many of the options for doing so are unique too, like having gladiatorial battles between leaders, empire-wide psionic battles, bidding wars, or intellectual debates between your federation's greatest scientific minds. That said, Overlords DLC gives you the option to restrict vassal voting rights towards your own, which makes becoming an overwhelming force not too difficult. Also there's not a lot of policies regarding actual governance/rules... for example you can't say be the Covenant and enforce a policy banning the use of machines, or an egalitarian-focused federation banning slavery. Most of us were hoping that federations would be a more concentrated, focused version of the GC (or potential alternative to it), but the laws ended up being pretty shallow or non-existent

Fleets - In theory, having an upkeep-less fleet that the AI can add to sounds awesome. In practice you'll probably have a random swarm of corvettes & destroyers that taxes your own capacity WAY more than what you get out of it. Fleet caps out at 600 TOTAL capacity, despite the collective tax easily exceeding that

Federation Types:

Galactic Union - Extremely limited usefulness beyond aforementioned defensive purposes, +1 leadership influence and the extra damage to crisis fleets. You can already begin to recognize the outdated elements here; +2 unity per federation envoy is beyond useless. Now if it was say... +2% monthly unity generation per envoy, then yeah I could see maybe see gaining 20% - 30% extra unity from a galaxy-wide federation to be worthwhile. As it is, most elements are extremely outdated

Martial Alliance - Not much better. End result is +1 leadership influence, crisis damage, and what... maybe +16% fleet damage when all's said and done?

Research Cooperative - I consider giving AI your tech to be a death sentence. I've been playing for approx. 3000 hours at this point and can tell you that I've lost games from AI mooching off my tech and then backstabbing me with it afterwards. Your mileage may vary. That said, the increases to Scholarium tech output (& taxation) and the possibility of pulling weak AI out of Protectorate status and into a more taxable specialist form are worth considering

Hegemony - Only one of two federation types I'd actually consider worth using. +15% vassal resource output is nothing to sneeze at, nor is the ability to tax subject fleet capacity without adding to it yourself and having AI not ragequit from adding new members

Trade League - I hated playing megacorps before discovering this federation type, now they're one of my favourite playstyles. The bonuses from this feed into itself & make everyone stronger. Fed Trade stance gives you energy credits, consumer goods & unity all at once. Automatic trade agreements augment this with no downsides whatsoever. The AI gets economically stronger which gives you more trade and strengthens your taxes. If you're a megacorp than you can put down branch offices on their planets to get even more EC, then buildings which add clerk/merchant jobs to boost it more. If you're an overlord than your taxes get stronger, and there's holdings that you can use to further tax their resources and convert them towards your ethics. You can see where this all going, every element feeds into self and makes everyone exponentially stronger as a result

Galactic Community:

Space UN, rife with filibustering and Reaper denial memes. Lo and behold, Overlord DLC turned this from your primary influence dump to a close runner up behind claims & building habitats. There's a lot you can do with this and I REALLY like the idea of politically sabotaging your enemies wherever possible. In practice this rarely comes up, and the AI tends to leave useless resolutions up for decades/centuries, especially galactic council members who are the only other ones capable of proposing the more interesting resolution tiers

Regarding the resolutions themselves, some are strong while others are horribly outdated or poorly balanced. In general most resolutions tend to be at least mostly usable up to level 3, beyond that many devolve onto hyper-focused nonsense that detracts more than it gives. A few examples:
-Tier 4 Unchained Knowledge gives +10% scientist output, but -15% pop happiness translates to -9% stability & resource output for everything else
-Tier 5 Galactic Commerce gives +10% output, but -25% pop happiness translating to -15% stability & -5% overall production net loss
-Tier 5 Ecological Protection, at absolute peak usefulness, gives roughly +13.5% production, at the cost of -30% alloys, -50% minerals & +100% terraforming costs (which typically nullify part of that production bonus gained from habitability)

The list goes on, but in short, not all resolutions are created equal and some fall off in the later stages, or are too situational to offset their downsides.

Additionally, and I'll say it again: Overlord changed the entire dynamic of the game and made elements of this DLC less appealing. Vassal contract influence now directly competes for GC resolution influence, and if you've subjugated a large portion of the galaxy, it's no longer worth voting on sanctions towards those in violation of galactic law, as most of them functionally just nerf your own taxation output.

Origins:

A really great update to help diversity empires. Note that you don't need Federations to unlock origins, but virtually all of them are locked behind some sort of DLC. Remnants, Common Ground/Hegemony, Doomsday, On the Shoulders of Giants, Scion, Shattered Ring, and Void Dwellers are locked behind this one specifically.

Overall, Federations was stronger when it came out, but some aspects got slowly eclipsed by other content as time went on, Overlord chief among them. Certain elements are out of date and could use a touch up. I do recommend it though, if nothing else to make the GC actually usable.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFederations contains interesting ideas and systems, but it is unfortunately let down by poorly balanced AI, MegaCorp's continuous impact on the game post 2.1.3, and overall pacing of the game itself.

AI even in 2.1.3 and prior was struggling in Stellaris. Since vanilla, the AI appears to poorly understand many of the new systems, and seems to generally struggle to form much of a logic path for it to follow in any manner that might be consistent with what ought to be an ever changing galaxy. The AI to the best of my knowledge seems to holdfast to its original beliefs, government, and alignment, regardless of the changes in time or events it might encounter. Making it feel stagnate as the player themselves go through many changes while exploring the galaxy past their humble origins. As new systems have been added, the AI has struggled to keep up under these new logic paths to follow, meandering as it tries to use the tools that the player now has available to them. A great reference to this is also a piece of gameplay advice, never use the ship auto-upgrade system unless its a direct upgrade to a component, such as a reactor or X-ray I to X-ray II. The system AI that the game uses to make micromanaging easier like that of auto-upgrade, will consider any new technology, even one centuries outdated, to be superior to the components currently equipped. The systems AI lacks even the ability to look at DPS, and make the decision that 2 > 1. So when you then expect AI's taking the role as other players to make intelligent decisions, its easy to see why it falls so short.

MegaCorp's filthy lingering poison is still felt since its release, I cover its impact more broadly in my overall review of the base game. Suffice to say, that due to MegaCorps systems still being kept in the game since its release, DLC's like Federations suffer, as without MegaCorp, this DLC while not great... would be more acceptable as an addition to the best version of the game: 2.1.3.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA questionably useful DLC even on discount, the only reason I felt that I had to buy this was due to the base game federation mechanic preventing you from declaring any war.

Once the federation members reach 3 or more, it is near impossible to get an unanimous vote for anything. This results in a federation never starting a war even when the opportunity is sitting in front of it. I was about to give up on this game entirely, but wanted to see if there was a way to salvage the game for me.

I was recommended this DLC by other players since it'll allow other options such as "majority vote" or "president decides" on declaring war as a federation member. This allows federations to actually start a war despite terrible AIs never being able to reach an agreement on a collective action. You have to keep in mind that there are no Mods that can replace this function, as Paradox would not be as silly to let Mods compete with DLCs that they intend on selling.

Thankfully this DLC worked as intended and I was able to declare war as a federation member. I guess it is also nice that the DLC comes with the Juggernaut and other megastructures, but those were not as important to me as the extended federation diplomacy options were.

However, my impression with this was that the Paradox introduced a problem to the base game (ie. incomplete federation function) so that they could sell the solution (ie. Federation DLC) to the players like me. I had to buy this DLC to salvage the game and I have to say that I'm not happy that I had to spend more money to keep playing the game I enjoy.

If you are having issue with the incomplete federation mechanic that made its way into the base game, I would reluctantly suggest this DLC to you as well. However, my gut feeling is that Paradox highballs the prices on DLCs knowing that the players would prefer to buy them on discount. Even on sale, this DLCs is barely worth it, and so I would have to not recommend this DLCs unless you feel absolutely forced to buy this like I did.\t\t\t\t\t
