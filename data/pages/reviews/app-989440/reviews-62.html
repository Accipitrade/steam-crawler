\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPhantom Abyss could only really be described as a sort of shared experience Platforming 'Race' Roguelike, and if that confuses the pants off of you I'll agree, but I don't think there's a better way to put it.

See, Phantom Abyss is about getting to the end of a procedurally generated, trap riddled temple to gather relics so that you can be freed from the main temple that is holding you prisoner (from what I could pick up from the temple statue npc). But the kicker is that once one player (1, singular, solo, uno, nobody else) completes a run, that combination of rooms closes permanently.

"But you said it's a roguelike?" Yes, yes I did. See each path you go through is procedurally generated, but not so much from a massive pool of rooms that are randomized so every single run is entirely different, but rather the game generates runs and those runs are played by multiple people at once. Meaning that there's a huge variety in the different runs you can have, but technically you're having them alongside people having the same run, if that makes sense?
Where this deepens is that you only have a single run to complete each specific challenge, and the game has a souls-esque ghost system where you can see the paths and mistakes the other players made during their own failed attempts, giving you clues as to danger as well as advice as to how best approach a situation. In addition to this, the curses of certain whips you can take actually interact with these phantoms at times (one makes it so that the phantoms steal any treasure they pass, rather than just leaving it there for you) meaning that some runs become harder the worse people have done on a run if you use certain whips, whereas other temples you're the first to go into or are the fastest to complete are made much easier.

That is where the 'race' part comes in, since obviously on top of the pre-mentioned player skill dictated time limit to completing the temple, its not always about being the fastest. Being the first to take a swinging hammer made of rock in the teeth isn't an accomplishment, after all. Taking your time to collect treasure and carefully take the safest path can add up in the long run due to powerful and useful blessings you can acquire from temple shrines littered throughout the start and end of segments. Blessings ranging from a simple 'heal 1/3 hearts' to having a glide if you hold jump, or being able to literally slide over air.

It all comes together into a frantic mess that just feels hilarious to play and could easily be good fun when shared with friends.

It's not free from it's issues, but many of them are definitely linked with being so early in early access, so they will hopefully be ironed out over time:

-Not a ton of variety in the map pieces. I had done something like 6-7 runs at the time I made this review and despite so few runs I saw a LOT of repetition, and even though the traps placements varied, the chests didn't. Plus since a lot of the traps are as simple to avoid as 'grappel over' or 'wait and then go' the actual influence of those traps didn't change a lot, even if their locations did. This is definitely something that will fix itself as development continues I'm sure, so its a fairly minor complaint (especially since there's a heavy element of 'easy to learn, hard to master' that makes the repetition less obvious at times)

-Traps and Grappeling worked fine, unless they didn't. Weird way to say this, i know, but the only way I can really think to do so in so few words. The longer version is that while things work perfectly 95%, there are a frustrating 5% of the time that they wont. And this 5% matters, because that's the 5% that costs you the run. These exact issues are that despite the sheer amount of wild leaping, sliding, sprinting and jumping i do in a safe area showing me I can theoretically throw my grappel out any time as long as I haven't done it recently (indicated by the whip end being limp) there were a good few times when the grappel just didn't want to work when making a jump, like the attachment point had invisibly tried to connect to somewhere out of range, even if the cursor is definitely within range. Slightly delaying the grappel tends to help this issue, but thanks to the safe area testing I did, it doesn't make sense it should even happen.
As for the traps, they also worked 99 out of 100 times, but there were a couple of times that seemed odd. <Edited. Apparently Im a dumbass and just forgot you can whip the spinners to stop them>, and the other time (In this case more likely a bug) was when one of the switching platforms (the ones that alternate on and off) made me fall through it the instant it turned off, rather than giving a tiny bit of leeway to jump as it lowered.
These are both minor things that are probably likely to be fixed, even if they rarely happen, but the truth of the matter is that easily 80% of the health loss i sustained during my runs was that first issue. So it's a big deal.

-Performance takes a visible hit when there's a lot of ghosts on the screen. Not unplayable, but VERY noticeable. Definitely something that will be ironed out by the devs down the line.
EDIT: This can also get pretty bad with the poison bombs when they start dropping down 7-8 at a time.

-The friends system feels kinda unremarkable for someone who doesn't have anyone else playing the game, basically convincing me I have only one use of my whips a lot of the time.

-Map generation has little issues here and there, with traps and pots sometimes spawning inside a pillar here and there. Very few, though, so minor issue.

-Tiny issue, I don't blame the game itself for (so this is just a heads up for anyone like me) but the long bladed traps combined with the fairly wide FOV constantly made me misjudge how long the blades were. I quickly got used to it, but the first time it was like ??!?!?

-Another tiny request would be to move the sensitivity slider above the keybinds, since people are gonna be futzing with that the most when they first boot the game up, far more than the keybinds themselves.

-Last complaint is that while the fire guardian and the laser guardian are fine (assuming there's only three guardian types in the first area of the game) the poison bomb guardian seems fairly inconsistently powerful. Sometimes it'll be completely useless to accomplish anything (sometimes not even appearing inside the right room), and other times it'll suddenly pop into the room next to you and nuke you from full to dead before you can get out.
The other two feel like they fit a purpose, that one just feels like you're being punished for exploring.

As I said, all of these 'issues' are barely that, and the game itself as an absolute ton of potential, and I'm looking forward to seeing it's future.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAwesome game!

Indiana Jones meets CS:GO bunny hopping courses. One of those games that's easy to pick-up-and-play, but hard to master. Will take a lot of time and patience, especially to get to best relics in the game (which are much, much longer courses), but it is really worth it. Asynchronous multiplayer adds a great aspect to the game and really works in this setting (asynchronous games aren't always great). Being able to watch other players on your own runs can really help, and makes the game feel less lonely, and more alive. It also adds an element of player speciality, as running against 20 previous players who have all died running the temple you are currently in is a fantastic sensation of both pressure and excitement. Knowing that you might be the one to finally complete the temple all those have died on is awesome, and it really makes you, as a player, feel like the spotlight is always on you.

Love it so far; can't wait to see what else they do with it in future updates.\t\t\t\t\t
