\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"Once the legendary relic at the bottom of each temple is found, the temple goes away forever and becomes the priceless trophy of the skilled adventurer."

This is taken straight from the store page, and based on how you like to play it may effectively be untrue.

- If you want to play like EA players have up until the Adventure Update, this remains true. In which case your temple will always be a sequence of 3 floors in a biome, after which you have a choice to dip out and take a relic or keep going into the next, harder biome.

- If on the contrary you want more variety in your temple, and progress the story, and climb through the ranks to get harder temple, completing temples does not close them. So much for having a unique, theme-appropriate twist for your game, huh?

But ultimately, the reason I cannot recommend the game following the Adventure Update is due to the diminishing amount of meaningful player choice, and a difficulty that fails to appropriately keep up with increasing player skill.

- Before the update, completing a run would bless your whip, removing the negative effect (so you would only have the beneficial effect with no downsides). After the update, you can't get rid of the negative effect, and blessing the whip merely unlocks a skin for it, that was already available through level-ups.

- Before the update, losing a run would make you lose your whip, so you had to pay some in-game currency or wait for/ask a player to run the temple successfully to give it back to you. After the update, you can never lose your whip. So... pick the whip you like best and forget the others ever existed, since you can get their blessings with no downside mid-run anyway!

- Since the initial release, the game has been severely lacking in passive blessings variety, and there are a handful of obviously superior choices. Once you get good enough to reliably clear temples, you can expect to be able to reproduce the same build by the end of every run, perhaps with a variance of one or two blessings. After the update, they added a different kind of shrine that lets you use a different currency to buy whip blessings (without their curse) for your run. But since this uses a separate currency that is barely used for anything else, it becomes a no-brainer to check the shrine and buy the objectively better blessing.

- Before the update, the game had no means whatsoever to adapt to the rising player skill. With the adventure update, you can now play adventure mode enough to get to a rank that feels pretty good in difficulty, and then past it into the final two ranks where the game just fills each room with legions of respawning floating homing heads, and more trap density. There have been a few occasions where traps were so dense I'm not certain that damage was avoidable, especially if you factor in the guardian that's keeping the pressure on. So basically, you're better off playing the easier but consistently reasonable Classic Mode once you're done with the story.

- If you're like me and tire of the overwhelmingly tedious Adventure mode, once you return to Classic Mode you'll realize the game is actually so easy that you can reliably speedrun through it without buy many/any blessings whatsoever. It's not like taking the time to buy these blessings added much run variety anyway, so why bother? This then has the domino effect of eliminating the value of gold, which has no use outside of runs, and keys, which are only used to buy whip blessings and 1 of each type to unlock the next biome (and only in Classic Mode, I believe). So you end up with a game where every run plays out the same, offers no difficulty and no incentive to play. Not something that should happen this quickly in a roguelike.

- And finally, since the initial release, players have been able to purchase an infinite amount of blessings from standard shrines, provided they have enough gold to buy the next blessing. And since you have to pay before seeing the blessings, unlike the whip shrines, this creates a situation where you have no reason to check shrines you don't have gold for, and you have no reason to skip shrines you do have gold for. The most baffling thing to me is that since initial release, there is simultaneously always a shrine at the end of every floor, _and_ a shrine at the start of most floors, and sometimes even a secret chamber with a shrine inside. But these latter two are never used, because you can spend all your gold at the guaranteed one at the end of every level.

To me, this is the clearest sign that the devs have not always enabled you to buy more than one blessing per shrine, and eventually reverted that and failed to adapt the game consequently. It's a sign that they don't know what's best for the game, nor do they know how to properly adapt the game to their decisions. And that is why I can't recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOkay, so I know it doesn't look like I've really played Phantom Abyss, but I have, just on Xbox. Whenever I saw that my linked Steam account did not link save files, I returned the game. I had Phantom Abyss with the PC Game Pass (which I canceled, but I still want Phantom Abyss), so I'll just buy it there. Anyway, Phantom Abyss is a really fun game with neat mechanics and a nice, Indiana-Jones-nostalgic vibe. Reviews are ranting about various problems with the whip and difficulty spike, which are real problems and should be addressed, but Phantom Abyss is overall great. The Guardians, well, I'll just say they need patches. The levels (i.e. temples) are AWESOME and are really fun, spare one room I don't understand. The whips are cool, but most of their drawbacks are pretty extreme, so I just stick to the long fall whip and the normal one. The story is, well, I dunno, I haven't finished it! The graphics are really pretty decent for an indie title, and all of the movement mechanics are fun to use, however not particularly smooth.
I guess my final word is, "Get the game!".\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a case of Early Access done wrong not because the devs have been inactive, but because they have no clue what kind of game they want to make.

At first, the idea was that each temple would only be cleared once, and then it would be sealed forever. Now it's only the case for the third, deepest stage, which is kinda awkward and inconsistent, and it causes logical problems along the way, but whatever. Each player who explored the same temple before you, and died (or cleared stage 1 or 2, because, well, what did I just say?), left their soul inside, and it manifests as a phantom, kinda like a ghost car in racing games, and it politely helps you with traps, buttons, secrets and chests. Except, if both of you open the same chest at about the same time, you have to stand in front of it, watching paint dry for ten seconds, and for the longest part of early access this was the phantoms' main contribution to the game. And it still hasn't been fixed, 1.5y later.

They later made it so that you started each run with 1 heart (OHKO), and could increase your health pool by collecting other players' corpses. That was a pretty great idea—now having a more crowded temple was an advantage rather than a detriment, and it made the async co-op multiplayer aspect very real. You may or may not wonder, why didn't players kill themselves immediately and reroll whenever the temple was relatively empty? Because dying caused you to lose your currently equipped whip, with its unique perk, and you would only regain it once another player "released your soul" by clearing the same temple (which meant more async co-op, usually with strangers, which was pretty cool). How that worked with the new temples that could be cleared by multiple people, I have no clue, but it was a good system. Back when it existed. Since the latest update you always have access to all whips, and they also can't be upgraded, so I just found myself using my favorite whip all the time (there was no point not to). And—naturally—this means each temple now includes at least one person who jumped off the cliff in the first room, because there is absolutely no point in attempting a ~30-minute run with only one or two hearts. This state of affairs remains to this day and it's likely here to stay, because the game mode has been shoved aside in favor of the sparkly Adventure Mode.

Adventure Mode abandons the concept of a descending three-stage temple. Instead, each player has a rank, which I believe can only go up, and you get a very short, messy temple based on your MMR. This temple has usually already been cleared by a dozen other players, so there is no sealing and no releasing, and overall little satisfaction from winning the game. The proper gamemode, now dubbed Classic Mode, doesn't give any MMR, so even if you have completed the proper game 100 times, you will have to start over playing tutorial temples. And corpses give you MMR instead of hearts, so they're, again, not so impactful anymore. The mode feels like a betrayal of what the game was originally supposed to be, and it's clearly an attempt to appease casual players, which by itself would be a good thing—but doing it by splitting the playerbase and development attention, and immediately hurting Classic Mode, is just not a great way of doing it.

The game keeps being reworked, but it has some core problems that have never been addressed, and that includes the insanely annoying problem with treasure chests. There are three dongheads, or guardians as the game calls them, who follow you for roughly the latter half of each floor (only one per temple), and they haven't been touched since the oldest EA version. "Ranged" guardians are more likely to harm you via contact damage, when they spawn in your face in a tight corridor/when you're mid-air, and the melee guardian effectively does nothing aside from perhaps deterring you from taking bonus rooms in later stages, and it can also hurt you if you respawn in its face.

The only reward for clearing a temple is a relic, and the goal is to collect them all, but the relic is predetermined in each temple, so you're very likely to keep finding duplicates, and if you get a duplicate your 20-30 minute run is essentially wasted. Room variety is pretty terrible, and the concept honestly gets old very fast with the way the level generator works right now, and it feels like there has been hardly any meaningful content added since launch (stupid story bits are not meaningful). Admittedly, making procedural worlds in 3D is a challenge, and combined with their idea of linear levels with huge rooms and no biome variety really does result in each temple feeling same-ish.
