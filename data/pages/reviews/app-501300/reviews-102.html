\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHaving seen exceptionally positive reviews for What Remains of Edith Finch and being a casual fan of the walking simulator genre (i.e. A Short Hike, Firewatch, The Stanley Parable) I was intrigued. While the game has a fantastic art direction and seamlessly stylizes key aspects of the narrative What Remains of Edith Finch not only fails to deliver a meaningful or comprehensible story but presents a story seemingly intending to insult or push the boundaries of sophomoric writing.

Story
The story of What Remains of Edith Finch often tries to strike a balance between taking itself seriously, humor, and child-like wonder to only fail to find a cohesive identity. Instead, the blending of each forcibly jars the player between a wildly insensitive tone and laughably poor writing leaving the player confused as to which of the two the writers preferred more.

When What Remains of Edith Finch does attempt to address serious subjects such as mental illness, child neglect and PTSD it often does so with an overly whimsical and out of touch approach that comes across as inappropriate.
  • During Lewis' story we are gradually introduced to a progressive understanding that the disappearance of his brother Milton led to his mental instability and substance abuse. Instead of having any level of commentary regarding these issues the narrative concludes with a "Yeah he was quirky and had a great imagination." Worse is, at no point is there any reflection into the abuse by Dawn (i.e. verbal abuse, physical isolation from the rest of his family) which had a direct impact to his emotional isolation, detachment from reality and his eventual death. Instead the writers opted for something significantly more out of touch like as a quick comment along the lines of "Ha Ha that is sooo mom!"

  • The story of Gregory is absolutely the most tragic and definitively the most unforgiving of the writers to brush off in the laziest of ways. Covering the most serious of subjects, child neglect and infant death I find it hard to imagine any writer could believe it to be a good idea to introduce these subjects while simultaneously ignoring the harsh reality of them. Worse is that the writers didn't just ignore the harsh subject matter but they in fact brushed it aside with the simplest of resolutions. Quite literally the only comments the writers had for this story line was "Well that happened. I forgive you"; no trauma, no coming to grips with the loss of their child. This was profoundly inappropriate and astonishing to find in such a highly acclaimed game.

Thankfully instead of dedicating the entire story to excessively insulting writing, the writers made time for amateur story telling as well.
  • A particularly underwhelming and confusing character is Calvin. Not only do they not have any dialogue but the motive for their actions seem unintentionally vague. For the brief moment his is seen he is given a number of possible motives for his actions. Is he doing it because he didn't want to eat mushrooms and his mom had just called him to dinner? Is he doing it because his brother said it couldn't be done? Is he doing it because that was the moment he wanted to try flying? Certainly any of these could be true, but why introduce the extraneous possible motives? Should I be feeling sympathy for Sam given this is the moment that defines him for the rest of his life? Should I be enjoying the comedic nature of Calvin launching himself 200+ meters into the water possibly only because he hates mushrooms? It certainly seems as though the developers want me to enjoy this moment given the achievement Loop-de-loop-de-loop. The writing of this character provides no clear context as to what the player should be thinking and feeling.

  • Milton's entire story line is "He painted. He disappeared." There is quite literally no other context or information regarding Milton. Considering the impact Milton's disappearance has on other characters (i.e. Lewis, Edith), it's surprising to find no depth was given to this story line. If the writers had done so, there would have been all the more reason to be drawn into a better understanding of Lewis' story line. Additionally, this would have allowed the player to better connect to the protagonist's trauma and understanding of the "Finch family curse". To lazily gloss over a character with such important interweaving story lines shows only a lack of effort on the writer's part.

Presentation
What Remains of Edith Finch's only saving grace is in the presentation. The entire game would be without merit if it weren't for some phenomenal atmosphere building and charming art style. The setting of Orcas Island is inherently picturesque. The Finch Estate is reminiscent of childhood fairy tales of which I particularly thought of the films Matilda and Pan's Labyrinth. Having these combined creates an exceptionally unique atmosphere fostering a sense of curiosity in the player.

The most impressive aspect of the presentation comes in as the story unfolds. Although I take serious issue with the narrative, the manner in which it is presented is exceptional. While progressing, the player is introduced to a continually unique take on the personality and story for each of the characters. These scenes and details reminded me how powerful visual aides are in compelling a story. More games could learn from the way What Remains of Edith Finch presents their environments and scenes.
  • Gus' poem wrapped around his kite string.
  • Barbara's comic book adventure.
  • Lewis' fantastical world that he creates which slowly blends into his reality.
  • The hand drawn family tree to be filled out as you learn each of their stories.

Conclusion
What Remains of Edith Finch has some of the best and worst content I have seen in a video game. On one hand the presentation is something to be admired and applauded. It truly is a standard that other games should aspire to.

On the other, the story and writing should be condemned. The story is an insult to narrative writing and to anyone at all remotely related to the subject matter touched on by the game. For a game that poses as having depth there is surprisingly little.\t\t\t\t\t
