\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL;DR - A Series of Unfortunate Events meets Gone Home

What Remains of Edith Finch was an interesting game. It tells a series of short stories or rather one large, connected story about the family of the titular character and all their woes.

I can't say that I found its story emotionally moving, but it did captivate me for its 2 hour runtime. While not every story is equally as interesting, they all tie together into a cohesive and engaging narrative.

This is very much a "Games Can Be Art" experience, and I think it succeeds as a testament to that. What Remains of Edith Finch feels more like a heartfelt indie film than a game. But, I don't think that's a weakness of the game. If anything its a strength.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a beautiful game, both visually and narratively. Through exploring a huge dilapidated house the game introduces you to each member of the Finch family. The environments within the house as well as the different game mechanics had the power to transform names in a family tree into complete characters. By the end of the game I felt like I really understood the personality of each member of the Finch family. The story is emotional, it's heartbreaking, but has the ability to treat events usually viewed as gruesome and tragic with love. Although I ran into a few glitches (most likely due to my computer) while playing, the game still left an impact on me even after I had finished.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a masterpiece. Plain and simple. I usually avoid narrative-driven games but the trailers for the game just looked too interesting. And I knew from some of the eccentricities I saw that it was either going to meld beautifully or be a disaster. Luckily it is the former.

I also think that we are finally entering into a new era where narrative video games finally become a respectable experience that can compete with good (and some times great) literature. Firewatch got us close. And I think Edith Finch gets us across that line. I can say with confidence that this can be this generation's equivalent of Faulkner's "As I Lay Dying". It's been a while since we have had a good solid competitor to a tried and true medium like literature. While cinema can do well with the narrative-driven scripts the 3d environment allows the imagination to be in charge while the game is the navigator.

It helps when everything is done right too. And Edith Finch does everything right. Right down to sound design.

Welcome to now; the time in history when there was finally justification for 3d interactive stories to compete directly with literature.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA hauntingly beautiful collection of short stories about life and death bound together in a videogame that uses the act of exploring a physical place as a way to link the stories. Similar to Gone Home with more depth (story goes back over 100 years, examines more characters, and the house is truly massive)

The voice acting, visuals, writing, and sound design are brilliant.

While the story pacing doesn't always work with the exploration mechanic (sometimes you get a flurry of stories and sometimes you get a long walk with little narrative depending on how you progress through the house) the overarching story and the storybook visual and narrative elements, coupled with the varying perspectives, makes this usually work out if the player takes some time to "meditate" on the house during exploration (particularly the eary bits) to even out the pacing. Worth noting that, while it does have moments that drag on a bit too long, this game never feels like it's slowing things down artificially (to extend play time) or that these are the product of hastily throwing nonlinear elements into a linear game. I have played many walking simulators that do that, even ones I really like, and Edith Finch does not fall into that trap; these are minor pacing issues not major annoyances.

A compelling and emotional game that I strongly reccomend to anyone who likes the narrative possibilities of story games. Parts of it will make you grin like a little kid reading their favorite book, parts will make you cry, on the whole, the experience is amazing and one I cannot recommend highly enough to fans of the genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWow oh wow. I heard from a few people that I trust is What Remains of Edith Finch is excellent. And everyone called it a walking simulator but great.

Then I heard it was made by the same people who made unfinished swan and I was curious. And finally I could get it in a bundle with Gorogoa for a few bucks more. I pulled the trigger.

I'm glad I did, Edith Finch is like a masterworks in how to make a walking simulator. However the entire game is so well made that I don't want to take away from it by discussing anything it does, in fact I recommend playing this as blind as you can, maybe watch a youtube video of the first five minutes just to see how well it's done and then play the entire thing blind.

Sadly this will take you only a couple hours, and that's one of the big issues I have with the game. It's short, and that's common for walking simulators. There's only a few that really have lengthy stories or tons of replay, the only I can think of right now is Stanley Parable. Sadly this is not what you will get from Edith Finch. This is a one playthrough and you're done deal. But you should enjoy it.

In fact I think that's a strong point. I want more. I don't just want to finish a piece, I want double the content, because I enjoy it so much. I didn't even finish Everybody's gone to rapture I remember thinking I could go get all the collectibles, I could just finish the story, or I could just go do something else and chose the last option because I didn't want to play more.

Edith Finch instead makes me sad the story is over, happy for the overall story, but wanting to see more. I want to see this developer do more, because they just make amazing little art games that really play with your senses.

On the other hand I have a little issue with the story, the story seems to promise you something of type A and delivers something else, again it's done extremely well but a few lines of dialog have me a little annoyed because there's quite a few lingering questions I had, but I feel at least one of the main threads of the game feel left open. It's done likely on purpose, and I'll accept that, but man, for a story that does all sorts of novel things with story telling, I just wanted a perfect story, and that's not here. It's a very well done story, it's a great "walking simulator' and it's a fantastic experience, but it falls short of the perfect game.

Yet because of how great and unique the game is, the fact that I won't even hint at the story lines, or what my complaints are, the fact that I played this in about two to three hours straight, and the fact that I adore every minute makes me have to recommend it. 20 bucks is a bit much but getting it on sale, and especially get it in the bundle (Gorogoa is a fantastic and unique game as well) would make this an easy recommendation.

If you hate walking simulators, I was there, and I'll say this and Stanley Parable seems to be the exception but as great as this game is, it is a walking simulator. There's only the very hint of a puzzle (nothing worth mentioning) limited interactivity, and no real "gameplay" but the experience of the game has made me a believe that walking simulators can be so much more than I've seen in the past, and that's a great feeling.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWRoEF is a game of exploration where the goal is to learn the story of an ostensibly cursed family. If you don't like walking simulators, then this game won't be for you; if you do like them, then I highly recommend this game. Similar to Gone Home, but with a more surreal and supernatural feel.

+ Beautiful graphics
+ The house is well-crafted and feels both lived in and fantastical
+ Each of the characters' stories has a distinct style and mechanics
+ The story as a whole is captivating and haunting

- Takes the term "walking simulator" a little too literally-- if there's a run button I never discovered it, so it takes time to move around. This might be on purpose, to make you pay more attention to the environment.
- Assets are noticeably re-used throughout the house
