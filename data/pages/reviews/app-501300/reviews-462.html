\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet the feels come to you.

The studio behind such classic games as The Unfinished Swan on PS3 returns for a game which won over the hearts of gamers and critics alike in 2017. What remains of Edith Finch was announced many years ago, and having played the final product myself, it's safe to say that the game is every bit of good as they say.

What remains of Edith Finch is a walking simulator which tells the tale of Edith, the last surviving member of the Finch family. The roughly 2,5-3 hour experience begins with Edith travelling to the Finch family home in Washinghton, where amidst woods towers a curious-looking, multi-floored Finch house. Once inside, Edith must search the house for clues which give hints about the personal stories and fates of her late family members. The game does admirable job of breaking the pace with simple yet engaging mini-games which serve as a way to assume the dead family member's point of view. These moments include some truly standout scenes, like a scene where a small girl imagines being a cat while jumping in rooftops and treetops only to moments later change into a scene of becoming an owl in prowl for prey.

The devastating and heartwarming moments of What remains of Edith Finch are powerful and stay with you long after the credits have rolled, but only if you are willing to invest yourself into the game. Walking simulators are a polarizing topic in the world of video games; with typically limited gameplay options and short length. What remains of Edith Finch breaks the mold of its predecessors with some clever game design and atmosphere. The moody ambient music and etheric, dream-like landscapes fully support the player's investment into the story. There is no sprint button here, either. Technical performance is excellent, same goes to sound design.

Some points of contention remain, however. The game's original asking price on Steam is 20 € which is a lot to ask for a 2-3 hour experience with limited replayability. When compared to some other walking simulators on the market, What remains of Edith Finch stands perhaps higher above all others. If you let yourself become fully immersed into the storyline and like to take your time with it, the game is well-worth the full asking price since you could always go back and revisit it again. For me, personally, this game could serve as one of those games which I replayed for example once a year.

For a frame of reference, I have played my fair share of walking simulators (Dear Esther, Beginner's Guide, Vanishing of Ethan Carter, Stanley Parable). What remains of Edith Finch, however, is a special breed. If you have never tried, or liked, these types of games. Perhaps you could give this an honest try. Maybe during a sale.

Pros:
+ A masterclass example of a good walking simulator: ambient, etheric, mystery-filled journey into the fates of the Finch family members
+ The feels

Cons:
- 20 € is still a lot to ask for a 2-3 hour experience\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA short and sweet game that had me feeling all types of emotions in its 2 hour runtime. Play this on a quiet evening in for a relaxed and touching experience.

GAMEPLAY
: If you're familiar with so-called "walking simulators", you'll know how this goes. You navigate the Finch house and the surrounding area in a first-person perspective, only able to zoom in to examine, move the mouse to look around, and manipulate a few objects. You will discover pieces of writing that have you take control of the people that wrote them, in the form of a sort of mini-game. These are widely varied, sometimes realistic, and sometimes fantastical. There aren't any fail states, so the player can take them at their own pace. Ultimately, you'll need to ask yourself if you're in the market for a much more relaxed experience if you're considering this one.

VISUALS:
This is one of the most wonderfully detailed games I've seen. There's minimal reuse of assets, aside from the books on the shelves, but there are so many of them that it's understandable. The whole house is richly decorated, with each room having its own distinctive feel - especially the many bedrooms, which relfect the personalities of their occupants. At times colourful, at times dark and murky, there's a lot on display here, with each story having its own visual flair - the surreal, dream-like experience of a baby, to a B-horror comic book, to a family vacation told through a series of pictures. The character models, when seen from a third-person perspective, can look a bit robotic at times, but they're brief and infrequent enough that it's not particularly jarring.

AUDIO:
The game relies heavily on voice acting to convey its story, so much attention has been afforded to it, and every character in the game is voiced splendidly. They all come across with their own personalities and emote believably when necessary - even the child characters, which can be especially hit or miss in other games. Otherwise, the game doesn't make much use of environmental sound effects or music, so it's overall a fairly subdued experience.

NARRATIVE:
You play as a girl returning to her abandoned childhood home to find out the truth about a curse that has afflicted her family for generations. It's not so much as a story as a series of character studies - as you play, you will see how the curse has affected a multitude of family members differently, culminating in the experience of the titular character. It's a fascinating journey, seeing the lives of different family members in different ways. And this is where it all comes together - the top-notch voice acting and detailed visuals all serve to characterize them, and thus tell their stories.

Overall, I would recommend this game wholeheartedly. If you've enjoyed titles such as The Vanishing of Ethan Carter or Nevermind, you'll be right at home here. Some may be put off by the price tag - $22 for a 2-3 hour experience. And at this point, you need to ask yourself what you want out of a game like this - something to pass the time, or a cohesive, well-paced experience. Over a week later, I can still remember every portion of the game, and the layout of the house - something I can't say about other games, except for those I've played many times before. In this case, I would say its brevity is one of its strengths.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tValued at 2.5 Dear Esthers, maybe rough parity with Gone Home.

In the ever increasing mystery house inspection genre, this is one of the best. The player character explores their overly renovated childhood home andexperiences the life stories of her colorful family. Although the game controls player activity more tightly than Gone Home, the way each 'family member' segment plays out - from exploring their part of the house to experiencing their last moments at the house - keep you searching for more information about them and the house.

Although it generally pushes players in a certain direction it can be easy to get lost in certain areas. Each of the stories play with different mechanics and narrative styles, but their freshness is a bit soured on returning to walking slowly around rooms looking for hidden passages.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhile What Remains of Edith Finch has done a great job at being a "fun" walking simulator (in that the gameplay is varied and doesn't just involve holding down W), it has failed to be a story that is as compelling as it could have been. Personally I can make a recommendation as this is a unique experience that is unlike anything else, but the game is merely 2 hours long and has low replayability, so make your purchase decision with that in mind.

The game tells its story by a number of short stories with walking sections in between them. Each story is told from the perspective of a different character and usually contains a unique minigame, giving the game a high variety, though the gameplay is merely functional and none of the minigames could stand on its own.

What is disappointing however is the story. Though by no means bad it is surrounded by an aura of artificiality which makes me not care about most of the characters. I say that because the short stories usually have fantastical elements, which make them great as minigames but hard to be taken seriously; unfortunately the story demands to be taken extremely seriously, as it involves a lot of deaths. Story spoiler follows The game is titled as such because the entire Finch family except for Edith Finch is dead, who learns about each of their deaths through the short stories taken from their perspectives. While there are emotional moments, those ironically only comes when nobody is dying. When you introduce the expectation that most characters will be dead 5 minutes after their introduction, it's hard to be sad when that expectation is met.\t\t\t\t\t
