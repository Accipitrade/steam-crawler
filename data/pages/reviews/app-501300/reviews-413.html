I really enjoyed the game. It's creative and moving. There's a lot to relate to and to think about. There's no character arcs or anything really but the ending at least gives the experience a sense of culmination. There are some sections that are genuinely amazing. It is however a walking simulator with no meaningful choices. There's very little actual "gameplay" but that's how the genre works.

I was antsy about paying $12 for what I thought would be three hours(stated by a youtube review NOT ON THE STEAM PAGE) of a walking simulator. However, it came out to 100 minutes of gametime for me and that's including me going to the bathroom and setting up the settings and activating every single thing I could find. This is shorter and more expensive than most movies. Enjoyment is hard to put a price on and I really did enjoy this game but there's games on my wishlist I'm sure I'd enjoy just as much as this and get more playtime from. It was maybe 90 minutes of story with no replay value at all for $12. That's just too low for me. Nothing worse for getting into the story than wondering if it was worth the cost.

Devs have the freedom to set their price and their content but honestly I just don't think there's enough content here for me to buy it again or to recommend it to others even on sale. I really did enjoy this game and it feels ♥♥♥♥♥♥ to give a game I like a negative review but there was just strictly not enough content in it for me to recommend buying it, especially at full price.\t\t\t\t\t

'What Remains of Edith Finch' gets my wholehearted and unreserved recommendation because it’s not one game; it’s an anthology of them – all interesting and all different, each expressing their subject in a unique way. I’ve played anthologies before, but this one was so rich, and keen to explore storytelling. Playing it was invigorating and emotional, and remembering it is a delight.

The Finch tapestry is a captivating cross-stitching of the fantastical and the morbid. Every story Edith tells you as she roams through her ancestral home ends in death or disappearance, but the game can rarely bring itself to call death what it is. People don’t break their necks in a fall or take their own lives, they learn to fly or become kings. Death is only a tragic footnote in a magical story. If ever a game deserved to be called bittersweet, it’s this one. I love that the credits include family photos of the developers as kids. Even this small touch shows that they’ve perfectly understood the tone and theme of their own creation.

Quite possibly, this represents an apotheosis, the culmination of previous experimental storytelling games like 'Gone Home', 'Dear Esther', 'The Vanishing of Ethan Carter' and so on. I played this several months after release, presuming it was just another perfectly nice walking simulator-style thing that would occupy me for a night or two and then I’d never think about it again. Now it’s lodged in my head for quite probably years to come. It’s a beautifully human game despite often ostentatious oddity, and it’s also a treasure trove of tiny, very clever, sweet and twisted details that it would probably take half a dozen playthroughs to uncover the bulk of.
