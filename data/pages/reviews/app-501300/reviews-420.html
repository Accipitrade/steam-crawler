\r\n\t\t\t\t\tJust finished this masterpiece. Before playing, I saw this game had critical acclaim, but I wasn’t sure why. Looking at the trailers it looked pretty weird if I’m being honest. All those strange scenes out of context made me a bit nervous of what I was getting myself into.

But after playing it, and understanding what those scenes were all about, I was blown away. Each story is expertly crafted both from a story line perspective and from a gaming angle. I loved the unique set pieces which were all so different from each other so I never felt bored. The scene for Lewis is probably one of the best gaming experiences I have had. Overall, the creative writing was brilliant, I loved the line from the Gus scene. "...Gus stood far apart, just flew his kite and bottled up the storm inside his heart...." . There were other many good examples of fantastic writing. Some of the scenes from the children were things that I could relate to and they did invoke memories.

In the end, all the stories were knitted back together neatly, and what is a rarity in today’s games, we had a satisfying ending. Highly recommended game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI played this game in it's entirety this morning on stream.

I can honestly say that What Remains of Edith Finch is a game I won't soon be forgetting. The atmosphere was spot on, the art style was perfect, & the story was fantastic, although incredibly sad.

During this game, I went through many emotions. I laughed, I damn near cried, I felt dread, I felt happy, and I can think of at least two instances that I felt a bit anxious. The story pulls you in and makes you want to explore more of the house, delve into the stories of each character, & ultimately find out why you (Edith) went back to the house in the first place.

The only gripe I can imagine for the game would be the price tag of $20 that comes with it. For the ~2-2.5 hours of gameplay this game gives, it can feel a little on the expensive side. However, I can honestly say that I do not, for one second, regret paying the full asking price. It was that good.\t\t\t\t\t

What Remains of Edith Finch is the first video game that brought up many sad memories I could relate to. It's the first video game that moved me emotionally and, ultimately, to tears.

It's hard to describe this game as a point-and-click adventure except to say that maybe this is just an emotional journey the developers created to take you along on a ride of anguish.

I relate 100%. Both of my parents have died. I recently held my father's hand as he died from cancer. I suffered through his journey while I watched him disappear.

Wow, and thank you to Giant Sparrow for allowing me to go on this ride with you.\t\t\t\t\t

There is no word in the human language that can summarize how incredible this game's story truly is. As a disclaimer, I will agree that this game is technically a "walking simulator", but don't be fooled by the lackluster title. What Remains of Edith Finch is without a doubt one of the top narrative-driven games of 2017. Being only the second game from the studio, this game shows a bright future for Giant Sparrow and I can't wait for any new titles they produce.

There are three core aspects that make any video game a masterpiece. These three aspects allow players the opportunity to experience a memorable game while also allowing them to be immersed into the minds of the developers. Those three aspects are:

-Uniqueness
-Style
-Storytelling

Uniqueness isn't how outlandish the game is. Nor how bizarre it is. Uniqueness is how the game implements innovative ideas (such as gameplay mechanics, storytelling methods, etc) to its fullest extent without leaving the player confused or with questions.

Style refers to the art of the video game. Each video game has its own style of visual representation. The developers must create a style of art which not only highlights the purpose of the game but allows players to be enticed by the game itself.

Storytelling is exactly what it sounds like. The game must be able to not only create a story that's interesting to the player but also create a world for which the story takes place in. The story shouldn't have holes in it and should be able to stay consistent with the laws of its world.

What Remains of Edith Finch excels in all three aspects. And after playing this game for the third time, I can explain to you how it does so. But first, I should probably tell you what this game is about.

The title explains the basis of the game. You play as a girl named Edith Finch (Edith Jr.) who's returned to her family house after leaving it nearly a decade ago. You're welcomed with faint memories of your family on their final days at your house. And while you're greeted with old memories, you're also introduced to new stories. Stories of family members long before you, memorialized in the rooms in which they lived. Rooms that were locked away from you to hide away those very stories. Rooms holding generations of madness and tragedy right beneath your nose. And as you learn more and more of these stories, you realize why you're the last person alive in your family.

This game isn't like most other story-driven games. I'll admit when I first started the game I was a tad uninterested in the storytelling, but as I progressed I started to see how unique this game is. Most games separate their stories from the gameplay creating for the player an outside look into the worlds they explore. Examples of that would be cutscenes, conversations between NPCs, and third-person narration. However, this game immerses the player by combining both its gameplay and storytelling. Doing so allows the player to explore each individual story as if they're experiencing it through their own eyes. Another amazing aspect of the game is how they tell their stories. Even though the player is in the real world, we're shown each family members story through the imagination of Edith Finch. The reason for that is because Edith Finch hasn't experienced any of those stories when they happened, and can only understand what happened from some form of memento left at their memorial. Those mementos could be poems about their death, journal entries, divorce documents, or even a stack of pictures. The example I would like to show you right now is the story of Calvin Finch. You explore Calvin's story by reading a poem by his twin brother, Sam Finch. The poem goes as follows:


How I Want to Remember My brother,
by Sam Finch


The thing I remember is that when he made up his mind, that was it.
My brother said he'd die before he ate another mushroom. And he did.
At Barbara's funeral we swore we'd never be afraid again. And he wasn't.
I think Calvin always wanted to fly but that day he finally made up his mind to do it.
I told him going around was impossible.
Maybe if I hadn't said that.
Or maybe if the wind hadn't picked up...
Then maybe he'd still be here but I doubt it.
I think he'd already made up his mind.
That's what I want to remember about my brother.
The day he made up his mind to fly

... and he did


Calvin Finch died from falling off a cliff from his swing. But while playing as Calvin instead of falling off the cliff you end up flying in the air. You're even able to go around the tree with your swingset before you fall off. That's because you're imagining what happened from a poem by Sam Finch. While playing as Calvin you could see Sam run off halfway through the story, allowing the player to infer that Sam didn't really get to see what happened to Calvin after a certain point. And because of that, Sam imagined that his brother was able to go around the tree with the swing even though that's probably not what happened. And each story follows a format similar to this, where you're shown a story which wouldn't be possible in reality and is left to understand what really happened to each person.

Another thing that makes this game worth buying is the detail put into everything in the world of Edith Finch. By simply exploring the rooms you could see what each family member liked doing and what they were like. You're even met with prompts of dialogue when exploring random things like bookshelves or fishbowls. Those prompts fill the player with more information on the world they're exploring and allow them to feel some form of reward to look around.

The story is also unique by not having a traditional villain. Most games make the villain a person or a group of people working to do harm to others, but in this game, the villain is the stories themselves. (Explaining any more would spoil the story).

Factoring in the game's price and quality, i'd give it an 9.6/10. What Remains of Edith Finch is an amazing story driven game that separates itself and other games by having unique modes of gameplay also while telling a fascinating story. I would reccomend this to a friend.\t\t\t\t\t
