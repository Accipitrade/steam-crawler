\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'd like to preface this by saying that some stuff I may go into may seem spoilery about how the game is played, but that being said the first three paragraphs and conclusion can count as my reccomendation. Also warning, essay.

I'm not quite sure what to say. This game blew me away. My friend recommended it to me and from what he told me I was expecting a linear story-based walking sim. As someone who like FPS Multiplayer games and Interesting Puzzlers it didn't seem like it was gonna be the game for me. But since I loved The Beginners Guide, I thought why not.

I'm so happy I gave the game a shot.

Firstly, I'd like to say that if you are the type of person that likes to get playtime for your money, whether that's £1=1h or £1=100h, this may seem like a hard sell. It's £15/$20 for 2-3 hours of gameplay, and not much replayability, apart from maybe going back for the achievements. But I'd like to say don't think of this as a game, think of this more as an interactive movie, in a sense. The game for the most part is extremely linear, there are a few bit and pieces of lore that you can miss, but apart from that if you follow the little white balls you'll never get lost. While this may seem like a downside about a game where you're supposed to be "Exploring" a house, but the sequence in which you explore it room to room aids you in understanding the timeline of this family your being introduced to.

Ok, to kick of the spoilery bit of the review, this talk about the bad stuff. First off, the FOV is locked at 70(or something close to that it felt) which coupled with how slow the walking feels going down the first long stretch of road at the start, really put me off at the start. Eventually when going throughout the house, and reliving all the stories, the walking seems to decrease in annoyance. The FOV being locked is because the animations are all made for that FOV, so editing it would lead to some graphical problems, and eventually I stopped noticing.

The next thing that irked me a little was the swing sequence, where you control the character swinging back and forth very fast on a tree swing. As immersive as the part is, it started to make me feel extremely nauseous. Especially since you have to play the sequence twice for the achievement.

The last thing i'd like to point out (and in hindsight most important review wise, whoops) is the lack of tutorialisation. Now, don't get me wrong here, the last thing I want is a walking sim holding my hand through all the "hard parts" where it can't tell me where to go as to not break immersion, and one of my favourite games of last year was The Witness because of how pure of a game it was. What I mean is that the game feels like it requires game literacy to play. Let's use Molly and Sam's sequences to explain. In Molly's sequence, she Transfroms into a Cat, Owl, Shark, and Eldritch Horror. Each time, the control scheme changes slightly, always utilizing WASD and Mouse only of course.I play games alot, and I would say I'm pretty game literate, but I was actually a little stuck for a while in the Shark section because instead of WASD moving you forward back left and right, it moved you up down left and right, and so I was really confused as to how to Catch the seal . In a different sense, during Sam's sequence (which I love btw, really smart was of transitioning scene to scene) you have to photograph a deer. Up until then, all you had to do was Photograph Sam or Dawn so I spent a lot of time snapping shots of the same thing, thinking that I had to annoy Sam or something. I then searched the entire back shore that I could see. I then triggered a Bird Call, which let me to photographing the bird. I tried getting shots of the bird a few more times the Deer was right behind it. Whilst I understand why the cue was there, I got stuck because I followed the pattern given to me up until that point, because I had nothing else to go off ( I know Sam mentions a Deer or something at some point, but I don't think that justifies how hard it seems to find it). Regardless, I understand why changing the Dynamic of the controls/objective is useful for storytelling and relation/immersion sake, for example Lewis' sequence, but I feel at some points it falls a little flat and once I realise that the game changed stuff up on me, I feel dumb, even though I did nothing wrong.

Anyway, ♥♥♥♥ being negative, time to talk about why this game I have 2-3 hours in has characters that impacted me more that most from movies and shows i've watched recently. This is gonna get super spoilery, to the point where I can't really do the spoiler tag thing without its all beign in there, or just being a wierd mess. So if you managed to make it this far, and you care about story spoilers, and aren't convinced to buy it, go watch a let's play and experience it in a slightly less immeresed fashion. The game puts you in the shoes of the family members, usually in the moments before they died. There are like 10-15 different variations of WASD&Mouse click/space/ctrl(?) controls inside these sequences, each bringing a different feeling that can give you a feeling about the family member you're playing as at the time. Also, everything you interact with in the game, you have to make the movement you would in real life(kinda sorta). Whilst these can seem annoying and fiddely at times, they to managed to add to the immersion of the game as a whole.

I've really use the word immersion a lot, haven't I? Well lets talk about spefically how to immersion impacted me as a player. When the game ended, whilst alot had been answered for Edith, it seemed I had 100 questions about this family that I wouldn't have ever known about. Edith at some point says she has all sorts of questions for her motherafter finding out all about the family, which she now can't ask because her mother has passed. MASSIVE ENDING SPOILER In the end it turns out your Edith's son, who has visited the house as a teen to leave flowers at your grave, and is reading your story. The fact that at the end I felt at the end like I was part of the family, before I even realised I was playing as her son, and that I went through the same intruige she had really stuck with me. The game had managed to put me into a position via storytelling and design, only to reveal that it was part of the games intention at the end blew my mind.

(Skip to here to avoid spoilers)
I played the game in the middle of the afternoon, and thought about it all through the evening. I was even up until 5 am
watching a let's play and trying to answer all the questions I had about the timeline and other stuff. I ended up having almost no sleep and going into work the next day and having a killer shift, all because I was so intrigued about this Ignorant, Stubborn, Cursed family that I felt I now knew so fondly. I wanted to know more, on one hand I felt the game ended to early, but I knew that it told the story in the perfect way to keep it interesting. In conclusion, despite a few graphical, and mechanical issues I had, this game hit me harder than any game I've played in a while, and yes, I cried a little lot. If you give this game a chance to pull you in, you'll enjoy it. Much like a movie, play it in one go for the best experience.\t\t\t\t\t
