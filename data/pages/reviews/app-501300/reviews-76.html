\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI am not going to be original in writing that the graphics were beautiful (even on Steam Deck where I played this title), the atmosphere is lovely, the writing is excellent, and I loved receiving what feels like a buffet of varied life experiences with an air of tragedy. It was delightfully disturbing and made great use of the game format for telling its story. I would describe the genre as interactive storyteller.

However, this game does suffer from a few pitfalls of the genre. My biggest gripe is that the game is so poorly lit that I strain my eyes to see all of those excellent graphics. Even in the settings with my brightness turned all the way up, it was often hard for me to see. I discard the suggestion that this is artistic styling at its finest--it's just plain annoying.

Overall, I'm quite happy with the experience especially considering that I was able to get this game on sale at my wife's recommendation.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBecause of the comments I wasn't expecting much from this game. It provided not much, as expected. Graphic is OK. Audio is also OK. Story - pff, it could be better. It could be worse - so no much complain here.

House - that needs separate paragraph! If you want a deadtrap - build house like in the game. I can not imagine worse house. Some places you need to reach using stone wall. Some - using wire. Some - tree. Some house elements are places on small poles. In reality this house would collapse in few years.

Achievements are illogical. But You never expect logical anyway. So, it provided what it promised - average game for average price.

Bad things - no skip (if you are playing story second time - of course you do not want to listen everything again). Another VERY BAD thing - game has bugs (game stuck on eating sailor). And then you need to replay everything without skip button - this becomes forever! Anything else aside of this was OK.

So, if you want some easy relaxing game (walking simulator) - then this is for you. I've got this on sale, and I think game worth the price. In total, I would buy it if I knew what I know now.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOften a game impresses me with one or two things it does exceptionally well or in a unique way. With "What Remains of Edith Finch," I think the strength is in the VARIETY of ways it finds to tell its various stories. You wander through the now abandoned home of the main character's family, learning of each person's fate as you go. Each tale has its own distinct visuals and setting, its own voice, its own method of delivery. All special and emotional in their own way, for their own sake. It isn't really about some overarching, linear narrative. Each person's story is worthwhile in its own right.

Encompassing all these distinct stories is the house itself, which I feel deserves appreciation on its own. The detailing in the setting makes it feel truly lived in - in a way most games simply do not take the time and care to do, which makes it quite the atmospheric experience.

Side note: Don't expect much interactivity or puzzle difficulty from this experience. It's mostly about the stories themselves. I dislike the term "walking sim" because I think it's lazy and vague, but if that helps guide expectations, so be it, because I think incorrect expectations would be the most likely reason for anyone to dislike this otherwise stellar game.

The game is relatively short, just a couple hours. I think it's more than worth that little bit of your time.\t\t\t\t\t
