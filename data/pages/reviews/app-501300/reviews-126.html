\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNo spoilers in this review, it is HIGHLY recommended you go into this game blind as this is essentially you following a set story with many twists and turns (literally lol).

This is a short game, one you could finish in about 2 hours played continuously. But my goodness, it is very very beautiful and will make you feel, feel so many things. It's a tragedy, but not hopeless. Seemingly supernatural, but also so grounded and real to the human experience. There are very unpleasant moments in which while almost entirely implied, it is not difficult to understand what actually occurred. The atmosphere, setting, and design of this house you explore is incredible that it personally makes me wish I could live in such a place, and it is so packed with small details that makes it feel like a real, living and breathing place that you will want to look around to find everything.

There are no instructions, so you have to determine where to move next and what you are tasked to do to progress with subtle guidance, and it is not always obvious what you are supposed to do. Maybe this was just me because I am stupid. However, this does not detract from the story at all and in fact getting to do these actions yourself really helps to put you in the characters' shoes (as is obviously the point). The game play is also very cleverly integrated, in which only through playing will you know what I mean.

Anyone looking for a short game that pulls no punches, is beautifully designed, encourages exploration, and contains an ending that will leave you both satisfied and wrecked with questions and emotions, this game is fully worth it.

**Note, my computer froze with this game running, which is why my time playing is weirdly long. Of course, replayability is limited, but it is absolutely not a waste of an experience. If this factor isn't a dealbreaker but still may be a concern, try to take your time going through it and avoid spoilers as much as you can. Personally, I've only played it once but I feel like I could run through it at least one more time just to find details I missed before.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is, quite frankly speaking, a walking simulator. The very idea of this might turn some people off, and that's okay. Walking simulators aren't for everyone. If there's one walking simulator I'd implore people to play, however, I'd ask them to give this one a chance.

What Remains of Edith Finch takes on little stories, as real as they are fantastical, all while the player uncovers parts of an anything-but-ordinary house. These stories are incredibly intimate and personal, just as the rooms in the house feel very much lived in and the act of going through someone's room and their old things feels almost intrusive. While some narrative-driven games or experience like to suckerpunch the player with big plot twists or emotionally-charged moments, WROEF takes a much gentler approach (which isn't to say the emotions cannot be intense, because they definitely can as they were for me at some points). Here instead, however, WROEF gently pulls you in and cradles you through this pilgrimage, then at the end of it all, gently releases you back into the world. How you choose to feel about anything and everything is your decision and you have every right to be and feel however at the end of it all, but you cannot and will not deny that this game has given you the gift of itself, and more than anything, we should be grateful we got to be here for it after all.\t\t\t\t\t
