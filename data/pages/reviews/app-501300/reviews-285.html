\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI feel utterly mixed about this game. To start out, Edith Finch is your typical walking simulator, taking place in a mind-boggling house that's frankly fascinating. Stepping into the game, as you near the Finch Mansion, it can be thrilling to think of all the possibilities before you. However, once you come out the other end, while its stories are harrowing and wont to make one sympathize for this family, the story feels fairly impersonal.
To begin to explain why, What Remains of Edith Finch, while being in the same genre of Gone Home and Firewatch, is far less focused on helping you build a personal relationship with the characters and the location, but instead, presents for you an extremely linear story, told through a series of vignettes. While there are background details hinting at different characters' relationships, for the most part, Edith Finch is far more tell than show. There is a set track of rooms you're meant to go through, and access to those rooms allows you entrance into other rooms. The closing off of these rooms may be interesting narratively, but it's extremely restrictive mechanically.
The most disheartening part about Edith Finch is that because of the way the house constructed, there is far less capacity for backtracking, and thus, you don't feel like you got to know the space. Additionally, because of the decision to tell the story primarily through vignettes as opposed to through the environment, the space feels incidental, which is a real shame, because there's some painstaking detail in object placement, as well as designs of books, posters, etc. What I'm getting at is that because you walk through the house from bottom to top, and then it ends there, the game puts far more focus on the vignettes -- which, while being incredible, and genuinely quite immersive your first playthrough, on subsequent replays just make the story seem tired. Most would consider the walking simulator genre one that is mainly designed for the first playthrough, and has little value after that, but I disagree. If there's more basis on environmental storytelling, it can still be pleasurable to revisit. For instance, I loved replaying Gone Home, and through the documents found around the house, discovering that the uncle of Katie's dad, who owned the place, bequeathed the house to him out of guilt for something he did to Terry when he was a child. Or discovering the Ouija board Sam and Lonnie used to try and contact him.
Edith Finch, while being a beautiful story, with some exquisite art and environmental design, really fell short for me in terms of depth. I think what I'm really meaning to say is that the way a story is presented in a walking simulator matters, and the more you can make the spaces matter, the less tiresome they can be to replay. Also, I have to say, while they were interesting the first time around, I wish I could play a NewGame+ that didn't feature the vignettes, and also had doors that weren't sealed shut. Let us get to the space as someone who lived there would know it!
Ultimately, though I recommend this game, but it's a soft recommend. Play Firewatch or Gone Home before this. Even Tacoma had SOME replay value.\t\t\t\t\t

Most games gives you a sense of accomplishment/achievement/satisfaction after completing it. This game, however left me feeling pensive and sad, perhaps even a sense of helplessness.

The ending hit me hard, even though it should have been evident from the beginning. I was glad I was inattentive at the beginning. The new menu that opened up after the credits makes it plainly obvious and undeniable. I guess the last death was too senseless for me to comprehend or accept.

Overall, this is more of an interactive story rather than a game. I later learnt that this genre is called "Walking Simulator". But it is far more interactive and immersive than Dear Esther.

One of the most thought-provoking games I have ever played.
