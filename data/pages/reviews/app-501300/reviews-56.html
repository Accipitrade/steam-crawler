I'm in a very deep depression right now, and this game has touched me very deeply. It's what I needed right now to feel something again, to feel life's bittersweet poignant nostalgia within its precious, fleeting nature. The developers put in their entire heart and soul into this thoughtful masterpiece. Yes, the story leaves you questioning and wondering, but that's the whole point (central message seen in this quote, towards the end: "If we lived forever, maybe we'd have time to understand things. But as it is, I think the best we can do is try to open our eyes. And appreciate how strange and brief all of this is.")! I don't think I've ever seen this high-quality experience in a walking sim.
The gameplay is unique and the storytelling is quite magical and gripping. As an aspiring writer myself, it felt like a masterclass in character development and artful conceptions.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe major plus is that the game does a beautiful and innovative job of using gameplay as an immersive storytelling technique, and for that reason alone I'd say this is a very mixed review. But, sadly, they wasted it on a disappointing story.

I wouldn't say it's a waste of time to play if you can get it on sale, especially since it's extremely short, but the way it's marketed and talked about is overrated and misleading, so don't go into it with your expectations too high. [Minor generic spoilers ahead] Don't expect to figure out a big inter-generational mystery, don't expect things to come together by the end, and don't expect to come away with any particularly deep, thought-provoking lessons about life and death. Expect something more like Final Destination--a shallow compilation of interrelated people dying in random accidents, for vague magical reasons or by impossible coincidence depending on your interpretation--except depicted with an aesthetic of nostalgia instead of horror.

That said, the story was told very well, and I hope this game inspires more people to find creative techniques to tell their own stories.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a delicately told, but cautionary tale of not letting go of the past, unforgiving sadness interwoven with love, and on a more subtle note, asinine superstitions -- which in this case, comes back to haunt a family full of tragic secrets spanning four generations. Fast forward to present day and we meet our 17-year-old protagonist, Edith Finch. She shows to be quite an inquisitive and determined young woman upon returning home and uncovering the mysterious truth behind the deaths of her entire family. We see snippets of this during an investigation of each character, where we witness their last moments with disquieting clarity, a storybook journey knowingly leading you towards their imminent death and the difficult process of learning to heal from loss after loss. Which begs the question, would things be different had they decided to move away for good? Or is Edie just an unreliable narrator, too consumed by denial and grief to look beyond an unchangeable past?

In the case of Edith, I interpreted her ending as making the best out of a bad situation. I mean, this game essentially features a number of children dying, I think they handled the subject in a relatively tasteful way since it isn't played for shock or unnecessarily drawn out.

Putting my thoughts under a spoiler so read at your own risk. Skip if you want the ratings. ;)
My personal take is that most of the family's tragedy results from a lack of guidence (might throw a lack of common sense in there as well lol), and recklessness/neglectful tendencies (Dawn's dad blatantly not checking to make sure the deer is deceased, poor baby Gregory's demise feels the most plausible to happen IRL but still due to neglect however unintentional, building a swing set on a cliff/next to picket fence, etc). Save for Dawn who at least tried to get on with life for the sake of her children. I can see how Edie would think it's a curse, watching most of your family die one by one and being somewhat helpless to stop it. I say somewhat mostly because of Molly. I know there's several theories as to why/how she died, few of which I agree with, but I'd like to bring up another point without spoiling too much: Apparently Molly was 10 when she passed, why would a ten-year-old eat what she did, in spite of hunger? A kid that age should know what is considered safe to eat (bar the Holly, I understand back then it wasn't common knowledge that it was poisonous) and honestly, she sounds a lot younger than 10 so maybe during that era 10-year-olds were more childish compared to now. If anyone has opinions on that, let me know. Keyword here is should. Edie, and most of the parents for that matter, are downright absent to the point of neglect (again, Gregory and Gus). Who is to say they took the time to even instruct her on basic do's and dont's of items that could be harmful to ingest. Anyway that's something I don't really see being brought up on the subject of Molly.


]Gameplay
Gameplay is nothing short of impressive, with each room proving the ingenuity of the design team and overall interactive experience, while also managing to maintain a sense of uniqueness to stave off boredom. And that is what makes WROEF such a great game. It's mini glimpses into several lives packed into 2-3hrs that allow memories left behind to speak for the long passed/absent owner. There are hardly any puzzles, so nothing keeping you from progressing should you need a break. As for achievements, it took me 1-2 playthroughs to collect them all, assuming you're going for them the first time.
Audio
10/10. If a soundtrack could be described as underrated, this would be it. Normally I'm not paying too much attention to a score that a scene has, however, this fits the at times bleak (with sparks of hope sprinkled here and there) atmosphere and poignant storytelling. Best OST in particular for me were Calvin, Milton, and Lewis' Coronation. Highly recommend giving the soundtrack a listen at least once.
Graphics
8/10. Aesthetically pleasing and ultimately creative. It really gives the feeling that I'm walking into a home long abandoned and full of unreleased memories. Kind of a shame we didn't see the attic, I also would've liked more things to interact with outside.

*insert shameless plug devs didn't ask for here*
I'd recommend playing The Unfinished Swan as well, either before or after this one. It also clears up a few questions about Milton... or it might bring about new ones. ;D
In conclusion, definitely worth full price if not discounted and if you like walking simulators that aren't afraid to tell you an unconventional story, this one is hard to forget.\t\t\t\t\t
