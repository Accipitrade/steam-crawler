I enjoyed how quite a few of the stories were almost whimsical, even though you know how they'll end. A lot of the storylines are somewhat obvious, I knew of (at least part of) the big "twist" becauseyou can tell she's pretty pregnant just by looking down. Being able to actually look forward to a piece of story in a game is something that doesn't come easy. It's not really horror, I could see how it could be to someone, but it's a different brand of horror than what would be expected.
As far as price goes, its probably best to wait for a sale. This game is amazing and all, but $20 is pretty expensive for the amount of content you get.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is very, very good. That said, I don't know if it's worth it's regular price, just because it is very short. I blew through it in a single day, but I can tell you that if you can get this on sale, you should buy it in a heartbeat.

In a video game, generally my critera for the story is as follows: it doesn't matter what the story is, what matters is how it is integrated in the gameplay. My favorite game of all time, Castlevania SOTN, exemplifies how a story can be perfectly woven into a game. It appears often enough to make you want to reveal the rest of it, but it stays out of the game enough that it doesn't get in the way of the game.

This game is brilliant. It's a walking simulator, don't be fooled, but it's not like other walking simulators. What I mean by that is that most of the time, walking simulators are frustrating. It isn't always clear what you're supposed to be doing or where you ought to be going, and in general, they suffer because you're either being told the story, or you're walking around looking for it. I hate most walking simulators because they usually feel like trying to listen to an audio book, but between every chapter, you have to do a lap around your house. This game is different for two big reasons that make all the difference.

Firstly, the story is always being revealed, so you're never walking around looking for it. It's always there. In between major chapters, your protagonist gives commentary. Whether it's her recalling her childhood, giving insight to what's going on, or simply musing about her feelings toward a character, this game is built such that between major chapters, it is constantly building an emotional connection between the player and each character, which is exactly what a game should do.

Secondly, the major chapters are not just revealing the story, they are games in and of themselves. Each major chapter has a different little task for you, and it's not some stupid crosword puzzle or some like that, it's actual honest-to-God gameplay that's fun and interesting. On top of that, each major chapter has gameplay displayed in a different style for each character that the chapter explores. One I remember particularly well is a chapter that has you walking around inside a comic book learning what happened to a particular character. Another chapter has you taking photos of what happened, and another has you play as a series of different animals. This is the only game I've ever played that made me repeat, out loud, to myself, the phrase "God this game is so cool."

Beside the gameplay, this story itself is actually fantastic. I'm a person who values a great story above most other things, and that this story really had me almost choked up is really meaningful. It has twists and turns, and is, above all, generally really sad. I didn't think that the tag "psychological horror" was very fitting about an hour into the game, but looking back on it, I find it incredibly appropriate. This game, while not being particularly scary, does a fantastic job of displaying horrors, both those in the real world, as well as those which are fantasy.

So, TLDR, this game is very good, and sets the standard for excellent story and excellent gameplay, and most importantly, an excellent intertwining of the two.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHonestly can't recommend spending $20 on something that only lasts two hours. Playing the game in one sitting would probably produce the most emotional impact, but I found the game to be abstruse and ultimately pointless.

What Remains of Edith Finch is a procession of stories focusing on each member of the Finch family. Some stories are fanciful and don't make much sense while some stories are straight forward. I kept expecting later stories to shed light on earlier ones, or for each story to serve to progress an overall story line, and while some stories do tie into other ones, there's no master plan at work. It's just a sequence of stories, premeated by Edith Finch's oftentimes pointless chatter. Most of the game had that awkward trying-too-hard-to-be-profound feeling. The whole game hinges on these stories having some sort of emotional punch, and while they're definitly interesting (and sometimes poignant) the downfall is that they're ultimately meaningless. There's no answer at the end of the game. There's no twist, there's no great reveal. Just a linear sequence of stories (and a whole slew of questions with no answers), and then roll credits. I was frankly shocked the game ended when it did. Buy this game if it's 75% off, but don't waste $20 on this.
