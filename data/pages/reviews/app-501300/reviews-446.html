\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEdith Finch is a brilliant piece of interactive art that moves the "walking sim" genre forward in several subtle ways. What would be notes or audio logs in other walking sims are playable vignettes (though I'm still talking about "playable" in the context of a walking sim, so take that how you will). These vignettes do an excellent job of putting the player in the headspace of the characters. It also uses the linearity of its story to create a dramatic irony that creates a palpable sense of tension and dread for each and every vignette.

This is especially true of the game's two best vignettes: Baby Gregory's bathtime vignette and Lewis' cannery vignette. The first one exceptionally captures the sense of wonder and joy that a young child sees in the world. The second perfectly captures the sense of day dreaming during work (about video games, no less). I don't think I've every related to a video game character as much as I related to Lewis in this game!

The other vignettes are also fantastic, and the whole game has a Tim Burton level of whimsy and charm throughout. Unless you're the kind of person who just absolutely refuses to play a "walking sim", then Edith Finch gets my total, unequivocal, recommendation. It's a must-play.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tContained within this little narrative game are several unpredictable little vignettes, some more fantastical than others. Some of them are goofy, whereas others are deeply moving, but they're all compelling in their own way, and as is always the case, the simple fact that this is a game makes the process of discovery that much more involving. But this also finds clever ways to incorprate gameplay elements into its story, and while some vignettes are barely interactive, others introduce entirely new gameplay elements which aren't seen anywhere else in the game, and which tie cleverly into the various stories. Even just moving around the house is a very active process, as processes like lifting trap doors and turning keys requires the mouse or analog stick to be moved in the direction you want the character's hands to move in. It makes for a very involved and thus involving game, and this is helped further by level design which, while linear, is constantly wrapping back in on itself, revealing an eccentric yet plausible setting which somehow ties into the game's core melancholy. But the real power of this game comes when the vignettes are combined with the framing story, regarding a teenager returning to her family's house to investigate the supposed family curse after all of her relatives have died. As powerful as several vignettes are, it's the culminative power which makes this something of a masterpiece, a rumination on a variety of themes but above all the ways we deal with death and tragedy. Not all of the gameplay ideas are as polished as they might have been, but it's never so frustrating as to get in the way of the deeply moving storytelling. This is up there with "Gone Home" and "Journey" as one of the greatest expressions of what this kind of game is capable of. Absolutely recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLoved, loved this game. It's in the style of Gone Home, if you are familiar with that title (if not, you should be). It's a walking simulator where you learn about your relatives by examing various rooms throughout your childhood home. The story is VERY good, very compelling and you will find yourself starting to care about these people - and what may or may not have happened to them.

The story-telling is quite creative and each vignette is unique to itself regarding the style in which it is delivered; these words may not make sense to you reading it right now but trust me, just play the game.

For me, it's one of those games that when done, I find myself staring quietly at the screen, thinking about what I just experienced and trying, for the life of me, to define it. Failing to define it, but still loving the experience anyway. And wishing, in this case, our main character had 50 more relatives so I could continue the journey.

Yes, it's a short game. You can easily knock it out in one sitting. In my opinion, it's still worth it though. You will continue to think about it after it's done, and then you will scour Steam for games like this one.\t\t\t\t\t
