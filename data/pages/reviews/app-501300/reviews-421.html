Every story you hear or play feels unique and delivers deep storytelling in an excellent crafted artistic way. Delivered through different gameplay mechanics and visuals, that I haven't seen used like this before. Resulting in scenes that look surreal or almost painted (Screenshot 1, Screenshot 2). It felt like diving into a mysterious fairytale and it managed to easily create an emotional bond between me, Edith and her family.

All in favour of delivering a truely well written and emotional tale that is dark, mystical and undeniable touching. Giant Sparrow delivered a masterpiece.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI’m not an expert in this genre, but I have played a few games like this. For the most part, I don’t like this type of game. If we jammed them into the same categories as books, they would mostly fall under the umbrella of “mysteries”. The basic formula being to intersperse puzzles with story. If you want to continue the story, you must solve a puzzle first. I generally don’t enjoy that, no matter how it is done. Either the puzzles are too easy, and thus extraneous, or too hard, and break the pacing of the story.

What Remains of Edith Finch does something that I haven’t seen much of yet in my limited experience. It attempts to marry games with stories in a way where, despite the game itself being neither challenging nor fun, it is used as a small but important metaphor within the story, and it is somehow made more interesting because of the points where it tries and doesn’t succeed.

None of the stories in the game are particularly long, or nuanced. It would take a lot of effort to build this scaffolding into a readable book. And none of the games are difficult in the least. A three year old could manage most. However the purpose of the games within the stories often serves to connect you to the characters and the stories in a unique way. Nothing earth shattering, but definitely several “Wow, that was pretty cool!” moments.

And what I find more interesting is that it sort of feels like the developers tried to accomplish this many times, and then sorted their attempts from least successful to most. Even towards the end of the game where the “Wow!” moments are a bit more dense, you probably won’t think back and see the whole set of these stories as solid gold. They aren’t. But because it saves the better ones for last, it definitely leaves you with the sense that it is much more than a “walking simulator” or “visual novel”.

And I don’t mean to imply that the early parts of the game aren’t enjoyable, or that the stories are less interesting. I only mean that as I got deeper into it, I began to appreciate what I think they were really trying to do, and that was to find unique ways to combine play with narrative.

I give it a thumbs up, because in a genre I usually don’t enjoy, this game taught me something new about game design that will stick with me. If you have any interest in this genre, this one is worth picking up.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t"What remains of Edith Finch" is like a delicious, truly wonderful ice cream. The first lick is so amazing, so perfect... but it's all you get.

The game showed a lot of promise. A lot of potential. And one couldn't be blamed for expecting a lot more wonderful things to come.

Unfortunately, just like the characters portrayed in it, the game ended before any of it could be realized. Don't get me wrong, the art direction was wonderful, the sound as well and the storytelling was compelling. I appreciate how the title delivered what it did - there was just painfully little of it.

It feels like even the story, as short as it was, was not quite finished. I can appreciate a good metaphor, but this wasn't it. What I expected to be the end of first chapter was actually the end of the game.

Do I recommend it? It's difficult to do so. And it's not a question of price. Even if you can get it at 80% off, the game will leave you unsatisfied once you finish it. It's like being offered a delicious, truly wonderful ice cream. The first lick is so amazing, so perfect... but it's all you get.\t\t\t\t\t
