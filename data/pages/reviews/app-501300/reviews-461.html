\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI both really enjoyed this game and it left me wanting more.

It is very heartfelt and engaging. Although we don't get to interact with all members of the family I really felt attached to many of them.

While some of the story is open to interpretation, which I think was a great thing, I had one problem with the story.
The game is all about a family that is cursed. This idea is never fleshed out and felt disregarded rather than resolved.

All in all, I would recommend this to anyone wanting a good story, however I was glad to pick this up during a sale, because it is quite short. It took me about 2 and a half hours to play through the whole story and go back and do cleanup for a couple of missed achievements.

The story here will defintely stay with me for a while. Even though it is a story of loss and death, it is told with such care as to be tasteful thoughout.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat remains of Edith Finch has a certain vibe of The Vanishing of Ethan Carter all over it. Although the two games are very different in how the play goes, there are certain similarities.
Both have a narrative element to it, the main character, who speaks in first person, and the interactions of this character are somewhat limited (although not entirely walking simulator, it is minimalistic) but here lies a main difference i noticed between the two games: While in The Vanishing of Ethan you have different interactions , and specially you have objects to pick up and use on other objects, or just objects to see in order to buil up a "scene" in the case of Edith Finch you just go around, opening doors, windows and doing some special interactions. These are done on "memories time" where you perform special interactions while learning about the fate of the finch family. These interactions are always on site and very very minimal but, i would have to say they were, much more creative in its ways (to tell "how" would be to spoil it) but it has to do more with the narrative and story of the scene than to the game itself.

So...this game, was much more limited in its interaction , it depends more on the narrative, but the narrative was very creative at times. It was well told, the scenes are nice, the music is great and voice acting pretty good. In a game where narrative is everything, of course these are the things that matter the most.

Personally, i would have to say i liked more The Vanishing of Ethan Carter, because that game works more on constructing the place, what could be , or feel like, a real place. Walking the creek on Ethan's game felt eerily real at times, and i felt absorbed by it. It was, i would have to admit, not as varied and colorful as Edith Finch, but it remained on one full mood, dense atmosphere through the whole game, and it didn't let go until the end. You were just going deeper and deeper. It felt like one big experience and the transition from one place to the next was fluid, much more connected.

In the case of Edith Finch because of the nature of the story and the different family members, it jumps from one place to another, from one era to another. That's expected of course, because of how the story is told. The result is i didn't feel as connected to this place, because it jump from one story to the next, but it did have more variety to it.
In ethan you would only cover one event taking several days, but you would be travelling to all places in site, it felt more expansive because you could go almost everywhere your eyes could see, even in the distance.

Then the story was one of the strong points of Edith Finch. It really is well told and has great moments all around. Although it has to be mentioned its a story with some heavy subjects included but also it has time for some lighter moments. The narrative was spot on and intense, but at the end it lacked for something that could close the experience in a satisfying way . Comparing it maybe, to another drama game, Valiant Hearts: The great War, wich is a very different kind of game (a platformer) that deals with another heavy drama subject (the first world war, narrated from the perspective of different protagonists through their letters) Edith Finch feels a little empty at the end, like you wanted to know more about the Finches and what happened to them... but you don't , really... and with Valiant Hearts you reach a good ending that gives closure to the story of the game, (and it is a sad sad ending!) but it feels more fulfilling

But, besides that, in the end, i have to say i did like What remains of Edith Finch... it shows for me (as Ethan Carter did), that games are becoming something else, they are evolving constantly, firstly because we can say now that there's definitely different genres in games as there are in movies, for different publics, different ages.
