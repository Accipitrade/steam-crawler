\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI chose this game because I share a commonality with the main character. My grandmother and I have the same name as well. I found the story to be very creative, mysterious, imaginative, and sad with all of the death. Because I'm not a fan of heights, I found (in my own words) "the climb to and moving about the sky" a bit frightening, even though it was on screen. And... maybe it was my settings, but I found the environment to be too dark, as I would have liked to see everything in the house a bit more clearer, because I'm nosy and like looking around at all the items in the house. Maybe that's because I play hidden objects games. I really enjoyed snooping around in the house, and the layout and decor was very creative and in some parts felt cozy and welcoming, although I can't imagine anyone having that many books in their house. Also, the game got to be a bit much with the "sky touching" room extensions, so I was glad when the rooms ended. Overall, I really enjoyed this game, and would recommend that mystery game fans give it a try. It was well worth it.\t\t\t\t\t

You walk through the main character's childhood house, reliving the stories of the deaths of all of their past family members. The whole game is an emotional ride that makes you think a lot about life and death. The creative ways that each story is told is amazing. The voice acting and look of the game are beautiful as well. A game that keeps you playing, you wont be able to stop playing until the end. I can't recommend this game enough to everyone I know.

ALSO: From user 's review, If you need, I didn't but you may.
Do yourself a favor if you get eye-strain or nauseated from the forced motion blur and/or the chromatic aberration and go to %appdata%/Local/FinchGame/Saved/Config/WindowsNoEditor and open the "Engine.ini" file and copy in the following:

[/script/engine.renderersettings]
r.SceneColorFringe.Max=0
r.DefaultFeature.MotionBlur=0

This will disable both effects which in my case completely removed any strain I was having.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tImagine you had a chance to unveil the secrets and stories of your closest ones after their deaths: that is the thought experiment at the core of "What Remains of Edith Finch". The Finches used think of themselves as a cursed family. But are they? You move from room to room in their abandoned house and search for the clues they left behind. Not just a game about grief and solitude, but also about the complexity of family history and the psychology of intergenerational trauma, you uncover the Finch family's tragic history chapter by chapter, person by person – which is less a search for truth and facts, but one for understanding the family members‘ individual struggles.
This structure gives it the look and feel of an anthology of short stories that differ greatly in atmosphere and even genre (brace yourselves for a bone-chilling horror tale as well as fairy tale like episodes with grim twists), but that are held together firmly by their themes and of course by its protagonist, i.e. you. And, perhaps most of all, by their setting, the Finch house that encloses the family's story from the depths of the cellar to the very top of the attic, and beyond. Crooked and derelict, it resembles something out of a Tim Burton film.
If you enjoy exploring abandoned places on your own, finding secret doors and hidden peepholes, you'll love this game as much as I did. Figuring out the specific game play for each episode is part of the fun. One episode nearly had me giving up though because the game got stuck again and again, but I dabbled with the resolution quality until it ran smoothly - keeping this in mind might spare you some trouble, wish I had thought of it earlier. Hence my 5+ hours of playing, which is fairly long: if you are on a small budget, be warned that you're likely to play no more than four hours, some players evidently need significantly less. Despite these minor points of critique, a big recommendation. Rather than demystifying the Finch family, the game lets you immerse in their lives with all their ambiguities and riddles. If you ever lost someone and know the painful experience of never being able to truly listen to their stories again, let alone all the untold ones, the premise of this game might hit home. It makes you think about what remains of a person, or of a family, or of a home after they are gone. This can get quite emotional, but in a surprisingly complex – and also oddly entertaining – way.\t\t\t\t\t
