\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere is a terribly-underappreciated film out there called Secondhand Lions, a coming-of-age story about a young boy sent off to spend the summer with his two somewhat-estranged eccentric uncles while his mother goes gallavanting about with her boyfriend. Over the course of the summer, he discovers all kinds of stories about the pair of men that border on surreal and are too fantastic to be believed. What it does so very well is that even though in the back of your head you know that these tall tales can't possibly be true, you want to believe in them or at the very least, you begin to doubt yourself.

Knowing nothing about What Remains of Edith Finch, throughout my playthrough, I was reminded very much of this movie. While Finch has a much darker theme that courses through its veins, it still retains a child-like whimsical nature that somehow makes the pill much easier to swallow. The game sees Edith returning to her family ancestral home six years after it was hastily abandoned and tasks you to explore it with her. The house itself is the first clue that something is a little off-center and should you wish not to have any of the experience ruined for you, I'd suggest you stop reading here (some spoilers ahead), pick it up, and enjoy its short 2-2.5 hour ride.

That said, Finch is a beautiful piece of interactive fiction that I feel could only be told in this format. It is not really a game - there are no obstacles or challenges to overcome and no riddles or puzzles that are going to stump you. In fact, Finch goes out of its way to ensure that the narrative moves forward so long as you are inclined to explore it. You are very much presented with a piece of yarn and asked to tug on it until the whole thing unravels, like a magician who asks you to yank hankies out of his coat, except here the magician is dead and the last hanky probably causes cancer.

It's this juxtaposition of fun and tragedy that permeates the narrative of Finch as you discover the family history by exploring the insane nutso funhouse that makes up the old home. Rather than simply finding pieces of literature throughout the house and reading them, you're cleverly invited to experience them, and each and every memory has this childlike element of surreality, whether the particular memory is macabre or whimsical. In this fashion, the game is able to toy with your emotional responses such that they end with question marks. For example, you might be thinking, "I should feel really terrible about this... but I'm kinda happy?" or "Well, that didn't explain anything at all... but it kinda did?"

SPOILERS AHEAD: As you'll quickly discover, the Finch family is probably the most tragic family in the history of the universe. Throughout the house - formerly occupied by the family matriarch whose name Edith carries (aka Grandma Edie) - are actual memorials to each and every family member that ever lived there over its 60-70-some-odd years of history. Bedrooms are left untouched except for an artful piece of grandma's work to say that this person once lived here and this is how it always looked. However, you'll notice that strangely for most of them, the depicted lifespan is exceptionally short. As Edith makes her way around the house (the 'goal' is to get to her old room), she'll find diaries, letters, notices, or mementos that allow her to dive into the often-tall tales of her ancestors and their lives coming to an untimely end. Yet despite the tragic nature of each story, there's a certain wonder and fantasy that, like in Secondhand Lions, make you question just how much truth is being shown to you. It is less, however, about the actual stories and more about how they are told that makes Finch a wonderful experience.

As spoilery as that last paragraph was, however, trust me that I have not given anything away despite the preceding warning. There is so much you don't know about this game (if you've been smart not to read about it) and the less you know about it, the better your experience will be. Despite this, I was not moved like other people were and found the game more fascinating than emotionally engaging, but your mileage may vary. In the end, though, I was absolutely pleased at this marvelous piece of storytelling and there are so few games out there that use the medium to its advantage. While the billion-dollar blockbusters continue to chase Hollywood, there is a lot to celebrate with studios like Annapurna trying something else entirely. This couldn't be a movie, and as a book, it would be less effective. While there are some negative elements to Finch, I honestly don't feel it's necessary to talk about them because they are negligible in the face of the experience itself.

GOOD. PLAY IT.\t\t\t\t\t
