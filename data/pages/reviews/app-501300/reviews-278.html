\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is, at face value, the story of a family supposedly under a curse. Told from the perspective of the final remaining member of the family, at just 17 years old, the player character, Edith Finch, is revisiting her ancestral home to hear the tales of the curse that claimed all of her family members. The game is short, it can be finished in about two hours, but there's a lot of detail here that is worth mentioning: the game never makes it expressly clear how most of the family members die, though some are admittedly easier to guess than others. The real genius behind this game, however, is that it gives you just enough detail to guess what will happen, then subverts your expectations with something else. Lewis' story is a great example of this: from the setup, you assume that his wandering attention leads to him accidently placing his hand under the fish chopper, but instead, expectetions are subverted and he places his neck under it instead (it's left ambiguous if he meant to do it or not). But a lot of stuff is left open for you to interpret and even guess at (sometimes accurately) before it's revealed to you. As an example, it's hinted several times that the player character is pregnant, from looking down and noticing the bump to her saying that the secret passages in the house are meant for smaller bellies before you are outright told so near the end. But the game's plot takes this one step further: it places outlier details at several points that leads to people being able to expand on the narrative, but the details themselves can be interpreted in so many ways where all or none could be true. One of my favorite theories about the game is that the story of a curse slowly killing the family is just that: a story perpetuated by the family matriarch which led to reckless actions and negligence. Overall, What Remains of Edith Finch has a deeply touching story that can be taken many different ways. I would share my own interpretation, but in this case, I feel that would dampen the experience for those who would play it themselves. Though these "walking simulator" type games aren't for everybody, I do feel that you can connect to at least one of the Finch's stories on a personal level, and that alone makes the entire game worth playing. A solid 9/10.\t\t\t\t\t

The story is so unique to me, and filled with darkness but also childhood whimsy (at least when it comes to all the cool hidey crawl spaces). While other people complain about not feeling particularly attached to any characters, I didn't mind because the story as a whole still hit feels of sadness and wonder. While having no favourite character I was still able to feel sad and disturbed for them all. The stories all had their own special way of playing through them which I really liked too. This felt like a good book or movie with lots of intentional (i think) open ends. You're left wondering. Easy to navigate, easy controls and smooth game play, but very short. Due to the length of time I would say get it on sale. I will probably replay in a couple years near Halloween for some dark atmospheric feels, even though it's not a horror game. I would say 8/10, and would definitely play as a bathtub toy frog again.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't get why so many people love this. Sure, the presentation is top notch and goes places I've never seen in any narrative game, but that's the thing - to me, it feels like this 3 hour experience is more about innovative presentation than an actual story. There's fragments of a story here, questions being asked without answers. In some cases it's not even clear what exactly happened to the family member in question unless you want to accept it was something completely ridiculous like magic or comic book monsters. Even the fate of Edith herself remains largely unexplained at the end. Is there a family curse or not? No idea. There's a scene that starts to go into the family history, but that scene is interrupted somewhere in the middle and you never get to experience the rest of it. It's like the developers didn't know the answer, either. They just wanted to create a mysterious atmosphere without explaining anything.

Gone Home was a complete story, this is not. For that reason I can't recommend it.\t\t\t\t\t
