\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor readers' own benefit, this review contains no spoiler about the plot. Rather, this review will focus on why What Remains of Edith Finch is an example of stories that can only be told by the video gaming medium.

As you can already know from the description on this Steam page, What Remains of Edith Finch is a walking simulator in which you, playing as Edith, return to the Finch house to find out what happened to other members of your family, stories of whom are presented as independent vignettes. While this sounds banal, the implementation is actually mind-blowing. Storytelling in typical walking simulators, or even most story rich games, is simplistic: you go to places, hear a narration or read some text, then move on, loop ad infinitum. That is not the case in What Remains of Edith Finch. To tell its stories, this game leverages the expressive power of nearly all major art forms: notably music, cinematography, comics, photography, choreography, poem, and painting in the vignettes, architecture and sculpture in the Finch house. On top of these rich presentations, the game adds to its recipe one final ingredient exclusive to video games: interactivity.

As a matter of fact, the developers have created 30 richly varied but simple control schemes (see this post on Game Developer for more details). Far from being just gimmicks, these control schemes subtly capture what makes video games fun to play, and they are specifically designed and adapted to each story with scrupulous attention to balance between interaction and narration. On one hand, the control schemes endow the stories with an immersion and resonance that those stories alone could not have if delivered in any other form of art. On the other hand, the mechanics are deliberately kept simple and intuitive: all control schemes fit in two analog sticks and one trigger button, and you do not need button prompts (which there are nearly none in this game) to know what to do even for the first time. Environments and interactions are also carefully constrained in order not to overwhelm players with information or to distract them from the stories.

That fine balance between interaction and narration, also known as ludonarrative resonance, is already a feat since video games as a medium can be a double-edged sword. Too many story rich games lean to either side of the equation: Some have stories that are fragmented or upstaged by their (sometimes unnecessarily) complex mechanics; Others have nonexistent or tedious gameplay and could very well be made into a novel/film/animation without losing much artistic elements. What Remains of Edith Finch gets the balance right, by selecting mechanics based on whether they serve the goals, not because they are complex and cool. Under these techniques, the game also offers a whole package of top-notch contents: both interesting gameplay and intriguing stories (albeit not necessarily to everyone's liking, being tragic, dark, eerie, and sometimes surreal), all within a stunning photo-realistic environments filled with meticulous details which make the scenes alive and immersive.

Another innovative narrative design of What Remains of Edith Finch lies in its use of words (see this mechanics analysis by Rock Paper Shotgun for more details). In this game, subtitles of narration dialogues appear as if they were part of the environment. Their location has a signposting role, hinting where you are supposed to go next and telling you that you are doing the right thing. They are there to gently and subconsciously nudge you into the right direction, and to give you a constant sense of progression. Sometimes, how these words appear or disappear even conveys emotional values.

In short, if video games were to be considered as an art form, then What Remains of Edith Finch would be one of the finest examples, as it is the most well-rounded title in terms of visual imagery, audio design, and interactive storytelling. In addition, the game is definitely not a good-looking but dull museum piece, but really fun to play. Although short in length (a first playthrough typically lasts 2-3 hours), What Remains of Edith Finch has much replayability. And its chapter replay functionality allows you to instantly dive back in your favourite segment, whether to ruminate on its story, to reexperience its gameplay, or to search for environmental details neglected by your first playthrough.

I sincerely urge you to play What Remains of Edith Finch. And I wish there would be more titles like it in the gaming industry.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInitially I didn't know what to write about this game, so I decided to take some time to ponder on it... And now I think I understand why I had no writing ideas after the walkthrough. It is hard to recommend What Remains Of Edith Finch simply because it is much more of a subjective emotional experience rather than an objective rational one. If you simply start to describe the gameplay, story, etc it will not transfer even the quarter of the actual experience. Nevertheless, I will still try to explain why you should or shouldn't try this game.

First things first, graphics: they are is good, vivid, atmospheric and even small minor textures have a decent quality - that's all that required from it really.

Gameplay is essentially the standard walking simulator with occasional additions of simple mini games to diversify the process. As in most walking simulators 90% of the gameplay consists of environment exploration and What Remains Of Edith Finch has no problems with it. The house and its surroundings are interesting to explore and the overall duration of the game does not let it become boring.

The music is simply beautiful: it fits the tone, it enhances the atmosphere, the soundtrack is worth buying separately. I can't say much more about it just because it is vey connected to the plot which we will move to in a second.

The story. Well, this is the reason the game is so loved by fans and praised by critics. I will not write any particular details here, firstly, because I do not want to spoil your experience, secondly, because I will not be able to convey its wonderful nature anyway. Nevertheless, I will say that it is one of the few indie games which story you really want to analyze and discuss with others. Not because it is particularly controversial or abstract but rather because of it is well-designed structure. The plot has a strong defined basis with ideas and facts but at the time it is specifically flavored with some ambiguity and open-to-interpretation parts, which overall makes the experience for each player different and unclear enough to be willing for them to discuss it. Of course, I have my own explanation of the story which I personally consider to be pretty logical but I do not think I will share it. Not because I'm greedy and not even because, after all, it is a review rather than analysis but because looking for answers yourself after completing the game is a substantial part of the total experience. Sure, nobody prevents you from going to youtube or internet to read the interpretations of other people but I would still encourage you to think about the story yourself or at least do not stop on the first article/video that pops up.

So, what can I say in conclusion? If you are a fan of good stories that make you think or you are ready to give such an experience a try - this is a great choice. If you are looking for something gameplay-oriented or challenging you can safely skip this one.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch

Positives:
+ Beautiful graphics
+ Very nicely designed levels
+ Cool and detailed house
+ Beautiful and tragic stories
+ Nice voiceover and voiceacting
+ Great sound and music
+ The atmosphere
+ Makes you reflect about life
+ Good variety
+ Not boring for one second
+ The Barbara Finch story part is by far the coolest
+ A big piece of art

Negatives:
- Low FOV
- A bit quirky controls
- Some parts are not fantastic (but still good)
- Too short (I want more!)

Well this game blew me over.
I've heard that it was great, but I thought people were over exaggerating.
But as it turns out, the game deserves every positive review it got.
If you like walking simulators, art, a good story, something out of the ordinary, this is the game to play. Highly recommended!

Total Score: 9/10\t\t\t\t\t
