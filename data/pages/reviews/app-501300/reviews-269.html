\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI recommend playing for the highlights: the few stories where the game is at its gut-wrenching best.

Beyond that? I don't know. I feel like its core thesis is extremely weak, which is basically a death sentence for pure-narrative games. I'll explain
*SPOILERS*


Throughout the game you explore the deaths of each and every member of the Finch family. The protagonist, Edith, has returned to the abandoned homestead essentially for this process; to learn about her family in a hollistic sense. However, all she learns is that they all died, some in ways quasi-ironically linked to the "family curse," only not really. There's precious little hubris, or much of anything to learn from most of the stories, just grim irony and sad stories of sad people. Adding to this flat-note is the fact that she actually knew quite a lot of the stories anyway, having been in the house when they happened. The core contrivance is at least 45% unnecessary, and she gets no closer to understanding her family or their relationship to the curse.

And the curse itself is just as muddled an idea, compounded by the fact that EVEN EDITH is dead by the credits. The thesis the game tries to convey with this, through the continued life of Edith's now-born child, is that life is precious enough to commit to with all you have, curse or no curse.

But whatever the developers intended, the family are so clearly, literally cursed that it beggars belief, and I came away utterly perplexed that Edith would bother bringing a child into the world at all when the chances of it living past 10 are statistically very poor indeed. Life, for the lion's share of the Finches, was an unworthy blip, and when those Finches died it traumatised the remainder. Killing Edith at the end only compounds the paper-thing silliness of the whole affair, and it would have been a much smarter decision to simply leave her condition unknown at the end of the story. Because I can't help but feel as it stands that the kindest course for a Finch to take would be antinatalism. After all, we have no indication that Edith is equipped to provide for her kid anyway.

Note that this is more than simply the plot being materially silly. It's a statement for living the builds itself on ridiculously shaky foundations that cannot support its weight, made more absurd by the pithy simplicity of the statement in the first place.

Stay for the charming visuals and clever narrative control, but don't engage too keenly with what it wants to say overall. It'll sour the experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great, linear walking simulator story. One where you enjoy letting yourself sit back and have it told to you. Some of the deaths are sadder than others, some of them are more ambiguous than others. The narrative cleverly makes the subtitles part of the scenery, leading you along. I never got lost or found myself not knowing where to go.

Very few gripes with this one, if any. It is a bit unbelievable so many people died in one family line, but the game explains this with a curse (it's stretching my suspension of disbelief a bit, and felt more like a way for the developers to have an excuse to fit so many creative deaths into one narrative, but that in no way takes away from the stories and the experience).

Good graphics, had no problems with the voice acting (which is good, because these voices are talking to you a lot as they tell their stories), no issues with controls. Good ending. Ties it all together beautifully.

Took me two hours to complete, so I don't know about getting it full price, but I'd definitely recommend getting it, whether at full price or on sale. Up to you.\t\t\t\t\t
