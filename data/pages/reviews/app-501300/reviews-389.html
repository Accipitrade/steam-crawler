\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a lot like Gone Home. A walking simulator, experiencing an incredibly lived in but cozy house, finding notes left behind by family after some major event, and piecing together a narrative from these notes. It's (essentially) a one map game for $20 that relies on the narration and story to justify itself, with the only challenges being tiny puzzles to get into the next room.

Well, not exactly. This game is *way way* better than Gone Home because it just does everything noticeably better. Where Gone Home's house relied a bit heavily on 80s nostalgia, Edith Finch uses a sort of fantasy house, impossibly made with rooms locked in their own style, where every room is locked in its style and time period, due to story elements the game reveals. Gone Home never gets past finding notes and occasionally finding keys or secret passageways to vary the experience, while Edith Finch will use different gameplay or perspectives during major story events to make every event distinct and often times to make the events far more impactful than you expected. These gameplay hooks are further complimented by some of the best voicework and musical cues that I've heard in any media.

I liked a lot of other parts of Edith Finch besides that, but it seems unfair to criticize Gone Home because they're mainly artistic differences. I liked piecing together the Finch's family "curse" more than the story of a teenager falling in love, and I liked that it was at times done in a nonlinear fashion. I liked that every family member in Finch featured a new narrator and even art style, but some of the gameplay is a little goofy or dumb, while Gone Home's didn't really stray from what was working.

It's hard to imagine a walking simulator being near as good as Edith Finch though. It plays to the strengths of the medium and doesn't distract from what it does good too much. I would almost recommend watching the experience somewhere, or just looking up the good scenes, but you really lose something if you aren't playing it from the start. I really think the beauty and tragedy of each death is accentuated when you're playing it and experiencing it from first person. I don't want to type of the philosophical ramblings people can make about death, or muse about the story bits that are intentionally left out, but yeah.

It's good. It's real good. I don't know if I can ever forget the morbid beauty this game created, and I'm thankful for that. But if you didn't like Gone Home (due to being a one map, sub 2 hour $20 walking simulator, not because of LGBT politics), you might not like Edith Finch for the same reason.\t\t\t\t\t
