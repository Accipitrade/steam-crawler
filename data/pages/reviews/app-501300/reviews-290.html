\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOh man.. What a journey. This game contains it all: humor, fantasy, depression.. you name it. The whole trip is fairly short but very tightly packaged; best to think of this as an interactive movie.

---Mild spoiler filled premise below---

Basically, you start off the game as a Edith Finch's -- the titular character-- progeny who is on their way to the Edith mansion on a ship. You are holding Edith's journal, opening which transports you to playing Edith. The whole premise is that Edith has returned to her ancestral home after many years. Her mother believed that the Finch family is cursed and getting away from the house would break that curse. To that effect, you are told fairly early in the game that the the curse relates to untimely, and sometimes mysterious, deaths in the family. Edith, you are told is the last Finch alive in her time. The entire game revolves around you controlling Edith as she explores the house and uncovers secret passages to enter sealed rooms previously occupied by past Finch family members. Each room contains a memoir about that person interacting with which takes you to their timeline, playing out the last hour of their life. This is so beautifully handled that I cannot being to stress how important it is to take in all details.

This isn't an exploration game that requires you find and collect hidden objects, you don't worry about missing out on hidden content. In fact, all interactable objects are indicated, so just go through the world slowly.

Another really good indicator of slow and steady exploration is the dialog. As you move to different areas, Edith will voice out her thoughts relating to where you are. These dialogs slowly build the whole narrative but can be skipped if you rush through places. Refer to the text that accompanies such dialog: it floats out into the world and hangs in the air, so use that to measure the pace. It also important to note that his game is about experiencing the story. So, again, take it slow and sink into the world.

I was truly left speechless after the end. It's a sad story, so I don't want to say enjoy. You know what I mean?\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have played a lot of walking simulators, but this one surpasses them all. The technical aspects are top notch. I loved the quirky "level" design, leading you from room to room and fate to fate. I adored the voice acting: Valerie Rose Lohman (voice of the main character, Edith) delivers one of the best vocal performances in a computer game, since Sarah Hamilton in 'The Longest Journey'. The graphics and music seamlessly enhance every slight switch of mood, from the comical to the deeply tragic.

But. What I enjoyed the most was the bravery of the designers to not make the game longer than it needed to be (I think it took me 4 hours to finish). It does not overstay its welcome but trusts the story that it wants to tell.

Which leads me to the writing: Brilliant, beautiful, and profound. I finished this game a few days ago and I am still thinking about it.

I have learned that "walking simulator" has negative connotations, but here it is just a method of opening a world of such playful love of tropes, genres, and game mechanics, as a way of conveying raw emotion. Should some not find it much of a computer game, I would argue that it is because it transcends this. It is art.\t\t\t\t\t
