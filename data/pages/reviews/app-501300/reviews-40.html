\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere's enough interaction and (very light)puzzle solving to muddy What Remains of Edith Finch's place among the usually hands-off walking simulator genre. The game seemingly attempts to correct that potential miscategorization by making the actual walking speed painfully slow in many places, and that's coming from someone who does want to take their time to see every little bit of environmental storytelling possible.

Though I do have to laud the mixing of the environmental and the storytelling on display here. Everything you're experiencing is explicitly a written recollection after the fact. Some are recountings within recountings, but all of them are being read to you while you're experiencing them. The words themselves will manifest around you as they're read to you. In some cases, you'll need to wrangle up those jumbled words to hear them instead of the also used, but more classic, mechanic of unlocking narration by interacting with specific objects.

While Edith Finch does more to play with its framing device than most, it is ultimately a story being read to you after the fact at largely its own pace. There is little you can do to change that and nothing you can do to alter its outcomes. That will be especially hard for some to accept when you're forced into committing the specific actions that originally carried the tale forward. These are memories after all, however fantastical they may be. But they are beautiful, fascinating memories in a truly intriguing place and are thus well worth the couple of hours spent exploring them.\t\t\t\t\t
