\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is an interesting game. Of course some might argue: does it even qualify as a game? It's one of those walking simulators (spit dirty word spit) but in total fairness to it I would say that it's the best I've played within that genre.

It's certainly pretty enough to look at and the music is suitably dreamy for the subject matter. Voice acting ticks the boxes as well.

I don't really want to spoil anything as I do think this is the sort of game you want to go into as blind as possible. What I will say is that it is essentially a group of short stories that do relate to one another in some overarching sense. The quality of the stories were a bit of a mixed bag with some feeling a bit underdone and others were I have to say quite brilliant.

I would personally recommend What Remains of Edith Finch but if you don't like walking sims then, obviously, stay well away. The other thing to note is that it is incredibly short as is standard for this genre. Get it as cheap as you possibly can and you might find some value here if just for the sake of something a bit different.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI had no idea going in that this game would or could possibly connect with me so quickly and so powerfully. It's a beautiful, wonderful game, telling a narrative and experience that is only possible through a game. It's essentially a series of short stories, with the through line being Edith, as she explores her childhood home, which just happens to have one room for each member of her family that has died. You go room to room, experiencing the story of that family member, as you make your way through the house until you've learned all about the family, and all that's left is Edith's own.

Navigating as Edith is varied enough to keep it interesting, with hidden key holes, peepholes to find, and interesting architecture to discover. It never feels like a slog, and quickly moves from story to story, with interesting and touching narration as you move between rooms.

The stories themselves are all quite unique, with different gameplay mechanics taking focus for each story. One you play as various animals; another you multi-task a boring job and wonder a fantasy world at the same time; another you are a boy on a swing enjoying a summer day.

This game really surprised me with how fast it was able to make me care about these characters, and in the end care about the story as a whole. It was very powerful, touching and made me want to wake my kids up and hug them, as a lot of themes in this game will remind you just how special life can be.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat is death?

Story (minor spoiler alert):
In "What Remains of Edith Finch", the player re-experiences the memories of the Edith's ancestors in very strange ways. Yes, it's a story-driven walking/clicking simulator, but it invokes an ambiguous feeling that lies somewhere amongst curiosity, uneasiness, and disgust. The Finch family perceives early, accidental deaths as an inevitable curse, and instead of fighting it, the family believes it's something to be proud of, something worth bragging to their children. There are hints of it throughout the game whether it's the furniture, the environment, or even the way "you" narrate, and the game does an absolutely brilliant job at presenting this ugly mindset of "dying is okay". The Finch family glorifies death in such a way that putting oneself at risk isn't reckless but rather "normal" for a Finch.

Gameplay:
While the gameplay is a bit linear (you can only experience it in one order), the compactness of meaningful content as well as the picturesque scenery makes up for the lack of freedom. In fact, I'd argue it's the lack of freedom that makes this game so free. Don't rush through the story: before turning a door knob or opening a diary, take a step back, and look around. Maybe you see a pair of sneakers on the ground, or some fast food containers knocked off the table, or some strange sign in the distance... So much of the game is not told but experienced through careful observation. The game is not long -- one can complete it around 2 hours if they just rushed through the story. Through the family pictures on the wall, local newspapers left on the desk, tools, toys, drawings, etc. you learn so much about who Edith's ancestors were before they died. Even if you're not interested in the small details, the main story itself feels surreal despite being set in a realistic world.

Is it worth it:
It's such a common question that's asked along with "how long is the gameplay" or "is it fun". The game without discount is 20 dollars, but the gameplay is only around 2.5 hr to 4hr. Does it mean it's not worth it? Absolutely not. The game does not have any flashy mechanics or cutscenes done with unlimited budget (still extremely good), but it carries across a strong message. The game is unapologetically straight forward in how they portray the Finches as this "special" family that's cursed. There will be disturbing moments that will leave you thinking for days, and that's one of the traits of a good game in my book. You won't be told what's right because even the narrator herself is biased. The game encourages you to think not the what-if's but rather the why's, and if that's not worth 20 bucks, I don't know what is.\t\t\t\t\t
