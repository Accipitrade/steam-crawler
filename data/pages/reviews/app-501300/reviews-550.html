\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've struggled for a few days on how to review Edith Finch, as I'm finding the words to describe my emotional response to it lacking.

It's a walking simulator, and presents a mystery - "What happened to the rest of the Finches?" By the end of the game, that's no longer really the question. For me, it became "How do you reconcile the past?" Having lost my grandmother to cancer almost a year ago, this game was a gut wrench of a game to play through.
Through Edith Finch, I was reminded that the stories that are left behind become the most precious parts of the person who is gone - and Edith Finch exemplifies that, I think.

The game play is pretty simple - you interact with objects and experience short story vignettes that give you clues to what happened to the Finches while Edith narrates. I found the game play differences between the vignettes to be really interesting and their own challenge - especially Lewis' story.

It's incredible to me that such a simple premise left me feeling emotionally bereft after it's completion.\t\t\t\t\t

However this is a VERY SHORT game. I finished in about 2h30 even though I didn't rush at all. So take that into consideration when evaluating tbe price.

In addition, while it's truly special, the game isn't without faults. I found the controls clumsy and frustrating during several of the "memory" sequences. And the ending was unoriginal (which came as a surprise compared with the rest of the game) and even, dare I say, distateful.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game, like many other "walking simulators" (Firewatch, Dear Esther, Gone Home, Ethan Carter, etc) is not for those who are looking for engaging gameplay. It's just an interactive story. However, If you're into these kinds of games then you know just how pleasant they can be, immersing you in stories.

After finishing Edith Finch, I can honestly say that this game, while interesting, did not end the way I expected. The game plays almost like a mystery, leading up to the solving or reveal of a secret......that never happens. Instead, the game ends in a rather cliche way that left me rather dssatisfied, and with many unanswered questions, the most glaring of which is WHAT IS THE CURSE?? It absolutely infuriated me that the developers didn't at least somewhat answer this question, and instead just leaves the player to believe that the Finches are simply the most extraordinariliy unlucky family ever, doomed to untimely deaths regardless of their choices.

With all that said, if you're thinking about this game, I would recommend one of the many other great games in this genre first.

Also, the story of Gregory is one of the most disturbing things I've ever experienced in all my years of gaming.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI highly recommend this game if you love story telling. Because this fascinating game is more than just a walking simulator, it's a series of short, sad stories each told with a unique enviroment and game mechanic. It's beautiful and meticulous in design. A climb down the family tree of the Finches, who seem to have the worst luck. It leaves you a little lost, heartbroken, but maybe hopeful.

I wouldn't recommend this game if you can't live without all the answers. Some mysteries will not be solved. Some stories will not be told in full. If everything has to be tied up with a bow at the end to fully satisfy you, you may want to move on.

I wouldn't recommend this game if you've personally lost a child. A large part of the game is reliving the final moments of close family members, many of them children. I cannot imagine how this might affect someone who'd really suffered such a loss. Maybe it would be therapeutic, but I don't think I'd want to find out.

Lastly, this is a short game, so if you don't feel like you can invest money into something like an exquisite piece of art because it doesn't continue entertaing you after three hours of looking at it, then you should save yourself the refund delay and just don't bother. Yes, it's short, but it's also very good. I'll likely play it again with my wife, and may pick it up again after a year or two has passed and I've forgotten it. There is replay value here, and possibly more to discover in the details of a cluttered, rickety house in the middle of no where.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSuch an intriguing, heartbreaking story! I loved how the game changes up its controls to match each family vignette. It keeps the gameplay fresh, and it connects to the actions you the player are doing to the theme of the vignette. The best (and also most brutal) example of this is Lewis' story.

The downside is that a few of those changing controls are frustrating. I'm specifically thinking of the can opener scene in Walter's story.

For completionists, the story is pretty linear, and I'd say the chances of you skipping over sections is small. However, I did almost finish the game without completing Milton's vignette. If missing a vital piece of story bother you (as it does me), do your best to pay attention to details so you don't miss anything.\t\t\t\t\t
