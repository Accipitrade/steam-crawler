\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat a wonderful way to tell a story.

This isn't so much a game as it is an interactive story. The game mechanics are very simple. WASD to move around, mouse to look, click to interact. Occasionally you'll have to move the mouse a bit to turn a key or similar. There's not much to it, but game play isn't why you're here...

You're here for the story, and what beautiful, whimsical, haunting, and engaging story it is. It draws you in and makes you want to know more of the story. The way the story is presented is simply fantastic! It's hard to write a review of this to much depth without giving it away, but I'll say the ending gave me waves of chills.

The only thing that would make this game better (more immersive) was if it was done in VR.\t\t\t\t\t

this game is as enchanting as it is haunting. it's kind of like a dream that turns into a nightmare, then back into a dream again, and so on-- you know what will happen, it makes you ache on the inside, but you still go along with it all because it's so beautiful and immersive.

it's conceived and written with a lot of care. the game is never distasteful with its subject matter despite the fact that it has serious triggers. if you're sensitive to suicide or child death especially, thread carefully.

the mechanics/minigames are all engaging, which directly contributes both to how enjoyable an experience it is and to conveying the game's message. special mention for the times you turn into animals and how wonderful and real it feels-- who doesn't want to be a cat or a bird for a moment?

the only criticism i have is about the fact that edith's father doesn't get a story, which stands out since he did die from the curse as well (presumably). she barely knows anything about him and about her heritage from that side of the family, which is also a bit odd. i really wish they owned up to having a mixed asian lead and didn't shove his story under the carpet like that.\t\t\t\t\t

Unfortunately, one of the strongest chapters, Gregory's, was spoiled to me, but I saw the rest with unexpecting eyes, and what a sensitive and poignant game this turned out to be.

If the Disney film Fantasia was made into a walking simulator set in the world of Life is Strange, it would probably look something like this. Every one of the dozen or so chapters plays out in a different way, some of them more traditional and some of them complete curveballs (Barbara's being perhaps the strangest, and one of the most beautiful levels I've ever seen in any video game, Lewis', which I will never forget).

What Remains of Edith Finch is why I love video games. A wonderful work of art.\t\t\t\t\t
