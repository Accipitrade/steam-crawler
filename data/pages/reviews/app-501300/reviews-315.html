\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you have any friends that are skeptical of the 'walking sim' genre and their contribution to gaming and art in general, have them try this. It really is the pinnacle of what the genre is capable of. It's as captivating as any film, but also forces you to interact with the world constantly, reminding you that it is still a game. It is moving, haunting, and you'll be thinking about it for days after you finish it.

The game narrator of the game is Edith Finch, but the majority of the game is composed of small flashbacks/vignettes from the perspective of other Finch family members. Which leads to my only criticism: the first mini-story is not so good at all, its ambiguity doesn't add to the game's intended arc, and you'll have to convince your friends to slog through it before they'll see how deep and meaningful the game becomes.\t\t\t\t\t

Long version
It’s interesting.
I must say it is more an interactive story rather than a game.
It left me with mixed feelings. On one side, we have the beauty of life, to appreciate our time here, to be thankful; on the other, we have tragedy and impotency that borders on dark humor.
I finished the game, along with its achievements but I don’t recommend earning them unless you want the XP as they don’t do much. Just finishing the game should do.

It left me saddened that I couldn’t do much. Not talking about the mechanics but rather the story. You are but a passerby, the stories have already been written and you are just going along, like a boat with no veil.
Still, I enjoyed the story. It felt like reading a good book that stays with you.
