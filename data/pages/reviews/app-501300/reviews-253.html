\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat remains of Edith Finch is a gorgeous walking simulator. The game is a treat for the eyes with immaculate attention to detail. The skills put into designing this world are seamlessly met with the imagination of the writers. The story-line is engaging and voice acting is sublime. However, progression is absolutely linear and is nearly devoid of any puzzles or gameplay mechanics. On top of that, the game itself is short (under 3 hours from start to finish) and priced reasonably high. Overall, this game resembles a movie/walkthrough simulator, as the player explores a story through an interactive video game. Even though the story is interesting and carries profound messages, such as how we choose to face tragedies and adapt to life unfolding, I could not help but feel restricted by the lack of gameplay. In my opinion, video-games styled like What Remains of Edith Finch are ineffective in presenting a story with as much gravitas as movies; hence, each field should stick to what it does best.

Would only recommend if on deep sale.
6.5/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I was a child, I imagined that there was a place where everything I lost ended up. All the toys, pencils, and other stuff you care about as a kid should’ve ended up in that magical world.
That’s a weird way to start a review, I know, but it has been on my mind, recently.

This game is about death and, though it skips the gravitas, the grief, that accompanies most depictions of it, it doesn’t skip the compassion.
In real life, death is often tragic, yes, but it’s also often wonderful, or ironically fitting. The curse of death, then, is more our inability to cope with it than the event itself.

My mother passed when I was twelve. Not that it is relevant to any of this, but, at her funeral, I didn’t cry. I was mostly concerned with where to stand, as to not inconvenience anyone. Grief, as told in most stories, is alien to me. Death is something too complicated to process with tears.

This game consists of a small amount of wonderful and ironically fitting deaths, all told from the perspective of someone trying to cope with them. A mother, a brother, or the person themselves, all looking at that end, trying to understand.

I sometimes imagine a field next to my childhood home to be that place I talked about, where everything I’ve lost since my childhood is washed up. I tell myself, if that were true, and I waited long enough, then a tiny figure would appear on the horizon across the field, and gradually get larger until I’d recognize it’s my mother. She’d wave, maybe call.
The fantasy never goes further than that. I don’t let it.

What I wonder about, is if the the Finches are any different from the rest of us. Maybe none of us truly understand what we’ve lived through, or felt as if we’ve had enough time.\t\t\t\t\t
