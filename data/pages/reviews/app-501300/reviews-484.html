\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wish I could give this game a "neutral" review, since it's not outright terrible, nor good enough to deserve a thumbs-up review. However, I feel compelled to give it a thumbs-down review to counteract some of the naively gushing positive reviews.

To summarize a friend of mine: "if Edith Finch were a movie or book, it would be boring, but because it's a game, it's novel". Also, if you look at the Steam reviews for this game and filter by negative only, the top-voted review from SlavFox critiques this game perfectly.

First of all, this game is not nearly as "revolutionary" or "unique" as many positive reviews would claim, though, it is certainly different than >98% of other "games" on Steam. That said, there are plenty of other great story-driven games that preceeded this one and which managed to be both more enjoyable and interesting (Firewatch, Gone Home, Dear Esther, Her Story, The Vanishing of Ethan Carter, to name but a few). The biggest flaw in this game is that it's basically an interactive movie, and a dull one at that. As you railroad your way through the house and click to advance the story, there is no challenge, skill, or effort involved. That isn't necessarily a bad thing (not all games need to be puzzle- or skill-based), but there is also no intellectual effort either. There is nothing about the story of this game which is emotionally or intellectually challenging.

Graphically, the game is really beautiful and it is enjoyable to watch the story unfold. It's just that the story is rather trite and not gripping, even though there are a few glimpses of real potential, namely the scene in the canning factory (and probably only that).

Ostensibly, this game is about a family curse which took the lives of everyone before their respective primes, but very few of the stories actually focus on that theme. In fact, most of the scenes in the game are just random windows into a family member's life. Most of them don't shed any light on how they died, which would provide intrigue for the family-curse storyline. Likewise, the majority of these stories are too brief and superficial to give one an emotional connection to the character, nor to understand their relationship to others in the family. Thus, Edith Finch feels more like a random montage of unconnected minigame stories rather than a story told using a video game as its medium.

Edith Finch feels like it had some great ideas, but is overall a bit disconnected and flat. I think that this game would have been more interesting had there been fewer characters but profiled them in greater detail, and also with more of a connection between them. Although it is beaufitully executed, unfortunatelly the core concept and story are lacking.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is certainly the most interesting example of a “walking simulator” out there, mainly because it isn’t just about walking. I’ve had mixed experiences with the genre, while I enjoyed The Stanley Parable for its interactivity and humour and the beginners guide is a great story with great use of environment stuff like Firewatch, which has interesting ideas, it just fails to utilise them well and Dear Esther, which is just bad, have made me doubt it somewhat. Edith Finch threw any worries I’ve ever had about this genre away and made me hopeful to have many more great experiences like it. Edith Finch knows the best way to tell a story in a game is through interactivity. Each short story about the death of a family member makes such well use of your control that it’s the kind of thing I’d like to see across all kind of games of all different genres. I won’t talk much about the specifics of the story to avoid spoilers, but while it is good the big attraction is how it tells that story and I hope that’s something that will be very influential in the future.\t\t\t\t\t
