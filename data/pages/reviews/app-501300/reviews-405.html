\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a good example of a game that got so wrapped up in itself, and fell in love with its own bittersweet-nostalgia style of storytelling, that it forgot to actually make it a fun experience to play. I'm not going to touch on the gameplay side of things, since this game clearly wasn't designed with that at the forefront, and simply used minigames as a mechanic to tell stories.

So I'll focus on the storytelling. This includes spoilers.

Some of the stories of the lives/deaths of the Finch family members were compelling, but due to how short each one was, most had to condense each tragedy into a few short and impactful minutes. More often than not, they fell short of being capable of generating much emotional resonance. Some stories, like the father who was knocked off a cliff by the buck his daughter shot, were even unintentionally hilarious.

The game relies more on tugging at the heart strings than it does explaining the 'family curse', too. It tries to find beauty in the brief and transient journey we call life, even as it descends into death, but ultimately it doesn't do a very good job of attaching you emotionally to the story. And it completely fails to explain what's going on, instead hinting around the edges and leaving you to fill in the gaps. That's okay sometimes in storytelling, but the gaps in this story are so large you could probably stuff a Finch family home into them.

I think the game is beautiful, the voice acting is superb, and the music / sound design is first class. For that alone, it's worth checking this short game out - just don't go into it expecting the story to leave any lasting impression.\t\t\t\t\t
