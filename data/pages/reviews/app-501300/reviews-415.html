\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, I'd bought this game a long time ago. It took me awhile because I knew it was a walking simulator, and I'd been burned on those before. When I read about this game though, I thought, "maybe this will be a good one, then." Eventually I bought it on a sale, but never played it.

Always wanted to, but never did.

I think the reason why was that... it had been built up so much that if I played it, I wouldn't want to reach the end, because something special would end... that I'd have to leave a special place.

And ya know, I was right.

This game (well, walking simulator) affected me in a way few games have. It really is a special game... it has a melencholy story that I related to quite a bit. The imagination is nearly unmatched in the gaming space. (And I've been gaming since 1981.) It has great voice acting, and well.... I suppose I could go on, but who knows if anyone will see this buried under all the other reviews.

I highly recommend this game. It's one of the highest "recommends" I felt I've ever made. You really owe it to yourself to play this. Once you do you'll understand why.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed What Remains of Edith Finch way more than I thought I would. I'd heard a lot of good things about this game, but I was just a bit blown away by just how alive this game feels. The world, the story, the characters, and how it is put together all feels very "typical storybook" yet very realistic at the same time. What Remains of Edith Finch is a walking simulator that at the same time feels like reading a book and or viewing a work of art.

You walk through the Finch household, traveling to each family member's bedroom to learn the story of how they died. In a house where the bedrooms remain closed off after each family member passes and a new room added with each new addition to the family tree, the house is a massive collection of stories for you to uncover. Each family member's story is presented in such a light hearted way, (via a literal story book in most cases) that makes everything seem cheerful and happy, until you look into the deep meaning of what story is being told to you. The morbid meaning in this story is so veiled in happy storytelling, it makes you sit back and actually think carefully about what you're being told with the context given. To top off an amazing story, the ending will (at the very least) leave you wiping your eyes and wanting to hug a loved one.

I've never played or seen a game with a premise like What Remains of Edith Finch, it's a very unique gem of a game that everyone should try. The world the house resides in and the world presented in each family member's story are very creative and unique to each other, it's easy to tell after having played the game which art style belongs to which individual story. There is, of course, a very central theme of family but also a story of loss and a lot presented to you that makes you think past the surface.

While I did very much enjoy this game, my main complaint is the price point for a 2 hour story. While the story is genuinely amazing, there's not much replay value and what you see is really what you get. That is potentially a major turn off for some people. If the cost is a sore point for you, you may not want to pick up this game or wait until a good sale to grab it.

If you want to feel like you're walking through a story book, absolutely try this game. It's certainly an amazing experience that can't be matched by most other walking simulators out there right now.\t\t\t\t\t
