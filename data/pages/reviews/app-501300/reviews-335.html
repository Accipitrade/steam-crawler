A Tragedy For the Ages

What Remains of Edith Finch is a narrative adventure game developed by Giant Sparrow. You play as Edith Finch as she returns to her family home to face the ghosts of her family’s past. As you explore the house, you will piece together your family’s history and uncover their tragic stories.

Positives:
  • The game is absolutely beautiful. The visuals are stunning, with particularly good use of lighting, and the way some of the stories are stylized is excellent. The way subtitles of the narration are painted on the world around you was one of my favorite aspects of this title too.

  • The narrative is flawless and incredibly engaging. Finding out about all of Edith’s family member’s and experiencing their stories is utterly enthralling and the constant emotional gut punches make it all the more compelling. I thought the ending was excellent as well.

  • The voice acting is delivered convincingly across the board. I particularly like the VA for Edith, but there wasn’t a single voice that stuck out or seemed lower quality than the rest. The rest of the audio in the game is also excellent.

  • The different stories are all wonderfully unique, and a few of them are essentially minigames. They really reminded me of “A Series of Unfortunate Events” so if you’re a fan of that property, I’m sure you’ll enjoy this game.

Neutral:
  • Some may consider the $20 price tag to be too much for a 3 hour game, while I did buy this on sale I would have been happy paying full price for it. If you think it costs too much, just wait for a sale.

Verdict:

What Remains of Edith Finch joins the ranks of the best narrative games I’ve played with ease, perhaps taking the top spot. This is a beautiful tragedy, and one that I will not forget in a hurry. Simply, sublime.

What Remains of Edith Finch: What is it?
A narrative-driven walking simulator taking you through the unfortunate history of a family fraught with untimely deaths.


Gameplay
The exploration is very light. While there are small things to discover, most of the journey happens on rails. You interact with items in the environment and move your mouse or press movement keys to manipulate them. While I like the concept, the mouse controls don't work very well and make everything look choppy and detract from the immersion.

Between exploration, you get to experience vignettes of the various family members at the time of their deaths. These are where the gameplay shines, as many of them expose you to different "minigames" that are unique and fanciful distractions with no two being the same. The minigames are great and imaginative for the most part, but they can look and feel a bit clunky at times.


Story
The overarching story, strangely, is not really the main focus. Rather, the focus is on the side stories following the family members' deaths and the relationships between the family as a whole. A few are rather emotional and tragic, with the narration from their relatives making things even more impactful. However, I feel like the pacing was off. The most emotional moments happened in the first half of the game--at least for me--and never really reached that height again. I was left wanting more. And while I liked the overall theme of Edith Finch, it was sadly lacking in the delivery. The end felt like a "That's it?" sort of moment.


Aesthetics
Though the graphics can be a little rough around the edges, the aesthetics are wonderful. Details abound throughout the house. I couldn't believe how much effort the devs must have put into even the littlest things like notes and papers. Very rarely found clutter that was too blurry to read. It felt like everything was meticulously arranged, minus a handful of repeated models. It's a treat to take in the surroundings.

Many of the vignettes explore different artistic directions and perspectives. Edith Finch feels very unique in that regard. You never know what you're going to experience next, and much of it can be described as things you can only experience as a video game.

The voice acting is very well done and the sound design is solid.


Complaints
  • Clunky controls and jittery animations.
  • Lots of invisible walls.
  • Poor narrative pacing that unfortunately soured the game.


Conclusion
A unique and imaginative experience that is possibly over-hyped. I wouldn't call it a great game, just good.


My Rating: 3/5

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEh? Sure. The Artwork, Graphics, Sounds, and Programming are all phenominal, and lead to several truly standout moments that I'll remember for quite a while. Aside from those few standout moments/segments, the "Walking Simulator" argument is valid and i did find there were many moments i wished i could've walked just a bit faster. Controls are also bizarre and clunky, i beat the game and I'm still not sure how the interaction system actually works and in what direction to be pulling the mouse in order to get things right. To be fair though, I just recently played The Room trilogy, a puzzle game with similar controls that were intuitive and refined to a crisp, much better than what can be found here. "The Game was only 2 hours long," but at the same time, with what the game was clearly doing, 2 hours was about the right length for both the story and the gameplay. That does still make it tricky to recommend for $20 though. I have many friends who would not spend more than $5 unless they knew they were getting several dozen hours out of the purchase.

Is this a bad game? No of course not, but be sure to research and make sure that the story interests you and keep that in consideration if and when you make the purchase. I didn't mind the purchase, especially because of those standout segments, but frankly I wasn't all that invested in the story, and was surprised to see I had beaten the game in 2 hours.\t\t\t\t\t
