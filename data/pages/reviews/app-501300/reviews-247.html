\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI rate What Remains of Edith Finch 1/5. Bad. This short, linear, first-person perspective, pretentious, emotionally heavy-handed, tear-jerker game is amazingly bad.

What Remains of Edith Finch is clearly intended to be about a lot of things. This game’s plot is designed to be open to multiple interpretations. Some tend toward a supernatural interpretation, saying the house in this game is evil or, at least, hungry and eats people (or their souls anyway). Others may say the whole game is a purely psychological metaphor for memories and the mind which is not intended to make literal, real-world sense. My primary interpretation is a bit more straight-forward though: The titular character’s entire family is half-insane and half-stupid. Yes, the entire Finch family (yourself included) is crazy-stupid and this game tells an overarching story of what happens as a result. I think that mundane interpretation best fits the available supposed facts of this game’s stupid plot. Essentially, stupid sh** happens and people die as a result and that’s somehow supposed to be scary (rather than perfectly normal).

Really, sh** happens even if you’re relatively sane and smart and if you’re not, sh** tends to happen more often. That’s not a deep message. That’s just life and perhaps that’s one of the worst aspects of What Remains of Edith Finch; the sh** that “happens” to people in this game tends to be frustratingly, breathtakingly STUPID, highly avoidable sh**. I’m talking Darwin Award material here. Hopefully, the fact that sh** happens more frequently if you’re stupid doesn’t come as a surprise and doesn’t feel like some sort of amazing life lesson. Of course, if you aren’t very rational to begin with, well for one thing, you probably love this game and so it might be too late for you.

Even so, my advice is to try not to leave your less than 1-year old kid unattended in a bathtub and don’t build a swing set next to a steep cliff, don’t go wandering around in a train tunnel and don’t go totally crazy and seal up all the rooms in your house while reading nearly nothing but cookbooks simply because people who used those rooms died (usually stupidly), okay? Finally, don’t go around telling yourself or others “It’s not your fault” when you (or others as the case may be) are clearly guilty of insanely terrible negligence. Face facts instead of hiding from them, otherwise, more stupid sh** will just keep “happening.”

I also never figured out why I was supposed to care about these half-crazy half-wits. The characters’ choices were so often insane and bizarre or otherwise irrelevant that they never seemed quite real or reasonable to me. Who goes around sealing up all the rooms in their house anyway and who puts up with living in a house like that? I guess, crazy people might. I don’t know, why not? Maybe your character (Edith Finch) is crazy too in her own way. That still doesn’t explain why I should care about this crazy-stupid bullsh** plot.

I wouldn’t put up with living in a house with its doors sealed up (unless it was an apartment complex housing separate families which is what this game could have been about but isn’t) and neither would most other people I think. We would fight about it or we would find a way to escape (sooner rather than later). Most of us would probably break the doors down, whether doing so caused a bit of an argument or not. The idea violates suspension of disbelief (and this is not the only idea in this game which does this). If this is all supposed to be purely metaphorical (which is a cooler way to look at this game), then the game should have revealed itself to be a dream (or something) at the end but it never does.

What the game’s main character (Edith Finch) is literally doing is supposed to be fairly straight forward; Returning to her childhood home for the first time in years to confront its dark, tragic, irrational and crazy history. But I have to wonder, why didn’t she return sooner? I guess she has an amazing talent for procrastination, even when she is sole heir to a house (which is clearly worth millions of dollars given its impressive location) sitting abandoned in a not very remote Pacific Northwest forest (you can see city lights in the distance) probably near or in the San Juan islands and/or Seattle. This game is clearly intended to be a thought provoking, emotional journey into the nature of life and loss but for me it came off as a half-imagined, pretentious mess.

Yes, I admit I did find the game thought provoking, but the thoughts that were provoked within me were all too often along the lines of ‘If a person dies a really, really, really stupid death, is it still extremely tragic or is it just plain stupid? Maybe it’s more tragic because the death has become laughable and makes the death feel less sad as a result?’ and ‘Is this unbelievably insane/inane “game” (which is so linear it practically plays itself) going to turn out to be some sort of parody of itself or of its whole genre? Oh god, I hope so.’ And ‘why did this stupid game win so many awards? It isn’t even good enough to be mediocre.’

Perhaps the most tragic thing about What Remains of Edith Finch is that, for now, lots of people claim that it’s a good game even though it so clearly isn’t. It isn’t even very original. The Vanishing of Ethan Carter touches on many vaguely similar issues as this game and explores them more interestingly and with better gameplay. What Remains of Edith Finch is the videogame equivalent of some academy award-winning movie which has nearly no replay value and so, predictably, in five years, hardly anyone will remember it and so it won’t turn out to have been very important after all.

This game reminded me of: The Vanishing of Ethan Carter (except that game was much, much better than this nonsense).

Good things:

+No bugs. The game runs well and the controls are responsive. Interesting method of opening up doors and such.

+Nice graphics which convey the story (and its emotionally heavy-handed tear-jerker moods) well.

+The part where you play as the stupid cat (and as a sea monster) was inspired and excellent. Best part of the entire game. In fact, I wish I could have just played as a cat the entire game. As a cat (or as several different house pets over the years) I could have wandered around the house while I watched the stupid Finch family go crazy and die. That would have been far more interesting.

Mediocre things:

+/-Though the controls are responsive, there is no way to re-map the controls to different keys.

+/-Some interesting but undeveloped (or under-utilized) ideas throughout. This could have been a much better game had it been about different people or families living in an old apartment building rather than different people in one crazy family in one crazy house.

+/-Mediocre voice acting. Your character constantly sounds like she’s about to fall asleep.

+/-The instrumental music sets the mood well but isn’t great to listen to on its own I think. Not amazing music but fairly standard.

Bad things (and 7 more bad things in the comments even if Steam shows only 3 on the activity page):

-Extremely, amazingly linear gameplay. No way to alter the course of events or the course of the story. No alternate endings. No difficulty levels. Beyond the ambiguous plot, NO REPLAY VALUE.\t\t\t\t\t
