\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInteresting at times, beautiful visuals, and well well narrated, but I'm going to be controversial here, and say that the experience isn't worth the asking price, and that the game has become overhyped or overrated.

Edith Finch is more or less a walking simulator, but with far more variety and better presentation than most I've played.

Through a variety of visual styles, some being very imaginative and impressive we see interpretations of the final moments of all the members of the cursed Finch family. The best part of the title is that you really have no idea what surreal, sad thing might happen next and how it will be presented to you.

And yet, I also got the feeling that the story was over well before it ever began. Just when I thought there was going to be a spectacular finale or even a revelation, it was all over and it left me feeling a little bit empty. You also never have much time to know or get attached to the characters involved, and I couldn't relate to the large dysfunctional family in general, so their deaths while sad, don't have very much impact.

I imagine that within a day or two, I will have forgotten that I ever even played this, it will be nothing more than another review posted on Steam.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWow. The preposterous level of positivty and awe surrounding this game is dumbfounding. This review will probably be something like a lone man standing in front of a column of tanks. To get to the point: This is a bad game, it's not worth $20, and the cultish devotion from the zombie hordes of rave reviewers (~7,000 positive reviews at the moment) is a 21st century example of "popular delusions and the madness of crowds."

SPOILERS follow:

Speaking of delusions, the moment my feelings for this game crystalized was when I found myself playing a hallucinating baby in a bathtub as some stupid, aimless monologue drones on about....nothing? And that's the main problem: The game is a superifically beautiful, meandering journey to nowhere. The journal entries that narrate your pointless trip start off well enough; intriguing hints of what might be to come. A whimsical sequence as you relive Molly's imagination(?), and I started to think "That was really cool, I'm beginning to see what all the fuss is about".....but soul-crushingly, it soon becomes obvious that this was apropos of nothing, that the game is little more than a string of pointless vignettes that make it a swing simulator, a can-opening simulator, a photo-taking simulator (I kept snapping random photos waiting to trigger the next piece of stupid, pointless dialogue) etc.... If you think there's something deeper here than opening cans or swinging, that I'm "missing the point" or not "paying attention"...I'd suggest you're fooled, that the variety of minigames and visual style are masking the game's inanity (like candy coating on a cyanide capsule).

If you like making a toy frog jump out of bathtub so that a baby can drown, or you think taking fake photos of the back of some guy as he pees on a tree makes for deep, engaging gameplay and satisfying storytelling, then be my guest. By all means, join the ranks of your zombie brethren. Judging by the neverending blocks of adulatory text that you'll see as you scroll down, chances are good that you'll be spellbound by this game, that you'll find it a breath of fresh air, that it will resonate with you for days after you've finished it, that it will be (gasp) "haunting" (lol), and that you'll be convinced that it represents the kind of deep, meaningful storytelling that video games can actually achieve.

As far as I'm concerned, it's nothing more than a string of cute but stupid little demos that a group of developers came up with and loosely tied together with pretentious, monotonous monologues.

I did NOT expect to dislike this game, and did NOT have an axe to grind. I had some free time and was looking for a great, novel experience. Considering the universal, heartfelt praise and the gorgeous trailer, I had no hesitation buying it. It's not that it didn't live up to the hype, quite the contrary- playing this game was deeply psychologically jarring. Not because of the crap story, but because playing it was like becoming Rowdy Roddy Piper and putting on the glasses in They Live; it suddenly became obvious that everyone else is a weird alien, and that I was the only human being on the face of the Earth who saw this garbage for what it is. It doesn't feel good to be the only one who doesn't like this game. I wanted to like this game. I don't. Clearly it isn't that the game is too esoteric (since all of the countless thousands who've played it think it's a revelation). If this is held as the pinnacle of what games can be, if this is the model that future games will follow, then I don't know what to say. Thankfully the market is so vast that self-important crap like this will always be niche.

EDIT: Coming back to this review after a few years to say that if you actually want a GOOD walking simulator that does story and atmosphere and puzzles and experience RIGHT, play The Vanishing of Ethan Carter instead.\t\t\t\t\t
