\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis atmospheric walking simulator is a perfect example of both emotional and ludic immersivity. We follow a story of Edith Finch herself, while walking through abandoned family house from first-person perspective. Each short story turns available only after entering the room of its owner, and each subsequent room opens after passing through the previous (though after completing the game you are able to access any room separately). Each little story of someone of Finches is an interesting piece of interactivity model. While discovering these stories of the dead, we are experiencing some activities, which those people performed.
For instance, childish imagination takes you to shark’s consciousness, and you naturally swim and eat seals. Or experience of another accidental death appears to be a swinging on a swing, which is fully controlled by player. And it is not difficult to guess what might happen, if you swing to intensively, being on a swing at the edge of a cliff. But you have to swing and swing until you eventually fly and die, because this death is a key to the next story. Here we come to nearly existential issue of gaming, of correlating yourself with the character, when game is designed so well and profound, that you even experience natural feelings – feelings not towards some side characters, who are scripted and controlled by computer, but towards your own current character, as you are literally in his shoes.
Probably, the most immersive experience here is provided by Lewis Finch’s novel. Edith’s brother had a mental disorder, which had developed after drug rehabilitation. Lewis created imaginary self in his mind. His job in fish manufactory was to cut fish heads from the edible bodies. Eventually, Lewis committed suicide by putting his head under fish guillotine. He was 21 years old. Now, imagine, though text is incapable to express that experience. Gameplay is controlled by keyboard under the left hand and mouse in the right hand. While listening to Lewis’s story we are performing his actions, and we are to see the world as he does. Screen is divided – on the left there is the imagined part, which is a kind of mini fantasy-game, while on the right there is his workplace depicted. Left hand is performing ludic actions inside that minigame, while right hand performs repetitive operation of cutting off fish heads. And player experiences kind of the same division of mind that Lewis Finch had. This is a simple, yet incredible feature, which made it possible to attempt to experience nearly the same that is happening inside disordered mind.
