\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is not a game. It's a story-driven walking simulator with a few "click-here" moments. This is fine, but this means that the game needs its story and world-building to be stellar. Only the latter impresses, unfortunately.

At its core, the story is very emotional and tender, however it does have a few missteps. Certain characters are underdeveloped, so when the game expects you to care about them, you don't. Also, the game is short (took me 2 hours to finish). There are simply so many characters in this game that 2 hours is not enough to truly understand them all. Furthermore, the game tries too often to pull an emotional fast one on the player. Sometimes sad music will start playing and I'll realize: "Oh, this is where the devs want me to start crying." The short length certainly hurts these emotional moments. Lastly, I wouldn't recommend watching the trailer on Steam. It contains significant spoilers for the last part of the game.

Now for some positives. The voice acting is excellent. The level of detail and level variability are impressive. The game looks beautiful. The level designers are clearly very talented at world-building and creating atmospheres.

Verdict: I bought this game when it was $6, and it is well worth that price as a good, story-driven game. It is not a particularly impactful story, though, so at the full price, this game is not worth your money.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith finch is a standout entry to the walking simulator genre. It uses a combination of heavily narrative focused level design and subtitles carefully positioned in the environment to achieve an outstanding sense of pacing throughout the story.

Like the rest of the genre, What Remains of Edith Finch focuses entirely on the storytelling while using a light touch with the interactive mechanics. I would probably summarize the interactions that the main character Edith has with her environment as a combination of children's pop out book and the cinema influenced level design of point+click games.

The game has fully voice acted dialogue, a haunting soundtrack, one of the best stories I've experienced in the interactive fiction medium, and an incredible eye for detail. Just when you think that the writer's have overlooked the age of a character, or the impact of the narrative on the environment around you, a replay of the game will surprise you with everything accounted for. There are also surprising layers to the seemingly simple story which can catch you off guard. There are other moments which will leave you absolutely enchanted.

Before I go in length to some of the more interesting aspects of the story I'll leave a quick grade on the elements of What Remains of Edith Finch.

Story 10/10
Sound Design and Music 8/10
Gameplay 7/10
Voice acting 8/10

Overall I think that What Remains of Edith Finch is fantastic storytelling experience. Wonderful polish, outstanding gameplay mechanics throughout the entirety of the game, impressive attention to detail and most importantly a story which I would say deserves more play throughs to unearth all of the secrets the game has to offer. From here on out I would like to focus on what I feel is just beneath the surface of the story in the spoiler section.

The story begins and ends from the perspective of Christopher, Edith Jr's son. Edith left him a memoir of her experiences confronting the home she grew up in off the coast of Washington State. When we open the memoir we are taken to Edith's perspective and she is used as the vehicle for the majority of the story. Very quickly we are confronted with the whimsical silhouette of the Finch house breaking through the treeline.

Throughout the story we are confronted with seemingly impossible truths delivered with a straight face. We're led to believe that Odin Finch was able bring an entire house to America from Norway by boat. We're told that Molly Finch was devoured by a monster. A young Calvin Finch was able to loop around a swing just outside the Finch home before being crashed on the rocks below in Orca Bay. And however incredible these stories might be, we're confronted with the mazelike interior of the Finch home reaching out to the sky on nothing but dilapidated wooden platforms. Every room still free of dust like a morbid museum. Fliers of Milton Finch lie sill in the creek outside seemingly unaffected by time. For every doubt you might have about the yarns being spun we're never confronted with the truth. Even well past the ending we see Christopher put flowers on his mothers grave where the dates on her tombstone communicate that she died during childbirth before the camera pans to the familiar silhouette of the Finch home against the sunset.

I think that a majority of people will absorb the narrative being fed to us in good faith since the veil of these half truths are never pulled away. What is important to think about though is the importance and influence that both Edith's Great-Grandmother had on her, as well as the influences of her Mother Dawn.

Both Dawn and Edie Finch are at odds with one another throughout the entirety of the narrative. Edie seems free spirited. She's creative and passionate. She is never shy to the attention the Finch family has had through it's troubled past offering interviews to papers, curious passer bys and even tabloid magazines. She is confronted by Dawn who acts as her caretaker in her later years. Dawn is very much a pragmatist. She is a teacher and a programmer. She has discipline instilled to her by her Father. She values critical thinking. She's also interestingly enough not buried in the Finch cemetery either due to her Christian beliefs, or perhaps buried in the same plot as her Husband Sanjay.

Ultimately I think it is useful to take a step away from the story being told to us by Edith Finch, and even put our confirmations from Christopher's perspective on hold while we parse some of the details from the story. I believe that Edith exists as a synthesis of both Edie and her mother Dawn. She still believes these folktales told to her, internalizing them and delivering them to the player as the narrative just as she writes in her memoir to her son. However, she also delivers many truths to us, peeling back the veneer of these intricately painted fairy tales to show us what is going just below the surface. Each of the vignettes is delivered with a fantastical perspective, while still grounded enough in our reality that it makes these stories feel like tall tales instead of outright lies.

Molly is mundanely poisoned by Christmas decorations when sent to bed hungry, yet she transforms into beasts before inferring she will be eaten by a tentacled monster.

Calvin's last moments will feel so vibrantly real to us as we remember swinging on the playground in our childhood, yet he is able to achieve the impossible according to his brother by completely looping over the tree branch.

Barbara's comic will account for every nook and cranny of the house as well as details we haven't even confirmed such as the railway up stairs damaged and the key to the basement present in her music box. Yet despite the grounded details, we're led to believe that she was eaten by 60s b movie monsters from the account of a discount crypt keeper impression.

Despite all of these glaring impossibilities we can still watch the tide churn around the sunken Finch house brought to America by Odin across the ocean, completely intact, still brooding in Orca Bay beyond a red buoy calling out to us in the fog on the sea. It makes it very hard to draw a line at where the truth begins and the borders of imagination begin.

There is however one story told to us that gives us insight to what is really going on. Despite the story delivered to us by Edith Jr who up until this point has been an unreliable narrator, I think there is one account that stands out from the rest.

That story is the fantastic height of the narrative where we read the account of her brother Lewis. Dawn insisted that Lewis get a job at the local cannery. Lewis often escapes his mundane job, the weight of his father's death and his brother's absence through his imagination. He imagines himself the King of his own nation, an adventurer. Yet when he is finished with work, he is forced to reawaken to the real world covered in the filth and death that surrounds him in the cannery. We learn Lewis' fate from the seemingly unbiased yet sympathetic perspective of Dr. Nuth. We also learn that Lewis took his own life, seemingly unable to cope with the juxtaposition between the real and make believe.

Ultimately I think that this is the underlying message of What Remains of Edith Finch. We see the influence of Edie's tall tales on her kin, and we also see the harm of Dawn's unwavering denial. We see all of the destruction brought to the Finch house and have to wonder how much of it was it was a curse, and how much of it was self inflicted. Were these rooms closed off to protect the Finch legacy, or to protect us from losing ourselves to own imagination.

I think it's this balance that the game is asking us to question. What Remains of Edith Finch is a reminder that while we may surround ourselves in fantasy, that we should still maintain what connects us to the real world or we too may become lost. \t\t\t\t\t

I just finished What Remains of Edith Finch, though... I don't really want to put it like that. I experienced it, I felt it and felt as if I had lived it. It's a strange feeling I got after beating it (the following is written down in a way to be just intriguing, but not discernible fully, unless the game has been played, and is a vague transcription of general themes only, nevertheless, proceed with caution.):

Escapism is a powerful thing and so is reality. Sometimes as creative, intelligent, yet fragile beings we recede into a place far away from said reality. This place can be blissful, it can feel eternal and free from all the disgusting blotches that feel as though they subsume the day to day under their oppressive ugliness and asymmetry. Often these things are small and insignificant, but we yearn to have them go away and dream of a return to a sublime past, which, in truth, was most likely never as rosy as we remembered it being.
With every droplet of alcohol in our bloodstream, every scream through a telephone, every book on the shelf and every dream of bird-like freedom, we forget the here and now. We often try to recreate our past in the things we create. The framed polaroids, the biographies, the memorials, the cemeteries. We sometimes "build the cemetery, before we build the house".
Until one day, we wake and it's our house, pets, family and friends, that seem buried deep in the ground beneath our feet, or castaway in an ocean. Until one day, when we don't awake and all that's left of us is a forlorn memory; on those same pictures, or those same notebooks, paintings and everyday objects, that we gawked at in impenetrable innocence.

Until all that remains is the little that was left.\t\t\t\t\t
