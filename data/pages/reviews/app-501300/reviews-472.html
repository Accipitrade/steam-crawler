\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe animation and art are great--very immersive, lovely, weird details etc. The story is promising (an especially wonderful moment is when you become a random cat) but ultimately the writing is pretty childish, Deus ex machina endings (ahem the train wtf) abound. It often feels like the writers weren't sure what should happen next, so they ended a story in the middle without resolution. The game's narrative fails to commit to a consistent logic--like, what's real and what's fantasy--which unfortunately makes it hard to invest in what happens. I think gamers who read (and therefore have some sense of what stories can do when well executed) will be disappointed. There are a lot of cool *almosts* in the game and the writer seems to be pretty young, so I'll definitely keep my eye on him and hope he commits to his stories fully in the future. I don't regret playing this game but it was a letdown.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a sad game. Not poetic, not beautiful, sad. The game itself creates a fantastic and enjoyable atmosphere, for the small amount of time it lasts. Now, I'd compare this to other narrative driven games to come out in recent years, like Life is Strange or maybe To The Moon, and those stories were certainly tear jerkers, but they had reasons for it. Something came of the sadness. Some kind of resolution or forwarding of a story. What Remains of Edith Finch doesn't do this. It has an initial setup that makes you feel bad for the main character, but intrigued to see where it goes. Without spoiling anything, I'll say the ending is depressing, with a message of basically "life is short, enjoy it." The game is great while it lasts, but at twenty dollars for a whopping two hours of gameplay, I'd say just put it on your wishlist and wait 'till it goes on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
