\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI was hooked on this game from start to finish. I played straight through with my daughter playing from time to time next to me but mostly watching. Considering most kids now, it's a feat getting them to sit still for longer than thirty minutes, she sat with me the whole time only taking breaks when needed.

We both loved the puzzles and the wild adventure each character took us through. It took me back to a time when I was a kid─ you know the times when you thought you were invincible. It wasn't just good storytelling it was the way each story was told. It's not just story, it's interactive story. You get to be each character.

You never knew what style the story was going to take and even though there are times you know what is really happening (I won't spoil) to the characters, it is played out so beautifully the dread is filled with a sort of haunting beauty.

The only negative thing I can bring myself to nitpick over is I got annoyed with certain parts where I had to move slowly and there was one part with a ...I think they are called view finders?? That I sat there cursing for several minutes trying to figure out how to escape. (I'm not proud to admit, btw.) When I discovered it was just to walk away I sat there for a few solid seconds thinking, wow...that was stupid. Well, you know, so was I.

Go play this game, absolutely worth it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt’s hard to say what this game is truly about. That might be a cause for concern for some, but for me; it’s actually a comfort. It tells me What Remains of Edith Finch is not afraid to be different. Sometimes, I feel as though projects are designed simply to mark off a box on a large checklist. Battle Royale - check! Open world - check! Microtransactions - check! This is why I fight so hard to find games like this one, because at the end of the day - It doesn’t fit inside a perfect box and it certainly is not targeting an audience. It’s simply a timeless story.

From a design perspective, this game is what some might call a ‘walking simulator’. It’s a casual exploratory experience that won’t have you mashing quick time events or fighting off monsters. Instead, you take a meandering pace around the Finch household, as one of the last surviving members of the estate. Mystery has surrounded some of the events in the household, and as one of the last heirs, you return to the old homestead in an attempt to put together the story of your ancestors. It’s an intriguing story, albeit not the most original. Where it does shine, however, is in the execution. Much of the gameplay will have you strolling around the house, looking for the last memories of your family. These memories are told through single-shot stories that explain what has happened in the family line.

These stories vary the gameplay design from a little to a lot, where some increase the pace, others slow it down and ask you to take a moment. Through this, the game is able to throw the emotional highs and lows of the game at you. Guilt, loss, regret, and fear are all themes that the game explores and it does so with a variety of tactics that will no doubt provoke a response from the player. I can’t say more without spoiling some of the game’s greatest moments, but suffice it to say that if you are stifling emotions, this game may be the tide that breaks the dam.

In essence, this game is not a game. It’s a piece of art. A living story. If you’re someone who has trouble finding the ‘game’ in a game, then I can tell you this is probably not your bag. However, if you appreciate a good story, wondrous intent, and don’t mind stopping and smelling the roses; then you owe yourself this experience.
