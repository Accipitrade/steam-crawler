\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a first person story driven game. It's not very long (around 3 hours) so I am not going to go deep into the contents of the game as it would just spoil the things that make it good.

While small What Remains of Edith Finch excels at what it does have. The visuals are fantastic and put together with the consideration that you are in a real place but also in such a way that each set is an interesting place and your curiosity it piqued to look closer at much of it. Both the incredibly high resolution textures and the light/shadow work ooze with deep atmosphere.

Sound and music also play a large part in bringing the environments to life. Each item you touch, each foot fall and each set piece is rich with the sounds. The voice acting is superb and along with the intriguing story do an excellent job of drawing you in. I was so enthralled I had to force myself not to play it all in one sitting (not that it would be a bad way to play it).

As tempted as I am to want to talk about the story of What Remains of Edith Finch I am not going to. It's like a movie where you're better off skipping the trailer. There are too many surprises and it's all about discovery. It's well written, paced and unfolds in just the right way. There are both elements of wonder, whimsy, sadness and loss. It's a story about how family members relate to each other in the face of unbelievably unlucky events.

The story based "walking-simulator" game genre isn't for everyone and for the length some might balk at the price but the quality of work done to make What Remains of Edith Finch is top notch. Like a good film I would even go back and play this again.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is single-handedly the most creative, inventive, refreshing, and rewarding experiences I've ever had in a game. This completely redefines what a game is and can accomplish.

Granted, it's more like an interactive story than a true game, but each moment of interaction does such an amazing job at making you part of the story, that it truly makes the experience feel personal and, by the end, profound. Although I won't admit to it, I may have gotten a little misty eyed towards the conclusion.

There's just so much the game has to say about life, "normalacy", and how we choose to define happiness. It may sound heavy, but it's presented so masterfully that these big ideas will sneak up on you unexpectedly in a way that truly feels like an earned revelation, rather than a forced insight.

The game gleefully mixes entirley different genres, art styles, and controls, and yet it all works harmoniously together to tell a story that is both epic and extremely humble at the same time. The art direction is some of the best I've seen, not necessarily because of amazing 3D models or luscious textures, but because the sheer attention to detail in bringing this world to life is evident in each and every pixel. Nothing is just pasted on, the entire experience (visuals, sound, music, voice acting, writing) feels very thoughtfully and loveingly designed, and has the unique ability to straddle both a sense of reality and a dream-like imagination, all seamlessly.

There's also has a slightly impish quality underlying eveyrthing. There's great wit, combined with some black-comedy, topped off with a sense of mischeviousness that makes it an experience that you will want to return to. Which is quite a feat, because the game is very linear and once you know the story, that's it...and yet I find mysef ocassionally replaying sections because they're just so darn engaging. This is, of course, taking into account that some of the subject matter is a tad dark. I'm really not ruining it for you to say that death is a huge theme, and even though some of the events are tragic, the sense of wonderment at it, as opposed to finality, makes it not only bearable, but something that you actively *want* to experience again.

I'm also blown away with the audicity of the developers to make a game that defies all labels. First off, is it even a game? Is it visual novel? Is it a walking simulator? Is it a seek-and-find? A Myst-like puzzle? A bunch of Wii-like mini-games? A piont-and-click adventure? Yes to all of the above, but without feeling like it's cheating any one of them. All the pices of this game fit together so well that you never see it only as the sum of its parts, but as a finely crafted and exquisite whole whose sole purpose is to inspire you and make you think.

Yes it's extremely short, but the story, ideas and art direction make it worth every penny. Plus, unlike most story-based games, this one actually does have replay value. This is also a fantastic game to share with those loved ones in your life who don't like video games. It's so good at crossing barriers, that even people who hate video games will not be able to resist getting drawn in.

What Remains of Edith Finch will stay with me the rest of my days, and is probably the only game I've encountered that so deftly interweaves the power and importance of both life and death; not as something that has to be endured, but as something to celebrate.\t\t\t\t\t
