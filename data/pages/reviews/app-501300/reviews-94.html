\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI went back and forth on whether or not to recommend this experience, but in the end I think it deserves a reserved yes,

Don't go in expecting a game. There's maybe two sections that would qualify, and even that might be a stretch. This is a story that requires occasional input. Now that said, it's beautiful, haunting, cohesive, and well-told. The narration is really well done, and all the actors put tremendous effort into making the story that much more engaging. The writers, artists, and animators have done a brilliant job in bringing to life a setting that manages to feel supernatural and mundane at the same time, and that takes some serious chops.

I bought the experience on sale - around $5, if I recall correctly. And for that price, I'm satisfied. If I'd gone in like I did (expecting a more interactive and game-like experience) and paid full price, I would have been pretty unhappy - but I didn't know exactly what to expect, and that's really on me. I'd had this recommended more than once, and I intentionally avoided knowing much about it. I think it's worth the $20 asking price for the right people, but that may just not be me.

If you want a story experience, I recommend this without any hesitation, and at full price. If you're not huge on a visual, even somewhat interactive, novel though, I'd either wait for a deep sale or let this one pass you by.

I'm glad I've gone through this, even if it wasn't what I was expecting. That's really why, in the end, it gets a thumbs up. I don't know how many times I'll go through it again, but it was certainly worth the sale price to go through it once - at least for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFirst off, this is an "Environmental Narrative Game". If you don't know what that means, a good description is given in TvTropes: https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/Main/EnvironmentalNarrativeGame but basically don't expect much in terms of gameplay; this game is more like a book or a film than an actual game. I'm judging it as an Environmental Narrative Game, and I recommend it with the understanding that you don't expect to be challenged.

The controls are as follows: look around with mouse, interact by clicking (and sometimes dragging) or E, zoom in with Shift, forward/back/strafe with either the arrows or WASD. There is no option to change the keyboard configuration as far as I can tell, which may be a problem for some people. Also, it seems that there is no Run key, but you won't miss it much, despite the fact that the character walks slowly (this is because, apart from the very start of the game, you won't have to walk a long distance between successive points of interest). I don't think the controls are explained anywhere in the game or the menus, but feel free to correct me if I missed it somewhere.

You explore a huge house with hidden passages, but most of this exploration follows a predetermined route. When you reach specific places or interact with specific things, you get a piece of narration. The rooms of the house, or more specifically certain items therein, correspond to stories of your family members and how they died, which you recreate playing as that family member. Most of these stories are optional, except the first one and a couple of others.

The atmosphere is surreal, as many of the stories are told from the perspective of that person's imagination, and the tone is melancholic, as the family's history involves a lot of early deaths (in childhood or adolescence). The narrative largely revolves around death anxiety and coping with loss. The plot resembles a collection of loosely connected short stories, although there is in fact a main storyline (Edith's story). The presentation of the stories varies, and at times is quite original.

It's sad, it's short, but it's well-written. I consider it a bit expensive for its genre, but do get it when it's on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
