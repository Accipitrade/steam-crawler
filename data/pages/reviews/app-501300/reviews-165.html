\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter "playing" this "game" and looking at all these overwhelmingly positive reviews, I feel myself like Alice behind looking glass. People keep praising this game and especially it's story, searching for some hidden sense, but the only thing I felt after I had finished the game is perplexity. We will talk about all this step by step but let's start from afar.

At first, I must admit that WRoEF has it's strong sides. Visual aspect is one of them. The game looks really beautiful and it has a lot of details. Sound design is also good enough and not far behind the visuals, which helps to create good atmosphere. As to different gameplay mechanics... well, just as in most walking simulators - it has bare minimum.

But you see, I played another "critically acclaimed masterpiece" before - Vanishing of Ethan Carter. And that game had only one prominent advantage - stunning visuals. WRoEF on the other hand is doing most things slightly better. While VoEC had no gameplay - WRoEF has a handful of gameplay. While VoEC had no puzzles - WRoEF has few. While VoEC had no interactivity - WRoEF has some. Etc, etc. Better but still very far from "enough". That's kinda a reason why I had a bit of sympathy for WRoEF. Unfortunately only a bit.

Now let's talk about unpleasant things. The first one is a gameplay in general. While I mentioned that the game has some interesting mechanics, most of the time (like in most walking simulators) we just walk. Slowly. S-slo-o-ow-wly. And listen to our own thoughts. The game has exploration tag, but I have no idea why. Yeah, probably it would be interesting to explore it's locations, but the game doesn't allow you to do so. Literally. The moment you want to check some interesting stuff, the game forcefully moves camera to the point where Edith (our protagonist) says something.

Oh, let's check this tabl... NO, you should look at the book shelf now. Oh, probably I've just noticed something interesting at the windo... NO, Edith is talking about stuff on the bed atm... Hm, let me check this doo... NO, right now you should check THIS door. And this happens every damn time. After 5-6th time you just stop to care. I mean, why should I bother with exploration if the game can do everything without my help? Thus, just press W, listen, listen, watch, listen, watch, sometimes make some fast interaction or solve simple puzzle and here is you gameplay.

But hey, many people claim that WRoEF is not about gameplay, but about story. It definitely should have some magnificent story, which will overshadow all it's cons, right? Right? Sorry, but I'm going to debunk this myth. You can see how many people post in their reviews this pompous ingame phrase "What kind of family builds a graveyard before building a house?" as if it has some hidden sense. You want to know what family? - I can answer this question right away. If the members of this family are dumber than CP 2077's NPCs so they can't make a step without dying from some stupid act - graveyard is definitely something they'll need the most.

I have no idea how anyone can feel even a scratch of empathy towards these freaks. There is no conflict in this story. Family is not struggling against some, for instance, financial hardships or something like that. They are just fighing with their own stupidity. Every single member of this family exists here just to die few minutes after with some ridiculous death. They die either from their own stupidity, superstitions, parental negligence, or because they are just mentally ill. All these family members deserve not empathy, but a collection of Darwin awards.

The moment you see beginning of the scene, you already know how it wil end.
Mother leaves hungry kid in closed room with hallucinogenic(?) berries - what could go wrong? (btw, you also store hallucinogenic berries near the toilet bowl, right?)
Family lets mentally unstable relative to work at dangerous factory - what could go wrong?
Father goes deer hunting with a kid - what could go wrong?
Mother leaves a baby in a bathroom - what could go wrong? (of course it's not your fault honey, probaly curse)
Father.... forgot(?) that his kid was outside, when the storm started(?), because he was listening to music(?), and... yeah, right, nobody expected that it can hurt...
Mother lets her kid to play on swings right at the cliff's edge... good idea, right? No ided why it didn't end well.
Granny mixes drugs with alcohol and the next day... you won't believe - she is gone! Etc, etc, etc.

You see? There is indeed a mystery behind all these deaths. Probably Cthulhu is involved. But jokes aside, can we eventually discover what really happened with Finch family? - No. During the whole walkthrough we as Edith were just rediscovering these old entries, which give us ultra-short description of each family member and his/her stupid/ridiculous/horrendous death and that's it.

It seems like in the very end granny was trying to tell us about the real mystery behind Finch family, about sunken house, about mystic lights, but bітсну mother abruptly ended her story and tore the diary into pieces. Thus, this situation left me confused. What was this all about? We were teased with this sunken house for so long, and in the end we discovered nothing. Nothing new in general about the fate of Finch family and nothing at all in particular about that sunken house. Understand how you want. We ended right where we started.

What about our protagonist? W-well, she is the last one from the Finch family. She is young, she is pregnant (22 weeks) a-a-and... that's kinda it. We won't discover anything neither about her nor about her baby. Who is the father of her baby? - no idea. What was the need to make protagonist pregnant if her pregnacy serves no purspose in the story? - no idea as well. It seems like even Edith herself doesn't care about her baby, because she is climbing big trees, jumping on the roofs, crawling in tight tunnels - definitely she and her baby won't live a long and happy life. Just like other Finch members.

Conslusions. Figuratively speaking, the game looks like some beautiful album with a music box inside. It's nice to look at and to listen to. Unfortunately gameplaywise it has not much to be proud of. In most cases just walk, listen and watch. As to the story, well, I think I described it good enough. If you put off rose glasses, you will see just a bunch of unrelated sketches, where not very smart characters are beforehand placed in lose-lose situations, so they can just die from some stupid act. Probably if it was a parody or comedy game, where you mock these dumbs for their acts of total stupidity (like in some "Alone home" movie) I could have a good laugh and even give it a high rate. But it desperately tries to squeeze a tear from you, while I simply can't take this nonesense seriously. If you think otherwise, you are free to confront me with your arguments.

P.S. Just curious. If you'll decide to dig a bunker under your house to hide there from the boogeyman for the next 30 years... how would your family members react?\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed this immensely, I'm not usually one to sit up all night to complete something and become completely engrossed, but I was with this. The detail put in to the design and scenery is immaculate, I could stare for hours at the forest, the ocean, the backlit posters, the broken train tracks, just everything, I've never felt so immersed in a 3D world, the visuals truly made this experience great I think. Well done to the VFX and visual design team for making something truly special to marvel at.

I loved the stories and ways that interaction played with the narrative as well. The controls handled extremely smoothly and intuitively, especially compared to much simpler visual novel/walking simulators I've played where the controls are buggy, unresponsive, and often uncreative. There was no lack of original thought put in to how to progress the story and use the medium to its advantage and that was incredible to experience.

The only issue I have is with the ending. Not the ending specifically, but, it made the overall game feel a bit rushed and it might have been worth exploring some of these stories a bit more. I felt a bit cheated, as I felt a stronger ending and a bit more fleshing out would have made things feel more complete. I'm not necessarily complaining about lack of answers, as I don't mind stories that leave certain threads untied and questions unanswered, but only when it feels appropriate to the greater story being told, which it didn't here. The lack of conclusion on the curse doesn't bother me really, but the fact that Edie's, Milton's, Sanjay's, and Kay's stories were left entirely unfinished felt like it ruined the flow.

I understand that a theme of the story is lack of closure, so it does make sense to some degree, but that didn't stop it feeling somewhat unsatisfying. After all, if part of the theme is lack of closure, what's the point of exploring their last moments before death and their thoughts going on at all? If that was a central theme, the story might have been stronger if we never got to explore the character's last moments and instead only explored from Edith's perspective. So to have certain deaths and stories left out, even if it kind of can sort of be justified a little by the context of the house, it made the story feel less whole.

Overall though, I highly recommend, and while this lacks replayability and a 100% satisfying conclusion, I would have paid more than I had when I bought this on sale if I knew how much work was put in to this game and how much I would enjoy it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game seems like it could be a nice experience, but my first impressions of it during the first minutes have been pretty bad. For a game with such a limited number of things you can do, it's ridiculously hard to figure out what the controls are, and as far as I've seen there is no way to rebind controls either.

The "Controls" section of the options pretty much only allows you to change the mouse sensitivity and axes, and there is no way to change the FOV (which is so low that it physically hurts my eyes) without downloading code altering software.

It also seems like some things in game aren't even supported by mouse and keyboard controls?? I got to the first interactable object in the game, a mailbox, and tried for several minutes (yes. really.) to open it using my mouse, as one would intuitively expect you'd be able to, but nothing happened, which led me to believe that it was locked or something?

I took a short trip to the discussion forum and saw something about the game being designed around using a controller, so I went back to the mailbox to see if using a controller would change anything, and was able to open it, but even when using a controller, I had troubles figuring out what combination of buttons and control stick movements I was supposed to use, and I can only in good faith chalk that up to me being an idiot for about 25% of that whole series of events.

This game really does seem interesting, and I definitely will finish it at some point, but judging by my first 15 minutes in it, it truly feels like it was designed by someone who is extremely artistically talented, but doesn't know a great deal about video game design. The aforementioned FOV has two settings: Mild Claustrophobia, and Oh God I Have Binoculars Stuck to My Face and Can't Get Them Off Please Help.

I will come back and change this review once I've played more of the game, because I know that I haven't played enough of it to give a fair verdict, but I really felt a need to to vent my frustrations, because I don't want to go through the entire game feeling this way about it.\t\t\t\t\t

Instead of a family, there were just memories of one
We all enjoy a good old tragedy once in a while, mostly on the pages of books or on the screens of our devices.
First, the writing on the wall – a bad omen, a dark cloud on an otherwise perfectly blue sky; then a slow, ominous build-up, almost tangible tension ending in a dramatic cavalcade of catastrophic events – the tears, the agony! – an untimely death, a heroic sacrifice; we wipe runaway tears from our faces somewhat shamefully and live with the impression for the next several hours before continuing on our merry way. This is an exquisite dessert – a single hundred-dollar piece of chocolate melting on your tongue; you welcome it, you enjoy it, and then it's enough.

Playing this game is like buying ten packs of this chocolate and stuffing your mouth with it like a pig until you can't breathe anymore. This is a whole damn tragedy syrup, and you're invited to chug it straight out of the bottle.
https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2621944634
Everyone is gone. Every Finch that has ever existed (except for you) died a horrible, mysterious death – 12 family members, 12 tombstones – well, actually, 11 tombstones and a single memorial. Little kids, adults, old men and women... You will live through their last moments, and what I think makes these moments so powerful is the fact that they will all die at the hands of you, the player – like it or not, you are the unstoppable force that sets everything in motion; merely a witness, and yet a cold-blooded, morbidly curious killer – you are the Finch curse.

Each story is a unique gameplay sequence that is not like the rest. Some of these sequences are the pinnacle of game design; the creativity on the part of the developing team is something out of this world, you never see it anymore in this age of first-person shooters unless you dig really deep and know what you're looking for. So, first they ensnare you by making you emotionally involved, then they keep you on your toes with constantly shapeshifting game design and magnificent storytelling – doesn't that sound like having a good time? But then... why are you crying?
https://steamcommunity.com/sharedfiles/filedetails/?id=2621816418
I hate to resort to the worn-out and battered "and the music ties it all together", but it truly does – the masterful work of Jeff Russo transforms each dreadful story into a razor-sharp knife that can (and will) cut very deep. I can't remember the last time a videogame made me let go of the mouse, cover my face with my hands and go, "No. This can't be happening right now. No."

It can. And it will.

What Remains of Edith Finch is a classic case of "I wish I could erase my memory and play it again". If you are reading this, consider yourself lucky – you might just have a front-row ticket to an interactive storytelling event of a lifetime.
