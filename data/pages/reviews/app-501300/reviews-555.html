\r\n\t\t\t\t\t9/10 -- Stunning interactive storytelling, ambitious narrative approach, no gameplay challenge (but that's OK), a few technical issues. Many moments from this game will stick with you.

Very brief (2.5 hours) but fantastically inventive, What Remains of Edith Finch is a collection of interactive stories about a family that has been burdened for generations with a mysterious curse. About 10 different stories are bound together by the framing device of 17-year-old Edith's visit to the abandoned house to come to grips with her legacy as the last surviving Finch.

To some extent, this is a "walking simulator" like Gone Home and the like, so if you're looking for thrilling gameplay challenges, look elsewhere. What's interesting, though, is how this game "simulates" not only walking, but also swinging on a swing, taking photos, flipping through animated flipbooks, playing in the bathtub (as a baby), and even transforming into various animals and monsters in a dream. The most ambitious sequence in the game (Lewis's story-- utterly fascinating) even presents the first-person experience of a mentally-ill teenager slowly retreating into his own interior world as he works a boring shift at the fish cannery. That sequence uses a half-and-half control scheme similar to that of Brothers to represent the growing split in his own mind. As a result of this "breadth over depth" approach, the controls are often a bit clunky, even when you're just walking around as Edith; on several occasions, I had to try four or five times to discover I really could enter that low/narrow doorway, and I lost count of how many times I closed a book or door accidentally because it wasn't clear which way I needed to push/pull. But the game will be patient if you are, and again, it has no fail conditions, so you can take your time and nothing bad will happen. Well, outside of the many bad things that are built into the story itself.

Edith Finch will leave you with plenty to think about, from fascinating imagery (the interactive horror comic, Lewis's hallucinations) to questions about what's really going on behind this story-- what is this curse all about, and what really happened to some of these people (notably Molly and Milton), and what comes after the final cutscene? There's also a subtle but intriguing callback to Giant Sparrow's PlayStation exclusive The Unfinished Swan that left me with some unanswered questions about the world of Edith Finch. If you've got a quiet evening free and you're in the mood for a slow-paced but fascinating and moving story about one family's struggle with its own madness, I highly recommend What Remains of Edith Finch.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHow Talented Artistic People Shouldn't Make a Game
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>

First I should say that I only played "What Remains of Edith Finch" up to just after the comic strip sequence.

At that point I decided the game was "candy coated brain pollution" and uninstalled it ..... I didn't really care what it had to say, what meaning it had, or how the devs could tie together what is basically death voyeurism.

Don't get me wrong .... even as it is, it is far better than the death porn that is 99% of the gaming industry. If you have the mentality that like fractured fairy tales and slowing down at a collision on the highway to look for a dead body .... and appreciate good writing ... you will love this game.

So what is so bad about this game IMO?
>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>>

When stupid people write books, movies or computer games ..... the product is usually an addictive product designed to appeal to human Darwinian desires. Sad, boring, predictable and not all that relevant pablum..... this is 99% of gaming.

When smart artistic people write books, movies or computer games the result is much more chaotic and interesting, but you need to be careful with them as things register with you without you even realizing it.

This is such a game. Pretty much everything is well crafted, but the the whole is candy coated poison.

The first problem is that the game forces the player to collude with characters doing stupid and suicidal actions with no recourse and no way to express how they feel about it. I spent a good deal of time trying to help the characters involved avoid their fates .... so much so that I got vertigo from the screen panning. There just was no way and eventually I just gave up and played along. This is a similar experience to the Stockholm syndrome (sympathetic hostage syndrome) as you are trapped by your investment in the game and wanting to help the characters and see the end. This registers on you without you even realizing it.

The second problem is that that writers seem to have a certain glee in crafting the demise of their characters and try to make pointless thoughtless deaths into something more, and glorify psychopathic behavior , both which are just nasty.

Two other games come to mind, both of which involve a child being murdered over and over and over ...... Little Nightmares and Limbo

Death voyeurism is a very sad human affliction. This game is just that, and it doesn't really matter to me what sugar coated message is at the end regarding family or the freedom to choose even when those choices aren't good.
