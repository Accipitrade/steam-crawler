\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWow! What a truly amazing game!

This is the "video games as an art form" concept at its best. A beautiful and immersive collection of genuine, creative, incredibly emotional stories, connected together by exploration and narration.

All the stories are stories of death. You cannot prevent the deaths; you must play through each story until your character inevitably dies - essentially the opposite of how most video games are played! Many of the characters are children, which makes those stories especially heartbreaking.

The stories are told in a variety of different formats; for example, one story takes you into a comic book, while another story happens completely inside your imagination as you monotonically chop fish at a factory.

I played it in one sitting, which lasted about 2.5 hours, and I was so engaged with the game that I did not leave my computer (aside from a couple of bio breaks) until I finished it. The graphics and visual design are exceptional - make sure to play on the highest quality settings if possible - and the music is moving and hypnotic.

Without the interactive components, this game could have easily made a great feature-length film... But the video game format has the advantage of allowing you to interact with the story and feel personally invested in it, which makes it more meaningful and relatable than just sitting down and watching a movie.

If you're looking for combat, strategy, puzzles, or non-linear storytelling - this isn't the game for you... But if you're looking for a touching interactive story about family and loss - look no further.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a beautiful walking sim, with a story that some may relate to, and others will not.
It was believable. Told like a "real" person would tell it, like a real 17 year old, in Edith's place, would tell it.
Like my now 18 y/o daughter would tell it, perhaps.

I wish I had played this when it first came out. I am glad I finally bought it and played it now.
Beautiful world, with some slightly annoying invisible walls, not as open as I would have liked. But, open enough for me to stop and take in the scenery, and the environmental storytelling it provided.
Took replaying a few chapters to get all achievements, no guides used. Probably easier to get them all in one run, if you use a guide. I'll leave that up to you to decide to do or not.
My time playing is probably a bit on the long side, since I like to wander, and see every inch of a world that I can in games like this.

I enjoyed it, as bittersweet and sad as it was, especially at the end.

Recommended, but mostly for people who enjoy a slower-paced narrative driven experience, and not for speedrunners, who want to zip through everything, and who may miss many of the little things that made the game resonate with/for me. It is a slow game, some bits were a tad tedious (the labyrinth...), but still interesting enough to make the tedium of completing it worth it, for me. YMMV.\t\t\t\t\t

Often we could only tell who they were from a name on the back of the photo, and sometimes all we could do was guess. Anyway What Remains of Edith Finch is a wonderful game and although it's short it is worth experiencing. The way this game tells it's stories is fantastic.

In the game you play a 17 year old girl called Edith who returns to the Finch family home after her mother passes. The home itself is an odd one and inside it's even stranger, as many of the rooms are sealed up. The reason being they are the rooms of unfortunate relatives who passed away in tragic accidents. You have to explore your way through the strange house to access the different rooms and discover the stories of Ediths different relatives who passed away over the years.

It's the way these stories are told is what sets this game apart. The stories are mostly fantastical imaginitive retellings that you will playthrough. So for one your playing out the story in a comic, another sees you turning into a shark and roll down a hill. It's a really entertaining way to experience each family members story and I was really impressed with the way the game balances the often light hearted and humurous stuff with the sadder and more tragic moments. I think this game is a great demonstration of how a game can be a medium for art and for great storytelling and it rightfully deserves the praise it has received.
