I played Gone Home and was disappointed. It was too slow-paced, felt a bit empty and the story didn't justify the tense atmosphere of the game. I thought this genre was not made for me...

But then I heard that Edith Finch was very good and elevated this genre. So I had to try it and see for myself.
And I was not disapointed this time. The game feels polished, the movement is smooth and precise and I enjoyed the unique gameplay parts each story asked me to use.
Every family member has its own interesting bit to share and it's a pleasure to discover it. the house does not feel empty at all, every part of it is used cleverly and is still easy to navigate through without long backtracking parts.

From start to finish, every objet you can interract with is positionned so you don't feel lost and the effort the development team put into this game to make the experience rich and entertaining.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn the surface, What Remains of Edit Finch tells an almost bizarre tale of a really unlucky family. What makes this game special is how surgical it uses gameplay and presentation to tell its story. The game only lasts two hours, but these two hours feature a staggering variety in gameplay mechanics and presentation styles, always supporting the narrative perfectly. The result is something that is simply much more than the sum of its parts.

What Remains of Edit Finch has restored my confidence in the future of story-driven games. It does not try to be cinematic, but instead presents its story using the unique possibilities only available in games. We also need more short games like it that focus on quality rather than quantity.\t\t\t\t\t
