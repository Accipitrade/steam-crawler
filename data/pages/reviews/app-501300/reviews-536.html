\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game depicts the narration of a journey in the old house of a family that is said to be afflicted by a mysterious curse; in each room, it is possible to re-experience the death, or transformation, or vanishing of one of the Finches.

Bad reviews may say that "What Remains of Edith Finch" is a walking simulator. This is inaccurate, in that each room of the mysterious house represents peculiar and innovative modes of interaction and most of them provide the opportunity for experiences that are totally new for video games and simulative technologies in general. In any case, I don't even see a problem with a "walking simulator", if you have the possibility to walk among amazing things and suggestive visions.

There's more innovation in this game than in most of the modern FPSs and RPGs; Although not perfect (e.g.: I would have preferred a finale with more specific explanation and consequences of the main character's actions), "What remains of Edith Finch" sets a new gold standard for interactive storytelling, to be taken as one of the points of reference for innovation in the narrative-based video games of the future.

In my opinion, this is a game to play and to have in your library.

vote: 8/9 out of 10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe most obvious comparison for this is Gone Home. Both are about young women coming back to a large house of eccentric design, filled with secret passages and hidden doors, and trying to find out what became of the family that lived there, using books and correspondence to discover clues.

While Edith Finch is a lot more visually imaginative (the comic book sequence, the cannery kingdom...) the gameplay is a lot more dull and straightforward. In Gone Home one feels like an amateur detective finding clues and piecing together a mystery but here all sense of agency is gone. Walk in to a room and the game directs you to what you should be looking at. Read a book and you're treated to essentially a one-button minigame. The cannery sequence might look great but gameplay-wise you're essentially just walking forward. If Gone Home felt like being part of an immersive theatre experience where one can rifle through the characters' things to find out more about them, this feels like an animated film where the W button must be held down to keep it playing.

There's a lot to like here and it's fun while it lasts but it doesn't feel anywhere near as satisfying a gameplay experience as a lot of similar games.\t\t\t\t\t
