\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI gave it a positive review, but I have to turn it into a negative one. The game has an elaborated story, with great atmosphere. The way the narrative is developed is very well done, each section told in an unique way. Good job!

The game tells the story of a family that needed therapy ASAP from the perspective of the last living member. I mean, it's tragedy after tragedy, all preventable.

Leaving a little girl's bedroom window barely close? Leave a baby alone in the bathtub? Keep a secret that an uncle has lived underground for 30 years? Finding it normal for a guy to get lost in a mind-blowing imagination while chopping fish at work? Letting kids do what they want during a storm at a wedding? A lady walking on the beach at low tide in the mist? Sure, nothing wrong could happen. Not like anything bad had happened in the family. A lot of this can happen to anyone, but I would stay out of a house where several of my relatives passed away. And the protagonist returns to the place, an abandoned house in the middle of nowhere, while she is 22 weeks pregnant. I mean, Geez!

It's very short, so I'd say buy if it's on sale. The real problem with this game is the FOV. You can't change it, so turn the reticle ON! I got really bad motion sickness from it. I had to play each story and then take a break. 85% of my playtime with this game is me laying down, trying to recover to go futher with the story. I can't give a game a go if can't play it properly. I couldn't immerse myself in the tales. I'ts not that terrible later in the game and this bad take from me can be very personal, but I found other players having the same problem.

It can be finished in a sitting and damn that's a nice sitting. Edith Finch, despite being so short and playing like an interactive movie, tells a sweet story filled with smaller character arcs about each and every member of Edith's departed relatives. The game carries a significant emotional power and with some good ass music to boot you'll find yourself either tearing up or thinking over all of the things this game tosses your way. There's no action, no complicated mechanics, no nothing. Just a tale. Edith Finch is a game completely worth your time if you don't mind short experiences and love great storytelling. 8/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're planning to buy it - I recommend you wait until it's on sale, it's not worth its full price. It took me about 2 hours to play through and I don't think I'll ever replay it. I didn't really know anything about it when I bought it, other than the good reviews. However, I think I would've enjoyed watching somebody else play it almost as much as I did playing it myself. Shortly put, you might as well watch someone else play it on youtube or likewise.

Now - I did think the game created an amazing atmosphere. As an architect student I was impressed by the house and how it set the mood. A bit eerie, almost chilling at some points. The artwork is stunning and I love the concept of the game. But I do think the story falls a bit short. I didn't quite feel invested in any of the characters and frankly, it left me bored at times.

It's hard to say whether or not I recommend "What Remains of Edith Finch". All I can say is that it was an overall good experience and that I don't regret buying it (on sale). If you can afford this game without a problem, then absolutely, go for it! But if you're in a tighter economic situation, I would probably recommend looking for a walkthrough that seems interesting instead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tConsidering I don't really play games of this particular genre, I was pleasantly surprised. The photo-realism of the artstyle was gorgeous and the whole experience was therapeutic and contemplative as it documents the story of the Finch family who appear to be cursed as every member has mysteriously vanished or died and it's left to Edith to regale the tale of her descendants through a journal and she narrates your every movement and the differing of perspectives as each short story about the specific family member unfolds were executed beautifully, offering some very innovative and unique takes to gameplay that I haven't seen in the medium before. The original soundtrack / score was melancholy while also being very calming which fits the tone of the story itself and the ending is quite a doozy which gave me goosebumps. Navigating the island home of the Finches whilst hearing the backstories was a delight and this was a stellar achievement in storytelling from something that isn't a Triple A title.

I think this game was deserving of the awards and acclaim heaped upon it during release and it's been in my mind to try out for a while after spotting it on several best game lists but now I got to experience it for myself and it did not disappoint.
The only downside is, it's rather short but it delivers the message with great execution and polish. An atmospheric bittersweet masterpiece with elements of Life is Strange in the exploration, Giant Sparrow take a bow.

9.7/10\t\t\t\t\t

The Verdict:
Get it. If you are a sucker for walking simulators, inventive, clever ways to deliver story and controller gimmicks. With a healthy dose of heartfelt story.

What Remains of Edith Finch sounds a lot like The Vanishing of Ethan Carter, which made me think that it was a sequel or made by the same studio. Both are not the case. Still, it received even more hype so I had to see what it's all about.

You are Edith Finch, Jr., who returns to her family home that they abandoned years ago. The Finches have, over generations, been cursed, and the story unfolds by you reliving the memories of each individual Finch. That's all I'm willing to say, this is the kind of game where you want to avoid all spoilers and be surprised by what the game throws at you.

I got it on sale for slightly more than a movie ticket. And at about 2-2.5 hours for a single playthrough, it's got a length slightly longer than your average feature film. I feel it more suitable to compare to a movie than other games because this game is such an atmospheric, story-driven experience without a lot of elements that would qualify it as a "game"; if that makes sense. But it still is a game, because:

Mechanically?
it makes full use of the interactivity inherent in games to deliver its narrative. Each different story takes place in a unique setting and gives you a new way to control and interact with the level. Those mechanics are fantastically creative and satisfying to play as they are highly contextual to the story MINOR SPOILER (an early level turns a child's midnight hunger into possessing and controlling bigger and stronger predators as your hunger grows) , eliminating any dissonance that you would often feel when playing mechanics that are too basic or have no real relation to the story. It's such an improvement over the mechanics in games like Gone Home (walking, reading, narration, little more than an interactive slideshow) or Telltale's quicktime events (induces tension, but has little to no relation to the action being controlled) by also reinforcing the immersion in the story and setting.

Art-wise?
The art style is not cutting edge definition but with realistic proportions. Rather like most 3D indie games. MINOR SPOILER Some levels change up the artstyle in ways related to the story. Very cool trick.
The music is excellent. Melancholic, mysterious, haunting. In a game where you are mostly exploring alone, the music does everything to create the atmosphere.

Narrative
MINOR SPOILERS The story goes deep into the Finch family, examining the effects that living under a supposed "family curse" has on what could have been otherwise a completely normal family. The story is both morbid from the death surrounding the family but almost always has a hopeful undertone, viewed from the eyes of Edith Finch. Before you play, be ready to absorb the whole story and finish it in one sitting. It's the kind of story that sticks with you.

Technically?
Having no graphically intensive scenes, the game should run well on most PCs. It doesn't do much to introduce any new technical gimmicks, but the creative implementation of existing controls contextualised into new actions really makes it stand out.

THE INNOVATION FACTOR
This game is clever in the many little ways you get to play out the story. Not many games have done this before and this game does it so naturally and beautifully it has definitely set a new bar for narrative-driven game design.

Now my favourite narrative-driven experience video game walking simulator thingamajig. Sorry, The Wolf Among Us.
