\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a story about generations and the potentially disfunctional nature of families. It is the heartbreaking story about how we clash, burn our bridges and lose sight of one another. It is also a story about how we cannot escape the past, and how we ultimately can only understand ourselves when we look to that past.

But mostly, it is a story about hope and passing on a gift - THE gift - of love.

Having said that, these themes are woven into a mindblowing, heartfelt narrative, that never failed to surprise me and kept me completely engaged. The different chapters of the Finch Family members are told in such creative ways that it often felt like a game within a game within a game. If you love titles like Gone Home and Dear Esther you should do yourself a favor and get this game right now. I promise you, you will not be disappointed.

9/10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeautiful story told through a game with some very interesting and unique twists and experimentation with narrative perspectives, perceptions etc... It really is brilliant.

Just LISTEN TO THIS !!!:

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Hs4cQOLiipY

The graphics, the music, the environmental storytelling, the design of the rooms and furniture. Everything has a place and time. Think Gone Home but with significantly more gripping premise, better storytelling and a more interesting house layout overall. There is a lot to explore and the game manages to create a lot of atmosphere around the local legend of "curse" I dont want to spoil so I wont say more than that.

The way the story is told through the eyes of the characters relative to their subjective experience and perception and even with corresponding gameplay mechanics is unlike much I've seen in gaming so far, not completely revolutionary either, but still a very refreshing take. It makes the story surprisingly personal and I grew attached to all the characters almost obliviously.

All in all an amazing game that will make you think, feel and maybe even question some things...

I can't recommend enough because games that try to portray stories like this are scarce, and I want to support the production of such titles. This is a very personal game that stays with you after you finish it. It's pretty much a masterpiece of storytelling in my book already\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch; the remarkable rare occurrence of a game that truly gives…..and takes….in almost precisely equal measure. An experience almost certain not to disappoint anyone feeling drawn toward this type of storytelling.

WRoEF is the simple-to-complete account of the peculiarly cursed Finch family, and the Winchester House-like abode that, in its very purpose, brought them all together for generations. Yet a togetherness with a price, and which ultimately serves to remove them from each other, from reality, and in case after case, from life itself. A house of sealed rooms, intended to keep individual secrets and personal suffering trapped inside, visible only through each door’s distorted and skewed peephole view. Yet a house of hidden passages and yet-to-be-discovered access allowing personal contact with each and every secret at its very source. (Each room’s secret offering, from within, an even MORE distorted and skewed reality of a component of a family history composed of wonders, terrors, disappointments, and small miracles.) Each room, each family member, each sealed door is its own account of imagination, isolation, hope and loss. But not without the love, and even individual sense of belonging, existent in even the most "cursed" of families.

WRoEF is one of the most creatively, imaginatively “told” games I’ve ever played, and one that that only very rarely disappoints. No actual puzzles interrupt the flow of the story, and (almost) every next step is clearly identified, as is game progress as recorded on a hand-notated family tree. Few games have offered this reviewer the variety of experience, with amazingly little repetition even in such things as movement and interaction. Variety of experience and storytelling seems almost to be the very goal of WRoEF, and in that aspect, disappointment is as rare as is boredom in the presentation of a million tiny details, all designed to entertain and amuse. Even the game credits offer a rare opportunity to experience, and even feel a special part of this remarkable game.
