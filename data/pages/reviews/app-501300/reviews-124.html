\r\n\t\t\t\t\t“Fear becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. Thinking that there is something to be avoided manifests something to avoid.”
― Vironika Tugaleva

Broken down to the absolute basics, this is a series of mini-games packaged together, hidden within a walking simulator. You walk through an old house belonging to your family, learning about your family history by literally stepping into their shoes for a short while. There are some endearingly ridiculous moments, and some mildly traumatizing moments all wrapped in a glittery bow.

I'm honestly not often fond of walking simulators, but this one managed to squeeze it's way into being one of my favorite short games. I went from balking at the price tag, to wondering where I could find more games by the developer. If you're looking for a beautiful visual experience that can be completed in one sitting, achievements and all, I'd say give this one a try. Take your time with it, and enjoy.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDevelopment team Giant Sparrow’s “What Remains of Edith Finch” is a love letter to our imagination. In our imagination, we can do anything, be anyone. In our imagination, we don’t have to worry about anything else. In “Edith Finch”, we are given a look into the creative magic of video games. We’re taken into the worlds of bath toys, cameras, and a swing set with old, creaky chains. It’s not about the individual objects themselves but the ideas they represent. They manifest in magical landscapes that seep into the gameplay, and the only thing we can do is stare in awe at the artistry on display. This is that type of game – driven by a magical narrative with a message that hits close to home.

You play as the titular Edith Finch in this game. I say “play”, but you’re more like a sleuth. A detective of sorts. You’ll be finding clues and uncovering secrets. The gameplay consists of walking, but I won’t reveal any more than I need to. All you need to know is that Edith heads back into her home, a grand housing structure that stretches far above in height and encompasses an even bigger volume of space – and memories – inside. She’s trying to uncover a mystery with each clue given to her by every member of her family tree (aptly named “The Finches”), and you’ll uncover it with her.

In “Edith Finch”, the end matters just as much as the means. You’ll see what I mean. Before then, we explore. Each area in the game is beautifully crafted from the contours of our imagination. In a dream, we might see a narrative unfold within another narrative; sometimes, our dreams take place in our memories, holding surreal settings and feelings that coincide with a tangible sense of time and space. “Edith Finch” functions in that same way; each area seeps into the overarching narrative of the game. At one point, you’ll be in the air. At another point, you’ll be maneuvering as another being. Most of all, you’ll be in the psyche of each of the Finches.

You’ll be surprised by how metatextual the game can be at times, using simple mechanics to convey a unique approach to the story. In this case, the story holds many stories. Each family member conveys a message that can be as simple as stardom and dreams but can also be as dense as divorce and regret. The best of fiction allows you to interpret its significance. “Edith Finch” lays its topics bare, but it also allows you to think about what they all mean when gathered into a body of emotion. It’s fun to digest in other media, but I imagine it’s even more fun within the interactive scale of video games.

This interactivity stretches into the many places you’ll explore. Allow me to indulge you in one example. You’re working with fish but then there’s something more; there’s a different mechanic and a depiction of how we multi-task between work and our feelings. You’re a factory worker and then a traveling wanderer and then a king. Giant Sparrow uses the basic mouse and keyboard controls in order to implement creative environmental storytelling techniques.

I know I said I wouldn’t reveal more than I need to, but don’t worry – the game is full of surprises. You’ll see it manifest in the way the game handles topics that may be closer to us than what we’re comfortable with, but they’re all handled with an air of maturity. The game’s tones range from playful to mysterious to dramatic, but Giant Sparrow is always respectful. In the same way that our imagination can take us into faraway lands, it can also be used to tether universal understandings of ourselves. The difference is that it’s portrayed alongside a parade of quirky imagery.

Giant Sparrow’s “What Remains of Edith Finch” encourages us to appreciate. That’s a beautiful thing. Sometimes, having a work of fiction do that can be surprising, but isn’t that what our imagination is? It can amaze us, comfort us, and nurture our understanding of the world. I’m reminded of stories I consumed in my childhood. I read all the Percy Jackson novels, watched a few Godzilla films from the Heisei era, and got scared by the Shake, Rattle & Roll films from the Philippines. These works nurtured my imagination and helped me appreciate my own life. “What Remains of Edith Finch” elicits those familiar feelings. The heroine of the game describes it perfectly.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you have ADHD or like to decide what kind of tempo you wanna play in, this game is a nightmare. I have ADHD myself. I think at least twice as fast as most people I know. I run through life, and yet, I catch details most people miss. I am not sure I can express in kind words how much I hate games that force me to walk around in... well, a walking tempo. It makes my skin crawl. It upset my stomach. It's like working on a computer 15 years ago, before speed was invented.

But why do some developers think that this is a good idea? Is it because they feel it's unfair if the player wouldn't catch every little atom of the game they've spent so much time developing, or miss any of the very obviously placed hints? Is it a way to make a small game feel larger because it will triple the time of walking from point A to point B?

The game is a nightmare to play. It is like walking around in a photo. Sure, it is quite beautiful, but has a very limited movement, you can only interact with a few spots here and there, and then you have to, for instance, swing a door open, like this would be an incredibly new thing that the casual player had not already done tens of thousands of times IN REAL LIFE. I can tell you: there is no need to simulate such mundane every day actions in a game. It is just another gimmick to make game-play longer. A sad side effect is that the slow tempo does not encourage you to go exploring. Walking away from the main path only to find a blind alley means you have to go all way back again.

It would be one thing if you could save the game, but OH NO, while you have to do exciting stuff like swing doors open or rotate keys manually, the game wants to spare you from the hassle of doing boring things like saving games. In fact, you're not allowed to save at all. The result? You will spend lot's of time playing through the same super boring passages with the tempo of a snail, because the save points are not close enough. Visualizing my soon to be heart attack I quickly uninstalled the game and requested a refund, before adding Giant Sparrow to my ever expanding list of condescending "anti-save-and-run" developers.\t\t\t\t\t
