\r\n\t\t\t\t\tProbably the new leader of the "Walking Simulator" genre (taking over for Vanishing of Ethan Carter): Flawless for what it is, and what Dear Esther and Gone Home aspired to be.

If this had been a movie, it wouldn't have worked--it'd be garden variety Lifetime Original trash. But in this form and format, it's beautiful. The environments are masterful, and all characterization is done through the environments. The pacing, flow, and direction are sublime. The minigame aspects of each vignette are hit or miss, but never interfere with moving the story forward.

The tear-jerking schmaltz would be insufferably florid in a movie, but there's something about having the player push the inevitable forward with his own hands (even though you have to have figured out that all roads are going to end in tragedy and disaster) that enmeshes you and hard-wires your sympathy and empathy centers into the story.

It's pricey for a 2-hour "game," but $20 makes sense for a novel digital experience, akin to VR. Also it goes without saying, this isn't a gamey-game, it's very much a walking sim. I procrastinated playing this because of the art-house factor, and all the accolades and awards from all the wrong people, but if you are at all attracted to the visual novel/walking sim/explore the house genre, this is the current cream of the crop.\t\t\t\t\t

What Remains of Edith Finch is absolutely a game of the Walking Simulator variety, no puzzles or challenges to overcome here. What it presents you with is a story you take in at a tempo you control. A story about a strange family history that took place in and around a bizarre and wondrous house.

The mood of the game/story reminded me strongly of childrens' books with a dark and tragic undertone, like Roald Dahl. With quality narration and dynamically animated floating subtitles (and a bit of nudging in the right direction), you're smoothly led through the quite various vignettes that spin the tale together.

The story gets, in places, funny, touching and sad. Aside from people who just don't like walkking simulators in general, I imagine not everyone's going to feel the ending satisfies. I feel it works. I generally like just a little bit more gameplay in these, just a few stumbling blocks for me to overcome to make me feel that I'm needed, but as a story you walk through, it's hauntingly pretty.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA great many walking simulators are simply roaming around pretty environments, triggering narration by passing by locations or hitting an interact button on various objects for a few hours, until whatever story they're telling comes to a close. However, rather than going for that bare minimum amount of actual "game" as so many walking sims do, What Remains of Edith Finch fully realizes the genre's potential as a storytelling medium by jam-packing its beautifully crafted scenes with interactivity, going above and beyond in immersing you in its already engrossing tale.

As the store page describes, you'll find various memorials to Edith's late family members as you explore her ancestral home, and each of these triggers a flashback sequence where you take control of that family member in the moments leading up to their death. These sections do away with the walking sim stuff for a wide variety of different activities, ranging from more mundane stuff like taking photographs or flying a kite, to exploring a dungeon maze within a daydream, or controlling a toy frog as it bounces around inside of a bathtub. Each section sports something totally unique, so no one type of gameplay ever overstays its welcome. Likewise, you find flashbacks regularly enough that even if you're the type to bore quickly with the walking sections, new gameplay opportunities keep popping up often enough that you'll have no trouble remaining motivated towards exploring around to find them.

In typical walking sim fashion none of these sections can be outright failed, but they're far more than just pressing E and watching your character play out some animations, or the sort of pseudo-interactive cutscenes that some AAA games use where you can fiddle about with objects while the scene automatically moves forward; you'll have to actually perform whatever the intended actions are to advance the story, and a few even incorporate some puzzle or platforming elements for you to figure out. Going with this above-average level of interactivity lends an extra layer of immersion to the whole deal, and it does a great job of killing any chance for that "ugh it's like I'm just watching a movie and not even playing a game" feeling to crop up.

On the story front, both the overarching tale of the Finch Curse and the individual stories of the family members are incredibly well-written. The slow unfolding of the overall story is perfectly paced, forming a nice trail of narrative breadcrumbs for you to follow as you move between flashback sections. It keeps just enough stuff ambiguous that the eerie, mysterious, and looming presence of the Finch house isn't lost on the player by the end of the game, but still gives you enough information that the conclusion is satisfying, forming one of those fun little "this is going to have hundreds of forum posts discussing fan theories" sort of endings.

Flashbacks are likewise wonderfully paced, starting neither too close nor too far from the family member's demise and advancing through events such that even in the more mundane stories there's never a truly drab moment. The deaths are believable and never fall into such narrative pits as ridiculously chance circumstances or suddenly life-threatening circumstances out of nowhere, while still maintaining a healthy amount of surprise happenings or otherwise hard-to-guess endings. Even in cases where the oncoming cause of the death is far more obvious, everything is presented in such a way that the entertaining lead-up more than makes up for your early realization.

Most individual flashback stories are narrated either by the deceased themselves or by a relevant 3rd party, rather than Edith. In the case of the former, this is yet another aspect that contributes to the game's amazing immersion factor, as it does a fantastic job of helping you understand just what was going through that character's head as events unfolded. Even in the case of the latter, the unique ways the various narrators present the stories (a letter from a psychiatrist giving an analysis of the deceased's mental state leading up to their death, or the brother of the deceased giving a eulogy, to name a couple) still do wonders for setting the tone of those flashbacks, in turn doing a great job at getting you into whichever mindset the developers were going for for a particular section.

Though people used to games averaging 8-20 hours may at first balk at this only being about 2-2.5 hours long, know that this is a more than satisfying length, as the narrative progresses at a pace more akin to a movie. Whereas many games often have lulls in the form of backtracking, storyless side missions, long gunfight sections between story moments, or the like, Edith Finch elects to instead maintain a constant and quick pace of new narrative events its entire duration, and as a result the average length of a game story is rendered far more compact at no detriment to its quality.

Graphically, the game is absolutely beautiful, and each section sports its own unique environments and objects that prevent the visuals from ever getting stale. Even the walking section is impressive, as the Finch house is filled to the brim with all sorts of unique doodads and heirlooms. I think the only thing I ever saw repeated was the titles on the spines of some books, and even those only repeated within a given room, as any family member who has a bookshelf has it stocked with things relevant to their own interests and not just some generic stock titles.

Sound and music design is likewise fabulous. From the flapping of a kite in the wind, the creaking ropes of a tree swing, or the fantastically-voiced dialogue, everything is crisp and realistic, acting as that last bit of icing on the immersion cake. The music is very well composed, genuinely capturing the emotions of the scenes each track accompanies, and it does a great job of setting the tones of both wandering about the house and experiencing the flashbacks. Even when they go the public domain route and drop some Tchaikovsky&#65279; in, it pairs astonishingly well with the scene it's used in.

To reiterate something I've mentioned across various paragraphs so far, by far the game's strongest point is how well it immerses you in its story and characters. The actual interactivity, gorgeous visuals, moving soundtrack, and captivating story all mesh together to form an experience that absolutely draws you into its world, and that act of being drawn in only proceeds to amplify the already high quality of each aspect of the game.

In closing, Edith Finch is the sort of experience walking sim devs should look towards as an inspiration, the type of game they should be striving to make. It absolutely shatters the stereotype of walking sims being nigh-gameplayless shells that only exist as a thin framework for the narrative, helping cement the genre as a legitimate game genre and not just glorified movies where you use WASD to move from scene-to-scene. Anyone who's a big fan of story-driven games, or looking for something comfy to play that won't threaten them with the regular gaming stresses of losing or getting stuck on a level ought to have an incredible time with it, and there's few other games I could so strongly recommend as being worth playing regardless of your genre preferences.

10/10, Gold Star of just really, really being fab.
