\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me be clear about a few things: I rarely feel inclined to leave reviews for games, even if I do find them entertaining, but this was the ultimate exception. Although this isn't known to be a game in the traditional sense, this will have an immeasurable amount of impact. You are paying for an experience that is absolutely worth it. Initially, I had only heard about the game and knew nothing about it, which, upon hearing some testimonies to it's reputation, I decided to give it a try. It is an incredibly beautiful style and does something that you will almost never find so well done in today's media, writing, or entertainment; that is, character development. Video games are frequently reputable and deserve to be commended for their character development which is tragically lost today. What surprised me so much about this game was how attached you can find yourself with as little time and prompting that you get with each member of the family. The music creates such tremendous environments and you find yourself completely engulfed in the style of each location and action as you discover each story. I am not a very emotional person, yet because of such a powerful delivery and sound understanding of profound character traits, I find myself still emotional and fighting through tears as I think about it. I cried a lot during this game. I had just finished the game and no sooner found myself listening to the soundtrack as I type this. It is excellent at drawing you in and making you feel it's story. There were many times that I was so engaged in a story that I would silently yell "no!" as it progressed toward the inevitable, or I would laugh along in sorrowful acknowledgement of those character traits that make you love them, or even small comments like "I know, I've been there" as Edie told us about the smell from the low tide of the Puget Sound. If you have not played this game, I recommend it with the highest possible enthusiasm I can summon, and I also recommend that if you can, make time where the game will not be interrupted or broken up and please, soak in the environment and story. I absolutely loved this experience.
One final note that will be directed toward the developers if ever they see this (slight spoilers): Thank you. I cannot adequately describe how much this impacted me. I deeply want to go back and see more of the details and curious touches you've put in your game, but all I can say for now with the limited knowledge and poor observation skills that I have is: I will never forgive you for the foreshadowing you put on the light switch in Dawn's room with the follow-up in the credits. That's when you broke me into ugly crying. I'm sure there is plenty more that I missed which would have evoked the same reaction (I really started crying at the end of the birth sequence), but the symbolism behind that hit pretty hard. Very well done. I am equally impressed as I am grateful and currently emotionally wrecked. Please keep making more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDear Esther by way of Gabriel Garcia Marquez, What Remains of Edith Finch is a gorgeous, magical-realist take on the "walking simulator." A charming piece of artistry and an audio-visual feast, this is a well-written and wonderfully voice-acted tale of lost family and unwelcome memory.

More linear than the cunningly put-together Gone Home, and with little in the way of problem-solving, Edith Finch's gameplay is limited, albeit enlivened by a few imaginative sequences I won't spoil. With just two hours of run time and limited replay value, thirty dollary-dos might also strike you as rather steep. I'd advise waiting for a sale. Despite these quibbles, however, I wouldn't hesitate to recommend Edith Finch as a shockingly imaginative amuse-bouche of interactive fiction. If you enjoy this, don't miss Fullbright's classic Gone Home and their follow-up Tacoma. For a superb slab of first-person narrative wrapped around fiendish puzzles, I also recently loved The Talos Principle.\t\t\t\t\t
