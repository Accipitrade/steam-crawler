\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch recounts the tragic ends of Finch family members while you explore their long-abandoned home as the last living Finch, briefly taking you back in time and putting you in each of their shoes as they experience their final moments.

As a narrative-driven exploration game—essentially an interactive storybook and lovingly referred to by some as a walking simulator—it offers little in terms of traditional gameplay and instead relies on a well-told and believably voiced story to hold the player's attention for a few hours.

The Finch family is by no means ordinary, believing themselves to be cursed for generations with plenty of evidence to support that conclusion. This makes them worthy of building a game around and gives the player a reason to be interested in the history of a random family, but fleshing out and resolving the mystery surrounding them are unfortunately not parts of the game.

At a glance, one might expect horror elements or a haunted house setting but there’s none of that here, at least not in the imminently dangerous or supernatural sense. The haunting in What Remains of Edith Finch is more the lingering and pervasive memories, history, and feelings within a home or family that can haunt those who remain and drive them away; the painful reminders of things lost, kept out of sight but not out of reach because they're all that's left; and how some cope by clinging to what's left while others have to let go.

As someone who's the latter, I found the game strangely beautiful but immensely depressing: The sense of loss, despair, loneliness, and unfairness evoked and memorialized by all these mementos and moments frozen in time in a world not unlike our own was overwhelming, making me feel like not only the last Finch, but the last person on Earth.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you're looking for a game to relax and explore at your own pace filled with charming (albeit a bit morbid) stories, you should try this. It's a strange feeling to have a game reliving deaths, but I feel that this game delivers them well. It also made me have an endearment for all of the characters even though I rarely see their faces. I especially love Milton and Lewis's stories because I secretly relate to them and that made it a refreshing experience.

Music is beautiful, and graphics are nice and detailed even when I am playing on a hopeless laptop. The story takes about 3-4 hour on average and I was hoping it would be longer. If you're particular about price, getting it on sale is pretty worth it. It's a beautiful game to have in your collection.\t\t\t\t\t
