\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYeah, I don't know. I don't know how I feel about this game. I liked it, but I also really disliked some of its biggest selling points, which, since this is regarded as a narrative masterpiece, means I am doomed to focus almost exclusively on the stuff I didn't care for.

I honestly can't figure out what this game was supposed to be. Tragedy? Comedy? Quirky Wes Anderson mix of everything? I got a lot of cognitive dissonance out of the writing, which would sometimes shine, and other times feel hackneyed or just random.

My ability to get lost in the house and the stories also kept getting disrupted by my suspension of disbelief going on and off. I could never believe the danged house or individual rooms had been abandoned -- everything was so clean and props never fit the age of the room.*

Besides that...no one acted real. Everyone just seemed to make decisions to force a tragedy and some heartfelt text. That text when taken out of context was, like, well-written, but with the context it just feels disconnected from reality+. I both liked and *really* disliked the writing in this game simultaneously. A couple of the characters tugged at my heartstrings, but most of them are surprisingly undeveloped before tragedy strikes and you're supposed to feel awed by their life and the events that shaped (or stopped) it. A lot of those moments honestly rang hollow.

TECHNICALLY IMPRESSIVE, though. The game is gorgeous, and the effects -- oh my! There are some great moments...and it made me long for the Pacific Northwest -- it's really cool viewing this and being familiar with Orcas Island, even if this doesn't quite look like the island much. The amount of detail in the house is stunning -- there are so many hidden nooks to explore, and so many of the objects feel unique and hand-crafted for their special little place in this cluttered-as-hell Frankenstein house.

Impressed by Edith Finch, but, yeah, I didn't love it. I'd still recommend it for those interested in either narrative design or adventure games.

-----

* (Was that...intentional? Was 7-year-old Chinese food still supposed to be on the kitchen table? Were rooms locked away since the '60s supposed to have fresh food and books published after in them? Was a flimsy wooden house supposed to really bit sitting in the rough PNW currents for 100 years, intact? The reality of almost everything in this game kinda falls apart if you think about it one second too long.)

+ Lewis' story is the epitome of this, as he's one of the most developed characters late-game. Despite the tragedy he's meant to evoke, his entire story is narrated through a letter from his psychologist that is *unbelievably* melodramatic and bonkers -- no psychologist would write a letter like that to a grieving parent, and no psychologist would have treated Lewis that way as a patient. (She basically set him up for suicide, wtf??)\t\t\t\t\t
