\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI enjoyed this and have played it twice. If you're looking for a stunning visual experience and some spooky stories to be immersed in, this game is perfect for you. However, if you're looking for puzzles, exploration or a satisfying resolution to the mystery, you may leave disappointed.

Pros:
- The game is beautiful to walk through. There is a sensation of being physically within the experience as you move the mouse to move your character's hands, unlatching gates or opening windows and then climbing and crawling through the spaces.
- Each story is a work of art in its own right, with a different type of presentation for each one. You participate in every story in a different way, adding novelty at each step.

Pro or con, depends on your preference:
- There is no exploration; the game is linear and you never have to wonder where to go next. It's basically a visual novel where you're unlocking the story piece by piece. I loved the linearity because it was easy to play and there were no choices to worry about. Others will find the hand-holding boring.

Cons:
- Although it's set up as a mystery where we want to discover why all these family members keep dying, there's no resolution to that mystery. This family is just darn unlucky. The stories are each interesting in their own right, but ultimately seem to have no connection to each other besides unfortunate coincidence. The first time I played the game, I was hooked by the mystery. The second time, knowing that there was no real answer, I found it a lot less enjoyable.
- A small point compared with the rest, but it was very dark a lot of the time, to the point where, even with the screen on full brightness, I couldn't see what I was doing and could barely work out where to go.

What Remains of Edith Finch is a brilliant experience in many ways, but it feels like a visual novel rather than a game, and the lack of a real conclusion lets it down. Whether you'll enjoy it depends on the type of gameplay you're after.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's debatable whether a walking simulator is a game. But I don't recall any Pope requiring transformatively moving experiences to have a "you lose" screen.

The stories are engaging, well built, and well told. The characters are fascinating. The world is beautiful, and bumbling around in it isn't just a device to aid immersion, but actually part of helping the game tell its own story. The control mechanics are intuitive and sometimes even brilliant. (The highlight of course being the much-praised cannery section, where the "real world" controls are tedious, but necessary to avoid obstructing the much more interesting and complicated "imaginary world.")

The game pretty much exists only to say "don't be sad it's over, be glad it happened." It wants to say it in the most beautiful way it can. It wants you to feel it. Give it a shot.\t\t\t\t\t
