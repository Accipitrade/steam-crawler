\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love "walking simulators". Albeit the term has negative connotations, I never felt it was pejorative - it's just a name of a genre. Dear Esther and Firewatch are among my favorite video games I ever played, and a friend recommended What Remains Of Edith Finch to me comparing it to those two games. After reading a bunch of reviews, I decided to get the game, expecting a beatiful, narrative journey, that would possibly utterly defeat me emotionally.

Instead, I got a collection of minigames ranging from fun to boring and from a bit emotional to utterly dull, wrapped in two hours of gameplay, and adorned with a barely interesting narrative.

Let's start with the good things, though. The graphics are very pretty, even if a bit last-gen. The color balance works well, and the environment design is absolutely top-notch (even if there's thousands of copies of the same few cooking books in the house for some reason). Interacting with the environment happens in a wonderfully animated, intuitive and elegant way - albeit a few times I've entered and accidentally immediately exited a room, fighting with the controls.

The music is... perfectly fine. It's good, and it fits the mood and narrative. Nowhere near the goosebumps-inducing soundrack of eg. Dear Esther; more of an atmospheric background for the story gameplay.

The narrative is presented through astonishingly good voiceover narration, with captions seamlessly appearing in the environment. The way the story is fed to the player works great.

Sadly, that's all that "works great" about the story. First off, the ending feels like it was supposed to be a twist ending - but for anyone who bothered to read the title of the game, it isn't. The "story" is just a collection of tales about a dozen or so characters, each having at most one personality trait - the toddlers have none, since, well, they're toddlers. The player learns what happened to (almost) every Finch, through the aforementioned minigames, which are mostly just excuses to fit interactivity into reading a journal, diary or letter. And... that's pretty much it. Some of the stories might be emotional, if you get emotional about a random person dying of their own recklessness, or in some cases, even pure stupidity. That's, sadly, pretty much all there is to say about the "story".

The Good:
  • The environments are beautiful
  • The music is nice
  • The narrative is presented in a wonderfully immersive way
  • A few of the minigames are okay
  • The controls are great, when they work

The Bad:
  • Each character has at most one personality treat
  • One thing a lot of them had in common is stupidity
  • The controls sometimes don't work, annoyingly, mostly due to the event triggers - eg. if you come within a certain distance of a door, you go through it, even if you only wanted to check out something lying nearby.
  • The ending wants to be a twist, but isn't.
  • Pretty much only one or two of the character stories is one or more of: intriguing, mysterious, or just unexplained.
  • All the "emotionality" was pretty cheap
  • The main mystery of the game is "why did they leave the house", and the answer is "for pretty much no reason".
  • The speaker placement in the living room is absolutely horrendous

Verdict:
If you thought one or more of Gone Home, WarioWare Inc. or The Plan was a storytelling masterpiece, or you get really emotional about random characters with no significance or connection to the player dying, then you're probably going to enjoy this game.

If you don't think a game about holding W for five minutes while watching a fake-deep story (The Plan) can be good, or if you enjoyed Dear Esther or Firewatch more than Gone Home, you're probably not going to like this game.

If it's discounted, sure, buy it, why not. For full price, there's similar, but way better games out there.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is short, narrative-based game following a family, the Finch's, who always seem to die off. I'll start with the time issue in story-based games. My Steam account tracks that I have a little over two hours in the game which is enough to complete the entire story. For the $20 price tag, this is pretty hefty sum of cash for two hours of gameplay. I took this into consideration and picked it up when it was half off. The gameplay is nothing ridiculous. It plays like a standard walking simulator that can be found today. The narration and soundtrack are decent. All in all, it's a decent game. However, what makes it stand out to me is the story of each Finch. Every single character has a fleshed out backstory of how they died, and each death fleshes out the character a little bit more. The story of the Finch's reminds me of any family: a hodge-podge of numerous people whose only real similar characteristics is the blood they share. That's where the game is different. The story is why you should consider getting this at $20, but definitely pick it up on sale.\t\t\t\t\t
