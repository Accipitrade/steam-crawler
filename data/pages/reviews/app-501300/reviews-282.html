\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch has everything that you'd ever wish for in an interactive piece of art. It has the vision, which never flails or fails; it has the looks - boy, does it have the looks; it has the sounds and the voices; it has controls that, in their simplicity, connects to what you're seeing in a sometimes spooky way; and it has an amount of creativity that is hard to find in any media. Playing What Remains of Edith Finch is like stepping into a storybook, where every page is its own room and story of tragedy. And that's not an over-exaggeration, at least not from my perspective: The evocations of events in this game are stronger than many others, to the point that this game should be used as a reference on how to create interactive scenes (as opposed to cutscenes).

This game is worth playing. I can't stress that enough. It should be high on your priority list, as long as you like games that make you think and/or games that bring out fantastical imagery. If you're curious about this game, I suggest you hold onto that feeling and play it, because you will be rewarded for it.

Aaand here comes the pointless part of this review. Now when I've gushed about what I really like with this game, I can fall back into my (sadly) slightly cynical ways. This is for those of you, like me, who are going into this game already knowing about it and hearing that it's an absolute master piece of gaming (or something of the sort anyway). The rest of you, shoo. You already have your answer above, go play the game.

Is this game a master piece? Well, in certain ways, which I think I already touched on above. It is very evocative and it does show the way in an industry that all too often doesn't embrace the interactive part of the medium of games. However, this is a game that is very much focused on telling stories, and while it might be controversial, here it goes: I didn't love this game like I loved something like Gone Home. And I think it has to do with characters.

What Remains of Edith Finch is in many ways a slide show, or a mausoleum. Not in a bad way, necessarily, as that is what I think the game tries to be. Throughout the game, we get to see snippets of the Finch family members, which all comes together to form their tragic whole. It's a House, you see? The problem with this approach is depth. Imagine your name, the place of your birth, your main characteristic and the most prominent thing that has happened in your life. Are those the whole of your person? Of course not. But that is how this game portrays the Finch family, with every character being defined by a snapshot of their personality and/or ultimate fate. This makes the message of the ending, to remember their lives instead of their ends, seem a bit... hollow to me. We don't get to actually know the characters all that well, and I think that impacts the emotional quality of the game in quite a detrimental way.

This is not a real reason to not play the game, however. As I said above, if you go into this game with curiosity, you will be rewarded. Don't go into it expecting to be wowed. Instead, be curious on what this game will offer you. It might just give you something different than it gave me.\t\t\t\t\t
