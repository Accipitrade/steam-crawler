If you've played Vanishing of Ethan Carter before, this game offers the same premise but with a light, magical touch. This game will touch your soul and make the eerie stories that have been told seem heartwarming, peppering them with emotions that you've never felt in a game.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is not so much a game as it is a visual story. Gameplay is limited to using the "wasd" keys to move and the mouse to click on objects. There are very few choices and those choices do not affect the story; you start the story, click throuogh the story, and it ends. It took me only 2.6 hours to get through, so my first criticisim is that the price, $19.99, is way too high for the content.

My second criticism is that, for a game that is all about story, the story is weak. The five elements of a story are: Characters, Setting, Plot, Conflict, and Resolution. The setting is great, very creative and beautifully rendered. Everything else, however, is weak. We hardly get to spend any time with the characters before they are gone; there is no development. The plot is incredibly thin; a character is intorduced and, within moments, is whisked away; rinse and repeat. Then the game ends. There is no conflict to speak of; it would be a stretch to argue that the main character is in a conflict with her family's history. It's a very shallow conflict. There is zero resolution; you repeat the same plot device about a dozen times and then stop.

In short, I cannot recommend this game based on its gameplay or story. There is a lot of good imagination and visual creativity here, but not enough to recommend a 2.6 hour game that costs $20.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a great game about family, love, conflict, and loss. If you're looking for a narrative experience with light gameplay that can tug at your emotions, this is a game you should pick up.

This game is very much in the same vein as Gone Home, but with more unique gameplay for every interactive experience. I enjoyed the way that Giant Sparrow visualized and conveyed the story or troubles of each family member through a sort of mini-game playstyle that is so unique from one person to the next. Without giving too much away, it really takes the narrative experience to the next level by putting you in the mind of the other person.

There isn't much replayability in this game considering it's extremely linear, but I am happy to have played through it and enjoyed the experience it had to offer.\t\t\t\t\t

Wow! What a hell of an experience! I absolutely loved every second of this game. I use the term "game" very loosely as it's more often dubbed as a "walking simulator", but I'd argue it is significantly more interactive than that.

The story centers around Edith Finch (the last remaining Finch family member) returning to her family home, discovering how each of her relatives had succumb to the family curse resulting in their unfortunate demise. As you progress into various rooms, you play as most of the family members in their final moments in extremely unique and interesting segments. I won't into the specifics however, as that would certainly ruin the first impressions of the game. I will just say though, that Lewis Finch's segment is the best story telling segment I've seen in a video game. It was simply an amazing experience from start to finish.

The world itself is beautifully crafted. From the cluttered book-laden rooms in the Finch's Home, to the stunning woodland surrounding it, you simply become so absorbed it becomes your existence. There's so much detail crammed into every space, I often found myself stopping in each room for about 5 minutes just to look around and take it all in. I would recommend you do the same too. While we're on the topic of visuals, I can say that the game ran great on Max Settings. A mild case of stutter at the very beginning, but it was quickly stomped out after an adjustment to the shadow quality. I probably didn't need to do that once I was inside however.

Another important thing to talk about are the controls. The way they used controls in each segment was very unique and told the story in a very tactile way. The Kite or The Swing Segments are great examples of this, but again, I have to note Lewis Finch's segment for the dual controls. You'll know what I mean once you play it. It's just amazing.

The playtime is somewhere between two and three hours. That might be a sting considering its base price is around £15. I would say it is definitely worth it, but I can understand the desire to wait for a sale. Just make sure you don't leave it too long lest you forget or come across spoilers or something. :P\t\t\t\t\t
