\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFair warning: this game deals with multiple topics that may cause some players discomfort (i.e. suicide, depression, death of children, and other minor topics )

The amount of visual storytelling in this game is breathtaking. The first time I opened up the pause menu, I took a good look at it, and noticed just how much story and information I got from just the words on the screen. Just from the dang pause menu! This is one of those stories where you could take a picture of any particular point in the game, and it would most likely look fairly good as a monitor wallpaper.

The gameplay here is not the focus, it is more story-orientated than gameplay centered. That is far from being a bad thing, but it is fair to note for those who need engaging gameplay to sell them on an idea. Even for those swaying away after that sentence, I urge you to take another look. If the story sounds interesting at all to you, give it a go. Personally, this game was a bit far down on my wishlist, and I only bought it on sale; I wasn't quite sold on the game, but I figured 'why not?' After the fact, I'd probably pay full price.

The story is told in such a way that it draws you in, you feel like this world is a world that you want to see more of, and know more about. Despite its short length (I counted around 2 hours on a leisurely stroll through the game, and an extra hour revisiting all the parts that I wanted to) I feel that I entirely got my money and time's worth of entertainment.

I highly recommend checking it out for yourself, and maybe check out the soundtrack, if you like game OSTs; this one is very well done. The sad themes that this game has adds a lot to the atmosphere. It didn't bum me out, certainly not, but oh boy, did this game make me feel a lot.

My rating of this game is an A+. Check it out at full price, or on sale, if you'd like to wait. I'm confident that you'll find enjoyment somewhere within the game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game falls into the often-derided category of "walking simulator" which is mostly correct. You do a lot of walking, and occasionally you do something a bit more than that, and the whole thing is pretty guided towards a certain end.

But make no mistake, it is an excellent experience and worth every last minute.

There is not a lot to tell about the gameplay, and not much to share about the story without spoiling things a bit much. You are a young woman re-tracing your family's history in the interest of explaining your own life. The balance of this game/story rests on how well the story you're about to experience is crafted. There are some minor issues, but none considerable enough to even list by name.

What Remains of Edith Finch is inventive, engaging, and moving. The story itself is well-written and delivered in the time it takes to pretty organically wander through each "chapter." There are relatively obvious, telegraphed moments, and lots of lovely little surprising ones, all of them supported by an amazing attention to detail that lets you discover parts of the story without being hit over the head constantly.

This is the kind of game that may or may not ever get the recognition it deserves, but elevates the field entirely by being a shining example of the possibilities of both gaming and storytelling.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a game developed by Giant Sparrow. I am ashamed to call this just a 'Walking Simulator'. Sure there is no challenge in this game, but video games should not have to be challenging by default. As long as it is interactive to a meaningful extent and gives feedback to the player based on their input, it is still very much a videogame.

There is no combat, you are never in danger in What Reamins of Edith Finch. The purpose is not to challenge your twitch or survival skills, but to open up your senses and take it all in as it ushers you in the shoes of Edith as she explores her old family home.

The cool thing about the place is how everything is left the way it was by the person who lived in that particular room till their dying breath. It essentially creates a snapshot of the time which is unique to the room. The attention to detail is staggering. There is so much stuff in every room, the densitivity makes it feel like someone had actually lived there. Even though Edith and the player never really met many of the people who once roamed it's corridors, the charactertization you get from visiting these rooms is more vivid and likely does a better job painting the person they were, more than any cutscene could ever hope to achieve.

The gameplay is mostly you wondering around the house and taking in all the photos, letters, and environmetal details to flesh out the story. However, it really caught me off guard when the game breaks away from this mold and delivers certain parts of the story in a way I least expected. I won't spoil it but it was beautifully integrated and unique to that part of the story. Most developers don't want to create a gameplay system which they will only be using once, but the developers here went out of there way to create varied gameplay systems to make the most out of the narrative, even if it was going to be used just once.

Is it not a long game, took me just a over 2 hours to finish it. However, the experience is something which will stay with me for years to come. When I finished it, I felt kinda sad but yet fullfilling and happy at the same time. Beautiful game, thank you Giant Sparrow.\t\t\t\t\t
