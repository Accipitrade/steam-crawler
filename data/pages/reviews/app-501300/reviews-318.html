Overall: 8.3/10

Story:
9.0/10
The story is awesome.
The pace is just right.
Each characters have their own unique stories which you can experience for yourself.
All short stories have a unique theme, art style, etc, adding variation to the game.

Graphics:
8.3/10
The main game is very pretty, with everything designed in detail.
The variation of art style for each short story makes them way more memorable and special.

Gameplay:
7.0/10
Gameplay wise, it is relatively simple with no complex mechanics.
While playing through the stories, there will be fun mini games to keep you engaged.
However this is somehow perfect for the game as you won't be distracted by the mechanics during the stories.

Others:
The big downside to this game is that it's only 2-3 hours long.
The replayability is low as it is story driven.
Buying the game at a discounted price is recommended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you follow the Playstation Access channel on YouTube, you'd have heard Robert Pearson raving on and on about What Remains of Edith Finch.

I became sick and tired of hearing about it from Rob across two or three videos. The B-roll in his videos made it look like a generic walking simulator. So, I bought the game to understand why he was crazy about it. Now I know.

What remains of Edith Finch stands out as one of the most beautiful games ever made. If there is one word to describe this game, it's poignant. Also, innovative.

The game takes place in and around the Finch house, a house that looks like it was accidentally designed by Wes Anderson as he was trying to create his take on a "Where's Waldo" book.

I was quickly intrigued by the strange, precarious architecture of the Finch house, the cluttered lived-in feel of its rooms, and the soft glow of homeliness exuding from its every nook and cranny. The visual artistry on display was magnificent.

The mystery behind the abrupt abandonment of the house and the premature deaths of the family members kept me hooked throughout. The latter is explained through playable flashbacks in very very novel ways; ways that successfully stretch what games are capable of.

And the stories filled me with the immensity of the tragedies of the Finchs. The writers weren't scared to go to dark places, weren't scared to wrap those dark stories in colorful visuals. (Once you play the game, this line will make sense).

The music, the graphics, the colors and visuals, the narration, the grounded story and writing all come together to create a masterpiece. If there was ever a game that perfected the Walking Simulator formula, it is this game.

Time to Beat: Take your time to play it. Savour it. Time does not matter here.

Overall Rating: This is a must-play masterpiece.\t\t\t\t\t

Though everyone had its fair share of troubles, one kind of suffering hits the hardest, death made all who are left behind suffer. No matter what kind of human drew their last breath. We all fear death. We all are affected by death.

Edith Finch tells me, that no amount of remembrance excuses the past. Moving forward can be hard like being stuck on muck, but it's required to live. Stories are not just stories, and media hold such immense influence.

When I play this there was this seeping terror whenever each story unfold. Each tore a soul from this fictional world and grew in mania and obsession.

I like tombstones and graveyards because they speak of what was. I hate this kind of terrifying discovery. Not when environments made it seem like the deceased still left their mark there.

Playing this game felt like walking towards a grave. One that excuses its bad decision by saying "There's a reason for this, and I must find it!"\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wouldn't normally recommend a walking simulator, nor do I go in for "man, this game just got me in the FEELS" as a primary reason to play a game.

In this case I'll make an exception. This game tells a compelling story, is extremely beautiful, and uses some very clever gameplay to do it. Much more than your average walking simulator.

There are a fewpoints where it felt like the artist was a bit too concerned with how clever their idea was to worry about if it made sense or not, but none large enough to interfere with my enjoyment of the game or appreciation of the cleverness.

Definitely one I'd recommend to anyone interested in immersive atmospheric storytelling games.\t\t\t\t\t
