\r\n\t\t\t\t\tExcellent interactive storytelling. The story connects a series of mini-stories per the former members of the main character's family. The story investigates a mysterious affliction that may be plaguing the family's bloodline. The game is tinged with creepy but it's subtle. As the player, you explore the old family house and get to 'insert' yourself into little vignettes of each family member's last memories. The game provides a different experiences for each character from a dream-scape where you transform from creature to creature to working as a fish factory worker. This game creates a very immersive storytelling experience.

The basement story was the most intense experience for me. I won't give it away.

I also like that the reader (gamer) really needs to bring her/his own imagination to fill in a lot that is left unsaid. Which is maddening, sometimes, because I want somebody to tell me what 'actually' happened, but find this refreshing. Most entertainment media doesn't require a lot of thinking or imagining for yourself.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNot sure this is a game - more like an artistic interactive experience.
This is a depressing story - you have been warned.
This feels like a puzzle box with drawers and levers - you just pull them as you unfold the story of the Finch family.
It's like a narrative ride - you're gently and smartly guided on a linear track through interactive short stories.
Each Finch member has their room - 50% of this game is about immersing yourself in each owner's little universe and backstory. A great deal of love & attention to details has been obviously poured into that.

The overall heavily depressing mood didn't work for me - the narrator's voice highly contributes to that - but then overly melancholic teenagers were never really my thing (I'm looking at you Gone Home).

Surprisingly enough, the creepy & supernatural vibe is hardly mentioned in reviews while this is the main aspect I recommend What Remains of Edith Flinch for.

I'd recommend getting this just for the Molly & Barbara stories - wish they all had been along the same lines.
Kudos for book titles too and getting "The Aleph" there.

No more depressed kids please. It's OK, you can still make deep & insightful games while keeping them fun.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBeautiful game
Plenty of Details, weird stories and an even weirder family.
Still, for what it is, better wait for a sale, 20 bucks are too much in my book.

For how much details were put in this game, the places where they weren't or were lacking stand out like a sore thumb tho.
Usually most of those I wouldn't bat an eye at, plenty of those are in other games as well - but this game lives from details and it's scenery, it's the main focal point of it, while other games only have it as an extra, so to speak. So I think it's fair to point them out.
For example, there are many book shelfes but the books repeat constantly. It being day when you arrive and as soon as you step out again, which is a few minutes later, it's night - for no reason. A comment about power being turned off yet a short while later (at lewis') you have working blacklights. Random bits of writing, like the news paper article about Ediths father, being way more pixelated than any other texture (which looks bad in itself) so you can't read them. Or stuff like photographs being way more pixelated than other textures. Or that the changes in a room do not change what you see from the outside looking into it (cordon you unhook in a room)
Those details, all of them, would have been so easy and fast to fix. yet they weren't.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've played most of the well-known 'walking simulators' of the last few years, from Dear Esther to Gone Home to Firewatch to Layers of Fear to Vanishing of Ethan Carter. I enjoyed most of them, but they're all pretty samey after a while, so I nearly gave Edith Finch a miss. I'm so glad I didn't. This game will surprise you at almost every turn, without any of its 'gimmicks' outstaying their welcome. It feels like a maturation of the 'walking sim' genre, working within the genre's tropes to keep the story moving along, but breaking from convention to explore its motif of untimely death in surprising, charming, funny and touching ways that would only be possible in an interactive medium. It's a small but perfectly formed game and well worth a couple of hours of your time. I got it 50% off on sale, but I would have still felt it was worth it at full price.\t\t\t\t\t
