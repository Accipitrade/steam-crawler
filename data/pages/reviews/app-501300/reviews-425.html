\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOne of a few, rare story-heavy games that uses gameplay as a tool to tell the story instead of just using it as a tool to advance the story forward. Without the gameplay, the story of "What Remains of Edith Finch" would be a pretty normal mystery about a cursed family, but the gameplay is what made it so moving and rich for me. The unique game mechanics that were highlighted for each one of the Finches kept the experience fresh with every new backstory and it gave a whole new level of characterization to each character. My favorite arcs were Barbara's, Lewis's, and Gregory's. The only arc I really didn't enjoy was Milton's because of how little it told about the character and how short it was.Overall, this is probably the best story-heavy game I have ever played that still remains a game and not a "movie with buttons" like David Cage's games or TellTale.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI purchased this little gem during the recent Steam Summer Sale, not really expecting much. I definitely got a pleasant surprise!

This game tells a detailed story about Edith Finch and her eccentric family. Even though the gameplay isn't super long, there's plenty to keep you interested; the music, the graphics, the vast attention to detail that these developers provide, as well as the overall creepy feel to it all.

As macabre and crazy as it sounds, I found myself getting excited and looking forward to each individual story of a family member. I kept wondering what kind of weird circumstance was going to eventually lead to their demise. There was an absolute sense of sadness throughout the entire game, but each story was so encompasing, I had to know more! And when it was finally all said and done, I was disappointed that it didn't keep going. I felt like a deflated balloon, but I can easily say it was worth it!

Now that the game is finished and I have a better understanding of Edith's family tree, I'd like to experience it again. It would almost be like visiting an old friend. I keep thinking about that house and how it felt warm and inviting even though it was a little dark and foreboding at the same time. And I've never played a game that had so many different themes all packed into one huge story. I feel like I probably missed some aspects of that house and all it's little secret passageways. I'm dying to explore it again.

You should get a glass of wine (if you're of age!), get relaxed and experience it for yourself. You'll know what I mean.\t\t\t\t\t

What Remains of Edith Finch

Recommended purchase point: 25% off or more

A bizarre, ethereal anthology of death, What Remains of Edith Finch is experimental, evocative and (at a 3 hour runtime) over all too soon. However, its wildly creative storytelling ensures the experience will not soon be forgotten. $20 feels a tiny bit too high for what's on offer - but when it hits $15 or less, purchase without hesitation.

STORY
The Finch family is cursed. Death dogs their steps endlessly, claiming each member in fickle - sometimes incomprehensible ways. But each of them left a chronicle of their death or disappearance, and as she returns to the abandoned Finch home years later (spurred by a mysterious key left to her in her mother's will) Edith Finch sets forth to learn what happened to her ruined family.

The game's subject matter is endlessly dark, and the Finch house itself possesses an aura of quiet menace (described aptly by Edith's narration as 'a smile with too many teeth') but What Remains of Edith Finch refuses to succumb to the temptation to become a horror game. At its heart, pondering how much truth there is to the wild and sometimes fantastical accounts of the Finch family curse, the game evokes quiet empathy. It doesn't mourn the deaths of so many Finches - it celebrates that they lived, and instils appreciation for their unique perspectives (whatever the truth of them may be).

What Remains of Edith Finch is a storytelling triumph. Make sure you start the game with 3 free hours up your sleeve, because you will be finishing it in one sitting.

GAMEPLAY
What Remains of Edith Finch appears superficially to be cut from the same cloth as the likes of Gone Home, a 'walking simulator'. However, the game is at its core an anthology of stories - many of them coming with different types of gameplay. The only constant is a first person perspective, whether you're commanding a kite, a camera or the slowly creeping tentacle of an abomination.

Most impressively, the controls change frequently and are never listed, but are always simple to intuit (a feat of design in itself). Showstopping bugs are few and far between, with only one gamebreaker popping up to mar my experience - and it was easily remedied by quitting and reloading the game.

PRESENTATION
Edith Finch is an instant classic in this field. The game's subtitles being present in the world (drawing the player's attention cleverly towards things to look at, before disappearing in a puff of lettering) is an approach so utterly charming it grabs one's attention within seconds and never lets go. The Finch residence is a surreal, almost dreamlike experience, its impossible architecture and engineering swooping madly higher and higher, boggling the mind.

Crucially, though, nothing is ever made of this by any of the characters - this is simply the way it is, and despite the house's peculilarities it projects a warm, lived-in aura of mundanity. All the juxtapositions at play work tirelessly to keep the player's mind off-balance in the most enjoyable way possible. In a longer game this might have become exhausting, but for what it is, it never outstays its welcome.

CONCLUSION
A staggering experience. It's frankly hard to assign a value to What Remains of Edith Finch - it's a game that could mean so many different things to so many different potential players. For me, 25% off makes a perfect purchase point for a powerful but sadly fleeting story.\t\t\t\t\t
