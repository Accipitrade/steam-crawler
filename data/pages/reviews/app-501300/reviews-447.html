\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI've had my eye on this game for quite some time now, but after watching the BAFTA Games Awards 2018 I just had to give it a try.

What Remains of Edith Finch is a first-person atmospheric exploration game that excels in its simple yet unique gameplay mechanics. While exploring your childhood home unraveling the the many mysteries of the Finch family, you will come across various relics that trigger memories from Edith’s deceased family members. These memories are beautifully conveyed through cinematic mini-games featuring the aforementioned unique gameplay mechanics. They all rely heavily on symbolism that the player has to interpret themselves.

I think the best thing about What Remains of Edith Finch is piecing together the story yourself as you go, with the help of all the subtle hints the game throws at you. The story itself mainly revolves around getting to know each and every one of Edith’s family members, as she walks around the house uncovering their stories. You might just get pretty attached to some of them. (Lewis’ memory is by far the best btw).

I want to give props to the developers for the excellent level design of the Finch house. It’s a really well put together structure, and an absolute joy to explore. The house is filled to the brim with all these little details that describe the personalities of its former residents. By paying enough attention the player can figure out parts of the lore that lies behind. The clever way of which you progress through the different rooms in the Finch house not only lets you experience the family history alongside Edith (big parts of the history were kept away from her growing up), but also leads to a fair amount of aha moments concerning both the game’s plot line and history of the house itself.

What Remains of Edith Finch is definitely not a lengthy experience. You can easily beat it in a single sitting with around 2-3 hours of total playtime logged depending on your playstyle. That being said, the feelings you are left with after playing it by far makes the game a worthwhile investment for those who are into deep and touching first-person exploration games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t9/10

In What Remains of Edith Finch, you play as Edith, revisiting her childhood home after the death of her mother. The game plays much like your standard walking simulator, for the most part, but also includes a few diverse gameplay pieces. You may find yourself playing an animal at one point, or playing the main character in a comic book, or even navigating a prince from an isometric vantage point.

The biggest argument, at least among my friends, is whether this story could be told just as well in another medium, such as film. I must say, I'm on the fence. As the game switches perspectives so much, it could be said that "playing" these different Finches could make them more human and relateable. On the other hand, the only real gripe I had about the game is how the text so often jerked my camera. Whenever story-related text would be narrated, the text would typically overlay some piece of the environment, such as a roof, and the camera would jerk in that direction and make me read it. In some ways, this undermines the agency of the player. The game has wonderful writing, and I know the developer would like me to pay attention, but please trust that I am.

The game absolutely embodies a literary genre that is often called "magical realism," the most prominent author of which is Gabriel Garcia Marquez. However, in my opinion, it actually surpasses any book I've read in the genre. Media in this genre straddle the line between being grounded in reality and existing in a fantasy world. However, I might argue that we all straddle this line. There are moments in our lives that almost seem to deserve a soundtrack, feats we hear of as children that strain credibility, or teachers, coaches, or grandparents that are larger than life.

The game's focus is on grief, but also on mortality. The game covers many, many reactions to the bleak events that inevitably take place in all of our lives. In many ways, the way that we lives our lives IS the way we cope with our own mortality. Certain characters hide from death, other embellish the stories of the dead, some turn to religion, and some turn to escapism (such as substance abuse and video games). Some attempt to live life to the fullest, exploring and experiencing as much as they possibly can.

In addition, the game does not shy away from presenting the cyclical nature of all life, including the haphazard way in which predators can choose their prey, and how quickly life can be snuffed out.

What do you do, as a parent, in the face of all this grief and loss? Do you protect your child? How? Can you protect your child? The telling of stories is such a wonderful part of human life, as expressed by this game and also suggested by the themes therein. If we can choose the meaning of events in our lives, why not see our role as paramount and the details as astonishing.\t\t\t\t\t

It isn't often that games have this much of an emotional impact on me while playing, but this one surely did. \r\n\t\t\t\t\tQuite disappointing really.

There are some pretty cool scenes in this game and it does play around quite a bit with it's interactivity but other than that it falls flat for me. I didn't feel absorbed in the story, I didn't care for any of the people mentioned, was left kind of confused by the ending, and there really wasn't anything profound in the storytelling. Added onto that is the extremely linear experience which kind of makes me feel like I could just read this as a book instead of playing it; many times I was walking down a corridor looking one way and the game jerked my screen 180 degrees to read some writing on a wall, this coupled with the weirdly limited FoV got pretty tedious.

Also I find the story quite depressing in a way where there's no redeeming quality. I actually quite enjoy depressing/dark themes in art but in this I just didn't get the point of it.\t\t\t\t\t

What Remains of Edith Finch is a short game, that could be classified as a walking sim if it wasn't packed full of areas that are full of different pieces of gameplay. Its more than interactive, but it doesn't have mechanics that make it more than a walking sim. It's like a game stuck in the middle of full and empty, and I love that about it.
