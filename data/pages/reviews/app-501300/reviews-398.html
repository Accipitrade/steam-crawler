\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike many other people around Steam, I would give this a five out of 10 - which is a borderline recommend.
I played this through, so it was clearly compulsive and I did want to see what happens at the end. In that way, the game was good.
Graphics were lovely and I was nicely suprised by the diverse experiences you get with it. VR would be the way to go with a game like this.
However, calling this a "STORY" is an overstatement in my opinion, but I won't say any more than that, as I would not want to spoil the experience for others.

I didnt find it as poignant as I was lead to believe, this didnt really elicit any emotion in me at all. I found Ethan Carter a much better game overall.

It is polished, it is lovely looking and the sound is excellent.

It just didnt do for me what it clearly has done for others.

A couple of "points" are further knocked off - this is £20 for 1.8 hours of gameplay. That just isnt fun when you think you can get 400 + hours into a £30 or even £40 title - meaning that Value for Money just isnt here.

Its lovely - but I just can't recommend it. Maybe for £5 I would have enjoyed it as I would enjoy a watchable film.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf I wasn't bullied into completely severing the connection between my brain and my tear ducts as a kid, I would have cried at the ending. But such is my woes.

When I decided to change my major to computer engineering to get a foot into the gaming industry is one way or the other, despite my insecure immigration status pressuring me to get something easy done quick, I did so because games had been an integral part of my life from way before my memory even lets on. The current trends in the game industry, and more importantly, my disconnection from gaming due to this unstable time of my life, however, were making me doubt my decision. This game has throughly reminded me of why I love games so much.

The walking simulator genre had never really grabbed me before. I couldn't shake the feeling that I was playing a movie. And I went into this game with that thought gnawing the back of my mind like two bonobos caged away from each other. Which is why the silencing of that voice when I decided to walk to the house through the forest instead of the road was so much more jarring and mystifying. While the story was good, the technical, visual and especially the auditory excellence of this game elevates it way beyond what i expected of a game in this genre.

When I hear games advertising 'immersion', I couldn't help picturing the advertising team laughing manically, twirling their mustache, and rubbing their ♥♥♥♥♥ all over the pile of money that one word had made them. I never belived it. But come the end credits, and I completely forgot where I was, who I was, why I was, and when I was. I have never felt as disoriented as when snapping back to reality when I saw an achievement pop up in the bottom right of my screen saying "Thank you, Johnaan!" or something of the sort.

The level of detail in the background, how the same house can feel dead and abandoned when walking through it, and so lived in and homely during the flashback-read through-interactive cutscene things, and and how those same flashback-read through-interactive cutscene things where prime examples of tonal consistency and wonderous creativity.

And the ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ sound design. I almost don't want to talk about it, cause nothing my dumbass could say about it could effectively capture the soul-sucking nature it flaunts. I can't really tell if it works so good because of its mutualistic relationship with the story beats and the environment, or because the articulate and beautiful compositions could completely satiate rock-hard snobby classical music enthusiasts to 'fulfillment'.

A slight gripe I have with this game is how the game stradles between the realistic and the fantastic. While the overarching story about a **********SPOILER************ dead mother educating her child (2018cantassumegender) about the tragic history of their family was a tear jerker (or a tear suggester in my case), the tragedy didn't make much sense to me. I am no author, or a writter of any sorts, but something felt off to me. It was starting to feel like a buch of bad things were happening to them just because "sad". And, depneding on who you are, this probably won't have an effect on how they emotionally affect you, since those small stroy vignettes where so meticulously crafted, but it was one thing that I couldn't shake.

And its probably worth mentioning that this game is not for everyone. Although it demonstrates a pinnicle of this genre, its still a walking simulator. If you prefer doing something in you game rather than just watching your game, this isn't for you, and you probably won't finish this game. But if you are willing to open up your mind just for 2 hours and some change, I can assure you this is a game you will remember.

Tl;Dr : meticulously paced game with a gripping story, overwhelmingly detailed and beutiful art and world design, and ear melting sound desgin, a game worth any price you get it at, but extremely linear and is still a walking simulator, so buyer beware. 9/10.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNot the ecstatic experience that other reviewers had led me to expect, but definitely worth playing. The game mechanics (at least on a PC) are excruciating - choppy graphics, unresponsive controls, and 2 or 3 horrific bugs that can force you to replay entire levels from the beginning (BECAUSE THERE IS NO SAVE GAME). There is zero documentation on how the controls work on a PC, and I was unimpressed with the developer feedback regarding bug reports (complaints were dismissed as due to "certain settings" on "below minimal systems" with no accompanying recommendations on how to tweak the settings). Although I was playing on a PC that far exceeded the listed requirements, I had to keep experimenting with lowering the graphics settings until I found a level that wasn't too jerky to play. The only way to get past the afore-mentioned bugs was to temporarily lower ALL settings to Low, then raise them again after the sticking points to enjoy the beautiful graphics.

The writing is intriguing but a little thin, especially compared to classic story games like Myst and Gabriel Knight. A lot of it really doesn't make much sense. But there is a powerful dream logic to the whole thing that burrows into your brain and won't let you go. The gorgeous visual design, haunting soundtrack and excellent voice acting somehow overpower all the flaws in the game and keep you not only playing but thinking about that house and its ill-fated residents for a week or two afterwards.

I'd rate it (6/10)\t\t\t\t\t
