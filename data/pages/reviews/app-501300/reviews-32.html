\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat Remains of Edith Finch is a nuanced, narrative exploration of and sympathetic challenge to certain ideas within both feminist and existentialist philosophy. How free are we truly to live our lives and to self-determine? Are we like fleeting birds: nameless, unknowable, and free? Or are we more like the branches of a tree: extensions of a shared history that binds us together, from which we draw the nutrients needed to grow? If what we want is to lay down roots and to be a part of something greater, then shouldn't true freedom make that desire possible rather than stand as its opposite? Through its tragic exploration of trauma and the brevity of life, this story suggests that airy freedom and rooted identity are fundamentally entangled. A nest from which the birds may never fly is just a factory that produces misery. Conversely, absent the option to nest, flying perpetually likewise becomes a kind of tormented Promethean fate, or an Orwellian mockery of true liberty. Only by honouring both past and present can we experience real freedom, sense of self and happiness, and give rise to a brighter future.\t\t\t\t\t
