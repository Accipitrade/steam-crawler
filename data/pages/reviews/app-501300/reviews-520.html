\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCongrats for winning Best Narrative at The Game Award, 2017!!!

Nominated for the “Whoooaaaaaaa, Dude! 2.0” Steam Award, 2017.

I had always believed that the game length is an important part of the game value, meaning shorter games should not cost much. I always felt pretty smart about my evaluation criteria, and thought this game was definitely overpriced. Then it proved me utterly wrong.

I took my time during the game and it still only took me 2.5 hours, but the game didn't feel slow/short to me at all. I hardly had a moment to relax during the game: was horrified during the first half of the game, and then literally cried through the second half. Truly engaging emotionally.

Graphics and music were great. Surprisingly, the gameplay was really complex for an interactive story and also made a lot of sense to supplement the plots: definitely not (just?) a walking simulator.

Depending on what you want/expect out of a game, WREF might not be for everyone, but it's definitely worth a shot. At this point I don't want the game to be any longer - it stopped at where it should, and game had great density that it felt rich. It might be hard for a lengthy game to constantly carry this much weight for too long.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's not often a game leaves me with such a ... haunting ... feeling when it's over. While other reviewers here said that this game was uplifting or made them appreciate the miracle of life, it instilled in me more of a vague sensation of sadness. But I'm glad I experienced it. Not sure whether I'm ready to or even want to play through any of the segments again, but the first time, short as it may have been, was worth the price.

This is one of those games that must be experienced rather than "played" -- at least the first time. Save your achievement hunting for a second playthrough (the entire game only takes 2 to 3 hours) and just let yourself be immersed in the world and the lore. Avoid spoilers, don't look anything up before or during the game. Just play it.

There really aren't any puzzles, there's no twitch gameplay; there aren't any quicktime events or shooting sequences. If that sounds boring to you, then by all means, don't play it. But if the very idea of a "walking simulator" doesn't turn you away, then ready yourself to experience one of the very best in the genre.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFor those of you still harbouring hesitations about "walking simulators"...here's one I'd definitely recommend you try.

So yes, it's quite linear, and no, it probably doesn't have an enormous amount of replayability. It's also quite short - about two to three hours - so I guess it's not that cheap at twenty buckaroonies. It is, however, one of the strongest examples of interactive storytelling you will ever enjoy, and an oddly varied experience given the bits of the game in which you briefly get to play the roles of cats, owls, and...well, I don't want to give too much away. Let's just say Tim Burton would be jealous (seeing what a boring old hack he's become these days).

For anyone who loves a good story in a game, with a willingness to sacrifice a bit of agency in order to have that superior narrative experience...it's a bit of a no-brainer. As for the rest of you, well: you only live once. And they do say variety is the spice of life. So if you only try one walking simulator before dismissing the entire genre, this could well be the one. Truly magical, wonderful stuff. And a bit of a tear-jerker at times, too.

Verdict: 9.5/10.

(PS Anyone claiming there is no "gameplay" in this title is either a drooling idiot, or hasn't played the game in its entirety. There's interaction and "mini-games" aplenty.)\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPSA: I really wish i hadn't seen the trailer before playing this.

If you're looking for something with the charm and pacing (and scope) of a Gone Home, this is a joyful explosion of unqiue creative ideas combined with an enchanting art direction, overwhelming fidelity and a pristine presentation. Moves without lulls and doesn't overstay its welcome.

Occasionally feels a bit too predictable, but of course that's entirely deliberate and used to great effect. Makes up for it in spades.

The Ending is competent but overshadowed by other segments, so it feels like it arrives a bit too soon and cuts our delightful stay in the game's world (much?) too short and leaves a few things unfinished. Which could feel a bit unsatisfying, but has never been more appropriate.\t\t\t\t\t
