\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a fun but incredibly short visit.

The price point is such that I think it would have to be incredibly awful to warrant a bad review. Two buck is perfect for the content inside.

The only real downside is the lack of options. You are on rails. You visit each painting in the pre-determined order. You cannot teleport. You are rooted to a spot.

That said. This is a stunning visit to a virtual gallery.

First we see the Girl Reading a Letter at an Open Window. It is serene. You can lean and sneak a peak outside her windows. She is quiet and does not sense your presence.

Then we visit The Birth of Venus. The scene is as the painting is. The woman moving to make Venus modest is immobile. Venus regards you and blinks. Her hands gently swaying across her breasts. The angels over her shoulder drift and blow Venus to shore.

Then we see Mona Lisa. Loved this one. Leonardo's drawings are on a nearby table. Leonardo's helicopter sits in a nearby room. On the left is a small fountain, softly bubbling. Amazing. Mona Lisa sits patiently. She tracks your movements. Her hands subtly trace the fabric of her sleeves. She is beautiful and the scene is gorgeous.

Starry Night was a little bit of a let down. The painting is well represented but it lacks emotion. It is there and the colors and textures of Van Gogh are pure eye candy. But this one is lifeless.

Finally, The Creation of Adam. Set in a fragmented cluster of rocks in space, Jupiter is the looming backdrop, this one is a little unsettling. It is perfect. If gods existed, this is how I would imagine their domain. Yahweh Sabaoth is dwarfed and insignificant against the infinite space. And his tiny presence is perfect for the fact that he is nestled in the anatomically correct limbic system. He is our imagined prime mover.

This was well worth the money if you have, even an iota, of interest for art.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is absolutely worth buying, and there is some beauty to behold.
However, there's much to say in the way of constructive feedback, and so I will focus my review on that.

First, I found it quite disappointing that this was an incredibly 'white christian' gaze. Of course, if that is what the creators wanted then good for them I guess. It's just disappointing to me. And I found 'The Creation of Adam' piece to be quite funny more than anything, that the creators chose this as the final artwork, evidently intended as a kind of 'finale', and it is really a rather ridiculous and amusing painting to me.

I felt also most of the works could have used a little more in the way of 'interpretive license'. The most obvious choice here is the Van Gogh. Perhaps I am spoiled by the Dreams-inspired Starry Night creation, which is exceptionally creative and beautiful (https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Xo27NdFIGss), but the Art Plunge interpretation was particularly straightforward for a surrealist piece with such a rich, expressive world. Of course, Art Plunge costs $2 and maybe they just didn't have the budget/scope to do more. Certainly I hope that these creators and others are able to find budget to take this concept further and bring more paintings to life, with more creative interpretations and evolutions.

The 'black hole' behind you was rather annoying in the way it cut out the sound of the painting's world, I would have preferred if the sound layered on top, so it did not interrupt the experience to look behind you.

As I said, I absolutely recommend this experience. My favourite by far was the first, "Girl Reading a Letter at an Open Window". Looking out the window in that world was one of the most impressive moments of realism I've experienced in VR, where I felt like I was not in VR and simply looking out a real-world window to a beautiful landscape. None of the other painting worlds reached anything quite so impressive of an experience as that one.\t\t\t\t\t
