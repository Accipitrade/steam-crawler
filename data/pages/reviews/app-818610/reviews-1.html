\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAprès moults réflexions, et quelque peu à contrecœur, j'ai décidé de ne pas recommander ce jeu. En effet, malgré trois essais réalisés à plusieurs années d'intervalles, je n'ai jamais réussi à accrocher à ce que propose The great gaïas. Ce dernier, bien qu'étant créé par une seule personne, qui de plus est visiblement passionné de RPG et plus précisément de D&D, possède selon moi deux défauts, l'un mineur, l'autre majeur :

Défaut mineur : La direction artistique. Que ce soient les portraits, les sprites ou les graphismes, rien ne va. Les portraits ont été à l'évidence créés par plusieurs artistes, dont certains possèdent des qualités douteuses. Le résultat est une différence de ton et de qualité entre les portraits. Certains portraits relèvent de l'anime, d'autres de la dark fantasy, certains sont centrés sur le visage, d'autres sur le buste. Les sprites sont aussi de qualités très inégales et là encore il y a un manque d'harmonie artistique. Enfin, les graphismes sont pauvres, au point où les villes et donjons se ressemblent tellement que l'on ne sait plus où l'on est. A aucun moment, on entre dans un nouveau lieu en s'extasiant de l'atmosphère qui s'en dégage.

Défaut majeur : Les personnages et leurs dialogues. Ici, on frise le ridicule. Des dialogues dignes d'un concours d'écriture au collège. Des personnages clichés au point où il est impossible d'avoir de l'empathie (mention spéciale pour le paladin). Seul le héros principal semble être complexe, mais le tout est amené si rapidement et sans transition que cela en est risible (de criminel à repentant en 10 minutes). J'ai entendu dire que l'histoire et les personnages s'améliorent dans les chapitres suivants, mais je n'arrive pas à y arriver tellement les méchants (oui on peut les appeler comme ça) n'ont pas d'autres volonté que d'être méchants. L'histoire globale est quant à elle intéressante mais terriblement mal amenée.

En définitive, dans le même registre, je vous conseille Divided Reigns et Chain of Echoes, qui sont clairement de meilleurs jeux et à des prix similaires.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me just get my rating out of the way to start. 2/5.

Now as for my reasons.
First off, this game is grindy; and when I say grindy, i mean GRINDY. I'm normally happy to have some grind in a game, but the problem is that's what MOST of my time spent in this game was, and not before I reached end: In order to progress I often had to stop and simply grind random encounters for about 1-2 hours between plot points. That's.... not something that you should really have to stop and do during normal gameplay.
If I HADN'T had to grind, this game would have been super quick as well: If I was just playing the actual story, it would most probably only be about a 20 hour game; with grinding, before you reach the end, you're looking at 40-60 if you're going to be skipping the super boss (Which is ALSO the actual find boss, so don't expect a satisfying story/ending if you do that!)
Now, I should add that grinding end game is HEAVILY dependent on getting a key item called the celestium sword so you can insta-kill the rare super XP monsters... the problem is you ONLY get this item if you've been being absurdly focused on collecting all the misseable collectable shards in the game. So if you get to endgame and haven't got enough shards to get that sword, go ahead and go home, you're done: You can't grind at a reasonable rate if you can't reliably kill the rare encounters, as the leveling experience (Which is STILL super long and grindy) is seemingly balanced around that.


Next up, story: You're in for a fairly barebones plot with heavy D&D inspiration, and a host of characters, most of whom are fairly forgettable. Don't expect much. I could spend a whole paragraph detailing issues here (For starters, atrius, your supposed best friend at the start, comes off as a raging sociopath that you just can't empathize with, especially since he's the entire reason for your characters horrific evil actions right after.), but I'll just try and keep it focused there.


Now, are there good things? Well... its a functional RP, so there's that. And while I do think its mediocre, there ARE some good spots (Wein being a decently depicted paladin instead of a stereotype for example). but its just not enough to rescue it from the heap.
Don't buy this game, 2/5, there are much better RPGs out there.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo, I've played through the Demo and then the first few hours of this game and my early impressions are a little mixed.

On the one hand, it is fairly enjoyable in a "old school" type of way, it reminds me a little (visually at least) of the first two Suikoden games.
On the other hand, it is a strong collections of various fantasy cliches in play.
The dwarf and elf do not get along very well, and since the dwarf is also a barbarian, he also does not get along with the wizard.
The protagonist is haunted by his past deeds (though be fair, you actually get to see them done here) and can't tell anyone for fear of them thinking it would make him a monster.
The party are the reincarnations of heroes of old, destined to vanquish the great evil their past lives were unable to fully destroy.

As I said, a collection of cliches, but still enjoyable in it's own way, especially as it offers you a fun game of "guess the next plot point".

I do recommend it if you are into that sort of old school stuff.
For me it's kind of like a warm bath, comfortable and relaxing, but not the first one I've ever had nor is it going to be the last.
Enjoyable while it lasts, but eventually I'll move on, and I won't really give it much thought once done.
but hey, few places are as nice to be as in the bath while it lasts.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI honestly don't understand the negative reviews (and there aren't many, but still). No game will ever be "perfect", nor will every game be the right fit for every player. That said, this game does a lot of things very well. For me, for an RPG to be enjoyable, it has to not only be fun to play (battle mechanics, exploration, crafting, etc), but it has to have a good story and well fleshed out characters. This game delivers all of that, and more.

I'm a completionist, so it took me about 200 hours, yes, 200 hours, to finish. But I wanted to craft everything, beat every boss, and get the "perfect" ending. And to do all that, I had to grind a fair amount, and use the guide. I could have finished in 1/2 the time, easily (which is still an epic game), but I never would have beaten the final boss, nor achieved the perfect ending.

The story revolves around Pots, a thief, who through a series of events, forms a band of characters - The Children of Light. They are all different races (elves, dwarf, etc), and each one has a different reason for joining the group. Further along, you get more involved in their individual stories, and what they and their people have suffered, which really is a nice world view of how events can have a rippling effect.

There is a coliseum to compete in, crafting to upgrade your equipment, quests - if you like the feel of a classic RPG, this definitely fits the bill. My only complaint (which to me was minor) is that I did do a fair amount of grinding to get all the achievements. Even early on, you realize quickly that if you're going to 1) complete this quest 2) beat this boss 3) win enough events to get better prizes, you're going to have to grind a bit.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tNote: Don't be fooled by the playtime. I played the majority of the game in offline mode because of a glitch that could crash the game in online mode. As proof of my actual playtime, look for my LP of the game on Youtube.

Intro

The Great Gaias is a RPG Maker game based on the developers' Tenat D&D campaign, and as such has a lot of worldbuilding concerning the gods, different races, and countries. The main protagonist is Pots, a thief guild member who quits after a mission forces him to do something he regrets. After escaping from a slave ship with some new party members, Pots and his new friends discover they're actually the chosen ones of a prophecy, which may be the chance for redemption that he's looking for.


Writing Quality

Like I mentioned in a forum post, Pots's sinful deed feels awkwardly written, since he regrets the deed while he's in the middle of it. His completion of the deed is necessary for a villainous character's development, but I feel like there should have been an additional factor to incentivize Pots into betraying his conscience and finishing the deed.

While Pots is the main character, many other characters get their chance to shine with their own inner conflicts and personal sidequests, but some of them, such as Hiro and Caelia, get left with little to no meaningful character arcs. It just feels like they exist merely to ensure that we can fill out all the slots in the character selection map. I feel like Hiro in particular had a lot of potential due to his blood relations with one of the major villains, but the two don't seem to acknowledge their blood relation at all.

My favorite part of the game is when where we explore the main enemy nation, which does a good job of showing how the fascist government uses propaganda, military worship, and secretly enforces famine to manipulate citizens against the elves. Additionally, it's shown that the citizens are otherwise normal people and some of them can see through their government's BS, though some like Eldromus are beyond redemption. I find that a lot of games that try to make their bad guys racist and fascist often skip the important developments that lead to such ideas being normalized, so I'm glad this game put effort into covering that. That said, I wish we could see what life is like for the second-class, non-military associated members of the empire.

Also, the fascist part isn't hyperbole, since one of the military school teachers quotes the Norsefire from V for Vendetta.

Gameplay

tl;dr play easy or normal instead of Core Rules for the most consistent experience, unless you want bragging rights for Youtube.

In most games, skills and normal attacks tend to have similar accuracy unless stated otherwise. On Core Rules, physical skills have accuracy penalties while having fairly powerful effects. Personally, I would have preferred weaker but more accurate skills so that there's still some challenge while reducing the RNG factor, since I'm the type of person who is nettled by wasted turns and resources. The accuracy rate is saner on the easier difficulties, so I recommend going for those if you have a similar mindset.

For resource management, AP is random and rather low at the start of each battle, which is understandable because some of the later AP skills are too powerful to use at the start of battle. The MP situation is pretty bad in the beginning though, since your mages have low starting MP and therefore won't benefit much from their equipment's passive MP regen. Like with physical skills, this is supposed to be balanced by how magic skills are powerful enough to justify their cost. However, the mages really need to have an even lower tier of magic spells that do less damage and cost less than 5 MP, since the 5 MP spells are overkill for early enemies. Again, the lower difficulties alleviate this by giving you more starting MP.

Like Nocturne: Rebirth, you can change equipment in the middle of battle without using up a turn, which really helps if you're playing blind and don't know what ailments the enemies will use. You can also do 1000 IQ maneuvers like equipping a new auto-revive accessory after using the old one up.

The bosses are balanced with the expectation that you're using buffs and debuffs wisely. Some of them also have interesting mechanics, like the bonus bosses of the final sidequest, though some mechanics will take a lot of trial and error to figure out unless you read the Steam guide. Still, it's better than simply fighting vanilla beatsticks all the time.

The game is really grindy, since you have to manage 16 characters by the end of the game. In such a situation, you'd expect that maybe the inactive party members would get half or quarter EXP, but they get absolutely no EXP. Oh, and there are story events that will force you to use specific characters or exclude specific characters, which means you cannot just bet everything on your favorite party. I get that the developers want players to use all the characters and watch them grow in levels, but according to the forum posts, people are trying to stick to only their faves, only to be frustrated when they run into events that force them to use their neglected party members. The only thing that makes up for this is that you will find situations where some characters are better suited than others, but there's also a very obvious tier list in terms of their overall usefulness and some characters feel like Masters of None, making it tempting to play favorites in leveling them.

There is a lot of equipment with special effects, and everyone has their own unique green/purple text gear. However, mid-to-late game enemies are very spammy with very game-changing status effects, making it feel like you can't really equip any Jewel other than status resist ones. There really needs to be a second jewel slot, allowing players to try out other types of jewels without fearing divine retribution from the enemy.

The New City portion of the game is a town building minigame, but it's implemented in the worst way possible. Instead of recruiting unique, meaningful, and interesting NPCs, you just enter and exit specific type of buildings to recruit anonymous NPCs of specific walks of life. Since every iteration of the town has its own Celestium Shards, you will have to spend quite a lot of time evicting and recruiting people just to get everything.

Verdict

7/10, an average but long game. There's interesting plot elements the further you go, but some early game writing and balancing could leave a bad first impression. Also, I feel like having a huge cast of characters hurts the game more than helps it, since not all of them are sufficiently developed and the EXP system and endgame crafting situation can't really handle them all.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you enjoyed classic console games from the 80s, this definitely fits the bill for a trip down nostalgia lane.

The game is quite meaty--took me 71 or so hours of in-game time to see just about everything. There were a lot of challenging boss fights. The game allows for grinding to counter some of the challenge, but for the most part I didn't do that, so many boss fights took a number of attempts to "get it right." The writing overall was a notch above typical RPG Maker fare--the designers attempted to weave a reasonably serious storyline, and I'll credit them with just how detailed it becomes--it's a very fleshed out history spanning a millenium, at least. Lots of hand-designed, well thought out places to explore.

If you can appreciate a game for it being a labor of love, this is worth the time, overall. The game was challenging at times--I checked out the forum a few times when I got stuck for too long.

Some negatives: a few sequences without saves later in the game that can be a little frustrating on your time (if you play like I do, and don't over-grind to over-prepare), combat animations are a bit slow and unskippable).

Overall, a good game, significantly better than most RPGMaker content I've played.

But with every movie with so much cast, the later part of the games lacks much interaction or developement between the many characters... But the story more than makes up for it.
