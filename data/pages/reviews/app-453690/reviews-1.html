Mouse-Only Crawling

Spent the entire game on my back in my couch using only mouse. I wanted a lazy, relaxed, tactical fantasy game and this is exactly that. The classic Holy Trinity RPG classes are here, equipped with their usual playstyle that complement each other. (Tank, Healer, Barbarian, Archer, Wizard.. and a Chaos Monk) ^_^

It's a medium difficulty crawler with some quirks in it's coding, but the game works just fine. Art-style is fantastic and the music is good to.

Took me 15 hours to 100% in a casual manner. One regular playthrough, and one Hardcore run that I almost failed. So some thinking is required there.

The achievement that requires you to amass 100K gold - can be sorted via a Workshop map. But I recommend doing everything else as intended.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA simple but charming digital adaptation of Hero Quest, which has crashed on me already once in 20 minutes of play. I'll probably play it up to the 2-hour refund window and keep it if doesn't crash again as I feel that it's worth it at 70% off.

I don't really like the single-room view of the game and would much rather have a larger view like in the board-game but I can't complain much for a dollar's worth.

I have heard this described as a game with limited replay-value but in-between 6 heroes (almost 100 party compositions), half a dozen upgrades per hero and Steam Workshop support with almost a 100 maps uploaded, I feel that there is a little more here than meets the eye.

In particular, the game being beatable in under 10 hours makes this a potential title to come back to every so often and beat on a lazy sunday or over the weekend. Different difficulties, including an iron-man mode will further complement this option.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tEch, you got me, I'm a total sucker for isometric perspective, classic RPG themes and turn-based combat. Bought this game quite a while ago, but never checked it out, as there were always more interesting alternatives to play. But the time has finally come, and I gave it a try.

Surprisingly, this is actually a quite decent casual little game. Don't expect a lot of strategic depth or polished RPG system - it's something meant to be played on tablets - but it's fun enough to click around for a while if you happen to be laying bedridden.

Painted graphics are really nice, dungeon locations look cute, the gameplay is simple, but engaging enough to spend about 8 hours completing the main campaign. Six not exactly balanced character classes to choose from to form your 3-man party, there are also very simple skill progression and item systems. Default difficulty offers a bit of a challenge and the the whole adventure will most likely be over before it will get boring. Game has a map editor, so there's some more replayability in the form of downloading user created maps through steam workshop. I actually played the whole thing twice back to back with different party setups.

If I wanted to nitpick, UI is not exactly stellar, often characters are blocking with their sprites certain tiles you'd like to move to. I'd also wish the game was somewhat longer, but I guess the price is fair for what the game has to offer.

As long as your expectations are not too high, you should have some fun with this title.\t\t\t\t\t

Pros:
- Fun classes

Cons:
- Overall pretty boring
- Poor performance and bugs
- Poor music and sounds

Overall Score 3/10
The game seemed like simple fun at first glance and I thought I could get into, unfortunately it was very poorly made.
For starters the technical issues were plenty. Ive had mission-ending bugs, Ive had clipping issues, Ive had tooltips remain on screen even though I closed the window. The QoL wasnt any good either, there are no keyboard shortcuts or key bindings, there isnt any option to rotate the camera, you cant leave mid mission and the worst of all whoever made that menu should consider hitting himself in the head a few times, who the hell puts exit game right under the options as a huge button? ended 3 runs early thanks to that poor design, at least there is a game speed increase, it wouldve been absolutely unplayable if lightspeed wasnt an option.
And there are other random things that are simply annoying, like one particular character having a resurrect spell where you target the remains of your allies to do so, and another class having an ability that makes clones of itself, very normal things you would think but oh no, if a clone dies it leaves behind remains as well, and believe me resurrecting a clone instead of the main hero is annoying as hell, WHY WOULD YOU EVEN HAVE THAT in the game!!???
This game could have potential but as it is its just a joke, a student project at best.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tUltimately, what should be a good game is let down by mechanics and the type of difficulty. It just gets very boring and very samey and very grindy in a very short while. This could have been a great game, but sadly it isn't.

UI Issues:

Drag and drop of portraits is finicky and its possible to glitch the portraits so you have 2 of 1 hero. if you don't place it within the small hidden inner drop zone then it doesn't register.

You can't see what is on the ground when an enemy or object is in the foreground. Since you need to target a location or object to move or cast a spell, you will need to jog your party around to click on exit locations and forget casting resurrection in combat if there is an enemy in the foreground hiding the body of your fallen hero.

AI is simplistic, so to cover this the game has preset levels, and since you only get one use of each ability (in general) per quest, knowing when to use each ability is basically a case of having played the level before.

RNG: enough said.

Most of the craftable items have absolutely no value. There is always a "best" item, because of the way enemies work.

Late game enemies deal unblockable chaos damage. Have fun wading through a room full of ranged unblockable damage while your heros are being spam-feared

In the end I didn't finish it. The story is boring. The levels are insipid,. the characters are mostly lackluster and the few redeeming elements are swallowed up by the mess of the rest. After recovering 2 pieces of the amulet, and needing to get the third, I literally felt a sense of dread of having to play another quest. That's when I stopped.

I'll add that the game marketing is extremely misleading.

The "Skull of Fate"? That just means your character avoids death once per Fate level you have, and survives with 0 hp (so 1 more hit will kill them). The game also has enemies that have Fate, mostly undead. There's no "twisted fate" to discover, this is simply a "if you take damage that would kill you, instead reduce hp to 0, consume 1 fate".

Likewise the "discover hidden doors". There's no hidden doors, they all are clearly visible and actually required to go through to complete the quest in most cases. They all also have exactly the same form of a raised tile which the game introduces you to in the very first quest.

Pick it up if it's on high discount, and you just want to waste an hour or two tooling about, but big miss otherwise.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTL; DR Dark Quest 2 is fun especially with friends, but it is short. It plays very much like a boardgame and you can make some comparisons with Hero Quest. However, it does have a few issues some of which are due to the implementation of multiplayer and others are more fundamental.

Note Dark Quest 1 is summarised within the opening cutscene and the first chapter of the game. I recommend starting with this one since the original game is less fun and less interesting.

Story
There is a Sorcerer, and he is evil, stop him.

In other words, the plot is fairly standard and its definitely simple throughout. At most you will get a few sentences in a cutscene, but this is not a character or story driven game.
What worldbuilding there is gets explained through the pre-mission summaries, but this is also rather simple, however, it does give you some slight insight into the state of this world and the motives of certain enemies.

Presentation
Aesthetically the game draws influence from Sword & Sorcery titles which is especially clear with the designs for the Barbarian and Evil Sorcerer. Visually these and the rest of the characters are good, with a cohesive, slightly cartoonish artstyle and exaggerated animations.
Furthermore, every enemy is visually distinct meaning that a cursory glance is enough to understand what enemies you will be facing in a room. I also enjoy the addition of less combat focused designs, such as the standard-bearers and the drummers which gives the roster some unique variety.
While these designs are competently made, they are also generic and nothing else sticks out as interesting.

No sound effect is particularly exciting but thankfully nothing is broken either. However, the sound effect which plays whenever a trap is uncovered and the little melody which plays when finishing a level are both a bit jarring to the overall soundscape.
The soundtrack is a better experience, it is filled with good songs with a healthy mix of instruments and a few atmospheric tracks to even things out. However, the OST is very short which means you will hear certain songs over and over, which can get annoying if you are doing multiple playthroughs.

Gameplay

As a sequel this improves on the original in a lot of ways, mostly by making the RNG better such as removing the chance for healing potions to not work but also with more practical things such as having Hero abilities be permanent upgrades instead of something you need to pay for between every mission.
However, at its core its still very similar with a basic chance-based combat system and mostly simple combat encounters.

The game itself is divided into two parts preparation in the village, and dungeon delving. The former includes getting upgrades, equipment and buffs, while the latter is the majority of the game including the combat.

Every quest comes with its own sorts of challenges including boss monsters, ambushes, and various types of traps. There are also the Skulls of Fate that can be activated for random effects such as wounding enemies or healing your Heroes. However, they can also be risky to use since they end your turn and since they might not do anything at all.
The core of the game is managing RNG and making tactical decisions based on your capabilities and those of your enemies. Is it worthwhile to use an ability for guaranteed damage or should you save one of your three uses for another room? Should you heal or can you afford to save it for later?

There are also mutually exclusive challenges for singleplayer and multiplayer. When playing solo, you are limited to whatever coins and upgrade potions you can find and these need to be split between the Heroes you are using. In other words, you will need to prioritise certain abilities or Heroes.
Unfortunately, this incentivises you to stick to the Heroes you acquire early on as trying to change to a new un-upgraded hero on later missions is ill-advised. However, the management of upgrade points can be completely nullified if you replay missions which will also break the intended gameplay progression as you get too strong too quickly.

In multiplayer this is not a challenge since each player gains resources independently, instead your issue is your hero count, as each player can only control one character. In the vast majority of cases this is fine and even easy with some minor tactics, however, on certain missions the RNG is heavily stacked against you which makes for a frustrating experience.
It is certainly possible to beat every mission with only two Heroes or even a single one, however, this is highly improbable and would demand very exact tactics. Therefore, I would recommend playing certain missions solo so that you can actually enjoy the game.

There are also a few quirks of the game that can be a nuisance. One of which is the movement on the isometric map as it is very difficult to click on the squares behind characters, instead you will select the hero or enemy you are trying to flank.
A few QoL features are missing, for example, in the village you are unable to buff Heroes in a group instead everyone needs to get a buff individually, you are also forced to spend one upgrade point at a time which means upgrading takes a while if you have a dozen points in reserve.
There is also Hardcore which does pose more of a challenge than the normal difficulty, however, it also accentuates some of the worst aspects of the RNG such as teamkilling. It also favours certain party compositions more than others which can make repeat attempts dull and repetitive.

In any case the game is rather short and besides multiplayer or trying out different parties there is little reason to replay the campaign. Luckily, they did include a level editor which includes all the tools necessary to make dungeons as complex as those in the Campaign. As a result, there are a number of player-created maps for you to enjoy if you want more of the game, and the option to create your own is also there.\t\t\t\t\t
