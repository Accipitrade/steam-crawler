\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDisclaimer: This review is from a co-op perspective only.

Dark Quest 2 is turn based game and actually a tribute to the 1989 Games Workshop boardgame title Hero's Quest, and does a great job with beutiful, quirky hand drawn graphics echoing of the 80's pen & paper rpg's. The lack of more generic 3d content is refreshing in the way it is made, and even though the animation might look a bit stiff remembering it's miniature game forefather might help. Another con due to the artstyle is the lack of customization, but honestly is out of the scope of the game and would feel wrong.

Beneath this rather simplistic looking surface the gameplay is surprisingly tactical in a way that encourages planning who will go first and doing what each turn.

The game is bascially a 'world' map where every mission marked on it is a dungeon of some kind. This is turn consists of a number of rooms and corridors filled with enemies and loot. Apart from money your most sought after bounty will be blue flasks of experience, which will let you upgrade your characters between missions.

Between dungeons you end up in the Village where you can rest, learn skills, buy potions, craft items etc.

Since each player can only control one character, a smaller party might have to decide on which one they will play depending on the type of challenge ahead. Luckily each player can keep a full roster of characters to play with and upgrades is not restricted to the character that collected the experience.

There are six different classes once fully unlocked and each one got seven or eight special abilities or skills:
* Barbarian - Think Conan with a big ass sword, a bunch of offensive mechanics and decent hp.
* Dwarf - Once fleshed out a decent tank, capable of digging in and holding the front. Also capable of some crowd control.
* Archer - A ranger type character with access to special arrow attacks and useful stuff like a heal, setting and detecting traps etc.
* Wizard - Can cast illusions, firewalls, lightning and wizardy stuff like that and is capable of dealing a decent amount of damage.
* Knight - A must have for a bigger group and can hold on pretty well tanking.
* Dark Monk - A strange bird this one, with situational abilities that sometimes are incredibly powerful. Can swap places with enemies and friends, make enemies attack eachother, kill any enemy and leach its hp, massdamage everyone in a room etc.

The abilities/skills mechanics takes a while to figure out but in a nutshell:
* Abilites can normally be used once per mission. Upgrades, charges and potions can increase this number.
* Skills are either passive or active but can be used every turn.

Over this layer you also have a system of hit points, magic shielding and 'Fate' that you also have to take in consideration.

This means that the meta game consists of trying to figure out how you can combine different skillsets with your team mates.
The way you approach a dungeon with a Barbarian - Ranger duo is different from the solution you find with a Dark Monk - Dwarf one etc.
And that is the best part of the game in my opinion. Every character got a different playing style. If the Dwarf controls the horde of regular orcs, the Monk will pinpoint and take out ranged threats, like pesky witches that slowly sting you hp using needles and a voodoo doll.
Testing out your theories and watching this play out is a satisfying feeling.

The game also includes a Editor making it possible to craft levels for the Workshop, and even though it is a bit clunky and undocumented adds extra value and longevity to the game.

For gamers that enjoy co-op games like Divinity or Card Hunter this is a no brainer.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI wanted to love this game. Huge fan of Hero Quest, still own the board game =D

For me the game breaker is the fact that when replaying a mission, the mission gets harder. More monsters/traps/ect.

How do you farm a previous mission to get stronger for the next, when the previous mission itself is vamped up so heavy?

Example, mission #3, first room, one bad encounter with 3 mage, 2 warriors, and an assassin mostly wiped the party outright, barbarian is left with 2 hp, so party throws up the white flag and hoofs it back to town. Not enough gold to revive and heal everyone. So we try and take the revived archer and the wounded barb on mission #2, That way we can earn some gold and revive the dwarf. And actually afford to sleep at the inn.

Mission #2 is now harder since we beat it once. Every room has more mobs and there are extra traps to trigger as well. The archer and barbarian do not make it. So one bad encounter basically ended the game. It was impossible to recover from an almost party wipe, even in the early game.

I don't understand the idea of making prior missions harder and harder every time it's played. How is someone supposed to recover from a bad adventure? I didn't like this aspect of the game at all and feel it ruins it.

Also as noted on other posts, why doesn't the hero that notices a trap on the floor, STOP? Would any adventurer you know keep walking over a trap they noticed before stepping on it?

Multiplayer is limited to one character each, any reasons? Why can't we control up to two each for a full party?

This could be a nice little game, but it needs work.\t\t\t\t\t
