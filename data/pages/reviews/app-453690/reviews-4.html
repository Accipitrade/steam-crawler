\r\n\t\t\t\t\tDQ2 is a party-based, turn-based dungeon crawler. As part of the larger campaign, you delve into ~25 different "dungeons", all of which use a similar castle-type of graphics. For each dungeon you will use a party of 2/3/4 (usually 3), choosing from amongst a total of six typical character types (warrior, knight, dwarf, archer, wizard, dark monk). Within each dungeon, you will explore roughly 10-20 rooms. Each room contains a fixed (I think it is fixed anyway), number and type of enemies. On your turn you can use a potion (1 or 2 equipped per dungeon run), use a skill (limited in charges) and/or move/attack. Attacks may be blocked or missed.

In between dungeon runs there is some minor character building using level-up potions (I forget the exact term) which may be found/farmed in the dungeons. That is, leveling up the various characters is comprised of locating these potions and then spending them on upgrades back at base. Any character may be upgraded, not just those that you used in the dungeon. As such, the typical concept of "experience" is not present. Stats are present, but these typically aren't leveled up either. Rather progression takes the form of passive and active skills.

Pros:
  • Character skills are interesting for some of the characters.
  • No permadeath on normal difficulty, which I prefer.
  • The equipment crafting/purchasing provided a short-term use for money and provided some level of interest to me for a time (noting that I had bought everything roughly 60% of the way through).
  • Cheap ($$) on sale.

YMMV:
  • Story was basically non-existent. I don't typically care about story too much in a game like this, but if you do look elsewhere.
  • I didn't care for the level-up system. Characters don't gain experience, they merely gain new powers, and even then they didn't even need to battle to power them up.
  • If you like finding exciting gear in dungeons (and honestly who doesn't), you won't find any here. All gear (aside from a couple of artifacts) is simply bought back in base.

Cons:
  • The tactical gameplay was severely lacking. You typically try to hit the guy closest to you. Some characters have some limited nukes they can use, but for the most part there aren't a lot of decisions to make.
  • Given the point above, success in battle was largely attributed to luck as to how often you or your enemy blocked/dodged. This is common in many games (e.g. X-com), but the tactical combat here is so brain-dead that the drawbacks of such a system are highlighted.

  • There was no real long-term team-building strategy involved, since you could always go back and farm the earlier levels for the level-up potions. That is, there was no specialization in character development. You always end up with a character with all skills maxed. If you had the patience, you could do this for all 6 characters. There are minor decisions as to which skills to level up first, which was fine; but ultimately such decisions didn't matter.

  • Though you have a roster of 6 characters, realistically you only ever fight with the same 3 or 4. It just doesn't make sense to spend level-up potions on all characters, when you can only ever use 3. The dungeons themselves don't force you to mix-and -match, so I didn't. As such, I never used the archer or wizard since I unlocked them later in the game.
  • All dungeons looked very similar.
  • There were only about 6 or 7 different enemy types across all dungeons.

Summary: I initially found DQ2 amusing. The base-building (crafting/leveling up skills) looked intriguing. All those shiny characters and skills, so much to craft. Unfortunately, I found the long term team building strategy lacking and the tactical battles in the dungeons grew tiresome. I did finish the game as a bit of a completionist (where practical), but honestly I was ready to quit about 60% of the way through. If you have time to burn and get this on sale, it may be OK. But probably you should pass.

Overall: 4.5/10\t\t\t\t\t
