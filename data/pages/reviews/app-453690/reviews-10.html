\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPurchased the game for around $2 during a sale. This is a reasonable purchasing price for a game that has potential, but lacks in any noticeable department.

*Interface/Controls
The game lacks a surprising amount of explanation for what its UI elements are, and how to use them. The options menu is adequate but you will not find any help. Learning how to equip items, how to upgrade, and more importantly, the movement of characters in the game is very difficult. You will spend much time pixel hunting, trying to move characters to specific parts of the map, simply because another characters model obscures it. There are no hotkeys to cycle through your heroes, levels end without prompting once objectives are met (despite rewarding exploration). You cannot change the camera angle, as the games camera is fixed and the map is isometric, compounding the pixel hunting issue.

*Gameplay loop
The primary objective of the game is to loot as much gold as possible while finishing the quest objectives with at least one hero alive. Unless you actively choose the hardest difficulty, death is only a temporary set back. There is no positive reward for killing an entire room of enemies unless it holds an objective. The rare moments enemies actually drop gold are a pitiful 10-20, while healing is the true precious commodity, the cheapest potion costing 120, and generic rests between quests costing 50 gold per hero, regardless of how much health is needed. Though exploration is pivotal to game progress, your tools are aggravating. Each individual hero (regardless of room clear) must traverse the length of the room to the exit.

In dungeons of 10-15 plus rooms, each encompassing roughly 5-10 squares of mandatory walking animation (even on fast setting) is a great bulk of game time. It boggles the mind that in general exploring heroes cannot be condensed into one square (one model representing the party). Exploring the dungeons does not become an engaging risk vs reward asset, but a tedious slog where players who've already played the game will know the optimal route. Of course, even completing a quest does not allow you to keep said map, forcing those who value their time to write it down or else wonder lost in the dungeons. As the core aspect of a dungeon-crawling adventure, the crawl is too literal.

On to more positive aspects, the combat is at least serviceable, though leaves many questions. Each hero holds unique playstyles and abilities, with minor overlap. During your quests however, again killing enemies are generally useless. Avoiding combat for new players is my recommendation, as well as learning when it is better to take the loss and retreat or allow a party wipe (reviving dead heroes can be cheaper than individual heals). To progress in actual power, acquiring blue potions (I don't recall the true name) will allow your characters to improve their abilities, as well as gold being needed to buy equipment, all of which must be searched out in the dungeon. Both gold and blue potions are "farmable" if you find the correct map. (In the late game I prefer "Executioner" personally) Out of the 6 total heroes only 3 are allowed per quest, which is questionable, leaving the rest of the heroes useless once you enter mid game (unless you wish to farm).

The enemies are varied enough themselves to not feel too samey, though you will recognize a pattern. Goblins have traps. arrows, and deadly spells, Orcs have support magic, high stats, and more bosses. Chaos, as the name implies, takes a bit of both. Beefier units, deadly unblockable spells, and magic that both supports their units and causes chaos for you the player. The game is mercifully short enough that by the time you get tired of a certain enemy type, you should be eased into the next.

*Other features
The game boasts a dungeon editor, as well as multiplayer support. These features I have not used, thus cannot comment.

*Story
The story is absent. While true you will have an opening cinematic, and a few in-game cutscenes the story is simple. Fight your way to the evil wizard and slay him. Avoiding spoilers, I will state that the boss fight and ending cinematic is just criminal. 10 hours of on an off gameplay to fight a boss with a few gimmicks and a story that gips you of any sense of accomplishment. Do you accomplish the primary goal? Technically yes, and it could've been a hell of a lot better.

*Final issues.
There are bugs in the game, and some abilities that you may rely heavily on for victory may just end up useless late-game. The trouble is, was it an intentional design decision by the developer or a lack of oversight during testing? The game as it stands has been out for a year, so I allow you to form that opinion. In short, I find many design decisions questionable of the developer, which hopefully by the time of Dark Quest 3 they will perfect their formula.

Is this game worth it? If you enjoy average dungeon-crawling in the style of hero-quest, for the price of a McChicken you'll technically get more value.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhat was supposed to be a night of celebration in the kings castle turned into a night of horror. The sorcerer and his minions had returned and attacked when nobody expected. Within a few hours every man, woman and child was butchered, including the king.

It is up to you to enter the castle to face madness and death on your ultimate quest to defeat evil.

Dark Quest 2 is a role-playing, turn-based dungeon crawler. The game starts as a very simple, primitive brawler, but gains tactical depth, the longer you play. That's because your characters change strongly throughout the course of the game and every new dungeon you enter is more demanding.

Dark Quest 2 lets you play with six different heroes. The barbarian, the dwarf, the dark monk, the wizard, the paladin and the archer. Each of these characters comes with very different characteristics, resulting in a very different game play experience, depending on the heroes you pick for your party.

While you start the game with a single hero, the barbarian, you can later play dungeons with up to four party members. During the game you can collect gold and potions. Gold can be used to craft better weapons, armor and magic items. Potions can be used to improve your heroes abilities. Additionally you can find special items in the dungeons that give you further advantages.

Each of the heroes comes with a very different set of abilities. The paladin has great healing and protection powers, while the barbarian shows advantages due to his physical strength. The wizard and the monk are physically weak but offer powerful magic. As expected the archer can carry out great long range attacks and the dwarf is a strong melee fighter and displays craftsmanship with traps.

Depending on the heroes you choose for your party and the talents you upgrade, you can influence the way you play. Defensive, aggressive or even stealthy.

In the late game you will counter an overwhelming number of enemies that you can only defeat with the right use of your abilities and tactics.

The game is very well balanced on normal difficulty and also very enjoyable. You will die a couple of times but you can re-play already finished dungeons to collect more gold and potions to upgrade your abilities and craft better equipment. That's the way it's meant to be played.

However, since the game comes with Steam workshop support there are ways to cheat. Some workshop maps allow you to easily collect gold and potions on specially designed maps. I do not recommend using these before finishing the game at least once. But of course this kind of help is helpful in hardcore difficulty mode.

Dark Quest 2 is a rather simple but very enjoyable old school game. If you have a nag for these kind of games I can strongly recommend Dark Quest 2.

Pros:
- it's fun
- simple but nice comic graphics
- very diversified heroes with very different abilities
- equip your heroes with special potions
- allows different tactics
- multiplayer mode
- Steam workshop support
- option to move very fast (helpful after first play-through)
- possibility to revive dead heroes in normal and easy difficulty (not in hardcore mode)

Cons:
1) Low content and none of the mechanics are fleshed out. (About 20 Items, 5 Traps, 20-30 Spells, 8-12 Enemy Types, etc...)
2) Most of the mechanics are heavy on RNG.
3) Don't know why there are secret doors, its so obvious to find them.
4) Moving characters around the room after fighting is a chore.
5) Literally the only treasure you find in most maps is gold (20-30 gold that to).
6) Blacksmith can craft 20 items most of which class specific - so 3 items per hero the entire game (2 Equip Slots Joke).

8) Hero resurrection is joke. You entire party can be revived with items for a pittance.
9) Useless NPCs, Merchant - sell some random things for gold (That is it, he does nothing else), Brothel - get a random bonus for 150 gold...
10) No town upgrades - No boss fights except the final boss...

Pros:
1) Music is decent but only 3-4 tracks on repeat.
2) Passable graphics and controls.
3) Tutorials were to the point and clear

Don't but this game as it is, it needs tons of content and updates.

Verdict: 3/10\t\t\t\t\t
