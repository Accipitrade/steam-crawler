The game has some English translations issues, but you should still be able to understand all important elements.(With a couple of exceptions) This is a great Turn based Tactics game, recent updates have added difficulty options. Easy makes the game mostly a cakewalk, Brutal offers a significant challenge(by letting enemies gain passives/traits, unit permadeath, no restarts, etc.)

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a great tactics game with some solid design choices. Combat is similar to Fire Emblem in terms of movement/attack range and units retaliating when attacked. Stage objectives have a decent amount of variation and enemies (especially bosses) have interesting mechanics to play around. Journey Mode has nodes similar to other roguelites, but you can traverse freely and even revisit past nodes. Lower difficulties are fairly forgiving and allow you to retry/abort combat as much as you'd like.

There are three clans and five classes. Each clan has a unique play style and different take on each class. Every one of the 100+ characters also has their own stats and personal skill which sets them apart even within the same class/clan. Each time a character levels, they draw from a pool of skills (class/clan/general) and max level characters will have 5 skills. While some skills are simple stat boosts, the majority of them provide interesting effects that have interesting synergies with other skills. There are also Artifacts that provide interesting team-wide effects, so there's plenty of room for theory-crafting.

The game already has a generous amount of content for its price and updates have been smoothing out a lot of the more noticeable issues, so I'll withhold my criticisms and simply say that the game is enjoyable in its current state and you can't really go wrong for the price.

-----

To illustrate how great the variety and team-building is in this game...

Ninelie Priests focus on summon animal companions. You have a character who spawns vampiric bats when enemies are killed, one who starts each stage with a War Bear, one who summons a Boar to take the hit when attacked, and more. On top of this, you have a skill that makes companions explode on death, a skill that has nearby companions follow up on your attacks, a skill that makes nearby companions tank hits for you, and more.

Bronian Priests summon stationary emplacements called "Works" that activate when any character ends their turn on them. There's a character whose Works spawns exploding drones, a character whose Works shoots lightning at nearby enemies, a character whose Works gives armor to nearby allies, and more. Then you have skills that mitigate damage for allies standing on your Works, summon Spike Traps when you summon Works, deal damage when an ally is attacked on your Works, and more.

For Artifacts, you have one that spawns a Ballista at the start of the battle, one that gives 100% crit and causes Fear when flanking, one that damages and poisons enemies that end their turn in grass, and more... Ninelie Scouts have boosted crit when attacking from behind in addition to skills which allow them to Backstab enemies in grass and spawn grass on plain terrain, so you can easily see interesting ways to combine them with some of these Artifacts (and each other).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's a pretty fun Fire Emblem/Roguelite mix. You control a party comprimising of 5 classes with 3 distinct faction. Every class of every faction has distinct mechanics and thus all 3 factions play differently. Out of the 5 classes there are several different characters which once again has different core abilities (e.g. Priest of the bunny race are about having summon and healing but each of them has different summons/beast and thus would be built differently, one can have big tanky bear while others summon multiple weaker mobs that depending on your build can be made stronger or just explode when they are dead).

Combine all the above with having party size of 4 - 6 per battle (depending on encounters) and you have yourself a very entertaining SRPG that can differ widely on how you play. As per rogulite, there are relics/artifacts with major power that further changes how you play.

Overall a very fun game and I am hoping to see the game grow.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTl;dr An x-com like tactics game with short chapter based campaigns, light meta progression, a diverse cast of characters and a large array of complex character abilities, gameplaying changing factions and synergies. Also a plethora of fluffy animal girls, including rare birb girls, that are all incredibly scritable.

A note on the roguelike tag
Contrary to a lot of the reviews as of posting, steam tags and the store front, this game isn’t really a roguelike anymore – whilst the ‘Abyss’ game mode still exists, most of the focus seems to be on the ‘Journey’ mode, which lets you retry fights until you can perfect them, and provides quite a few opportunities to revive injured party members by visiting various nodes on the world map, rather than simply deleting them from your roster. So, if you’re more of a casual player, there’s definitely a lot of content for you.

Gameplay
If you’ve played a tactics game before, especially Fire Emblem, you’ll probably be able to pick up the general gist of Prime of Flame pretty fast – you have a team of units, they can move up to X grid spaces and then make an attack. Some of the units have special movement modes like flight. All your guys go at once so you can coordinate them, and then the enemies all get their turns. Like in Fire Emblem, everyone gets to counter attack, so it’s important to stay out of enemy threat range, or learn to lure squishy enemies into your high damage frontliners to score a free attack – although unlike in fire emblem, you only get one, so it’s possible to ‘clear’ enemy counter attacks by charging in with your tank, before attacking with squishy assassin units.

The main appeal of Prime of Flame is that it takes this concept and goes 300% with it’s weird class abilities and character synergies.

The game currently contains 3 factions, that dictate which cute anime girls you can recruit during your playthrough. There’s currently a roster of 30 per faction, divided evenly between the 5 classes (Scout, Priest, Hunter, Warrior and Guard). Whilst all factions technically share the same 5 classes for the purposes of party wide upgrades, in practice there’s 15 classes all with their own gimmick and unique pool of level up perks.

For example, the mecha faction warriors build up ‘Assault’ stacks by moving, and then use them to fire off various gadgets. Meanwhile, the beastgirl clan warriors are all Barbarians that enter an uncontrollable rage after hitting 70% HP, gaining a huge stat boost, but forcing them to mindlessly attack the nearest enemy each turn. Meanwhile, the more magically themed owl girl warriors build up stacks by attacking enemies in order to transform into an ultimate form, which drastically alters their abilities, and comes with a sick looking alternate sprite.

Each character in the game then has a further twist on these core class gimmicks – for example, one Owlgirl warrior transforms themselves from a front line bruiser to an artillery pieces that can fire orbital lasers at enemies on the other side of map, whilst another might instead gain the ability to force enemies they hit to attack their allies.

The factions themselves also have squad wide passives that drastically change up gameplay. For example, the Owlgirls heal fully at the end of each round, completely removing the element of attrition from the game – but as a drawback, they’re all incredibly squishy, forcing players to find creative ways of avoiding damage. Meanwhile the mecha girls faction takes inspiration from the Banner Saga, gaining double their armour value, but allowing enemies to directly lower it over the course of the fight.

Combined with dozens of handcrafted encounters with a variety of objectives, and wildly different boss fights, there’s quite a few different builds and strategies to try out before getting bored – though I have heard that veterans of the genre might find the game a bit too easy in its current iteration.

Both game modes also allow you to level up your characters from a pool of interesting class specific perks, as well as collecting various artefacts and equipment pieces to improve the whole party. Journey mode also includes some light meta progression, allowing you to pick an extra starting perk for one of your characters and bring along various consumables that save you a bit of gold on future runs, as well as unlocking a broader range of artefacts. Overall, nothing that would prevent you from completing the game or force a player to ‘go back and grind’ before facing high difficulties.

Translation Issues
The main drawback of the title is the fact that the English release is obviously an afterthought. It’s definitely not the worst translated game around, with the team appearing to mostly stick to key words. There’s definitely a few mishaps though – for example, a lot of abilities use the around keyword, which I believe means adjacent and diagonal, but sounds more like a broad descriptor to us native speakers.

It can be quite annoying in Abyss mode where the highly specific and complicated enemy traits can be difficult to parse until after an enemy has used it to wipe your team. In the Journey mode however, you can safely restart after seeing the boss use it, but in the perma-death mode, it can feel a bit cheap to lose a run to grammatical issues. Similarly, some of the more complicated character classes can have some oddly worded perks that I felt pretty hesitant about picking – it certainly isn’t an issue with every boss or perk, but you’ll definitely run into a handful of problems during your playthrough.

Overall, though, the devs seem pretty active about collecting feedback and refining the English translation, and at least as of this posting, there’s a semi-active discord community that are more than willing to clarify mechanics.

UI and In-Game Help Files
The UIs currently a bit of a mixed bag. Between fights, it’s pretty extensive, but a lot information – such as move speed and attack range, is needlessly hidden during combat, which can be especially confusing for enemies like bears that can attack diagonally. You can usually get a pretty good idea of this by clicking a unit, which then displays which tiles they can move into or attack, but it can sometimes be slow to read.

There’s also a ‘see all threatened tiles’ toggle, which frustratingly cannot be toggled on whilst a unit is selected.

Speech bubbles sometimes overlap with each other or other UI elements as well, likely due to the spacing not being optimised for other languages.

The game’s tutorial is actually quite good, but a lot of information about the game’s inner workings isn’t explained at all. However, for most things, the game does a very good job of presenting tooltips to specify the different keywords mentioned in an ability – though attributes like armour and attack are noticeably missing them, leaving you to guess based on the icons.


Art and Theme
The art is surprisingly good for such a cheap game, with each of the 90+ characters possessing hand crafted attack and move animations as well as bits and pieces of voice acting – albeit in Chinese. They sometimes spout little speech bubbles in order to flesh out their personalities a bit more between fights though, so there’s that. As you might have summarised by now, there are indeed no playable male characters in the game – possibly for some convoluted lore reason, but most likely because the devs are just really into cat girls. None of the models are especially sexualised so you don’t need to worry about your parents barging into the room, but I know it can still be a deal breaker for some people.

There’s a pretty decent variety of different biomes and enemies as well, which at least aren’t also limited to being monster girls.

Overall, at 15 hours I feel like I’m only just scratching the surface, and would definitely recommend it to anymore looking for another tactics game to mess around with.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPretty good wanna be roguelite. At the time I bought it it was a rather unforgiving roguelite game with a lot of different mechanics and quite a few paths to go. A month after that they optimized the game to still be inforgiving if you are braindead, otherwise you roll through the encounters barely noticing any danger.

Don't get me wrong, the game is amazing but after difficulty adjustments it just became too easy. Before these it seemed a bit too hard. Overall I'd say it still is great but now after passing journeys 1-6 with each clan and completing 100 stages of "endless" with each, there's no much replayability except for hunting for achiements.

Sure, you may now try and complete the game with the least likeable characters but that's the most replayability (forced/artificial replayability, I might add) you may possibly find.

Nonetheless, I have 149 hours played so far and I'd say that I enjoyed the game 80% of that time, which immeadeately makes it worth buying, despite its current flaws.
The devs are quite active in the discussions and are seeking for the ways to make the game even better.

There are still some a) translation and b) UI issues but those are negligible comparing to how great the game is in its current state. I personally have reported a few in the discussions but overall the game's worth its price even before these are fixed (if ever).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is a bit of a mixed bag, so it's hard to decide if I recommend or not.

What I like:
The three different playable factions each have unique play styles and each faction has a large roster of different characters to use. Each faction has unique units and abilities, and each class within the faction has their own list of possible skills to choose from. Though units of the same class and faction, such as berserk units, have similar base abilities, each character is unique and both plays and functions differently from the others in its class.

Though some of the animations are a bit choppy, the game looks good. There's good variation in character design and being able to choose a random team of four when starting a run is a great addition. I also love that you can restart and retreat from stages without penalty, allowing you to choose the order in which you progress through stages.

The game is flawed, but relatively fun. It seems like the game is updated fairly frequently, so by the time you read this, many of the issues below may have already been addressed.

What needs improvement:
First and foremost, the full game is better than the demo, so keep that in mind. The demo and the main game are not exactly the same, there are small changes and differences between the two versions. From what I can tell, you also can't carry over progress from the demo, so keep that in mind if you were planning on dumping a lot of time into the demo before buying the game.

Once you reach the third difficulty and unlock the fourth area, the game can become considerably less fun. Some of the encounters become essentially impossible to beat. Some of the mission objectives in this game, instead of killing the enemy, require you to prevent the enemy from reaching a certain point or require you to reach a point before the enemy does. While variety is good, these levels are often frustrating garbage, ESPECIALLY in the fourth area. In one level, there are 4 bears (each with 3 movement, guaranteed stun attacks, and debuff immunity), as well as a giant field of frost that slows you down, between you and the exit. You have to beat 4 units (that have high mobility, ranged attacks, and attacks that place obstacles in your way) to the exit. It doesn't matter how strong your units are, without high movement, you're screwed.

There are plenty of glitches that affect core gameplay which I suspect will be ironed out with time. One ability is supposed to prevent damage of 5 and less, but it doesn't actually stop damage of 5. At least, it didn't in the demo. The Battlements ability is supposed to prevent enemies from retaliating when you attack them, but it instead prevents your unit from retaliating when attacked. I have no idea if these are translation errors or bugs as the game has its fair share of both, which, to be fair, is to be expected of early access games.

For the mixed elemental attacks, two of the fire combination effects are displayed as using the water element. While this could be intentional, my guess would be that oblivion blast is actually supposed to use ice instead of water, but I have yet to test this.

There are also issues with map interactions. For example, If you interact with the moundety spirit map interaction, you get the option to exchange one of your treasures for a random new one, or keep the relic you already have. If the game closes during this, instead of forcing you to keep the relic you already had or remembering what the random option was so that you can go back and do the exchange later, it just deletes the treasure that you already had and gives you nothing. Many map interactions give you the option to exchange/buy things like treasures or to level up your character, but the game unfortunately doesn't let you back out and look at the map or your characters while choosing.

When highlighting enemies, the game doesn't actually show how much damage you would do if you attacked because the damage indicators don't take into account ability effects. This isn't too big of a deal as, again, the game is in early access.

While some characters feel ridiculously strong, some of the characters, like many of the Ninelie scouts and some of the bomb characters, just don't feel worth using. If you can't bring a scout up to 5 movement, they aren't usually worth the investment. By the time you properly position your scout to backstab an enemy without leaving them vulnerable to attack, other units could have already killed the enemy twice over. It also feels weird to use my unit slots and character experience on characters that deal damage based on the number of enemies, which is useless in stages with spread-out enemies and in most boss fights. Most summon characters are also weak as their summons don't adequately scale with enemies. It sometimes doesn't feel worth it to invest in upgrading most of the scout characters when there are guards and warriors that can become virtually invincible with the right combination of upgrades and equipment. Only some of the scouts have the damage output/survivability to be worth using. when it comes to being overpowered, the stackable equipment that gives warriors +2 damage reduction, lone wolf, Ink Lotus, and Rosery, for example, might need to be nerfed a bit. Between armor and damage mitigation, it's not hard to make some units so strong that normal enemies just can't hurt them.

The volume is fine until you click on any of the interactions on the map. As soon as you do, you're greeted by deafeningly loud audio that's twice as loud as everything else.

Some of the enemies and bosses won't attack until you come within a certain distance of them, meaning that some bosses will just stand there and let you kill them. I built one of my characters with the Stasis Works, Crossbow, and Overload skills, and I literally spent the entire boss fight standing in one spot spamming end turn. I beat the boss without him ever leaving the center of the room or even getting a single attack off.

The game allows you to use your mouse scroll-wheel to scroll through your items and to scroll through the completion screen at the end of a run, but for some reason the scrolling is PAINFULLY slow.

The Bronion altar isn't really explained and, while I'm all for discovering things through trial and error, the worry that it may require me to sacrifice two of my units in order to use them has been cause for hesitation. I don't know if that is what is required to use the colossus, but if it does require you to lose your units, that would be something important to warn players about.

Though there is more about the game I could discuss, the last thing that I wanted to focus on was the story. I don't have any major problems with what little story there is, but the story progression feels somewhat underwhelming. I'm not someone who plays games for their story, but I have to admit, it feels a bit weird how inconsequential everything feels. You never really see the effects of your work. You go out to retrieve a tome of knowledge to help a clan with their forging, but nothing really comes of it. You retrieve a lost relic from guarded ruins, but you don't unlock the relic afterwards. The relic isn't even visible in the final boss arena or anything. As you play, you don't actually encounter anything during your run nor do you unlock anything afterward, that would tie the story to your in-game progression, so the story and the gameplay feel very disconnected. I love roguelike games, but adding one or two story-related interactions that are guaranteed to appear somewhere in the stages of a mission would go a long way toward tying the game and story together. A dedicated story mode could also be nice.

Regardless of its issues, the game has a lot of interesting game mechanics and a lot of potential. If you aren't sure whether you want to buy the game or not, you can always try out the demo.\t\t\t\t\t
