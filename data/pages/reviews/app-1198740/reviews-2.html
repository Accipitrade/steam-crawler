\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game is primarily tower defense. I'm not sure what the description means by "the best of RTS" because the RTS element is weak. Simply put, the S is missing from RTS.

UNIT BUILDING
You CAN build units, like in RTS games. You might need them for certain objectives in particular missions. However, they cost as much as towers and are much less effective. They're not especially useful for defending your base.

RESOURCES
RTS games include resource harvesting. Some have a degree of resource management. Every TD game I've ever played that includes resources gives them in two ways: post wave grants and amounts for enemies killed. Defense Grid also generates interest on unspent resources (which I don't recall seeing anywhere else).

This game uses the standard TD mode of resource generation. This isn't a problem in itself but it's not a part of the RTS aspect of the game.

BUILDINGS
RTS games have structures to build, like tank factories and starports. Colony Siege has some non-tower buildings you can build. RTS games generally let you build as many as you can afford anywhere you want. Colony Siege dictates the types, quantities, and placement of each building within a mission. The only thing the player gets to decide is if and when to build them. Given that one of the buildings increases the tower/unit capacity (which is shared) and another gives access to the tower upgrades, players will need to be building at least some of them. So, this isn't really an RTS component.

TECH RESEARCH
Not research per se. The player has the opportunity between missions to unlock new techs by spending credits. This is fine but there is a problem. The tech progression looks freeform at first glance. Unfortunately, there is an expected progression that isn't readily apparent until you're in a mission and lacking a tech you need. I saw this clearly in regards to bonus objectives (such as "build x of this mech"). The thing is, lacking that tech meant replaying past missions to get more credits to unlock the missing tech and then replaying the mission I couldn't get the bonus objective on to get the bonus objective. I dislike having to replay completed missions. It feels like artificially lengthening the game.

BATTLEFIELD TACTICS, FREEFORM BUILDING, TERRAIN USAGE
These three items are essentially one category because, in Colony Siege, it's all the same. There are two reasons.

First, Colony Siege follows the basic TD success formula: expose the enemies to your damage for as long as possible. Same basic principle as credit card interest. The longer you leave your credit card unpaid, the more interest you'll be paying. The longer the enemies are exposed to your towers' damage, the more damage they'll take. It's why the switchback pattern is so prevalent in TD games and it's why towers that slow enemies are so good, especially in an area around them (like the Temporal tower in Defense Grid). They keep all the enemies in their AOE within the damage zone of multiple towers longer. It's often more effective than trying to upgrade a bunch of towers early.

Second, the enemies' routing pattern means that blocking off all but one route and filling the remaining route with a switchback pattern of towers will beat the level every time. You'll just need to account for the mission bosses, which are much more powerful than the other enemies, and the fliers, which seem to have designated targets or target areas instead of routes. If you've upgraded your command ship appropriately, you'll be able to handle the fliers that ignore your towers without units or towers.

The switchback tower pattern means Terrain doesn't matter except for the narrow points on the routes you'll be closing off. It'll require fewer towers at those points. It's sometimes fun to watch a spring trap launch an enemy unit or two into a magma pool but it isn't really worth the cost of the trap for the effect, given how long it takes the trap to reset.

CONCLUSION
So, the Strategy part of RTS is either weak or missing. Otherwise, it's a standard TD game with a couple gimmicks tossed in.

It might be worth the $18 CAD it costs but I wouldn't pay more than that for it. Honestly, I'm not sure if I recommend it or not.

If you want a solid, challenging TD game, I recommend both Defense Grid games.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is pretty great. A co-op tower defence game where you can also build and command units like a lite RTS. You get a good amount of towers, units, buildings and other things to unlock as you progress and the levels give some flexibility as to how you approach them. Your ground units will only total about 15 though, so don't imagine any Total War scenarios.

The co-op works really well, I played with a friend and you can each choose to specialise in different areas such as the towers or the troops and command and unlock them as you wish. Or you could just both do everything, the game doesn't mind. The graphics aren't amazing but they get the job done, the units and turrets look cool but maybe some of the environments can look a bit sparse. When the game gets going however, there are plenty of baddies on screen and it all moves along very nicely.

I'm at 14 hours playtime now and think we're about 2/3rds of the way through, but we intend to play hard mode afterwards and do the levels again. Also there is an endless mode so there seems to be some replay value to the game too.

Overall this is a really enjoyable co-op tower defence game and I'd definitely recommend it to anyone looking for one.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMy Experience

My co-op partner and I had a blast with Colony Siege. There is something immensely satisfying about funneling enemies down a path lined with towers of increasing power, which is why we see more tower defense games released every year. And when a partner is present, the two of you can divide and conquer, focusing on different lines of enemies or different types of towers, etc. While Colony Siege also allows players to construct units RTS-style, we did this only to the extent that we needed to complete certain level challenges.

Meeting these challenges and taking advantage of the terrain makes players feel like tactical geniuses. Not only can you extend the length of the enemy path by taking advantage of topography, but you're often able to route enemies through environmental hazards that soften them up for your towers farther down the path.

In addition to making you feel smart, the game can sometimes feel like a satisfying "power fantasy." You'll unlock various "strikes" as you proceed through the campaign that allow you to rain artillery of various types from above, and in some levels you'll pilot a recovered ancient alien spaceship that absolutely levels any enemies that are unfortunate enough to find themselves in its way.

The only gripes I had with the title were:
  • I wish that there were more advanced platforms in earlier levels that would allow you to play them (perhaps in a new game+ mode) with the benefit of technology from later in the campaign.
  • Often there would be 1 or 2 fliers that would find their way to barriers you'd placed on the map. Although these fliers almost never presented a real threat to the barriers (at least on the normal difficulty), dealing with them sometimes felt like swatting aggravating gnats from your ears.
However, I wouldn't let either of these gripes dissuade you from purchasing the game and aiding in the defense of the colony. It's a blast, especially with a partner.\t\t\t\t\t
