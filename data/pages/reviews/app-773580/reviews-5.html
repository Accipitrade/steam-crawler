\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStandard disclaimer: Play clock inaccurate - offline time not counted.

There are a lot of game-development games out there these days. Most of them, however, feel like clones of one another: highly stylized, abstract, and in many ways pretty thin. For players wanting an actual game development business sim, there haven't been many good choices out there. Enter GameDevStudio, an actual nuts-and-bolts sim that offers a lot of latitude and options in developing the games that will make your company money. No abstraction, no stylizing, just a sim that lets you run a company designing and making games.

Unfortunately for those looking to scratch that game dev itch, this title is a case of "all the parts are there, but the assembly falls short."

The Good
There are good things to say about this game, and it really does deserve praise. In particular:

Making Games is Outstanding
The game design aspect of this sim is outstanding, and it's really what games like this should be. First of all, the sheer number of options given to the player is staggering. Genre/theme combinations are only a piece of the design options you're given. How that game is presented is entirely up to you: camera style, story design, audio design, voice acting, and so so SO much more. You can actually design a game! You have all the options at your disposal.
Game creation also feels good. It's not stylized, it's a work interface. No floating "bubbles" or "orbs" or other goofy displays of things happening: just a good old progress bar, process control diagram, and checklist. It's great! You actually get the impression that work is being done and can keep tabs on what's going on at every step. You can also influence the game during development in a few key ways, such as researching and resetting your price point, handling publicity, and more.
Frankly, it's probably the best process representation to be found in a game like this.

Office Development Has Creative Aspects
Along with the more usual, mundane things like putting in water coolers and sofas, you're able to actually affect your employees' ability to do their jobs with certain objects that you can tailor. Bookshelves, for example, aren't static items; you can decide which books will be stocked, which determines which skills it boosts and by how much. It's an attention to detail not often seen, and it shows development foresight.


The Problems
Sadly, there are, however, a number of serious problems that keep this from being the great game it could be.

Your Employees Are Worthless
There's a term to describe the people you hire in this game: former employees. People who just "don't feel like" doing a task because it's repetitive and actively work slowly if told to do their jobs tend to be demoted to unemployed in actual business. In this game, if you tell an employee to do their job doing anything other than developing a new game, they'll complain and work slowly. Since you only start with a few genres and themes, this means having to deal with their endless whining as you try to research new formats to build games in.

Graphics Are Just Bad
Usually I don't say a thing about graphics because they're unimportant next to gameplay. However, this is a profoundly unattractive game. Its 1990s aesthetic isn't a bad thing, but the sprites are of rock-bottom quality. The object thumbnails in the buy menu are frankly ugly and look too similar in many cases: that a door and a bookshelf look nearly identical is inexplicable. Faces of employees are deep in the Uncanny Valley. Unfortunately, things don't get better in the game world, with the entire presentation of the game looking like it's straight out of 1994... and not the high-end games, either.

UI Design is a Mess
Frankly, the UI design was pulled straight out of the 1990s. Menus are convoluted and tedious to navigate, tools are unintuitive (to move objects you need to be in... demolition mode?). There are multiple ways to do certain tasks, like rotate an object, which is usually good. The problem is that the number of options for one task rules out another - two mouse controls to rotate an object means dismissing an object in buy mode is a keyboard entry rather than simply a right-click like so many builder games. As an added bonus, rooms by default are dark - lights don't work when nobody is present - meaning you're doing your construction nearly blind. You can't override the lights, and heaven help you if night falls. All you can do is turn out all the lights in your actual room and squint at the screen as you try to figure out why you're getting warnings on a bookcase. No, this isn't a joke.
Navigation and camera control is a flat-out pain. Edge scrolling? Nope, use WASD or "grab" the map with the left mouse button and drag... which doesn't work when you're holding an object. Smooth zoom? Nope, you have to click zoom controls that are off in the corner and choose from one of five zoom levels ranging from "embedded in your employees' skulls" to "orbital view."
Finally, object management is crazy. This game has clearly never seen a modern office, where space is at a premium. Unless each desk has about two meters of clearance behind it, it's blocked. It doesn't matter if the "blocking" object is a whiteboard against a wall; if the employee can't do yoga at their workstation, they're "blocked." A coffee maker can't be in the same room as an air conditioner because... we don't know.

The Bottom Line
This is a game that seemed like it would be a walk-away winner. Unfortunately, with the exception of the detail put into actual game development, it was a tremendous letdown. The personnel problems, the UI, and the off-putting graphics were just too much for the extremely well-done game development aspect to overcome.\t\t\t\t\t

Overall, a great game with a lot of depth to the game development process.

The sheer amount of features is unrivaled by any other game in Game Dev Simulation genre. While not perfectly feature complete and smooth, still it is impossible to find similar game with equally deep game dev studio management and attention to detail.

There are some issues I have found so far though:
  • In all of my playthroughs all console companies went bankrupt, except for "Microsoft" before 2000. I tried to roleplay Naughty Dog multiple times and Sony went bankrupt always, sometimes in the middle of PS One lifecycle or right after releasing PS2. It is just too random and I have found no way to disable this and play more historically accurate playthrough
  • Engine development is currently lacking some depth. While in Game Dev Tycoon you were limited by (somewhat unrealistic to be honest) research points and there was a bit of thought process which features to research and later to implement, in this game I just mindlessly click the "Research all" button as soon as new techs appear. There is no interesting way to spend a lot of resources to create the perfect engine and sell the license to it, for instance. And therefore no need to license an engine yourself, since it is so simple to create your own. So as of latest patch, there is no real choice or tradeoff to engine development. Also, I have not found any need to create new and better engines, since it is possible to update the first one endlessly and revamp it occasionally.

Aside from aforementioned, the dev is very dedicated to improving the game and overall does a great job. I hope he keeps adding new features and improving existing ones. Good luck to him, I will be looking forward to updates and recommending this game to my friends.\t\t\t\t\t
