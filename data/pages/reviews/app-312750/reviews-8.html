\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI have really good memories of playing FFIV for the first time on the DS years ago.
I've been playing through the series recently, and I was super excited to get to IV again, back to that world I had so much fun with.

Regrettably, it's all wrong. This port is wrong on so many levels. From the choice to remove the stylized fonts in favor of a bland sans-serif, to the mobile-focused UI, to the frame rate (or lack thereof), to the fact that the screen is just zoomed in from the original aspect ratio rather than properly scaled... it's all wrong. The voice acting, characters, and story are all still here - but it isn't the same.

The original DS version hasn't aged well either, but this is just insane. There is a reason Square Enix is trying to bury this with the Pixel Remakes.

If you're looking to play FFIV for the first time, just go for the Pixel Remakes. You're doing yourself a disservice by playing this first. If you're looking to enjoy the DS version, maybe consider busting out your old handheld. If you have literally no other choice, I guess it serves its purpose. But know that you are getting a hollow experience.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe 3D remake of Final Fantasy IV, originally released on the Nintendo DS back in 2008 (2007 in Japan,) is a fine alternative for those who have experienced the original or Pixel Remaster and want to relive Cecil's adventure in another way; equally, it's a worthy introduction in its own right.

To call it 3D is somewhat misleading, of course. Yes, the world and characters are rendered in three dimensions, but we're still observing things from a fixed, top-down perspective, as in the original game. What this version does do is add a few new gameplay features, and rebalances certain mechanics to give a subtly different experience to its 2D counterpart. The player can obtain "Augments" throughout the game, these give party members new abilities and also boost certain stats upon levelling up. There is a New Game Plus feature, which allows you to carry over unused Augments from the last playthough; the second playthough will also contain two new superbosses. Finally, when ported to Steam, an incredibly important autosave feature was added, which makes the game a lot less frustrating. Since the game saves after every battle, getting a Game Over 45 minutes from your last save is now a thing of the past, and you can pause in a middle of a long dungeon without finding a save point if you need to stop playing. And of course, if you want to only use save points for the original experience, that option is still available.

As this remake was initially released as part of the 20th Anniversary of the Final Fantasy series, it received a fair amount of care and attention in the presentation department. The script was retranslated into English by Tom Slattery, who did a very thorough job - though somewhat flowery, it's a highly accurate translation, and true to the spirit of the original Japanese. Really, in what other game translation would you learn words like "Epopt" and "chiliad"?

The cutscenes are nicely directed, and the voice acting, though hampered by low audio quality, is very good also. You can hear the actors putting as much emotion as they can into the roles, which is very welcome. After all, this was arguably the first Final Fantasy that gave us strong characters with emotional arcs; the likes of Rydia, Cid, and Tellah are surely beloved by fans around the world, so it's great to hear actors do them justice. Lastly, the remixes of the soundtrack are all tastefully done: my favourites are "Rydia," "Troian Beauty," and "The Land of Dwarves."

I do have one problem with the game, and it's a big one, though probably something that only matters to a niche audience. It's the Steam Achievements. Put simply, to get every achievement in the game, you'll need to obtain rare items from defeating enemies. The drop rate (assuming you have the "Treasure Hunter" Augment)?... 1/125. The amount of items with this drop rate you need to collect? TEN. And these are not dropped by easy to find or farm enemies either. The equipment you gain from all this is hardly worth the trouble, since you'll only get it at the very end of the game, and the pieces can't be carried over to a New Game Plus. The long grind totally disrupts the flow of the story as well, making the ending a lot harder to enjoy, since you'll be totally burnt out by then. I get that this tedious extra content is intended only for the truly hardcore fans, who'll complete it on their very last playthrough and use the equipment to help them against the (equally tedious) superbosses. The problem is clearly on me, for wanting to get every achievement in every game, while not wanting to engage in something so obviously grindy and unfun. I still think this counts as poor design, however.

That said, unless you're an OCD achievement hunter, you'll find little to object to in this version of Final Fantasy IV. It's the same classic adventure with some major graphical upgrades and minor gameplay tweaks, which put together deliver a slightly different, but still pleasant experience. Call it a retelling, not a replacement; play the 1991 original if you want the history, and enjoy this one for what it is.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWhen I first tried playing this game years ago I remember hating the graphics and especially the character design. The pixel art of the original was low-fi but had all the more effort put into it because of it. This however, to me, just looks plain cheap. Years later, I thought "how bad could it be, I'll get over it".

The camera is zoomed in on the screen to a degree where you can't look at the screen and make a decision about where to go. You just walk in a direction to find what's there. Oh, nothing, okay. Gaming is about making meaningful decisions, and deciding where to go is one of the fundamental basics. And they messed it up. What's worse, they KNEW they messed it up, and thus provide you with a big map overlay that covers the entire screen. This is the way they intend for you to find your way. And so you end up barely seeing the actual game.

The game is capped at 30 fps. When you run around so zoomed in at 30 fps the npcs and objects start to get blurry. You stop to look at what's on the screen. And then you experience the delay from when you push to move your character until it starts running. It's small, but it immediately feels cumbersome.

The battles are capped at 15 fps. The game graphics already look terrible, but now they also move like stop-motion clay dolls. The worst of both worlds.

There might be an improvement to story and some fun deeper mechanics in there, but I will never find out. Just play a game that looks and feels good. This is probably the worst way to experience FF4.\t\t\t\t\t

There is one caveat to this game, and that is if you play in the original Japanese. You can play it with the workaround above, but all playable character names will be improperly converted and will look like semi-random text everywhere until you change their names via Namingway. However, the Deck has no built-in Japanese IME, so you can't rename garbled text to .

Other than that, this should be identical to the DS remake with maybe some bugfixes and minus the secondary screen. I would definitely play this as soon as someone allows Japanese input on the Deck or fix the name garbling.\t\t\t\t\t
