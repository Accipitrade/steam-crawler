\r\n\t\t\t\t\tRemakes are a tricky thing to review, ports of remakes doubly so. In my 3 hours with the game, and my experience with other games using this particular engine and my experience of the original final fantasy IV
Reviewing this game as a port of the DS remake of the incredible FInal Fantasy IV, it works. The User interface, while better than the Final Fantasy III port on steam, steal feels a tad like it was made for touch screens, and the font (although easily modded) looks very generic and basic - not what you would expect from a triple A franchise.
Reviewing this game as a remake - again it works. While not to everyones tastes, the 3D graphics are clean, and cute and while the textures can appear rough I think it adds to the charm. It is also worth noting that this is an extremely game to run because of the simple graphics. The voice acting is laughable at best, cringy at worst. It is clear to see that the budget was limited with this remake, but it is playable and enjoyable. I believe the engine was used for a few other DS Final Fantasy titles, all of which I have fond childhood memories of.
Finally, reviewing this game for what it is, Final Fantasy IV. It is incredible. Not only one of my favourite final fantasies but also one of my favourite RPG's. Play it. Now. Bear in mind, the original version of the game was known as final fantasy II in the west and included a few differences to the Japanese original to make it easier. I have played the original western version of this game and the main difference I've noticed is that instead of one cure all remedy item, there are multiple items for each different ailment. since this is a RPG trope, its not something I've particularly struggled with but something to bear in mind for other players who may be less experienced.
TL;DR - Please play this game, although depending on your tastes there are other versions out there that may be more enjoyable, such as the original and the PSP version. If you only have a PC and don't want to emulate or you really enjoy the visual style like myself you could do worse than to get this game\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA classic in every way among the pantheon of mainline Final Fantasy entries. This game has probably been ported more than any other FF game, yet no one is sick of it because it's just that good. This 3D remake remains faithful to the original in terms of mechanics and characters, but a few battles -- like Barbariccia -- differ significantly from the SNES version. Updated translation and such compared to the original (though it thankfully keeps Tellah's "You spoony bard!" line). I believe this was the first game to introduce the ATB system which became the basis of battle for several subsequent entries. I was especially impressed with the presentation. While the DS-era graphics largely remain, the added voiceovers to cutscenes in my opinion really add a great deal. Alongside this, the characters and world are realized incredibly well, and I especially enjoy seeing the main characters directly interact with each other rather than only the hero. The ever-changing party is well-rounded, and the rotating members force you to fight differently depending on who is joining you. There's no job system, so each character specializes in one class and they grow along that route. I actually prefer this to the entries which include a dizzying array of classes for characters to take up, which can lead to a feeling that you may be ill-equipped for the next step because you've chosen an ineffective class. It's not an overly punishing game, though of course some parts do require more strategy than others. The only thing to be aware of is the Augment system, which, if managed the wrong way, will result in you missing on some useful abilities. So I'd recommend following a guide about how to distribute them properly, as there's no way you could really know. Overall, this is just a really great game with solid mechanics, story, and presentation; while purists may not prefer this version, you really can't go wrong with it.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIn my ongoing and likely futile effort to write a Steam review for every game in my library (#219 out of 555)... it's time for Final Fantasy IV.

Fun fact: at one time Square-Enix meant to go back and produce faithful remakes of all their old games. They made it through exactly four before giving up, and as you might guess, Final Fantasy IV is the last of their efforts. I dunno about you, but it seems to me that they stopped before getting to the -really- good ones. Oh well.

So: what's the deal here? Well, this version of Final Fantasy IV is based on the Nintendo DS remake, which exchanges the old low-fidelity sprites and 2D backgrounds of the original SNES game for very crude 3D polygonal visuals, a new (slightly expanded) script, full voice acting, and a few adjustments beneath mention. Around the same time they also released a slightly -more- faithful remake, Final Fantasy IV: Complete on the Playstation Portable, which polished up the SNES games' visuals and also included the "After Years" sequel game. Naturally, Final Fantasy fans very quickly divided into camps based on which of the two new versions of Final Fantasy IV they thought was superior.

And they're fine. They're both fine. The PSP version was a bit more faithful to the original visually speaking, but the new 3D visuals in this version are a lot more engaging, as is the voice acting, both of which serve to make the (very basic) story hit significantly harder than it does in 2D. Cecil can only portray so much emotion through a sprite, after all.

As for the game itself, Final Fantasy IV is one of the foundational classics of the genre, and it's definitely a classic in both the good and bad sense of the word. It's an old game, and it shows its age, mostly with a very barebones and simplistic plot, that relies heavily on cliches. But you need to remember: back when Final Fantasy IV released, Japanese Roleplaying Games had only just been established as a genre. The idea of them being story-focused games had not yet emerged--as a matter of fact, the game largely credited with creating the JsRPG genre, Dragon Quest III, had must more in common with Western-style roleplaying games than what we now think of when we think of JsRPGs. And it was Final Fantasy IV that changed that, with it's cliched, but memorable tale of Cecil, the fallen paladin, and his saga or betrayal and redemption.

The verdict? Keep your expectations in check. Final Fantasy IV is still a very good game, and honestly it still holds up fairly well, but it's also very much a game of its time. If you're looking for a complex narrative with nuanced characters, you're not going to find them here. What you will find is a historical relic that still fun to dive in today, with or without aid of nostalgia, thanks to to the remake's charming attempt at localizing it. In terms of the classic Final Fantasy games (everything pre-Final Fantasy 7, essentially) this version of Final Fantasy IV is easily the most accessible to new players.
