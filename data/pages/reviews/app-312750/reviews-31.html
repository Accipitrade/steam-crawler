\r\n\t\t\t\t\tShortly after finishing Final Fantasy III (FFIII) on Steam, I continued straight into Final Fantasy IV (FFIV). This might have been a small mistake, as there might have been a little bit of Final Fantasy fatigue. That said, despite some similarities, including in terms of the 3D remake, FFIV does change its formula just enough to make the game stand apart from its predecessor. FFIV is an enjoyable Final Fantasy game for a solid 30 hours.

Gameplay
Like its predecessor (and most games in the series), you control a small party of characters that set out on a grand adventure and occasionally (scratch that... frequently) engage in turn-based combat with a variety of monsters and villains. The first major difference has to do with the availability of classes. FFIII came with a great deal of flexibility as you could change the class of any of your four characters. FFIV changes this formula by having predefined classes for all of its characters with a maximum party size of five. Although this is somewhat restrictive from a gameplay standpoint, the predefined characters seem more appropriate in terms of the story. The adventuring takes places on the world map, in towns, or underground in dungeons. As with FFIII, random encounters can occur in most outdoor and underground locations. For the uninitiated, the frequency of those random battles can be somewhat tedious.

Characters/Customization
While the flexibility of switching classes is gone in FFIV, the characters, their backstories, and ongoing stories are better fleshed out in this version. Your main character is Cecil whose story evolves through tragedy and triumph. Along the way, other characters join and leave his party. Whereas in other games you can control who you want to bring along, party membership in FFIV is fixed with storyline progression. In terms of customizing your characters, you pretty much only control the equipment they wear, although in practice even those choices are limited by what is optimal for each character or, in a few cases, based on the weaknesses of your adversaries. You also get to apply augments to characters that apply small passive bonuses and/or provide additional abilities.

Story/Atmosphere
The overall story of FFIV is not particularly more grand or impressive than FFIII; however, the uniqueness of each character and their backstory do help in making the story a bit more memorable than FFIII. Other than that, given the similarities in the remake graphics and character models, the general UI layout, and so forth, the differences between the games are minimal. If you liked FFIII, you will probably like FFIV, but if not... Although this is another "save the world" story, the atmosphere of FFIV never gets quite as grim as some of the subsequent games such as FFVI or FFVII. May also be worth noting that FFIV is a standalone game and not a sequel to FFIII; this is true for most Final Fantasy games: some gameplay concepts and lore (such as names of ancient beings) that gets rehashed from one game to the next, but that's about it.

Graphics/Sound
As with FFIII, FFIV has somewhat dated graphics but still suitable for a remake of a game from the 1990s. Music and sounds are also dated but appropriate for an early Final Fantasy game. I was surprised that some cutscenes had spoken dialogue, but I am not sure it was worth it, aside from highlighting the importance of certain milestones in the story.

Replay Value: Limited. There is actually a New Game Plus feature in this version, but I am not sure there is enough variety for a second playthrough.

Overall Score: 7/10. FFIV is still a solid, fun game, even if more elaborate systems and storylines are introduced later in the series.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you hate randomly getting screwed, avoid this. This game is all about the battle system, which is terrible. You need time to take in what's going on and make the right tactical choices, but A) the enemies fight on while you decide what to do, yet a delay of 1 second could ruin the outcome, and B) due to the UI it takes too long to select your actions. And the enemies do not wait while you are going through sub-menus. Oh, wanna cast Curaga on your party? Too bad, you got turned to stone while you were trying to get to that menu item. This snowballs into a disaster that would be comical if it wasn't enraging.

And don't count on the auto battle to help, you can only set an auto action out of combat and you cannot change it during combat. In FFIII, auto was way better because it repeated the last action you did on each character. In FFIV = Oh, you set Rydia to auto Firaga? And then got enemies who absorb fire? Guess you can't use auto in this fight, hah! And auto sometimes turns itself off between combats, so you're sitting back letting auto do what it may, but it's actually off so you're just getting pounded while doing nothing.

The solution to ALL this would have been very simple, just make it turn based like in FFIII. But no.

This is a huge annoyance because you're lucky to go 10 seconds without a combat when you're outside towns or castles. This is not an exaggeration. It might be as little as 4 seconds. If you do get lucky, you might get 15 seconds between fights. And in some areas you have to retrace your steps a lot to fully explore, as there are many dead ends, divided maps where you have to go to another level to find a way to the other portion of the current level, etc. So, the bad combat system will be shoved down your throat all day.

I can't take it any more. I'm close to the end, but the game has nothing more to offer me but rage. And the story isn't up to par with other FF games. I felt nothing for this trite melodrama. Bye FFIV!\t\t\t\t\t
