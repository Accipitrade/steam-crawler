\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis version of Final Fantasy IV is like the Crash Bandicoot 4 of Final Fantasies. Let me explain;

Final Fantasy IV was an excellent Super Nintendo game that translated well enough in all of its subsequent re-releases. It doesn't matter what platform you play this game on, it's a fantastic example of the JRPG genre (except maybe the Playstation port, which has annoyingly long load times but that's not relevant here so never mind). Similarly to Final Fantasy II, IV raises the stakes throughout by introducing characters who rotate into and out of your party, some of whom seemingly perish. The story is also fairly familiar, centering around a powerful kingdom forcibly taking crystals of power from neighboring kingdoms for reasons that are to be revealed. Unlike previous Final Fantasies though, this one does a much better job with characterization and fleshing out the party members, both temporary and long-running. There are a handful of tropes used that I don't really care for, such as heel-turns and revelations about character relations. There's also the familiar Final Fantasy storytelling trope of there being a higher being causing the majority of the drama, but unlike Necron, the main antagonist is fairly well telegraphed by the third act.

As for gameplay, this is where the Crash Bandicoot 4 comparison comes in. It doesn't matter if you're on easy or hard mode; the random encounter rate is high, so resources like HP, MP, and restoritives are definitely going to feel like they run dry quickly depending on your party makeup and which encounters you run into. If you're interested in completing all of the side-quests and unlocking all of the achievements you'll need to collect rare items including several tails, 4 hidden summons, and an item called rainbow pudding. Some of these items can drop from enemies you encounter very early on in the game, but what's stopping you from getting them despite the high encounter rate is a pair of dice rolls: the first being, will an item drop and the second being, *which* item a monster *can* drop, will it drop. In the case of the Rainbow Pudding for example, the overall odds of that dropping can range from 2% (if you have a special ability called Treasure Hunter, which can be missed and doesn't unlock until late in the game) to as low as .04%. If more than one item dropped per enemy encounter, that issue might be somewhat reduced, but that's not what we have here.

Whether or not you want to collect all of the items, if you want to fight either of the super bosses in Final Fantasy IV you need to beat the game once and make it quite far into a second playthrough to fight one of them, and you need to steal an item from the final boss if you want to fight both.

Final Fantasy III 3D was also a challenging game that required players to grind if they wanted to unlock all of the achievements, but with that game it was possible to calculate exactly how long it would take to complete the game. The RNG elements of this game take away that roadmap, potentially making the process of completing this game longer than a standard playthrough, or two, or three.

Do I recommend this game? Absolutely
Even if you own the Pixel Remaster? Absolutely
Even if I want don't want to complete it 100%? Absolutely
