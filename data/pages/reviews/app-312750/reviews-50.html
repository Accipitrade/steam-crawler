\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis was a very tough game to review...

Music
-This game's music is wonderful. I love it. I caught myself smiling and humming along with the music in dungeons, battles, towns, castles, airships, etc. Nobuo Uematsu wrote the music for this game, and it is GOOD. If it's not your cup of tea, then of course that's fine, but I loved it and just cannot stress enough how great this stuff is for music without vocals. Love it. Thank you for this soundtrack, Mr. Uematus. So. Good.

Controls/Porting/Technical Aspects
- This is a port of the Nintendo DS version of the game. I never played the DS version before. I'd only played the U.S. Super Nintendo version and the ported U.S. Super Nintendo version that was ported to the Nintendo Wii later on. It plays great, I had zero crashes, there are very few controls which you can remap, everything worked wonderfully, and yeah not much else to say here. Good stuff.

Story
- Okay, I can't ignore this. The story in this game is pretty awful. It starts out okay, but by the time you go underground, the story has devolved into "get a crystal, friend tries to sacrifice himself, get a crystal, friend tries to sacrifice himself, and so on," and sometimes they switch the order up a little so you might get two crystals before a sacrifice. I almost want to admit to being facetious, but this is kinda exactly the plot. =/

Let's see, there are a couple of villains. One has some plot reveals later on, but they're pretty cliche and you don't learn it until maybe 2-4 hours before you finish the game. The main villain is evil just because. If you've seen a terrible Final Fantasy game villain, you've seen them all: bad dialog that's beneath "so bad it's good" quality, visually appealing design, and no believable or interesting motives for wanting to do terrible things.

Speaking of plot reveals, the biggest pieces of the game's storyline are revealed...I'd say less than 5 hours before you finish the game, and I'm including a lot of grinding and covering the whole map in my time estimation. It wasn't a very satisfying reveal for me, and you drip-feed interesting tidbits along the way when your game takes a while to finish. Sure, it's not 100 hours or anything, but I clocked in a little under 40 hours on my in-game clock when I finished, and they crammed about 45% of the game's "surprise reveals" and background information into the final 10% of the time the player is playing.

What I mean is things happen all throughout the game. "Events" are everywhere, but lore reveals that matter, background info, teases about the main storyline, and even good interpersonal dialog between characters are rather few and far between. Instead of drip-feeling delicious little hints about the plot and lore to get your brain trying to figure things out, things just happen in the game until the game dumps basically the entire plot into your lap a few hours before you're done. I am unsure if I've ever seen a plot more poorly paced plot post-NES in a video game.

Also, there's a lot of prophecy and prayer in this game. First off, the prophecy is absolute bollocks, talking about "birthed from dragon's maw" or some garbage. Much of the prophecy is stupid but it's written in quasi-Olde-English, so you're supposed to think it's brilliant when the moon is mentioned in the prophecy and the moon is important to the plot later. as for prayers, all sorts of people pray, but no one really says who they're praying to. It doesn't seem to be God, or a deity in that world. One character say "pray to the moon" within the final 15 minutes of the game, but it seems like no one else in the entire world knows of the moon's relevance beforehand, so it doesn't explain the other prayers earlier in the game, such as your healer's, Rosa. This game's lore could really have used some love to be at least decent.

Before I finish talking about the plot, I would like to say that I really like most of the characters, though one character gets mind-controlled...like a lot, and it got really old. Another character joins your party so late in the game and with so little major dialog and party "banter" that I never really felt like I knew who he was until I was watching the ending, and that's too late. Cecil, Palom, Porom, Rydia, Rosa, Tellah, Edward, Yang, and Cid, on the other hand, were mostly very shallow characters in terms of depth given to them in writing, but I liked them and their personalities and such stood out in a good way for me. I think this version has dialog that wasn't in the U.S. Super Nintendo version, though there are still spelling errors as early as the first boss fight (Mist Dragon intro). Thankfully, the phrase "spoony bard" is still there. though. ;)

Graphics
- Some folks may not like the art style they chose for this game. I think the art style was perfect, given the often cutesy/silly tone of the game and some of the characters and their banter. I would've certainly been on board with better graphics, but this was just fine. I really liked the look, and the zoomed-in look as not a problem at all because of the map. Next up...

The Glorious Map
- You know how screenshots make this game look super zoomed in? And you know how the Super Nintendo version looks super zoomed out? If you think ahead, you'll say "I'll miss all the secrets because I won't spot them!" Oho, this game has a map overlay you can bring up while you run around, and it fills in where you've been, marks opened and unopened treasure chests, and shows the % of the map you've revealed and can easily be used to help scout for secret areas. I loved this map.

I was prepared to hate this game due to not being able to find secrets as I thought the zoomed-in camera would be a problem, but instead, I loved this aspect of the game much better than the Super Nintendo version, which I had a harder time finding my way around in and locating hidden goodies. Be prepared to find pretty much all the hidden gear in the game because this game makes it very easy if you just use a teensy bit of effort and the sweet maps.

Gameplay
- The game plays almost identical to the U.S. SNES version, and I love that about it. You have the biggest party size of any numbered Final Fantasy game in existence (5 people in battle at once). The game gives you numerous characters who come and go throughout the story, thus giving you a variety of different party builds to try out and have to adjust to. It works great and helps make you a better player by making you adapt instead of making you choose the best party and never using anyone else.

Character Progression
- I loved seeing all my stat changes each time I leveled up and I love how each character is a "class" instead of everyone having the same/similar stats and feeling so samey. However, from level 71-99, you get NOTHING for leveling up unless you equip an augment: a bonus item you get in a number of ways which are typically either hidden very well or obtained by giving these rare augments to other characters before they leave your party. They often completely throw off the balance of the game (100% counter attach rate, cast 2 spells per turn, penetrate reflect with spells, and the list goes on). If you want them, you will likely need to follow a walkthrough to know which ones to give to which characters, and when/where to get them because most of them are very easily missable.

The game doesn't even tell you which stats each augment will boost, nor does it tell you that augments are what increase your stats after level 70. If you didn't equip them or look at a walkthrough, you would never have any idea as to why you were getting no attribute gains or HP/MP at each level up. This made the game far too walkthrough-reliant.

I had some serious issues with the game, but do recommend it, though only marginally, thanks to good gameplay, amazing music, and improved visuals. Screw augments, though.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGood but flawed port of the DS version which remade the game with 3D graphics. If you haven't played FF4, it's a solid RPG which marks the point in the series where they started putting effort into the story. This version cleans up a lot of outdated elements that didn't age well. The encounter rate is still kind of high, but battles are quick. There's still some places where it'll sucker punch you like old RPGs like to do, but you can continue from a game over now so it's less punishing. This is probably one of the best iterations of FF4 out there despite having some issues.

+ best translation of the script by far
+ tons of playability improvements, e.g. submaps, quicksaves, continue, autobattle
+ expanded and voiceacted cutscenes (a bit hammy at times)
+ some additional content; does not include the extra dungeons present in some versions
- 15 fps cap in combat, suggest hex editing to raise it to at least 20 (see guides)
- can't cast weapon magic from item menu due to an oversight; have to waste a skill slot for it
- jagged unpolished character models, clipping, some graphical noncohesiveness
- camera often zoomed in way too close, slanted camera view hides details

Probably between 20 and 30 hours for an average playthrough. New Game+ is available if you want more.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI never played the original NES version of this game when it came out in the 80's, but my brother did. He was old enough to play and follow the entire Final Fantasy series from the 1st to XV. When talking about video games the other day, he was astounded that I never played this game and instantly had me pull out my steam account and download it.
Upon the first few minutes of the cinematic opening, we both said at once, "They're talking!?"
For me personally, I've found the game very fun, and destpite never playing the orignal, have really enjoyed the remaster with its added cinematics and voices. It's been interesting to play through a game that broke the mold of storytelling back then as well as helped define the roles and abilites of RPG characters. My brother has shown some dislike because of the loss of some of the abilities like "duping" and some other cheat mechancis. Maybe I haven't figured it out yet, but like I told him, I can't hate losing what I never experienced. And so far, it hasn't hindered my experience or enjoyment of this game.
As far as the chibi art style...I don't mind it. I think it kind of goes with the story and doesn't take away from it. And yes, the world might be small and the story and characters are very predicatablea and cliche...for this day and age. Keeping into account this game came out nearly thrity years ago, this was a very compelling fantasy/adventure story of its time. However, I think the upgraded graphics and audio do a good job of bridging that gap. All in all, I think the remake of this Final Fantasy game was well done, a great salute to a great game, and a need-to-play game for any fan of the Final Fantasy franchise.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFinal Fantasy IV is a great game. This port however is an embarrassment - I can not bring myself to defend it.

A quick bit of history, this is a PC port of a phone port of the (excellent) DS remake of the original SNES game. What I wanted was just to be able to play my DS copy on my TV. With this I got that... and some further changes.

The aspect ratio, I've not seen anything like this done by a developer of SE's caliber. The 4:3 aspect from the DS, in order to fit a 16:9 display, has had the top and bottom cut off and then black bars added to the side to fill the rest. The result is that you actually see less than you do on the tiny DS's screen, and that animation can actually move large creatures and bosses awkwardly off the top of the screen a little - you can notice this early on with the Mist Dragon's idle animation.

The UI is horrendous. It is the phone UI with its big round buttons and overinflated scale that takes up half the screen in battle. Important if you're trying to mash your finger onto a screen to perform an action but an eye-sore on PC. Icons are scaled up and bilinearly filtered - resulting in a big ugly smeared and edge-clipped hand cursor in the menus. The characterless arial font that they've replaced the DS's font with is even poorly clipped itself with little white dots appearing between certain arrangements of letters. A real disregard shown for any polish.

There is a gameplay adjustment as well. An auto-save before every battle. So, now, gone is the whole point of dungeons in a game like this; where you would struggle on to the next save/rest point, the compromises you might have to make and rationing of items and MP. Now, if you fail, you're simply started right where the battle last began, to continue on as if nothing had occurred.

Through that hacky aspect-ratio induced letterbox and under all that horrible phone UI... is still the wonderful game of FFIV. What a port though...\t\t\t\t\t
