\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI absolutely loved this game and would highly recommend it to anyone who enjoys mysteries!

I played the original Head as Code last year and loved it even then. I wish I had had the patience to wait for this remake, because there are massive improvements in visuals/music/UIs. The new CGs are absolutely gorgeous, and the music provides the perfect sense of foreboding. There was clearly a lot of care and attention to detail placed into this game by its creator.

While similar in setup to other ontological mystery games like Zero Escape, HASC manages to stay fresh and surprised me with its twists and reveals. When I originally played, I had several different theories going, and it was great seeing how different the truth was from what I expected.

The ending was extremely satisfying in its explanation and execution, and I would say is one of my favorite aspects about the game.

I enjoyed the characters quite a lot, but I would say one downside for me is that not everyone gets a good character arc. To be fair, I also have this complaint with the original 999, but still, I wish that some characters got fleshed out more.

One other downside would be the lack of gameplay. There are active search segments and a few puzzles, but the interaction from the player is pretty minimal. Mainly, you click on items and the characters solve it for you. The original HASC had more puzzles, and I wish this were something kept in the remake. I will add there is still player input in terms of choices and deciding which storyline branch you’re pursuing.

Still, the positives of this game far outweigh any negatives. If you are on the fence, give the game a try! This game is a part of a series with Birth Me Code and a third title that’s not yet released.

Despite Simon not clicking with me for a lot of the ride, the mystery and flow of the plot and other characters definitely made up for it once I got into things

Overall, the presentation and music is very good. The CG art looks especially nice and the UI is very useful for indicating what you've already looked at. My only issue is that, unless I missed the button, you cannot access the story's flowchart from in-game, you need to go back to the main menu which felt a tiny bit clunky. I ​also feel like this visual novel respected my time for the most part with it's fluid pacing which I very very much appreciate

Another thing that will either be a pro, con or non issue depending on your perspective is that there aren't really puzzles for the escape rooms like you'd see in Zero Escape - instead, you're given Active Searches where you can explore various objects in the room and if there's a puzzle, Simon basically solves it by himself and tells the player what happened. I'm not super upset about this because I generally use a walkthrough for these parts in other visual novels and I know this game was made by a very small team. It does however mean that you'll want to go into this game for the story and not gameplay elements

Overall, I'd give this a solid 7 or 8 out of 10\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI do think coming to this game after playing the second in the series didn't really matter or spoil anything for me in the long run, other than one character's presence which will be obvious fairly immediately. It's taking its cues from a lot of Kotaro Uchikoshi plots, though most specifically the Zero Escape series again. The "death game" of this one isn't so much a "death game" as it is "a game where people happen to die" though it definitely still follows the same style, multiple endings, lots of gruesome deaths, and the like.

As far as the gameplay goes... all the puzzles in this one are basically just "click everything to eventually win", I think there's exactly one puzzle in the game that requires a modicum of thought that the game won't just solve for you if you don't get it correct right away. Which is fine, I guess, it's not so much about the puzzles overall anyways, but the amount of interactivity is low, and the flowchart, while expansive, is this time traversed simply by multiple choice decisions at specific parts of the story, rather than the interesting & complex progression system the second game has. Which, fair enough. This one does feel a bit more fast-paced in general - very little downtime between more exciting scenes, and shorter scenes in general - this took me less than half the time to complete as the second game, notably - and it ultimately does end up feeling a bit short, but not unsatisfying.

I think one thing this game has that the second didn't was at least some degree of closure, as in, the game doesn't really have a "sequel hook" the same way the second game does. I don't know if it's a fully "satisfying" ending but I don't think it was intended to be, and while there are -some- unanswered questions they're mostly minor and/or answered by the overarching plot that game 2 goes deeper into anyways, so.

Presentation-wise it's pretty solid, the character art is all generally good, the music sets the mood well though it does reuse tracks quite often, the environments are pretty nice (though the one metro station scene that gets repeated very, very often by nature of the game makes the whole game feel very "sterile" in its presentation). No real gripes on this front though.

I do ultimately wish this was a bit longer or had more to its puzzles, and while, again, I wouldn't say it's an all-time great in the "death game VN" genre it's still a pretty enjoyable read, and one that's not inclined to waste your time while still having interesting characters and a compelling narrative, and I respect that a lot.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game wears the Zero Escape title on its sleeve. If that doesn't turn you away or sell you on the title, nothing else will. With that said, it's a competently put together game. It's got a good level of polish to it, an interesting presentation, and manages to be more of a hook the longer you play. I played this game with a few friends and we were chatting a lot, and it was honestly a lot of fun. We got increasingly into calling twists and trying to figure out what was next. We even succeeded a few times! I wouldn't exactly call it a party game, but hey. It was a fun couple of hours to kill.

It's not as good as 999 or VLR, but it's very unfair to hold it to such heights. It's good on it's own and has a satisfying, interesting conclusion to it. It's very obviously a title preparing for a series and there's no masking that. Almost in spite of itself, though, it's still got a finished feel to the end. That's unique amount series titles, where you feel like you can walk away and go 'yeah, this was enough for a complete story'. It does that. It's probably the highest possible praise you can get for a title in the series.

The only issue is that the 20$ is probably a bit high, but I hardly know how to price games. I feel like a 15$ would be fair and anything below that, honestly, a steal. Music's a bop, characters are fun and memorable, and it's almost a shame it is so heavily 999-based. If I hadn't played 999, this would've been excellent. As it is, I feel like I've been spoiled with something incredible and now it's hard gushing about something that was merely good and well put together. Yet on the flipside, if you haven't played 999, how would you ever know about HASC? Chicken and the egg. So, I'd honestly recommend it as it stands. Indie dev with a competent product? If 999 appeals to you, this is an excellent way to burn twelve hours.\t\t\t\t\t
