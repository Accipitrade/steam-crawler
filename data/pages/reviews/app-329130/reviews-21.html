\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn excellent game, well worth the price. Reassembly is a building-focused space combat "massively single-player" game, where you select a faction, spawn a galaxy, and are free to go about that sandbox as you please. Don't be fooled, this isn't an in-depth space sim like Freelancer or anything in that vein. If you're expecting negotiations, diplomacy, relations, or anything in that vein, you will be disappointed.

Abandon reason, know only war. If they aren't flying your colors, they are hostile. Exterminate the opposition and be the last one standing.

But Reassembly isn't a game about that. It's a game about building the coolest damn spaceship of them all and mashing them together and watching the fireworks. The building mechanics are slick and intuitive. I didn't think I would enjoy it, seeing as how I have a single brain cell to spare, but soon enough I found myself spending hours and hours making dumb ship designs to throw into Tournament mode (what if I built a ship with only torpedoes? Answer: you get frustrated because it can't aim, and then forget how frustrating it is when it lands a salvo and chews through a battleship like a hot knife through butter.)

There are some who say there isn't a whole lot to the game, and my response to that is it's really a game where you make your own fun. I wish there was a multiplayer element, among some other things - as fun as it is fighting your friends' Agents in Tournament is, it's very clunky. I've had a lot more fun building up fleets with differing capabilities and watching them clash in Tournament mode, but maybe you'll have more fun building an unstoppable juggernaut and plowing a path of destruction through a galaxy.

It's an outstanding game, and one I heavily recommend. Seriously, give it a try. The gameplay is slick, the soundtrack is soothing, and the visuals are fantastic. Other people have written much more eloquent and detailed reviews than I have, so I'll just leave you with this.

If you want to watch warships broadside each other with plasma cannons while hundreds of missiles race out and dive into flashing screens of laser point-defense, this is the game for you.

...

Also, there's a ♥♥♥♥-ton of mods in the workshop that add on every kind of faction you could ever want. So if you want to see something but it's not in the base seven factions, well, give that a try. I know I've spent far too long watching ADS Apollo-class Destroyers split ships ten times their mass down the middle with a gigantic railgun.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReassembly seems to be pretty niche game as far as I can tell, but it's actually quite fun and ended up becoming my time vortex for a while.

The game is essentially based on the "Reassembly" mechanic, hence the name. Ships are designed by connecting hull shapes and prefabs to each other, and the blueprint is then assembled by each of these pieces materializing one at a time in 'chains'. When a ship suffers damage, the lost pieces begin to reassemble in a similar way.

Stations also exist, which are designed like ships but lacking thrusters. These remain stationed in the sectors in which they are built and use ship-delivered resources to continually produce new ships using factories. Some of these ships can include factories themselves, which generally means that the rate at which ships are created increases exponentially.

If you are piloting a ship with at least one factory, nearby ships that do not have factories will transfer their resources to you for your own use. You can also choose to transfer resources to them for use by their factories. You may then decide which of your ships you wish to produce, and begin doing so at your own will. The ships that are produced will remain under your command, although you can dismiss them at any time or recruit other nearby ships using a credit-like currency if you do not wish to produce them yourself.

The units you command will continuously accompany you but in the meantime also perform duties that they are designated to fulfill, which is decided based on their physical designs. Essentially, a ship designed for combat will always remain near you, whereas a ship equipped with cargo containers will collect nearby resources and transfer them to you or to other ships or stations with factories, unless they have a factory of their own in which case they will also produce their own ships.

It is possible to command fleets up to about 60 ships in size, and in my experience this also has no performance drawbacks at all. This means you can lead massive swarms of fighting ships into combat, which is especially enjoyable to watch when attacking agents (naturally occurring, similarly large hostile fleets built by other players).

Despite the reassembly mechanic, poorly equipped ships can easily be destroyed in combat. You will generally need to retreat from any hostile zones in order to fully recover from damage. I've found that it's effective to produce large fleets of unshielded, relatively small fighters, and command them in a larger, shielded ship while remaining in the back line.

If you decide to do this, I would also recommend using the 'Engage my Target' fleet order, ensuring to find a new target after the previous one is destroyed by your fleet. This order causes all units you command to aggressively pursue and attack your targeted enemy, much more so than the 'Fire at Will' order in which they will instead hold their position near you and simply fire on nearby enemies. The downside to this is that they will obviously only engage your target, but somehow this usually doesn't seem to be much of a problem (at least with very large fleets) as long as you continue acquiring targets. The 'Disengage' order can also be used during a retreat.

Overall, it's definitely an interesting game and one I would recommend you purchase (perhaps on a sale if you mind the price tag). The only slightly aggravating thing I've come across in the game is enemy ships that have 8-10 large thrusters in each direction, giving them superb acceleration and the ability to even evade ships with afterburners. This can be frustrating as you have limited attack options when getting close to them is nearly impossible. To be fair though these are player designs and can't really be 'moderated', if this were to be considered a real issue.\t\t\t\t\t
