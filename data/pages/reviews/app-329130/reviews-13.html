\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAddictive top-down spaceship shooter and spiritual predecessor to Nimbatus (very similar game I have yet to purchase) as well as Istrolid (free) that is easily moddable through the workshop.

First, Pick a faction. Each faction has unique attributes to it, such as used weapons, playstyle, size, health, etc. and more factions can be added using mods (some of the unplayable factions can also be unlocked using mods).

After that, you can build a ship and start fighting. Building is quite easy as most blocks have a few snap-points that just snap to other blocks. You can then put more blocks, guns, thrusters, armor, force fields, resource storages, and lots more on your ship. There’s a wide variety of fun weapons to try out. You can even attach a special part to your ship that allows you to spawn your own ships directly into your fleet at the cost of resources.

Fight and destroy other ships to get resources. Sell resources to space stations. Use money to upgrade your ship, buy new equipment for your ship, or hire nearby AI-controlled ships to become part of your fleet.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHello again, friends and comrades! After owning Reassembly for a number of years, I thought I'd finally get around to making a review on it (as Steam keeps insisting I do). For the TL;DR, suffice it to say, this is one of the most peaceful, beautiful single-player experiences one can face on Steam. Artful, stunning, and tranquil amidst the void, Reassembly is one of the most serenely enveloping games I have ever owned.

Now, if you want to read further...

The game is intrinsically simple. A player chooses from one of several factions (unlockable by defeating especially large ships) and is then thrown into a two-dimensional, vector-drawn universe of polygons, angles, and mesmerizingly pulsating lights. Just the look of the game is a signature of brilliance; for its ships are inherently unique and yet stunning to view in action, its particle effects are statisfying every time they fire, and though the environment has its own special solace when one is alone, the world truly comes alive with motion and the bee-like movement of galactic civilization once one enters an area with other ships. The presentation isn't nearly done, however. The game's UI is reminiscent almost of Asteroids, with simple and elegant markers denoting everything you need between the resource you collect, the research points you have to spend, and your location in the universe. Menus are sleek and simplistic, button clicks are satisfying and well-designed, and the ship builder (which I shall return to later) is remarkably intuitive.

Now; for the sounds. Yes, oh, yes, the sounds deserve a paragraph all of their own. Reassembly's music is an anxiety-melting symphony of synths and echoing noises, reminiscent perhaps of Minecraft or Celeste in sound design. The OST is something you hardly notice, yet that is entirely its purpose - drawing you in subconsciously with the plucking of electronic strings and the sigh of electronic voices among its geometric world. Sound effects in the game are muffled, giving it a serene 'underwater' feeling and selling its setting in space while keeping them from being jarring or otherwise startling even when they do fire off at their most abrupt. Thrusters make an almost symphonic hum as they fly, whereas weapons are percussion pulses of plasma and softly-grinding drones of lasers and the vibrant 'thrum' of flying missiles among a bassline of firework-like explosions.

Now that I've rattled on about sound for as long as I reasonably can, it's time to delve into mechanics. In Reassembly, you collect points, simply called 'P', by destroying other ships, exploring the universe, investigating strange objects, and harvesting naturally generating plants as they die. From there, you can either feed it to factory-bearing ships for research points to unlock new blocks and devices (called 'R'), or if you have a factory yourself, you can use it to build your own fleet! And that's it. No; I'm serious. That's all this game needs to be as engaging as it is. Due to the sheer variety of the faction starcraft and fighting agents and the challenge of staying alive in a hostile universe, this single gameplay cycle is utterly absorbing and engaging for hours on end. Plus, it feeds the best part of the game, the ship designer!

Now, when most people think ship designer, they think of something like Space Engineers or Airships: Conquer the Skies. A grid (or, if we are so bold, even a VOXEL grid) of square modules and pipes and systems interlocking and interlacing at right angles (or other slightly more interesting angles if one is willing to spend countless hours fiddling with joints, rotors, and bugs). In Reassembly, that norm is thrown right out the window. All parts, all modules, all of EVERYTHING, is set on a dynamic, geometric system of interconnecting nodes which can fit literally anywhere one pleases! Not only does this serve as an exemplar in creative shaping and arrangement, but it also means that players practically have the capability of developing any ship they please (within 2D)! While the game has no dedicated rotating joints, it certainly doesn't need them - as it would otherwise the beautifully and elegantly utilitarian nature of its design.

In short, Reassembly is not a game for those who want goals. Rather, it is a game for those who want to spend some hours sitting back, absorbing themselves in content and the fireworks of galactic war as ship modules shatter from ship modules, angles snap to angles, and the stars fall under their grasp through trial, error, and the safe knowledge that there is no requirement but patience and a willingness to let the need for some final objective go.

There are few games I would dream of calling a work of art. Reassembly is a game that I declare not merely an artwork; but a masterclass of design.

10/10, would assemble again.\t\t\t\t\t
