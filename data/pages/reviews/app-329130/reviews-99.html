\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's Reassembly:
Customizable Spaceships,
Maybe look at guides.

Reassembly is an interesting 2D sandboxy game where, simply put, you make spaceships out of varying sets of shapes and modules in an attempt to devour all other living things in the galaxy and unleash an age of your own reign. Or you could just get the second playable faction and make giant solar panel stations. Doing that also works.
Anyway, I would suggest playing Reassembly if you like to create pretty things, functional things, or just spacey things in general. I personally like it for the large amount of customization which can be expanded even further with (apparently) easy modding that I have yet to dip my toes into.
One of the few downsides I can think of is the fact that the various "factions" are limited to specific sets of blocks instead of it being all blocks at once, but that kind of makes it more balanced, otherwise you would end up having giant solar panel stations with nuke all over them.

As an added note about the last line of that haiku, I would suggest reading through some guides if you're having trouble at first. Unless it's just really not your cup of tea, it will probably get fun eventually.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love this game. Spent hours, no days building and optimizing spaceship designs. This is a designer's game.

Pros:
-The controls are flexable so you can fly however you want, a very welcome feature in these top down 2D games.
-The attention to detail in physics is incredible, e.g. if your ship has no off-axis thrusters, it turns really slowly.
-Open ended - no one strategy to dominate. You can go for speed, armor, shields, range, or build cost. Or you can just be a farmer or a gatherer.
-Multiple races - just when you think you've optimized a ship series for a particular race, you can play a different race with different abilities and different parts!
-Really pretty
-Self-building ships: the trouble with other building games (e.g. StarMade) is that you always have to rebuild your ship after a battle. In this game you don't worry about that, although there are design strategies for having your ship regenerate faster.
-Good combat AI, so you're not frustrated with the AI pilots being idiotic which breaks many other games.
-Ability to customize the build sequence of allied ships and stations

Would like to see:
Fleet vs fleet battles with the asynchronous mulitplayer! There are ways to do this with the sandbox but it's not very accessible.
Better (maybe customizable) combat AI: e.g. turning the ship to hide damaged armor or shields from enemy fire while it regenerates, or maybe being able to specify a preferred range of engagement.
Better non-combat AI: Having AI ships being more exploratory would be nice, otherwise they exhaust their local resources and just end up sitting around.

My dream is to build an agent fleet that can go into someone else's universe, replicate like crazy and be hugely successful, and completely take it over. Muhahahaha.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSometimes, two children building spaceships out of LEGO will be in the same room and without adult supervision. They will probably start by dogfighting their plastic creations, but sooner or later they will be taking turns to throw each spaceship into the other. This is the game of those models.

Reassembly's ship creation is simple but pretty intricate in its outcome - you strap 2D weapons, components, inert hull pieces and engines to your command module until you're happy with the result. Ships can be any size, shape, scale (ships can be worth 50 points or 8000), tactical role and degree of effectiveness. You could build the Serenity, a Star Destroyer, a butterfly, a cartoon house with drone launchers in the windows, a space station shaped like Dover Castle or even a sniper rifle with a spinal death weapon for the barrel and a point defence laser for the trigger.

At any time during the customisation you can press the 'test' button, and the AI will fly the ship against available stock designs in a 1v1 arena battle. You can also use the tournament game mode to watch battles between selected designs from any of your save files, any of your friends' save files and any .lua files someone has shared elsewhere on the internet. The devs run occasional open tournaments for ship designs, as do some youtubers.

Single player is utterly devoid of story, it's basically a sandbox galaxy in which various mutually hostile factions hold territory. Kill things and provide R (the production resource) to your factory ships to gain C (research resource). Use C to increase your ship's build limit and unlock new components to play with, or to hire friendly ships to follow you around. Install a factory on your ship to be able to spend R on your custom escort ships, which you can release for more C. Conquer and comsume the galaxy, find a wormhole, travel to the next galaxy and start over. The visuals are pretty nice, the opposing factions are diverse and the fact that your escort ships are ones you yourself have designed makes the campaign feel more exclusive and personal in a 4X-ey sort fo way.

The 7 playable factions generally handle differently, which adds some replayability. Some factions have diamond or rhombus hull pieces, changing their look and design priorities. there's a faction with really cheap shield generators and sniper cannons that can spawn resource-generating space-plants, there's a faction with cheap high-damage weapons and deadly beam drones but very little effective armour, there's a faction whose only direct weapon is a spinal plasma launcher you build by stacking modular components on top of each other. There's one faction that gets nuke launchers and melee spikes.

On the downside though, some of the factions get their playstyle from a lack of versatlity and a poor choice of components. It's pretty demoralising to look at a new faction's full array of hardware and think 'is this it?' Tournaments can be dominated by Faction 4 (the modular plasma cannon one) and by 'spinny ships' (that exploit the AI's ability to lead targets, making them impossible to hit), the music is good but can be intrusive and gets repetitive after the first dozen hours or so. The campaign has almost no ships bigger than 3000P, meaning a player with the cap of 8000P gets to stomp everything with little effort or interesting events. They are small complaints but they do add up sometimes.

To finish, I'll say that Rassembly holds its players' attention not with a scripted or even emergent narrative, or with a great challenge, but with the promise of having something to tinker with. Working out the strengths of each weapon or design, using those extra 200P to give your flagship a little more punch, and reverse-engineering a design you saw on youtube that one time, are what contributes to an addictive and engaging experience that's well worth the £11 it asks for.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat game, I'd even go as far as to call it superb. Basically you start as a small ship being part of a faction. You go around collecting resources and fighting enemy ships, bringing the resources back to your base or to motherships to be rewarded C (the "money" currency in the game). C is used to upgrade the max points you can spend on your ship, meaning you can create bigger ships with better guns and upgrades. C is also used to buy new weapons and things like shields you can put on your ships. You can recruit other ships also with C but it's a stupid thing to do since you won't really earn any C if you spend it on getting allies.
So you go around the galaxy, restoring your faction's stations and earning C and resources by doing so, destroying ships and their stations to enlarge your territory, but the enemy factions do the same thing to you. They destroy your stations and your hive ships and as you are expanding your territory in one direction, it's easy to lose your grip in another part of the galaxy meaning you have to upgrade all the ships in your faction as you unlock more total points to spend on ships, otherwise they will be too fragile when the enemies attack your base. You personally customize all the ships in your faction any way you want to, mixing functionality with design or just going flat out functionality with ugly ships.
After a while when you've earned a ton of C, upgrading the total points you can spend on ships, you start to build hive ships which can create new ships. You get to choose 4 ships that you can create but you can replace those ships whenever you want to without replacing the already built ships, meaning you can build 4 ships of a certain type then switch them out and build the next 4 while keeping the ones you already built so you can basically have a whole fleet of all the ships in your faction. This is when the game becomes fun for me because now, you're basically free to do whatever you want to. You started as a drone for your faction and now you have become a moving station, building ships from the resources you and the ships you produced gather for you, growing your fleet larger and larger all the time being able to take on more difficult enemy factions. Or you could just stay as a fighter ship if you're into that, collecting resources for your faction and letting it expand on its own.\t\t\t\t\t
