\r\n\t\t\t\t\tBuilt a spaceship that has two giant dragon heads with flames coming out and lasers for eyes.

11/10 would shoot lasers out of my dragon eyes again.

Silliness aside, I play a lot of computer games and love this one. I rarely write reviews, that's how much I enjoy this game. So let me help you decide since you're still reading because you're too lazy to watch a video clip of it on youtube (j/k, i read reviews of games before I buy too). Do you like:
- sci fi games? (Starcraft, EVE Online, Alpha Centauri)
- building characters in RPGs, building empires in 4X games, or creating custom units? (think Armored Core, Alpha Centauri's unit editor, etc.)
- 2D arcade shooters, or people who like shooting things in general?

This game has elements of all of those. Has a fun difficulty to it too, learning the mechanics, and being caught offguard by something new because you fight other people's spaceships in space sometimes, lol. Testing your shapceships in automated AI duels against other player's spaceships is entertaining too, like watching a tournament in eSports. Very entertaining game, excellent graphics, awesome soundtrack. Music is the kind of rare video game music that you can listen to for hours and still enjoy. The music reminds me of the PC game "Shatter" soundtrack - a game which I've never played before but love the music of (look up Shatter OST on youtube). Highly recommend Reassembly. So wow.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReassembly is a fantastic game. In practice its very much a twin-stick-shooter, though it retains a surprising amount of depth for a game which is so simple at its heart.

There are many things that make this game great, but to simplify things for this small review, and to add information to anyone on the fence on weather to buy it or not, i will put these points into separate short sections.

1) Ship Building.

The core mechanic of the game, building your ship is both rewarding and a time sink. When building the largest ship in my fleet, i spent 40min before i was satisfied with the end result. But when i was satisfied, i was very satisfied. I glowed with pride as i flew my behemoth through space.
The fun doesn't stop there. The ship is made up of lots of different parts, as you can see from the imagery. At first you would be forgiven for assuming that this is simply a method of allowing design flexibility. While this holds true, it opens up an interesting mechanic. You see, every part of the ship is a separate entity. A finished ship is less a single unit, and more a collection of blocks stuck together. In practice this means that slapping hull plating to your ship doesn't simply increase health, but creates an armored point wherever you placed it. Weapons have less health, so placing them in your ship created areas of vulnerability. If you gun is destroyed, you cant fire. If your jets are destroyed you cant move. This means that the layout of your ship is as important as what you put in.
Equally, your jet engines work as individual units, rather than just giving your ship '+movement speed'. So, if you don't have any jets placed at an angle, you cant turn. If you have no jets on your sides, you cant strafe. No jets facing forwards means you cant reverse. In this same vein, the more boosters you have, the faster you move.
All these points turn ship building into a sort of puzzle game. Though one in which the answer is what your preferred gameplay style is.

2) Combat.

While general space exploration is a big part of the game, combat is the fly in the soup of game mechanics here. (Not in a bad way, as everyone will want one). Combat is simple as it is deep. At its heart, you pick your target, align guns, and fire till dead. But on a deeper level there's alot which goes into combat. Every ship has a 'core'. This is your command bridge, your heart. When this goes, you die. Until it goes, you will regenerate parts slowly. So when approaching an enemy you look for the quickest way to take out this heart. With the variety of ships on offer, this never gets old.

3) Pacing.

The game is fantastically paced. Tutorials are done on the fly, so you learn by playing. At the start, you have a small ship. You gain resources by exploring, with small dogfights along the way. You use these resources to improve, and eventually you have a medium ship. Now you start fighting abit more and getting a better income from doing so, whilst supplementing you income with exploration. Then you get a big ship. Now exploration becomes the back seat. Combat is your bread and butter. You fight and fight. After abit, you enter the forth stage of progression. You may have dabbled, but until now you've been focused of improving yourself. Now you build a fleet. Soon your at the heart of a 30 ship strong armada, and fights take on a whole new focus.

The fantastic thing about this formula of progression is with the unlocks. There are alot of races (8?). You start with only 1. To unlock others you must locate and destroy a ship worth 1000 points (you start with around 300 and grow to 8000 so i found i was doing this around the second / third stage). Each race has some unique items and generally some sort of separate theme to its shared items. This allows great re-playability.

---------------------------

So, the review summery. This is a great game. Part Space exploration, part space combat, part lego. I'd advise this to alot of people, as there is something for everybody. In particular i'd point people to it who enjoy games with creation elements, such as Spore. If creation isnt your thing, but you like space combat sims, i would still advise this game. The races come with pre-made ships, and the game has the ability to export ships as code. So you can always trawl forums to locate ships.

I highly recommend this game.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a mesmerizing and addictive game. The idea is not new: design a spaceship, battle other spaceships, mine resources in order to buy ship upgrades, repeat. But this game stands out for a few reasons.

First is the realism of ship design. You assemble your ship out of a stock of components in a editor reminiscent of Microsoft Visio (but better; MSFT ought to hire this guy). In most games like this, you might, say, upgrade to Engine IV, which makes you move at speed 8. In Reassembly, your ship's behavior is instead calculated as a consequence of your design. Speed, for example, is a result of your thrust and mass, but in addition, maneuverability is a result of where you put your thrusters, how they're angled, your ship's center of gravity (which is indicated in the ship editor), etc. If your ship doesn't handle the way you like, it's your fault, and you can easily fix it.

Second is the AI behavior. All ships seek resources which grow on space rocks. Lots of small ships hover around these rocks harvesting what they can find; a few medium-size ships prowl around the perimeter hunting the small ships; and occasionally capital ships arrive and destroy everyone. It feels rather like an ecology simulator, with minnows nibbling at coral reefs, big fish eating the minnows, and so on. Since the universe is dynamically generated, I assume this 'ecology sim' behavior isn't hard-coded, but is emergent behavior of the ship AI. Anyway, it makes you feel that you're inhabiting a living world.

It's beautiful, too, with a minimal color palette standing out against stark black space. Hundreds of ships can be onscreen at a time without the game apparently breaking a sweat (thanks Reassembly developer, for actually caring about performance). And the trance-y music complements the minimalist esthetic nicely.

If you built Lego spaceships as a kid, or you have scuba dived in a coral reef and liked it, you should play this game.\t\t\t\t\t
