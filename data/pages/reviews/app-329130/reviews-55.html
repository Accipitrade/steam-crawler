\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReassembly is a top-down ambient space sandbox that gets pretty much everything right.

You start with a small ship and head out into a galaxy filled with varied but distinct factions. Destroyed enemies and space plants that die off naturally both drop "R"esources. At first you need to return to a friendly space station and transfer your R to them, rewarding you with "C"urrency that be used to upgrade your "P"ower limit, unlock new blocks and technology, or recruit other friendly AI ships into your fleet.

It won't be long before you get into real meat of the game - the ship desginer! With familiar features like drag-selecting blocks, duplicating, mirroring, and so on, it's very easy and satisfying to use. Blocks snap together at points on their edges, just like Lego. Each faction has its own palette of blocks and technologies that can somewhat limit the range of designs you can build, but there are loads of mods that offer expanded palettes and even entirely new factions with their own custom block shapes.

Eventually you'll add a factory block to your ship and start creating a self-replicating doom fleet - but watch out for "Agents", the game's system of asynchronous multiplayer! They are groups of ships designed by other players around the world and uploaded to the Reassembly servers via wormholes. Each new game you play includes some of these player fleets for extra Fun (tm). You never now how lethal they will be until you meet them, so be sure to prepare a capable fleet first...

With a sublime chill soundtrack and crisp vector graphics, the hours will fly by while you Reassemble your way toward taking over the entire galaxy. It's also a plus that the game runs smoothly on pretty much any PC or laptop from the last decade.

Recommended 10/10!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tReassembly is a great game for those who wish Captain Forever gave you more time to build your ship.

But vague, incomplete comparisons aside, Reassembly is a game where the player selects a faction and is dropped into a 2D galaxy sandbox where here is no particular win condition. Dropping into the game, the game states that ships similarly-colored to the player ship and apart from some tutorials to teach the player how to do stuff just turns the player loose.

Early Game
Some immediate goals the game may give to the player is to reactivate the faction's stations. What defines a station in this game is simply a ship without thrusters and they usually have ship factories. The initial situation seems to be that the player faction was pushed back by some galactic enemies, as there are destroyed factories in enemy space that need reactivated. Station reactiviation itself is easy, the player needs only to hover next to the station for about 5 seconds. The trick, however, is clearing the area of hostiles, which is hard early on with the small starter ship.

Mid Game
Eventually new components can be purchased as well as higher "build caps" so the player can build bigger ships to make themselves more combat effective. This is around the time agents might wander into the player faction's territory. Agents are ships that another player built and sent through a wormhole that ended up in the player's game. These ships are AI controlled, but they're typically pretty overpowered and are sometimes built in some stupid "meta" fashion without any kind of aesthetically pleasing elements. They're hard to kill, but it's doable. If they're too hard, I'd recommend just slapping a bunch of missiles on a ship and bombarding them from afar.

Late Game
Later in the game, the player probably has a massive dreadnaught that can stand up to pretty much anything. With everything unlocked, though, it gives thet player more options for ship design experimentation. Most if not all of the agents spread around the galaxy have probably been dealt with at this point. This is pretty much where the only thing left to do is galactic conquest, creative experimentation, exploration, or just starting a new save with another faction.

Combat
There's not much to say about the combat - it's just great. It's crisp, the weapons feel good, and the combat feels physics-based because of the way ships break apart when their core is destroyed. (Which is very satisfying to watch. I'll make an Imgur album of moments like that)

A Living World
The AI in this game is actually cool. Enemy ships might attack the player on sight if the player seems vulnerable or just run away if the player has a massive dreadnaught as mentioned in the Late Game section. Stations in this game build ships, those ships go out and gather this thing called "R," (something that grows on asteroids) bring it back to the station, and that station can use the gathered R to build more ships.

Unfortunately, the AI doesn't seem smart enough to form up and attack other factions, as I have witnessed them just hanging out around their station, but it's still cool how the AI has something to keep themselves busy instead of just wandering aimlessly. It's not as if factions never collide in this game, they just don't seem to ever coordinate attacks.

Factions
As mentioned, this game has multiple factions. The playable factions are Terran, Farmer, Tinkrells, Bees, Crystallitet, Sentinel, and Red; Terran being the default faction. What I didn't mention is how stylized and inspired each faction is. They're not just recolors of the default faction, each faction has their own aesthetic, build patterns, ship parts, naming conventions, etc. This allows the player to project an ideology onto said factions. The Red faction gives off a galactic tyrant vibe, so I kill them on sight, the Farmer faction has a pretty peaceful-looking aesthetic, so I generally avoid them, and the other factions seem to give off something in between, so I sort of just treat them as neutral.

There are other factions, but the playable factions seem to make up the bulk of the game. Factions are unlocked by destroying the largest ship (The flagship, basically) of said faction. I'm not sure where the game positions these flagships, as I've only ever encountered them by accident. They're not that big, but they are the largest ships in their respective faction. I remember the Crystallites in particular had some pretty big ships, indimidatingly so. I actually had to spend some time drilling into their flagship with torpedos (Very powerful weapons) to bring it down.

What Happens Next?
In the time I've put into the game, about 40 hours were spent on the default faction. I wasn't focusing on galactic conquest, but such a task would be pretty tedious and I was mostly just messing around with a beautiful and unstoppable ship I created. After a while, I created a new save with the Tinkrells faction and they are very different from the Terrans. Terran ships are more about having a wide array of weapons to choose from, weapons expected of human ships, and Tinkrell ships are more about weapon amplification. Through stackable muzzle attachments that amplify damage, fire-rate, range, and projectile velocity, the player can experiment to create multiple different flavors of plasma cannon, which is what serves as the base of all of this faction's projectile weaponry.

Another playstyle the player can engage in is never actualy designing a ship. That's right, this game actually supports a playstyle that involves never having to design a ship. Going into command mode, (Default: 2) the player can target allied ships. While mousing over an allied ship, the player can then bring up their ship design storage (Default: 3) which will then populate the target ship design into the main window, name and all, where the player can then drag it into the design storage area. Here's a screenshot of what I mean. All of those entries are copied from my faction's default ships that I've seen flying around. I've even been copying station designs.

Building a Fleet
This is a good time to get into how fleet building works. Fleet AI is very basic. You can only tell them and I mean all of them at once, order groups aren't a thing to Disengage, Engage Your Target, and Fire At Will. It's basic, but not in a bad way - it does what it needs to do.

New ships are built be having a factory attached to the player's ship with enough room to spit out the core of the ship that's being built. Once the core has been spit out, the ship will build itself and be the player's minion. They even give the R they collect to the player, which is mega cool. This is also how new factories are built, which is a critical part of holding territory. If there isn't a factory churning out new ships, the sector isn't going to hold.

Playstyles
Why is the "Building a Fleet" section below the "What Happens Next?" section? Because I got through my entire playthrough without focusing on building ships and factories. I just wandered the galaxy in an unstoppable doom dreadnaught designed to near-perfection. I only started focusing on building ships and factories on my new save.

So this is a game that's more or less about designing cool ships, and it allows for a playstyle where the player copies designs from already-existing ships instead of making custom ones. This game is a good example of how a sandbox is done.

The Verdict
This game is good for creative people, same goes for a lot of other games I praise. Go play Captain Forever, which is a free browser game that's similar to Reassembly. If you liked playing that game, you can probably justify dropping $15 on this game.
