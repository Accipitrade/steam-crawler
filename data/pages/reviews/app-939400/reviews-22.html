\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis may be the one time I feel like putting my own thoughts in a steam user review might actually be helpful, because I really enjoyed this game, but I was very nearly put off playing the rest of it after completing the first story, and glancing at some of the negative review tells me that a lot of the people leaving negative reviews thought similarly. I figured I'd leave a review that might help those people feel a bit less crazy, as well as explain what the rest of the game did to reel me in after the first story left me feeling unsatisfied.

My problems with the first story come down to both a lack of proper conveyance as to what you're actually able to do, as well as the solutions to the problems presented becoming predictable after this was realized. I went through it twice, trying different things both times, only to end up with the bad ending again with no signs that I did any better. It wasn't until I looked up a guide that I learned that you can interact with the environment. More frustratingly, one puzzle required me to scroll down on the mouse wheel in order to see certain important elements. The fact that this is even possible is not conveyed at all, and it never comes up again. Once I figured out I could do these things, finishing the first story with a good ending was just as simple as finding the spots I was supposed to click on the screen, which left me feeling like I got the best ending, not because I did a good job, but because the game screwed me about until I looked up a guide. That being said, it wasn't without it's good points. There were two puzzles in there that did feel thematically appropriate enough to serve the story's message. One of them I probably could've figured out on my own if I hadn't already gotten frustrated enough to look up a guide. Having completed the game, I get the message this story was trying to get across, but the way it was executed resulted in it falling kind of flat.

The second story comes along and either helps fix my problems with the previous story, or turns those problems into positives. This one starts by forcing you to interact with something in the environment in order to proceed, which would've helped convey that you can do that if it was present in the first story. That segment is followed up with a puzzle that was able to help me understand what was going through the head of the main character through game mechanics alone, and this isn't the only puzzle in this story to do that. Without wishing to spoil, the predictability that dragged down much of the first story ends up working to the benefit of the second story, through the ways they subverted my expectations. In the first story, it felt like my expectations were being subverted about what I could do, rather than what I should do. I never felt that way in story two.

The third story switches up the format by becoming a point-and-click, the puzzle design of which is pleasant enough, but what sucked me in about that particular chapter was how the game kind of tricks you into getting the bad ending, but unlike story one, shows you what you can do differently to achieve the best outcome. In this instance, it felt like it wanted me to see the difference the two choices made, and it presented the "here's another things you could've done" thing in believable way. I saw the consequences of the bad choice first, without feeling frustrated and like I was being railroaded into the conclusion they were going for.

Each of the three stories could be summed up with the phrase "subversion of expectations". The first story subverted my expectations in a way that made me feel like the way forward was hidden from me by simply not knowing what I could actually do in the game. However the other two stories subverted my expectations in a way that made me feel like I was naturally ending up in the scenarios the devs wanted me to end up in, rather than getting me stuck in the conclusion they wanted by being overly obscure, as well as getting me to feel and think the things that the main characters of those stories might be feeling and thinking. I wish I could go into greater detail, but I would end up spoiling my favorite parts of the game. If you end up picking this game up, and you end up as put off by the first story as I was, I'd suggest you give it a little more time. You might as well, you've got 2 hours before the refund window is up. Even then, that $2 price tag ain't gonna do much damage to your wallet.\t\t\t\t\t
