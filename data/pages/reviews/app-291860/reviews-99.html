TL;DR: Addictive and fun game, typical The Behemoth type of jokes (great if you enjoy them like I do), some great soundtracks, and Pokemon-like world exploration.

Pit People is a different type of strategy game than most of you would imagine.

If you want to play something similar to Civ V, then well... this game isn't for you, because in this game, RNG plays a major role, however, unlike those card games where you fail to topdeck = gameover and rage quits, Pit People's combat execution is very smooth even when the combat itself is mostly random: your characters decide who to attack, arrows miss most of the time, area of effect usually kills teammates, blablabla.

But it is preciously of this that made the strategy in this game unique and fun. What you are able to control in this strategy game is to set up your favorite team with ABSURD creatures you catch with Pokeball, and equip them with ABSURD weapons to make them look sillier and deadier (seriously I've been smashing helmets with a sunflower and hitting ennemies with ice cream). Rainbow Horses? 10/10

Oh well, the combat itself is really well done as you have to take into account the randomness of your troops and get the most out of them. The AI in this game is rather smart, and with proper management RNG isn't an issue at all. Multiplayer is also insanely fun as you and somebody else team up and work your way towards victory using chat and pins. The sense of accomplishment once you win a match feels SO GOOD OMG :D

The only issue is that the combat can sometimes be hard to understand. Even when the controls are super simple to get, the actual combat scenes can, well, become a mess of explosions real fast... don't expect anything better when over 20 little guys with swords explosives tootbrush flowers bugs unicornpowa and snakes are literally hitting each other at the same time. Still super fun to watch the madness go off though xD

If you want a fun game and an easy one, then this is the game for you!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\t7.5/10

Pit People is one of the few occasions where I took the Early Access plunge, which is why I'm able to pump out a review so soon after release. We only had a few missions to polish off. I'm a sucker for cooperative turn-based strategy, and was a big fan of both Shadowrun Chronicles and Card Hunter, not to mention Divinity: Original Sin 2. However, the strategy in Pit People is a little...different.

As the description notes, Pit People is all about positioning. When you move one of your party members on the hex board, they will randomly attack anything in their range. Therefore, if you're trying to take out a specific target, you'll need to move your party members so that this enemy is the only one they could possibly attack. This was a strategic layer that was new for me, and it was interesting to learn how to best navigate within this system. However, after a while, many of the battles seemed to play out similarly. Although there is a difference between the static abilities party members have, each party member still basically only does one thing (although it's neat to mount a party member on the back of your Spidaur and carry them around).

The customization comes in when you create your party composition and outfit each member with items. Although you'll pick up certain "human" characters progressing through the story, you'll need to go out and capture enemies to create a diverse crew. To do so, you'll need to take a cage and a character with a net. If you kill all enemies but the one you want to capture and you move your party member with the net in range, you'll snag a new potential party member. For the majority of the game, my party consisted of a cyclops, a tank (Josef), 2 vampiresses (vampiri?), and a fairy. The vampiress gains life through dealing damage, and deals extra damage to bleeding (heavily damaged) enemies. The fairy burns enemies with fire from a couple hexes away, while the cyclops takes up two party slots but hits especially hard.

Unfortunately, the item system in the game was the most baffling thing to me. You can acquire items through completing quests or you can buy them in the marketplace, but it doesn't seem possible to see the statistics of an item before you buy it. On your party outfitting screen there are some bar graphs that are supposed to represent statistics, but it was hard to really understand what I was looking at. The only item that I knew for sure was making a difference was the "Alpaca Lips," a mallet used by my cyclops that dealt extra fire damage to all characters (including both himself and allies) in the vicinity. The marketplace also contains outfitted party members that you can buy for a somewhat steep price.

The main draw of the title, in my opinion, is the humor. Certainly, Pit People retains the humor that has earned The Behemoth such a following. Many of the items are hilarious - some of my favorites include a ballcap that says "Mom" for the Troll Mom, a stack of pancakes that I used as a shield with my cyclops, and a space helmet and gyroscope wings for my fairy. The character designs are absurd, with the gnomes being a highlight sitting in their floating armchairs. I also cracked up often at the voices, especially my fairy that screamed every line or my co-op partner's cupcake that sounding like a babbling baby. Who wouldn't want some little iced creams?

I had a good time with Pit People, although all the fights felt similar late game. However, I have to give The Behemoth credit for changing the pace a little during a main mission towards the end. I can't say I enjoyed it as much as any of the games I mention up at the top of the review, but it's definitely a pretty good time with a pal. Lastly, the music in town is amazing - look it up and jam to it!\t\t\t\t\t
