\r\n\t\t\t\t\tStill in early access, but quite a fair price as these things go, especially from a dev with a good rep.

The game may or may not be close to the finished article, I don't know, but there are a few obvious gaps in gamplay styles. I'm not sure what their plans are, but i hope they don't mess it up by trying to be too clever or add nonsense.

Basically, leave the city, capture monsters/heroes, use them in battle. There are no paid perks and after a short time of yomping around the map I'd picked up a few characters to play with. I'm not sure the balancing is quite right, and the leaderboard is very basic and does nothing to encourage participation.

But the real strength of Pit People is in the actual battle gameplay, which is very straightforward and easy to get to grips with. I'm not sure if the game is designed for tablets too, but it has that vibe. You command your troop movements, but they decide what to do when they get there. Sometimes that's a joy, other times a pain.

I liked the game, and I have definately had a great time with it and got my money's worth. But I will wait now for the launch version and take it up again then.

In the meantime I'd have no hesitation in recommending this game to anyone who likes turn based strategy. It isn't Advance Wars, but it's still pretty good.\t\t\t\t\t

The level of customisable aspects for characters seems equally as vast as in previous titles.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLike Pokemon, only more horrifying, and otherwise superior in every way.

If you enjoyed the classic gameboy Pokemon games for any reason other than the connected TV series, you will love this. You've gotta catch all the fighters, level them up, and complete the story mode.

It's superior because it's both simpler to learn and harder to master. Instead of choosing from a menu of options, you just decide where your fighters stand. Next turn, you can have them stand somewhere else. Then somewhere else. Then somewhere else. Then, depending on how good your choice of location for them, you'll either win or lose.

The spatial aspect of it means you cannot memorise and execute a standard strategy like you can in Pokemon. Here, you must react to whatever situation you're put in, so even the exact same battle won't play exactly the same twice. This adds so much depth to the strategy, as you need a contingency for everything.

However, it's much quicker to pick up, as your options are "stand here" or "stand somewhere else". You don't have to learn what a list of items or attack types do. Although a detailed working understanding of the game is a big help, it's not essential to gain the experience of actually playing it.

My biggest gripe with the game (though Pokemon and most other games are equally guilty) is that it provides no detailed guide or wiki to help me fully understand what I'm looking at. What does it mean if a tile is on fire? What does it mean if my fighter is glowing purple? What does and does not count as a helmet? How is damage calculated? How big a buff is a singular level up?

I wouldn't mind so much if the game were terrible, because I could just set everything to easy and play through for the story. However the game is so good that having to learn the details through trial and error is frustrating, because I genuinely want to learn the details.

If there were a very detailed guide or wiki that gave enough information for a FULL understanding of the mechanics, I could see this as an e-sport, and I could also see myself spending more time reading that guide than actually playing. That said, as it is, I still enjoy perhaps one battle per day, though I still can't figure out what it means when fighters glow purple, and this annoys me.\t\t\t\t\t
