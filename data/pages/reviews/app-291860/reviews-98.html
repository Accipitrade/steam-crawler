\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI`ve never had an acid trip, but had I had one this is how I would imagine it to go down: bright, colourful, full of bizarre encounters and even more bizarre messages to take home, that would all make perfect sense in the end. We`re talking a good trip, of course, the kind of one where a flying giraffe would tell you that you`re a good person no matter what.

Pit People is a turn-based strategy game that just came out of a year Early Access. Unlike a lot of games of the genre the battle is presented in an attrition-like manner where it would take at least 4 turns of good pounding until the fighter would yield. This considerably lengthens the matches as the player has only up to 5 fighters at a time. However, the fighters are very versatile with a huge number of races (full of perks and weaknesses) and customization options available.

If you have been following the Behemoth`s games you`ll know exactly how it all looks. They always had a kink for visual gags and most of the time this would work really well. PP is no exception, but rather their magnum opus of visual gags thus far.

Gameplay-wise there is a potential turn-off for seasoned strategy players. During the battles the player doesn`t choose who gets hit by your unit. The attack is conducted at random at anybody (or thing) that is within range. A questionable simplification, perhaps the game is targeted at a wider audience, not just TBS lovers?

Recruiting system in the game is somewhat innovative, drawing inspiration from Pokemon. Aside from purchasing at the market, people and creatures are captured during battle encounters by throwing nets and placing them in cages that are bought before in the town. Now the problem with it all is that the target has to the last thing standing which sometimes can be difficult given the random attack ‘feature’.

The game tends to push the player into acknowledging ‘the unfairness’: the home base is regularly moved by the bear, some battles being a spike on the learning curve when you are pitched against 3 absolutely waves (looking at you, Jerkimedes), and some battles even acknowledging the fact that they are unfair, i.e. the ‘Unfair challenge’ in the pit. Hence, the game presents with a somewhat of an identity crisis, it wants to be fun and colourful, but then smash your face into the dirt if you’re getting all cosy and casual about it.

In the end PP leaves a good impression. It is something I plan to get back every now and then to try that unfair challenge or just wonder around doing some wacky side-quest I will eventually stumble upon.

Charming art style, great soundtrack and made me chuckle.

The gameplay isn't anything ground breaking. The central mechanics involve assembling a team of fighters from 19 different creatures, each with their own strengths and weaknesses. The primary strategy is the creation of your team and their loadout. The secondary part is moving them around the battlefield. You don’t tell them who to attack, just moving them within range will cause them to attack that target. However, if there are multiple targets it’s pretty ambiguous and possibly random as to how those are chosen, meaning you have to position your units carefully if you want to target a specific threat.
Judging by how short the story is the game seems to put most of its replay-ability and last-ability into PVP and side quests, and personally competitive play isn't my thing. There is a myriad of side missions, which (aside from a few escalating difficulty mini-campaigns) are often just one battle. These are pretty diverse in their objectives (given the limitations of a turn-based fighter), so it’s clear the developers have taken time to keep these missions fresh and plentiful.
The game provided some challenge, but nothing I wasn’t able to overcome the second, or third time trying. Either from foresight or a team re-build. There is an insane mode available which significantly increases the difficulty for those that find it too easy.

The stagnation of this game comes from reaching a point in the game where your loadout combination of Pit People and equipment allows you to pretty much take on every mission the first time. You constantly unlock new items, but these are almost entirely cosmetic. Which means you end up scrolling though a lot of reskinned weapons and armour in an attempt to find the ones with different stats and even they are often worse or comparable to what you currently have. Which is another reason why I feel the game is heavily geared towards PVP. The game is very limited in its progressive ability. It’s very much a game of “learn and master”, rather than “invest and progress”. Which for a PVP game it has to be, in order to be balanced and give each player a level playing field.

I personally cannot see the appeal of this type of PVP. Sure, there are 19 different pit people you can use in a 6-fighter team, and about half of those can use equipment. So, the variety for strategy is relatively high, but with RNG aspects thrown in, and a distinct lack of strategy other than the placement of your characters, I cannot see how the competitive scene will stay alive without a continuous stream of new competitive players.

I may seem critical of the flaws in this game, and I want to clarify that this game was a blast. It’s charming, it looks great, it made me laugh, it kept me engaged and I wanted to play more, but it was over before it began for me. Regardless for £10 you get a solid slice of the fun cake.

Overall, I feel like, if this game had more progression it would be SPOT ON. Where gear and enemies were not all just reskinned, recoloured identical copies of the 30 before it. I would want to see more of a “working your way up a ladder” approach as opposed to nailing down the mechanics and exploiting them. Although there are 19 creatures, because there is no progression they are all added to the pool of enemies from the very beginning, so they get samey quickly, in other words it FEELS like there is a lot less. I understand that this isn’t a triple A development studio, and their resources are limited and progression takes a lot more balance. Currently the game sits around a 7-8/10, because it’s a beautiful, heart filled game I would recommend, but lacks lastability.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo be fair? : Best game I've played in 2018 thus far and toppling any others I've played so far.

I am a huge fan of tactical games and I may be a bit biased with this review coming from a person who enjoys games such as Final Fantasy Tactics and Fire Emblem and even grew up with them. Though I will say this; Pit People is an insanely good game. At first I was skeptical during early access, a lot of the battles just felt way too simplified and it felt almost boring to me. After the full release those problems were quickly disperesed and even more so I got to play the game from start to finish.

Pit people is like any other TRPG I feel like- marked on a grid and having 6 characters at your disposal. These characters can be a complete mix to your liking, and I would even go so far to say you customize them into your own classes, and even more so being locked to 6 only characters and some even taking up 2 spaces in that roster can lead up to some interesting unit combinations. You can go with a fair and balanced team, and or a team that's all over the place and used just for fun. The game is simple yet so complex I feel at a huge amount of times. Not to mention having a Narrator over the game brings me joy as I bash my opponents too and fro. (I also want to point out that 1000+ items of customization- and how all the items are balanced and made for unique playstyles and things you can test out)

Not to mention the comedy and art style is always top notch. I haven't laughed that hard in a long period of time- the sense of humor these guys at Behemoth make is always something to quote even. The art style is something I also enjoy- so simple yet unique.. Not to mention the soundtrack is something to behold. It's so much to behold that I have to turn my music up and just listen to those amazing tunes. Now, of course no game is perfect, and I do have some slight complaints even if minor.. I noticed something odd with the cutscene quality from early access to what we have now. The bear cutscene remains in tact, but every cutscene has it's own style almost and it just feels.. off maybe? I'm not quite sure..

And that's the only complaint I can really think of? Either way if you enjoyed final fatnasy tactics, tactics orgre, fire emblem, hell even xcom then I would full on reccomend pit people-- and even if you don't like tactic games- do it for the comedy alone along with it's amazing world\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAs a fan of previous Behemoth games like Castle Crashers and Battle Block Theater, I decided to pull the trigger and purchase Pit People in early access in hopes that the game would live up to the experiences I had when playing the previously mentioned games.

I purchased this game nearly a year ago. At the time the game was enjoyable, new, fresh, but its problems came to light after nearly an hour of playtime. There was no depth to the game plain and simple. I had hoped that development through early access would improve conditions, but now nearly a year after my purchase the game is fully released and the problems that it had in early access still plague it to this day.

The battle mechanics that you experience in the tutorial levels will be the same that you encouter throughout the game. You move your characters, end your turn, deal damage, tank damage, rinse and repeat ALL GAME LONG. The only thing you control in battle is where you position your units and even that mechanic is surrounded with RNG as you cannot target enemies, your units simply roll on which units they will attack. Your units will level a lot as you play and there is no significant difference between a level 1 unit and a level 100 unit. Both will deal roughly the same damage and have the same HP. Unless you are trying to collect every item in game, there is no sense of progress.

Sure there are many unlocks and different unit types to control that add some levels of uniqueness to each battle, but the vast majority, I'm talking 85% of all items are simply cosmetic and offer nothing new.

The majority of my in-game hours came from the multiplayer versus mode the game has. I was hoping for this "Strategy" and "Turn-Based Combat" game to actually offer some options to outplay your opponent. To my dissapointment multiplayer is as lackluster as singleplayer. There are zero ways to outplay your oppenent other than them moving their units close to yours and giving you the opportunity to hit them first. With the lack of targetting control and the RNG involved in attacking units, the multiplayer rounds became as stale as singleplayer.

I waited for the game to come out of early access in hopes that these problems would be addressed by the developer. Sadly I was greeted with more useless cosmeetic items and rushed cutscenes. Looks like I wont be touching this game again.

DO NOT make the same mistakes that I made. DO NOT purchase early access games even if you liked the developers previous games. I know that I certainly will never purchase an early access game after this experience.\t\t\t\t\t
