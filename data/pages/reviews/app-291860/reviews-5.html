\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you can look past the shallow, slow combat, then you might enjoy Behemoth's unique humor, art, and world present in Pit People.

I could not. You control up to six characters, and the only thing you have control of in combat is movement. There is a good amount of unit types, but they normally only have one mechanic: a Cupcake which only heals but can't attack, a Cyclops that moves slowly that can sometimes throw enemies, and a good 17 other units with similar features. But it doesn't have a noticeable impact on how you play the game. You keep your tanks in the front-line, ranged in the back-line, and that's about it (generally). Environments don't have enough going on to keep it interesting in either its campaign or open world, so it's a lot of marching forward to the closest enemy. Fights go on for way too long as enemies have large health pools, requiring many turns of...moving your characters to the enemy to continue. Every party member has to slowly move into position and attack, which becomes unwieldy in large battles, so there's a lot of time spent doing near nothing in combat.

Progression doesn't exist. There are different weapon types, and tons of weapon models, but there's very little difference in their damage. One weapon might have a certain element attached to it, but that's about it. Armor's the same. You level up, but you don't get to customize a character's stats. Leveling up results in the character receiving full health and a 0.24% increase to their damage. Gear is the largest determinant to a character's stats.

The campaign is very short, consisting of eight quests that usually don't offer anything particularly unique. Characters receive little to no character development. While there's the distinct Behemoth humor and art present, it doesn't hold up well gameplay-wise.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPit People? More like "Castle Crashers 2: The Strategy Game."

If you've played Castle Crashers, you know that it's all about fast-paced, button-mashing action. But in Pit People, the focus is on strategy and careful planning.

The first thing you'll notice is that the art style is very similar to Castle Crashers. But instead of playing as knights, you'll be commanding a group of colorful characters, including knights, archers, and even a giant cyclops.

But the real difference between the two games is the combat. In Pit People, you have to think about positioning and timing. You'll need to move your units around the map, carefully choosing where to place them so they can attack the enemy without getting too close and taking damage.

And speaking of damage, the game can be unforgiving at times. Your units can die quickly if you're not careful, and you'll need to constantly keep an eye on their health and make sure they're not overwhelmed.

But despite the difficulty, there's a certain satisfaction that comes with winning a battle. You'll feel like a genius when your carefully planned strategy pays off and you take down a tough enemy.

In conclusion, if you're a fan of Castle Crashers and you're looking for a game with a bit more depth and strategy, then Pit People is definitely worth checking out. Just be prepared to spend some time honing your tactical skills, and don't expect to win every battle on your first try. But if you're willing to put in the effort, you'll be rewarded with a fun and satisfying gaming experience.\t\t\t\t\t
