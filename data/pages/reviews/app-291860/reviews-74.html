\r\n\t\t\t\t\tWho doesn't love The Behemoth? They make some great games, with awesome music, fun graphics, and more zany humour than you can shake a stick at. Sadly, this game just doesn't hit the mark.

If you've played any of their previous games, you know exactly the sort of humour in store for you, but really this is a game that needed to take itself just a little more seriously than The Behemoth is capable of. The story is weak at best, and this time around the jokes don't keep me entertained. Combat has so much potential, but each encounter ends up a drawn out affair. Like all their previous games, there are tons of characters and items to discover and unlock, and also like their previous games these unlocks are (for the most part) aesthetic. Here is the major downfall: that includes weapon unlocks.

It's sad and somewhat baffling that Castle Crashers, a 10-year-old beat-um-up has more detailed statistics for its weapons than Pit People does. You would think that a turn-based tactics game would give you plenty of information so you could make informed decisions regarding the makeup of your team, but you would be wrong. Guesswork regarding equipment aside, the different characters get just a little too absurd, and can end up making the already-boring battles frustrating.

Overall, this feels like a game that could have been so much better had less time been spend on ridiculous ideas and a bit more time of building a solid foundation for those ridiculous ideas to grow from.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe latest game from The Behemoth!

Pit People is the fourth game from the The Behemoth and this time its a Turn-Based Strategy game! Previously, I had gotten Castle Crashers and Battleblock Theater, 2 other Behemoth games, from around $6.00 USD and absolutely loved them! I played so much out of them, offline or online It didn't matter, although that does mess up the hours that Steam records. Anyway, I was really excited about their newest game Pit People since I have experience with Turn-based Strategy before, and I have been pleasantly surprised with quality of this game and the immense replay valve included.

Gameplay wise, Pit People does it with a unique style, unlike other games of its type. Each character doesn't exactly obey your commands as while you can move their position it is up to them to attack which is great for making you plan your attack ahead of time but annoying when you have a character that uses projectiles or attacks the wrong person you wanted. While I have seen some criticism against this system, personality I found it alright, yes it was annoying but it was enjoyably bearable if that makes sense. Another main feature are the Quests and the multitude of items. Pit People compared to Battleblock Theater's collectable has so, so much more although they don't show up in your Steam Inventory which would have been nice and they ca nbe annoying to collect them all since they are needed for 100% completion. Now all the Quests included at the moment are pretty unique compare other the Quests the other game would have, making a very fresh take on the genre and there are plenty of them including the Story Quests. There is also The Pit for Online battles, the returning Co-op and Insane and Permadeath modes to make your life terrible.

As for the style, Pit People continues to use Dan Paladin's distinctive cartoony art style as with the other Behemoth Games. But another feature that Pie People has is the unique character design and world. Unlike other turn-based strategy games with usually follow a medieval or futuristic theme, Pit People basically has a combination of everything and manages to make them blend together very well. For example instead of the normal healer unit, Cupcakes are used, Rainbow Horses are the heavy artillery, and Troll Moms produce minions. This gives Pit People uniqueness in the sea of turn-based strategy games. Pit People also see the return of Will Stamper as the Narrator/Antagonist which if you have played Battleblock Theater is a reason to be excited.

Now for the music. Returning from Battleblock Theater is composer Patric Catani, and his work on Pit People is some of his best work yet! Most of the tracks seem to have a different genre of music. As with other Behemoth Games, there are some copyrighted track features that fit the theme of the game. Big props to Kormac's "Wash my Hands"

Overall, Pit People is a wonderful turn-based strategy game from the great Behemoth. Bringing something fresh and new to the genre. With great gameplay and a whole boatload of replayability along with the Behemoth's classic comedy and cartoony art style makes this a must have for any turn-based strategy fan or a person wanting more from the Behemoth!

And yes, I got this on sale.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI tried to love this game, and I love the Behemoth, but the laser-sharp focus which made their other games great is not here in this lazily designed turn-based strategy game. It can't decide whether it wants it to be focused on positional combat, or countering specific types of enemies, and therefore succeeds at neither.

Before I dig into what is wrong, let me mention a few things it does right: Stamper is still amazing as narrator. The problem is you don't hear much of him after the opening. The art style is the same we know and love from previous games. The visual creativity is still there too. Want a butterfly man with a shark head swinging a banana? You got it. However, this is about it for good points.

It rarely matters where you place your units, as the enemy can easily move around them every turn. There are not zones of control and most combat spaces are just open fields. Therefore, there is no point in putting fragile units behind your front line. This means that there is little to no strategy, and its basically a question of who can smack the other harder or faster. Most battles, it is clear who is going to win after the first few turns, but you are forced to slog it out in a mostly unentertaining series of melee bouts.

There are so many different types of counters that it is impossible to plan for them. Want to counter guys with helmets? Hope you brought a mace! Lots of robot guys? Hope you didn't bring guys who do fire damage. etc, etc, etc. This is especially bad in story missions, where the entire enemy force will be made up of 1 type, and if you didn't happen to bring the units or weapons that specifically counter them, you are SOL. (Oh, and you never know what you're going to face beforehand)

Fights are LOOOOOOOOONG. Hits typically do around 5 damage, and most enemies have around 100+ health. So when you run into a group of 5 random bandits, prepare for a 45 minute battle. Longer if the enemy brought healers, or happen to be outfitted with stuff that renders your weapons almost useless. Almost every battle takes the same course, and after a while, even the variety of unit types becomes boring.

The world map is huge and full of roving groups of baddies. This, coupled with the length of battles and the inability to save anywhere but your home base turns this aspect of the game into a chore. Trying to get to a quest? Either dodge every enemy you see or enjoy taking hours to get anywhere. Oh, and you are limited to how much loot you can carry, forcing you to backtrack to your base to drop it off.

Ultimately, I think the Behemoth bit off more than it could chew. All their other games are action/arcade games about precise timing and skill. They have never made a game like this and it shows. Better luck next time, guys.\t\t\t\t\t

This game's plot-relevant areas often have some kind of gimmick that turns out to be more of a nuisance than a unique spin on the gameplay. There's also a stealth segment in this game.
