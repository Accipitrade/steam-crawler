Seriously buy this game if you love strategy and a good laugh. Think "The Behemoth had a baby with Larian Studios (makers of the Divinity series, Legendary)
.
.

Okay, I am not easily impressed by comedy in any form. But this... this has me cackling the whole time.

The voice over guy is perfect, the game is very tactical and rewarding, it feels like I'll be exploring forever as progression is very steady. Which is a good thing!

The characters and weapons are so varied that it feels like I would have the same experience the 2nd, 3rd or 10th time through. The weapons just keep flowing in all different manners and make for very interesting mechanics.

I have put in 7 hours since loading the game for the first time.

Only CON I can conceive is that I don't see why this couldn't come to a mobile platform, there's is nothing astounding in terms of graphics here and it would just be perfect to have this on the go. (Android, IOS or NSwitch).\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tYou should probably read this review, because it's one of the only actual reviews for this game.

It's not a review if you just reference Castle Crashers or Battleblock Theater and say you'll like this based on those. No selection of these games even shares a basic genre, let alone deep aspects. Art and humor style aren't the only factors here. I'm very tempted to keep hating on fankids, but I promised a review.

Key TLDR Points
    \t
  • Fun 2-player Co-op campaign mode (local is best)
    \t
  • Plays well with controllers
    \t
  • Puerile humor and absurdities galore
    \t
  • Shallow but still very fun strategy system
    \t
  • "Gotta Catch 'em All" collector aspect for creatures and cosmetics
    \t
  • Casual feel, can be easy, but difficulty has no ceiling, with optional hardcore (permanent death)
  • Fairly short game with relatively little replayabiltity

Because this game is highly stylized, even comparing within the genre, it makes sense to address gameplay and style separately.

Gameplay

Pit People is a turn-based strategy game played on a hexagonal grid. In terms of strategy, it brings nothing new to the table, and is actually highly simplified - to the point where it could easily be a mobile game with almost no changes.

The only action - only - that you can take with each unit each round is to move. You can move your units around the grid to put them in range of the enemy, which varies by what weapon they hold. But then the target is randomly chosen from any target that is legal for that unit.

Everything else is up to deterministic steps that are out of your direct control. While this removes one layer of strategy, it does add an interesting layer of meta-strategy. If you want to guarantee your unit attacks a target, you must try to arrange that unit so that the desired target is the only valid one. This means that positioning is vital. If you're hoping to be able to tell unit X to use ability Y on enemy Z, you'll be disappointed.

There are some other benefits to this simplification. The game's simplicity means that turns can happen pretty quickly. When playing co-op, this is very important. If you want a deep strategy game, there are options. You could play the Heroes of Might and Magic games, for example. Many of them are brilliant. But playing those games co-op has always been a bit of a burden, because one person's turn could take twenty or thirty minutes if they are sucked into a complex combat when you aren't.

Pit People tries to make sure that this never happens. Turns are simultaneous, with overlays showing where each unit will go. One unit's destination can be the hex that a unit is currently on, provided that the target hex's occupant will leave it vacant when moves happen. When all moves are chosen, every unit moves and attacks together (barring some staggering of the animations). This does lead to some confusion, because with overlays showing movements scattered around units that will shortly be elsewhere, you can sometimes lose track of what you're doing. But given the difficulty of making all that information appear on the board while keeping the pace quick, I find that the game's solution admirable.

There are a few special abilities possessed by a few units, but they're mostly pretty usual stuff. A percent chance to stun, for example. But there are a few interesting gems, like the Spidaur's ability to block movement if it is exactly two hexes away from an obstacle.

You collect new and ever more bizarre team members by a somewhat tedious and annoying capture system, but the fun of collection is still a strong drive in the game. Collection of creatures and ridiculous gear (pizza-slice shields, traffic cone hats, absurd haircuts) is one of the more fun aspects of the game. While fun variety of cosmetic choices exists for most units, I found myself wishing there were more creatures and gear choices of combat significance to be made, and more cosmetic choices for the non-human units.

Style
The art style is easily seen in the game's store page. It's highly consistent all throughout the game.

The humor of Pit People is a mixture of the absurd (which I like) and puerile (which I put up with). Units have personal names like Parallelogram or Staples, and part of the game's premise is that the world has collided with one giant space-faring bear, and faces the antagonism of another one, whose invasive commentary and nagging is a large part of the game's humor.

For me personally, none of it makes or breaks anything. I'd feel about the same about the game if it had taken a serious attitude. I left toilet humor on the playground, and have never understood how so many adults never did. Style doesn't do anything for me on that level.

But for those who really like it, and always wished there was more Cards Against Humanity in the world, you will really appreciate it, and will enjoy yourself thoroughly. Good for you, but perhaps not all. The game's visual and humor style is the one its most consistent and pervasive aspects, and will turn some players off completely. Those players deserve to know in advance that the poop jokes are coming, and that your units WILL wind up with with names like Infinifart and Buttcake, and that you cannot rename anything ever.
----------
Personal Note
I do want to mention that I'm troubled that the game doesn't seem to include any people of color. Whatsoever. While the generically paper-white appearance of the characters could be argued as neutral, I have to disagree with the stance. All nonhuman creatures in the game feature color. Only humans feature this generic appearance, making it seem specific. The fact that white is a presumed default is precisely the problem. They probably just forgot about it, which is kind of the problem.
