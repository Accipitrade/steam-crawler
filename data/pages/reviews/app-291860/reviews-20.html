\r\n\t\t\t\t\tFire Emblem, with all the strategy traded in for comedy.

So, this game frustrates me, because I want to like it. The writing is consistently hilarious, character designs are excellent or ridiculous to support the comedy, and the locations are wonderful and varied. But then you start a battle, and you notice that after deciding where to move allies, your turn's over. That's it. No weapon selection, no accuracy details provided, and barely any indication given that an attack will deal more or less damage. Mind, I played this game both alone and with co-op, and found the problems consistent across both types of play, though it's even more prominent in co-op.

So, my issue with this game is that EVERYTHING in a battle is predetermined by what units you have and what equipment you gave your units at your home base. To elaborate, you rarely have an idea of WHAT enemies you're going to be fighting, and unlike fire emblem games where battles follow a chapter system, this game opts for an overworld for grinding enemies for experience and money. So, going into a story or optional quest, you might have picked a team that's balanced and been working well so far, but then you learn that the ambiguous sidequest marker on the map is a battle full of zombies, and your team deals less damage to zombies. There as no way of knowing this beforehand, and the distance to objectives is randomized thanks to a mechanic where the world map is shuffled every few times you leave the home base. I understand the appeal of a randomized world or at least a few variations of one, but it makes the experience feel almost roguelike and less like you have any say in a battle's outcome until you've already arrived and lost, then returned with more appropriate units.

Archers and ranged units consistently miss their mark thanks to an undisclosed accuracy stat (which is never directly shown), which melee fighters more or less ignore; it makes ranged fighters borderline useless, since they lose mobility in exchange for attack range. You can get through a large part of the game by making a team of shield-bearing tanks with various weapons and hoping RNG decides to let you block incoming attacks. This isn't helped because you don't decide who your units attack - it occurs automatically, and frequently results in your units ignoring enemies close to death in favor of other foes in range that are predetermined to be higher-priority targets, like healers or glass cannons. This isn't like XCOM's RNG or missing shots in Steamworld Heist, you have literally no control over units in battle outside of giving movement orders.

I'm really frustrated by this, because there's clearly a lot of love put into the animations, the art, and the unique ideas behind each race and unit type. However, there's so much RNG burying everything that it ruins the entire experience for me. This isn't a roguelike, battles in quests are scripted and have the same units every time. Replaying quests with different units after learning what the enemy has is a valid strategy; it just requires you to reach a battle you have no chance of winning first. I'd be okay if the RNG was removed, and hard values were used instead. Let me gamble with a mortar weapon that WILL hit any of my allies in range, don't give the weapon a high chance of missing, then a chance to hit random surrounding tiles after that calculation. Since unlike XCOM, your fighters are almost always densely packed at all times save for a few ranged units, at least let me know what the chance of success is instead of just a 'warning' that the attack might miss.

The game features a leveling system which is simultaneously broken and not good enough. Leveling up units fully heals them mid-combat, but you can't always predict the order that units act in, like in Fire Emblem. Each level only adds about 1% to each stat a character has, which sounds nice, until you realize that most characters never eclipse dealing 20 damage with attacks. After playing for ~23 hours, I have no frame of reference for how much health units have, or what stats enemy units ever have, since the game prefers to use heart icons instead of hard numbers when inspecting your units (see screenshots 7 & 8 next to the trailer). Likewise, unless you hover over your stat meters, the game never tells you specific details for what your attack, defense, and miscellaneous stats are. I get this is to make the game more approachable, but it's not hard to add a simple number next to the meter to give a better idea of what's happening. Enemy stats are never displayed, forcing you to learn the game's mechanics before making a wild guess as to what stats an enemy might have; this would be interesting, if the game did a better job of letting you examine units you own, other than vague survival guide descriptions and meters.

That being said, I do have praise for this game.

I LOVE the idea of every unit being able to act at once, or following orders in quick succession based on the order you've given your orders. It speeds up the game significantly compared to other strategy games I've played, and keeps the pace up even in longer battles. I feel frustrated that I can't plan out actions based on this, since there's RNG attached to ally attacks that move enemy units (Cyclops and Hair Trolls), and only two types of recruit-able units provide team mobility options (Gnomes and Spidaurs). Learning which units are strong against fire damage and pairing them with fire-wielding allies is a fun rush, but there isn't much depth after you learn there isn't a reliable way to deal damage of every type of element without depending on specific units and weapons, which is a strange decision. Many units can't equip weapons or armor, which led me and friends to play with teams of mostly humans due to their versatility compared to other races.

The writing has carried me for the length of the game I've played so far, and I'll probably end up finishing it since it's just that good. It just bothers me that the gameplay is such a slog compared to the Behemoth's previous games, and they've poured their hearts and souls into the goofy artwork for the game. Environments are frequently strange, with specific arenas frequently designed around a joke or gag. I just wish the moment to moment gameplay was the same quality as the writing and art...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tGreat sense of humor, story telling and funny characters. Love some of the special quests when clearing the maps. At first it was way too simple and the turn based combat not very exciting, but once you switch the hard mode and go into hard mode arena, the game shines.

You can see the thought that goes into positioning, special teams, utility, countering and bonus damage. The character combos from the 8 vs 5 AI can create a challenge (though the AI also misses opportunities that allow you to win when you should not have. ) Making a team that can counter just about everything was a lot of fun too. I just wish more players played the arena.

Best to play blind without looking anything up. Everything you need is in the F1 booklet.

Even after completing the game I continued to clear maps and do achievements and I log in periodically to collect items and colorful units.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis game had a lot of work put into it. The people over at Behemoth worked really hard on the art, story, and jokes. And those are all great. But the combat is just... It's not good.

The biggest problem with this game is the genre. It's a turn-based strategy game with absolutely no emphasis on... Strategy. All you're allowed to do is move characters around. That's it. You can't target enemies; your characters just attack whoever they feel like. You can't use special abilities. You can't tell a unit to block instead of attacking. You can't even use items. All you can do is move. As such, it doesn't feel like a strategy game at all. It feels like a slapping fight.

But you can't even beef your characters up for a slapping fight. The equipment does not improve your characters at all. Leveling a character gives them a .24% increase to health and attack per level. So at max level, you can have around 24% increase in health and damage. So two level 1 characters could easily kill one level 99 character. That's it. So what you start with is what you're going to finish with.

I think the problem here is the genre. Behemoth made a Tactical RPG. Admittedly, it's more of a "party" TRPG, but it's still in the genre. And most people consider Final Fantasy Tactics to be the golden image of Tactical RPGs. FFT had stand alone levels, jobs, gear, and attacks that were all very specific. You could make your character overpowered by finding gear, grinding job levels, and forming synergy between units. Those are the appealing factors for this genre. But when you compare Pit People against FFT, it absolutely cannot compete. It has none of the appealing features that TRPG players are searching for.

I love the team at Behemoth and hope they continue to make games. But they shouldn't make TRPG games.\t\t\t\t\t
