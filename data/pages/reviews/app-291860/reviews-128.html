\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPit People is a fun tactical strategy game, but it has some strange mechanics that, while relatively unique, often fall rather flat.

Damage tends to stay very low in Pit People, which can be a problem because unit health doesn't seem to follow the same standard. Taking out an enemy in a single turn is near impossible, and even healers are surprisingly tanky, which can end up making many fights into battles of attrition around a single choke point.

All unit targetting is completely automatic, as a unit will target any enemy within range at random with priority given to closer targets when applicable. There are ways the player is expected to manipulate this, including making sure only a single target is within your range or flooding an enemy's range with minions and such to mess with their targetting, but it comes off as more annoying than anything else. However, there are some clever uses of this random targetting that make up for it, like one unit who can make people target other units on their team making enemy AoE units completely backfire and splash onto their own units, but those are far too chance based for my liking.

There's a vaguely Disgaea-esque distiction between human and monster units, where human units have a large amount of customization for their stats and equipment while monsters are limited to a single or often no customization option/s. That being said, most of the customizations boil down to damage+dodge or defense+block for melee, and damage or number of shots for ranged characters.

Probably my biggest qualm with the game though is the fact that it is nearly impossible to see numbers on ANYTHING besides damage and status infliction. How much defense does my Gorgon have compared to my Mushroom? Who knows, one bar is slightly taller than the other! How much health does my Archer have? 4 and a half hearts worth! How much is a heart worth? Who knows! How much health does that Fairy have left? Somewhere between 1/4 and 1/3, and that's all you get to know! EDIT: As it turns out, most of this can be found out if you mouse over the stats in the customization menus.

Despite all my mechanic complaints, Pit People is still a very fun game, but don't go into it expecting the height of strategic gameplay or you'll be pretty disappointed.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPossibly one of the best turn-based X-Com style strategy games already, with an easy-to-learn hard-to-master combat system and an extremely addictive """recruitment""" system in which you find the bloke with the funniest name and store him in your """home""" just because they're called "Meme" or "Wonkydong".

Visually, the game retains the novelty of the iconic Behemoth art style even after all these years, already putting it far ahead of many games - especially in the AAA market - which lack any kind of personality. Everything is lively and original, just like Castle Crashers or BattleBlock Theather.

The music is quite something, too! Again, as expected from a top tier indie developer, many of the tracks really stick out as something out there and original with a much smaller emphasis on orchestral compositions and a much larger focus on combining unique sounds you don't hear every day or using singular instruments which really stand out.

Of course, since this is an Early Access game on Steam in 2017, you can easily expect one of two things about the dev support: Either the dev commits fully to noting every single mistake, responding to bug reports from their players and working with them on fixing said bugs and mistakes OR they couldn't care less and leave the game in Early Access for years to come. I'm extremely pleased to say that Pit People's dev team lands itself in the former category! I've personally reported a few bugs (/ grammar mistakes) and had the devs list the problems on their site then fix them within upcoming patches and work with me to find the root of a bug more complicated than spellchecking.

Overall I can see an extreme passion for this game from the fantastic dev team and can't see it dropping below "Overwhelmingly Positive" in the near future. Definitely recommended if you enjoy strategy, witty dialogue and charming visuals!\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTake this with a grain of salt as I played this for less than an hour.

This game was on the top of my wishlist for a very long time before it came out as an early-release title. I love hex strategy games and thoroughly enjoyed Castle Crashers, so I figured combining a company that made a game I liked with a genre I liked was a no-brainer.

Unfortunately, I ended up putting in for a refund for two main reasons:

1. The game is a console port and it shows. The mouse and keyboard controls are extremely bad. If you're willing to play with a controller this may be less of an issue for you, but strategy games play pretty badly on a controller in the first place.

2. It's actually EXTREMELY casual. There's a super-hard mode available but it doesn't make the fundamental gameplay any less casual. The only command you give your characters is where to move. ALL other decisions are handled by the AI, including which character or characters within range to attack if multiples are present. What type of action is taken is handled in a relatively intelligent manner (the AI doesn't attempt melee attacks when out of range for example), but the bottom line is still that the only choice the player has is where to move.

The writing and art are great as expected from Behemoth, but I just can't recommend it as a strategy game when so few options are available to the player.\t\t\t\t\t
