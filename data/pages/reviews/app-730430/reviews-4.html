The clearest indication of how unique and unexpected this game is: one of the game's events is an entire freakin' musical - perfectly scored, written and sung, and totally pertinent to the plot. It's like Suzerain but 10x better.

I'm not even very far into this game but I can't rave enough about how incredibly polished and enjoyable it is: the graphics, the UI, the writing and the voice acting are all absolutely TOP notch. I was definitely not expecting this kind of AAA quality from a $15/£12 game.

The gameplay is similar to Suzerain, if you've played that: as President of your country (in this case, the USA rather than a fictional nation), you encounter largely text-based events where you must make decisions about your policies and alliances. The tone of the game is more satirical than Suzerain: it's still serious, but very much a wry commentary on the populist nature of Western politics today. What's remarkable is it largely manages to do this without picking sides, and it equally satirises both left and right.

But the gameplay has several other unique features that elevate it even further: to start with, the game introduces you very early to a larger storyline/plot that only you're privy to, and which is meant to guide your decisions throughout the events/encounters. I've rarely seen this in a game of this nature: you're not just mindlessly making decisions without an endgame in sight, but rather asking yourself how every decision you make contributes to your end goal.

Unlike Suzerain, you also have more control over your own Cabinet and how you use your staff, and you can even collect certain items that can be useful later on. This hybrid of RPG elements with choice-based text-heavy events feels absolutely unique and adds so much unexpected depth to the game.

But perhaps the most impressive aspect is the writing: it may not matter to some, but it's written in such flawless, perfect English that is rare to find in a lot of games today, and the story and characters are fleshed out without being complex. It's a genuinely immersive experience that doesn't force you to read thousands of words of background.

Have I said it contains a WHOLE FREAKIN' MUSICAL?!

Biggest surprise of any game in the last year for me. 100% recommended to anyone who appreciates great game design.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAfter a promising start this game has worn rather thin, and I'm only a quarter of the way through. I'm stopping now because the gameplay systems are beginning to expand in ways that unpleasantly remind me of "We. The Revolution" which was a neat core game that tacked on so many superfluous gameplay systems that it undermined itself, which wasn't helped by the narrative ♥♥♥♥♥♥♥♥ the bed in Act 3.

I do enjoy the core loop of This is the President, if only initially. Managing my subordinates, responding to crises, and all the political finagling one would expect. Unfortunately it's burned me out with the copious amount of random events that pop up during each month/turn. Interrupting my play with two to three events with yes or no answers that are entirely guesswork as to which one is the good one. Did you award the Presidential Freedom to a guy who foiled a terrorist plot with a tip? Too bad, he's the terrorist and framed his friends! It just feels vapid and needlessly punishing, and the game straight up tutorializes save scumming so you are encouraged to go back and fix it. With the catch being that the game saves at the beginning of each month, meaning that events that occurred just before you were planning to end the turn means a good few minutes sitting through unskippable textboxes (which can be sped up but not skipped) and replaying all the events you already did.

As for the humor, sometimes it's great, I loved it for the first couple hours even. But it wears off as the jokes tend to be either "Lol, so random" or "Ha! Politicians suck!" the latter of which seems to be the thesis statement of the game dragged on for far more hours than I care to play. Then the game slaps you in the face with tonal dissonance as a school shooting happens or Chicago has race riots. Neither of which played for laughs but feel strange in a game where you talk the astronaut slated to be the first man on Mars back into the rocket by singing him the National Anthem. But that event ends in tragedy if you fail to pick the single correct set of dialogue options and he gets gunned down by federal agents surrounding his house, and really just encapsulates the game's problem with humor.

Overall, it's a good core idea that stretches itself too thin and slowly wears you down through frustrating design, slow text, a wildly inconsistent tone.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMechanically convoluted, tonally dissonant, and lacking a clear vision of what it wants to be.

If you're looking for a gritty political simulator, you'll be irritated when the game asks you to devote your time to enhancing the reputation of a street drug bearing your Vice President's name, or when you lose reputation for not allowing a budget-driven sci fi blockbuster to shoot inside the White House.

If you're looking for a tongue-in-cheek Mad World presidency, you'll be irritated when the game asks you to tackle school shootings, terrorist incidents, and drug crime.

If you're looking for a competent strategy game, you'll be irritated when an option labeled "host a charity benefit for hurricane relief" actually means "host a charity benefit in the ruins of still-battered town, hindering the ongoing search and rescue efforts," or when voters disapprove of your banning gambling in apps for children.

Ultimately, it's not even clear to me what game the developers *wanted* to make -- only that they've failed to make *any* of those options, and you get to experience all of those failures, all at once.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe reviews don't do the game justice, it's actually pretty good and enjoyable. If you liked Suzerain you'll like this.

This game feels pretty polished in its style, visually it's very rich and pleasant, both in UI and illustrations. It's got nice music too. But most of all it's absolutely, dumb RICH in texts and events. There is so much going on, a single turn takes me about 30 minutes to finish and I love it, I love reading into all the events, they are very well written, and immersing in the minutiae of running the country. Characters are deep and also well written.

The humor doesn't always land but overall it's pretty good, appropriate and not forced. Compared to Suzerain the game feels more light-hearted, and it's its own fun. The fate of the humanity shouldn't always be at stake, sometimes it's amusing to just observe human behavior and what people are capable of.

You'll like this game if you like political simulators, games with a slight tongue-in-cheek style, and visual novels because there is so much great writing to enjoy!\t\t\t\t\t
