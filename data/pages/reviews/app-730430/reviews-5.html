I felt that some of the dialogue options when talking to other leaders was a bit vague, and sometimes I would come across to the other character not as intended due to this.
This game does have a lot of potential, and I'd love to see some updates or DLC's in the future, adding more to the game. (Especially a realistic type mode where you don't have to play out the main story and can run the Presidency essentially how you want with probable outcomes, that's also slightly more educational too about the American political system, and international relations. In other words the game feels a bit too simplistic at the moment.)
I've focused on the negatives but as I said, overall I am happy with the game, and if you like these types, and have an interest in politics it's certainly for you.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really liked Suzerain so the comparison was enough to catch my interest. I'd say on balance I prefer This Is the President (TITP) - there's a different economy aspect, but the game is faster, has much more content, and it's easier to read.

With that inevitable comparison out of the way, I can judge the game on its own merits. I like playing political sims, but I also like playing video games - why this distinction? Because simulators often don't have much, if any, gameplay, as they're designed to simulate reality in a sterile way. A flight sim for example will treat you like a real pilot with lots of different buttons to press, rather than CJ from GTA: San Andreas who presses just one button to get into any vehicle.

TITP puts you in the shoes of a newly elected president, but while simulating this political scenario it also offers you actual gameplay, such as:

  • micro-managing a team of salaried political operators - their pay, their stress levels, their individual demands, and hiring and firing constantly due to a high turnover of personnel
  • using said operators to achieve solutions to problems - whether diplomatically, violently, secretly, or even plain legally. You also have to appoint six of them to positions for passive bonuses
  • maintaining your own private funds - it helps a lot if you take the time out to develop a profitable operation in order to fund everything you need to do
  • maintaining an approval rating - you will be surprised on this one
  • collecting items for immediate use or as back-up for later on
  • making use of the services of outside businesses - some legal, some not
  • enacting domestic policies, pursuing common objectives with foreign heads of state, and delivering on annual promises to congress

Gameplay mechanics are introduced at a moderate pace - usually just one per month - in order to help the player learn these mechanics. By the end of TITP, there is a lot of leverage for the player to make use of, so the choice of pace works for the game's benefit. I played the game on Challenging difficulty and was pleased with how much the game attempted to push me off balance while walking the political tightrope.

Some people seem to think that TITP is simply a President Simulator where you can roleplay as your "perfect President", but unlike sims it has gameplay partly due to its pre-developed plot. Your character has indeed just been elected President, but he has a shady past and its your job to find the solution that this morally grey individual has been seeking. You can still act as the President and pursue reforms that you'd like to see in real life, but everything you do pertains to a single united goal - past Amendment 28 in order to obtain your post-presidential immunity from the law. You can improve healthcare for Americans, you can discourage abortions, you can replace bureaucracy with an efficient AI with datamining algorithms - in the end of each of these, the only actual point of doing them is to achieve a certain approval rating as part of your wider strategy to achieve an ultimate goal. All of these pertain to that one single goal your character got himself elected President for in the first place!

So this is an important aspect of TITP that some people don't pick up on - you are already corrupt! Imagine if Flight Simulator saw you play as a pilot with a drinking problem, a secret affair with his co-pilot's wife, and a sordid history of plane crashes. You wouldn't just be doing 7-hour flights from Heathrow to Dubai, you would also be dealing with all of his many personal issues in order to negotiate his way towards a single, overarching goal in life. This is how TITP introduces gameplay to a simulator-like setting. This isn't President Simulator, it's an actual video game where you must explore every possible political option - whether good, evil, or in between - to achieve your primary goal of passing Amendment 28 and obtaining that legal immunity forever.

The plot therefore has to be good enough to hold your attention throughout a single term of four years (this is roughly 20 or more gameplay hours), and I believe there are enough twists and turns to keep a player interested:

  • the primary antagonist is another shrewd political animal who finds ways to counter you throughout your four years in power
  • your wife is a veritable Lady MacBeth who will never stop surprising you with what she is willing to do to support your ultimate goal in office
  • a perfect balance of humour and seriousness. There's enough to make you laugh, and yet enough to make you take the job of President seriously
  • countless subplots can be manipulated to your own ends - helping an individual here may prove helpful later on, which adds depth and intrigue to the main plot as a whole
  • therefore your actions have long-term impacts on the increasingly involved plot - even a decision you make as President-elect may come back to haunt you in your final year
  • at one stage, you have to consider killing thousands of American citizens just to achieve one tiny step towards your aims!

To use an example of the plot's strength, here's a subplot with very minor spoilers but don't read if you want to go in blind:

I watched as Russia fell to a coup and refused to recognise the new government. I then helped smuggle the ex-President of Russia into the US using a member of my staff - a hitman who also specialises in secret missions. Later on, Russia began provoking a war in a neighbouring (fictional) state, but I had promised to Congress that I would improve US-Russia relations. In order to prevent the war, I agreed to smuggle back the ex-President to Russia where he would become a political prisoner. Again I used my hitman to successfully deport him back. The result? My approval rating went up because I prevented a war and I successfully delivered a promise of improving US-Russia relations.

The most surprising aspect of TITP to me is the humour-seriousness balance. There's no decades-old, tired stereotypes like in the Tropico series - the humour is current, but still willing to mock everyone on all positions of the political spectrum. At the same time, the game is perfectly serious. I'm not an American myself, but I understand the process of effecting Amendment 28 is pretty close to - if not 1:1 with - how the US political system actually works. You need to first start lobbying for a new bill to be proposed by others, you then need to achieve majorities in Congress, you need a majority on the Supreme Court, and then you need to have the entire thing ratified by US states. Reading this back, it's almost as if TTIP is indeed a President Simulator - but believe me, there is plenty of actual gameplay and humour to keep you hooked.

Playing TITP I felt like I could do anything I wanted. I could use Harry Truman's concept of Presidential power to achieve my aims both at home and abroad. Absolutely everyone was at my mercy. I actually felt like the most powerful man on the planet - after all isn't this the universally-popular opinion of what a US President actually is?\t\t\t\t\t

This is fundamentally, at its core, a text-based narrative something similar to the Goosebumps books where "if you chose option A go to page 92, else go to page 104" with cool JPEG's and animations and SOME voice acting. You do not have much choice in anything really. See the rest for the reason why. Else, I do not recommend it if you think this is a simulation game where you act as president, because it is not.

The rest is spoiler of the plot:

I thought that this game would be a cool simulation-type game where you chose what happened and whatnot, but no, it is a forced plot-line from the beginning and you do get "choices" but they either lead to successfully completing the story-line or not. If you do not like being a villain, a hypocrite, and basically not having much choice then this game is not for you. You do not get to be a "good guy" because the story-line is literally you being a crook yourself escaping prison. So hypocrisy pseudo-simulator 2022. I literally feel like this is Trump. Should have been called "This is President Trump".

I HONESTLY FEEL this is to appeal to Trump and those that wanted to see Trump's plans go through. Literally. A Trump simulator and that's being honest.
Fair warning that I do not like Trump, but I do not like Biden too much either. I'm independent. But it doesn't take an idiot to know Trump is a man-child and Biden is your grandpa's grandpa.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI really liked the idea behind this game and went in expecting to enjoy it, but was ultimately disappointed. The premise is that you're some sort of corrupt business executive who, to avoid being prosecuted for your various crimes, decided to become President of the United States and pass an amendment that would give you lifetime immunity. It's an absurd plan, but that's clearly deliberate, and I did find the idea amusing when I started playing. However, the humor ended up falling flat for me- it basically felt like someone shouting "Haha, politicians suck!" and stretching that one joke out for 20+ hours. I keep hearing the game called a satire, but it's hard for me to even see it as that- satire is something that uses humor to make a point, while this game seems to be going out of its way to avoid actually saying anything. It doesn't give any meaningful criticism of any person, institution, or ideology, and even its bashing of politicians in general starts to feel pretty toothless given just how over the top and absurd the in-game events are. I obviously don't know the developers' thought process, but the whole time I was playing I was left with the impression that they were so focused on not offending anyone who might disagree with them that they ended up creating a really shallow and hollow parody.

Then there's the gameplay. As others have pointed out, it shares some similarities with Suzerain in that much of it plays out as a visual novel with you making choices on how to respond to various events. Most of the correct answers feel completely random though, and there's very little true strategy- it's mostly just trial and error. The worst part for me was the "yes/no" options that pop up between tasks. You'll usually get 2-4 quick events after completing each task, with the choice to decided whether or not to commit to a course of action that the game presents you with, and you'll get a small reward or penalty depending on whether you pick the right one. I absolutely HATED these- it was complete guesswork with no rhyme or reason as to which option was correct. You decide to reward someone who helped find a group of terrorists? Well, turns out that he IS the terrorist and framed those people! The game probably thinks it's being funny, and maybe it would have been if this happened two or three times in the game, but you'll be dealing with dozens of these choices throughout your playthrough. About halfway through the game, I stopped reading them and just chose "yes" every time they came up, since there was no point in me actually trying to figure out which option was right.

This game had potential, but I can't recommend it. There were some parts I enjoyed, such as finding ways to sway congressional representatives to vote for my amendment or plotting to replace supreme court justices, and it makes me feel like the game could have been great if it took itself just a little more seriously- it could still be comedic and it could still make me play a villain, but at least let me have fun being a manipulative bastard instead of mostly just clicking an option and crossing my fingers. Ultimately, I found it to be a tedious, frustrating, shallow experience, and it gets a thumbs down from me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tTo be clear: this game is not a "simulator game". I find it difficult to place neatly into a single genre and while I'm tempted to use the term "choice-based interactive novel" I feel like that leaves the wrong impression.

Story:
I don't want to just repeat the game description: President, shady past, goal of passing amendment to escape justice. You get it. My feeling was that it mostly came across like if "House of Cards" was written less as a thriller and more as a satirical comedic tragedy, if you can imagine that. It ranges from very dark (pushing journalists in front of trains) to very outrageous (weird cult worshiping the vice president).

Politically this game is kind of all over the place, and nothing feels like it's off limits. Many situations you deal with are clearly based on real world events and the game is not afraid to make a mockery of these as you manipulate the government to serve your own ends. Yes, you are intended to play as a corrupt politician and no there is not really a viable option to play it straight.

Overall, I found the storytelling in this game very entertaining. Some of the stories made me laugh out loud and the characters are written in a charming way that made me sad to see them go at the end. A special mention would be the final act of the game where it really cranks the outrageous factor to 11 and delivers some truly hilarious and memorable moments.

Gameplay:
The entirety of the gameplay revolves around making political decisions as president (big suprise). These take three main forms:
1. Pop-ups: A message will appear and ask you a yes or no question that you must answer to advance. Usually something along the lines of "A clothing retailer started using your likeness and campaign slogans on their t-shirts, should we press them for compensation?"
2. Assignments: These are one of two cards that appear on the side of the screen with a timer that tells you how many months they will be available. They have some pretty meaty flavor text that will explain a situation and request members of your team with specific abilities. It's usually pretty obvious what team members to send but occasionally will throw in some curveballs like "We'll need a tech guy to set up the website and someone who loves nature." These resolve the next month after you've assigned them and tell you the outcome.
3. Situation missions: This is the other kind of card that appears on the side of the screen. With these you choose a few of your team members to bring with you into a situation and take context specific actions to advance the story. Based on who you bring you will be able to do actions like "Launch a social media campaign accusing your opponent of harassment" , "Break into your opponent’s house and try to find incriminating evidence", or "File a lawsuit against your opponent". These are really fun and make you think carefully about what the best approach to a situation is.

The rewards for successfully completing these is either money, approval rating, or items (used to increase approval rating or sold for money). They really facilitate the games writing which is the main draw of the game. There is a LOT of reading in this game so be warry of that if you are scared off by long paragraphs of text.

The team managing aspect is where the strategy of the game takes place. Each month you have to pay your members a salary and manage their stress points. If they gain 5 stress points, then they burn out and you can’t use them until next month. If you do that too many times, then something… bad might happen. You can also assign them to positions in your administration that each have different effects depending on the specialist. One press secretary might increase approval rating by 3% each month while another will decrease approval by 3% but lower team member stress gain. It’s a fun system that feels rewarding when you find a really good setup.

Overall + TLDR:
This is the President is an entertaining mostly text-based story game where you play as a corrupt politician. Thematically it blends satirical comedy and noir fiction with interesting stories and charming characters. The writing is fun, and the gameplay feels interesting even when there are a few “Gotcha!” outcomes from some of the decisions. The UI and soundtrack are nice and don’t start to offend even after a bit more than 20 hours of playtime. If you enjoyed either of the This is the Police games this is very much in the same style. Overall recommend 8.5/10
