\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a game for a specific type of player. One that likes a narrative, with some variation, some quick thinking, some puzzle solving and people management. Replay is small, unless you just like trying out differing things (I do) but nothing major really changes unless you completely fail.

If you are worried this is going to smack too hard of real world politics, it doesn't much. Sure you can draw parallels but it doesn't try to smack you in the face with it as much as you might fear.

You're choices do matter and that can effect things later on in the game and managing funds can be a royal pain but I never like rage-quitting.

All I can say is that if the price is right, if you like this sort of game you'll enjoy it. If you are on the fence about it wait for a sale. No matter what, you'll have some fun.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe game is made from what is considered to be in the USA, "a leftist platform," while this game isn't entirely bad, and has some adult humor that is quite funny. Additionally, not a negative, the construction of the game is like a "choose your own adventure book" style.
However, the point where I had to call it a night on the game was when they brought the Migrant Caravans into the fray. I chose the option that best matches my view point, and the one that matches the US Constitution, and the US as being a nation with laws. Additionally, there was a "riot in Chicago", and when I aided the city to maintain order, and just like the Caravan issue, I lost points. I don't have high hopes for the Texas Separationist challenge since I tried to send an attorney, a journalist, my press secretary, and I was denied the ability. I was told, "they need someone who can physically intimidate."
So, needless to say, as a Houston, Texas resident, one who witnesses the effects of a failing real US border, and real cartels, and real migrant caravans piling in over the Rio Grande I have a few issues with some of the story lines.
I am not saying not get the game. But, if you don't like what is going on IRL, you may not like some of the choices you will be forced to make if you want to gain points.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tOn paper, this game sounds great. It's like "Scandal" where the politics play a secondary role to the damage control/spin of covering up the players' dirty little secrets. I went into this game honestly wanting to love it, but I encountered some issues I could not overlook.

First and foremost are things like spelling and grammar. I saw at least one "United Stated" misspelling, which is a problem in a text-heavy game where you are the President. I also saw instances where a character was referred to by both genders within the same paragraph, which can be confusing since there are so many different people to keep track of. These are little things, yes, but all the more reason they should have been ironed out before release.

The main component of this game involves assigning minions to various tasks. Each employee has multiple ways to accomplish a task. While this is cool in theory, you are given no indication of their odds of success or how difficult a challenge will be against their skills. A difficulty indicator (something like "3 out of 5 difficulty" or "60% chance of success") would go a long way to informing a player's decision-making processes, especially since another key component is monitoring our staffers' stress levels to keep them from burning out.

These people work for the President of the United States, so one would think they are the best of the best in their fields. Or at least highly competent. But failure strikes in weird places. I'll give an example. A journalist was suspected of obtaining classified files from my administration. I sicced my hacker on him and chose the option of hacking the journalist's laptop remotely. The hacker failed this task every time, even though it sounded like it would be right up his alley. Later on, there is an option to have this same hacker pilot a drone with explosives to murder someone who is blackmailing you. The hacker always succeeds at this, which is baffling to me since this seems like it would be a much harder task by comparison.

It seems like choices are linear in the sense that there is only one ending for each option. Only a success or failure ending has been written for each employee's option, not both. This goes against the very nature of the "play as you want" philosophy the game offers.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI haven’t finished it yet, but now I am almost halfway through it and feel that I need to give some thoughts. I’ll add extra thoughts when I am finished
I recommend it, but there’s a big IF attached.

You will enjoy this game IF:
  • You have existing familiarity with previous entries, This is the Police or This is the Police 2. So you are comfortable with the mechanics of the personnel cards, dragging them onto event slots to deal with them and managing your account balance.
  • You love reading
  • Fine with the illusion of choice, (I mean the illusion of somewhat choice, as you are able to make decisions, just not all of them)
  • Aren’t turned off by having to restart a month here or there because you misclicked or your underlings completely stuffed up the assignment you gave them
  • Ok with agreeing/disagreeing to decisions not having a clue on what the outcome might be on your wallet or your ratings
  • Having to tweet thinly veiled elusions to something you partake in constructed out of prompts instead of being able to go “Nearly got me First Lady Cancelled after she scrawled in a priceless book that went up for auction. Lol.” or I have no idea what I am doing, why did you vote for me?? XD
  • You like having to resort Moon Logic to determine who you send into a situation when it isn’t clearly hinted in the brief how to tackle it. Even when you think your pick might be on the money, be prepared to be proven wrong such as “No, Ollie I won’t be sending in the orphans from that orphanage that you help out at to infiltrate a hippie cult – possibly linked to terrorism – in the middle of nowhere just so I can recover the Director of National Intelligence’s son whose vote in Congress I’ll need to progress ARE YOU OUT OF YOUR MIND?! I JUST WANTED YOU TO HAVE A SURVEILLANCE OPTION, LIKE WITH A DRONE?! REMEMBER HOW YOU CAN DO THAT?! ~THAT’S HOW WE OFFED THE GUY AT THE START~!”. However, if you are getting wacky options that straight up lead to burnout, you may want to consider aborting, because sometimes your staff can't detect anything and it may lead to their untimely death.

Money is always going to be an issue as you progress. I just got over a hump where I nearly went permanently bankrupt because I forgot I could continue to reclaim my fortune after not doing it for several turns. Remember that the stress levels dealt to your staff when they go on a mission can be lowered and raised depending on who you have appointed to which position. This can lower the expenses of having to pay for extra services to fix up your team.


Couple of things are a bit off with the experience too:
  • I am not facing the press, who I was expecting to barrage me with questions about most of the stuff that goes wrong during my presidency.
  • Despite appointing my staff to positions of power, I can still send them on false passports out of the country without them getting spotted? Surely in this day and age someone would have to notice.
  • I don’t like the monthly increments in gameplay so much, mainly because of how the revenue and wages are distributed in massive chunks. I was expecting to have more control on when I could receive my money and when I could pay my staff, down to the day. Being able to restart a month is fine, I would’ve enjoyed this if I had to manage the money and pencil in my meetings to see foreign leaders and mayors, etc. This point still stands if Archie is going to doom me to another presidential term in an attempt to ruin me.
  • Air Force One does not fly around the map when you travel to a mission, nor do regular airplanes fly when you send off your staff to do things. It made me imagine that everyone is just walking/swimming to where they need to be.
  • Music is on a loop and does not reflect the situation you are reading. I think it would've been cooler if you could select one of several podcasts which integrate on the choices (through splicing different audio snippets) which you made on the previous month as you do the next month, to play in the background as you figure out who to send where next.
  • Would've liked a better summary of what my appointed staff were providing in terms of Stat boosts/burdens to my money/rating/stress/special missions. Would've made gameplay a lot easier to deal with if I had known the reason why my missions were stressing out my team members by 3 to 5 were partly to blame on my own mismanagement, maybe I would've tried shuffling my positions around.
