\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThere's a lot of nostalgia here. I've been playing the Mega Man series since they originally released on NES and I remember how pumped I was when the "X" variety was rebooted for the SNES (I even had a centerfold poster from a Game Pro magazine for MMX that I hung on my wall for years). This was all long enough ago that I was renting these titles from Blockbuster and Albertsons. Then when MMX4 came out on PlayStation, I was even more excited to see franchise continue and grow beyond the original Nintendo platform.

To the franchise veterans, these games are exactly as you remember them (although a bit smoother. I think you'll notice this more on X4 especially when triggering enemy immunity using the Z-Sabre) and the bundle also includes the Japanese release versions as well (the toggle for these is in the screen tips at the bottom of the game select menu). It also includes a 3-round challenge mode, where you square off against two robot masters at once with a limited number of lives and a choice of three special weapons. I appreciate some of the small additions, like various filters (you can play the games in their original, pixelated form, with a smoothing filter, or with an RF progressive scan filter applied) and lots of artwork/action figures showcased in the museum.

To people that might be new to the franchise, this is like opening up a time capsule from an era when side-scrolling action platformers were king. The games consist of an opening stage, followed by your choice 8 robot masters to defeat in whatever order you please. Each one has its own unique element and their levels are based around that element, at times loosely. Each robot master you defeat endows you with a special weapon that is strong against another robot master (usually, this is intuitive, e.g., a fire weapon will be strong against an ice-themed boss). When you've defeated all of them, you are granted access to multi-level zone to fight your way to the final boss. If you like this format/genre and want to check out how it was done in the 90s and early 00s, you can't go wrong. Although... The story is very, very thin. There's enough there to support the premise, but you won't find more beyond that.

With that said, I have some minor gripes. I wish there were more monitor resolutions to choose from (they probably have the common ones in there, but mine was not listed). Tabbing out does not restore full screen. However, pressing alt+enter to toggle windowed and full screen modes will restore it. It would've been nice to include all of the animated cutscenes in the museum as you unlocked them. Lastly, for the X1-X3 games, being able to save multiple passwords would have also been nice.\t\t\t\t\t
