\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIf you liked the Megaman X games for the SNES/PS1 back in the day, or you want to try them on modern hardware, this is a great pack. While the second half of the series is.. lackluster (X5 is averagely good, X6 suffers from extreme rush, X7... inflicts the suffering on you, and X8 is a competent entry), the first four titles are amazingly great (aside from X3 which okay at best, painful at worst). Megaman X was made back when developers didn't expect their players to be idiots who needed hand-holding. You figure things out yourself, you power up X and Zero as you play, and it's all your own skills and ingenuity that does it (unless you look up cheat codes because hey, manual saves didn't exist for the SNES games). Speaking of manual saves, you CAN save and load data for the SNES titles, just like the later PS1/PS2 ones now. That's nice

If you're new to Mega Man in general, Megaman X is honestly a good starting point. You'll miss some interesting backstory context if you skip the Classic games, but the X series just has so much more to do. The controls of all the games (except for X7 and, oddly, X3's air dash) are pretty responsive and tight, and X is pretty agile after you get the Dash upgrade (and fear not, he starts with it in all but the first game, and you STILL can't miss it there). It's a simple, timeless, entertaining formula: You control X (or in some games, his friends), a blue robot based on the original Mega Man who has the unprecedented capability to think, feel, and worry. X's mission is to fight haywire robots who want to take over the world and wipe out humanity. You accomplish this by choosing the 8 Maverick Boss stages in whatever order you want. Each stage is full of platforming and enemies, along with hidden upgrades to power up X. At the end of each stage, you fight its boss, and X earns a new Special Weapon from each boss. Each boss has a hidden (but usually logical) weakness to another Special Weapon, which makes the fight easier. After all 8 bosses are beaten, the final series of stages starts, and beating that ends the game. Been that way since the NES, and it's fun even to this day

The stage design, bosses, weapons, and story differ between all 8 main Megaman X games, but the core elements above are all there. Each game progressively expands on the gameplay, adding new features and characters as time passes

Megaman X: First game, set the formula, introduced X, Zero, the villain Sigma, and set the series' standard for Armor upgrades (as well as the Dash upgrade, which became a default feature after) along with other things like Ride Armors
Megaman X2: Introduced the Ride Chaser bike, optional side bosses, and minor plot differences based on the player's actions
Megaman X3: Made Zero playable for the first time as an assist character, introduced even more optional armor upgrades dubbed Chips, and massively expanded the Ride Armors
Megaman X4: First game on the PS1 (unless you count the PS1 port of X3, no idea which came first..), introduced animated FMV cutscenes, more improved graphics, the ability to play the full story as X OR Zero, voice acting (though tbh, Capcom coulda REALLY done better on the cast....), and, as a consequence, shifted Zero's play style to melee combat with learning special techniques from defeated bosses, rather than X's Special Weapons

Each one still feels like a unique experience despite sharing so many base elements, and the pack's seriously worth it whether you're new to Mega Man or not
