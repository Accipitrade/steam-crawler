\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMega Man X Legacy Collection (Vol. 1) is definitely a fine addition to the MMLC Series adding the X-Series (Vol.1 inclues X1-X4) to the Legacy Collection lineup. However, a few shortcomings with the collection itself holds it back meeting the expectations the the Classic Mega Man Legacy Collections had set. Some of these include:

Custom Controls and Controller Support
A main annoyance, although only purely affecting quality of life, is the inability to save custom control schemes between sessions. The triggers must also be used rather than the bumpers for X1-X3, also another quality of life item. This was quite suprising as the Classic MMLC (CMMLC) saves control schemes between sessions as well as making quite accessible in the Main Options before entering any game.

Input Lag (partially me whining cause I suck.)
A major gripe with the MMXLC is the is noteable input lag, for me the issue was mostly in X1 and X4 and whilst X2 and X3 had some is wasn't as noticable as the other two titles. This significantly effects the gameplay of the collection as many platforming sections that require precision were made much harder than they should have been. X1: Sigma's Fortess 1 and X2: X-Hunter Stage 2 are repeat offenders.

Gave Saves
In contrast to the Classic Mega Man Legacy Collection, MMXLC makes use of the password systems from the original games to save and store progress made. This comes with minor impacts on gameplay in the final stages as progess though final stages and secret weapons, Hadoken and Shoryuken , are not saved in the password system and must be recompleted/reobtained. This is a large change from the CMMLC allowing the player save in any location, though being quite cheesey acting as a 'save state' it brought alot of flexability to saving progess. MMXLC does supplement the 'save states' with a less forgiving Rookie Hunter Mode to a degree but misses out on the flexability of progess saving system of CMMLC.

Faithful Repoduction?
The MMXLC does a very nice job of emulating the X-Series including alot of the quirks and gimmicks imposed by the limits of the original systems (SNES and PS1). However, this leads to uncomfortable and sometimes unwanted time periods where the game would slow to a snails-pace during combat or having a large amount of entities on screen. MMXLC also stays faithful to many iconic quirks I've associated with the series growing up like the the cutting of background tracks to make way for other SFX. An option to toggle these would be quite nice.

Overall, the Mega Man X Legacy Collection, dispite its flaws, brings new life to the X-Series with the inclusion of Rookie Hunter mode for people new to the Mega Man franhcise and X Challenge for the veterans and long-time Megaman X fans. After some tweaking to how the games run and perform on less limiting systems such as PC and other consoles. Shortcomings aside, Mega Man X Legacy Collection definitely haas the potential to be a stellar nostalgic hit but is not quite complete though I still recommend it to newcomers or verterans to the X-Series alike whether it be a physical or digital copy of the game.

Other Notes: Review does not cover MMXLC Vol. 2 and X-Challenga Game Mode.
