\r\n\t\t\t\t\tMega Man X was actually the first Mega Man game I played - many years ago - but due to the high level of difficulty, I wasn't able to complete it. The final boss, Sigma, has 2 forms and both can be challenging (near impossible) if you don't know his attack patterns or have all the items in the game to maximize your health and armor potential. The final form, in fact, used 3/4 of my energy tanks as I tried to wear him down.

There is a glitch in this port of the game's programming, which does not allow all 4 energy tanks to be used and I believe it's a problem found in most emulators according to net discussions I've read.

Mega Man X - as well as the rest of the Legacy collection follows the same flow as the previous Mega Man games. You are given an introductory stage to learn the controls. You must fight 8 Robot Masters, steal their signature weapons, and then use those weapons against the individual robot masters in an order relative to which master is weak to which weapon. Nothing has really changed from the original game's archetype except the presentation.

Graphics and Sound in the Mega Max X Legacy are typical of the SNES. They are orchestral, heavy on bass and genuinely good to watch and listen to. Many of the chip tunes are catchy and will stick with you even when the individual robot masters do not. Many of them are rehashes of older robot masters from the classic NES games - only with superior graphics, sound and presentation.

Mega Man X games have taken on a more Japanese "anime"-ish style and by the time you reach X8, you may feel visually overwhelmed.

Control is tight across the legacy collection. I was using an Xbox controller while the game was designed for an SNES controller.

In Mega Man X, I found it impossible to do the Hadouken fireball using the D-pad, yet the analog stick made it easily repeatable- although - mildly awkward since I use the D-pad for movement rather than the analog stick. The D-pad was unresponsive when attempting this move.

In Mega Man X2, I found it impossible to do the Dragon Punch without the joystick. After grinding for an hour to get it, I completely neglected to use it in the boss rush or against Sigma.

X3 has some very difficult bosses, stages and sections where you're forced to fight multiple bosses without being given much health recharge. The game has been precision engineered to be "hard".

X4 is colorful and a little less difficult than X3. I appreciate it when the "normal mode" is fair enough that you can beat individual stages using the default X-buster weapon. This may be my FAVORITE X game of the X franchise. It feels well balanced and the boss characters are memorable.


The worst of the X games is arguably X7 (part of the Legacy Collection 2). all the initial X games are good games - if not repetitive. Each game has its own gimmicks. Some gimmicks appreciated while others aren't.

I find playing through these games to be a nostalgia fix. Binge playing 30 YEARS worth of Mega Man titles in just a few months has basically ADDICTED ME to Mega Man.

They are all "Nintendo Hard" - far more difficult than you'd expect games targeting kids to be. The amount of logic and muscle memory required to solve the challenges in the game would be IMPOSSIBLE without reading game magazines/help faqs or watching others play the game.

If there's any true overarching criticism I have for these games, it's that they feel repetitive and unnecessarily difficult. But those two tropes defined the classic gaming eras - between 8 and 16 bit - so in that respect, it's expected.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThe Mega Man X series has always been my favorite series in the Mega Man franchise, and having all the Mega Man X games, from X1-X8 in a convenient, modern package is perfect. (Because oh my goodness, good luck buying physical copies of the original games, especially X2 and X3)

The visual filters are hit and miss (under NO circumstance should you choose the "smooth" filter, it just looks wrong, I recommend the CRT filter), and the choice of wallpapers are a bit inconsistent (I suggest using the X Challenge for a wallpaper/border that can work across all games), but of course, the real star of the show are, the games.

Legacy Collection 1 offers Mega Man X1-X4, and they are all fantastic games (with X4 being my favorite, and X3 being, well, X3), and it is worth mentioning that the version of Mega Man X3 in this collection is based off the original SNES release, and not the enhanced PS1 release with an arranged soundtrack and FMVs (the PS1 version of X3 can be found in the 2005 Mega Man X Collection). The addition of the Rookie Hunter mode does help new players ease into the game, but in several instances, Rookie Hunter mode makes the game a little TOO easy, but since it can be turned off on a whim, it's great for those who want to switch the game to the intended challenge, which offers the best of both worlds.
Oh, and for all you achievement hunters out there, this game offers some pretty challenging achievements, it even taught me elements of some of the games that I never knew about!

And then we have X Challenge, where you square off with two Mavericks at once, and some of the combinations you fight are just the stuff of nightmares...This mode is just a novelty (and achievement fodder), and nothing more.
The icing on the cake with this collection is the extensive art gallery and music player for each game, as well as a detailed catalog of any and all Mega Man X related merchandise, which is a very interesting look for those who are curious.

If you're a Mega Man X fan, or a Mega Man fan in general and have wanted to get into the X series, this collection has definitely the best way to jump in!
Now go take down those Mavericks, hunter!\t\t\t\t\t
