\r\n\t\t\t\t\r\n\t\t\t\t
\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI love these games, but this is a pretty bad PC port. It runs like ass in fullscreen mode for me, even though I have a 3.5Ghz Core i5-6600K with 16GB of RAM and two GTX 980s in SLI. That's a lot of processing power to be unable to emulate an SNES game at full speed. For some reason, running in borderless windowed mode instead fixes the performance for the most part, but as others have mentioned there seems to be a small but noticeable amount of input lag. And apparently you can't play the game if you're not connected to the Internet, unless you use a modding tool called "Special K" to spoof online connectivity, which is more than I'm willing to bother with.

Also, it would have been really nice if they had included Maverick Hunter X, which was a great 2.5D remake of MMX for the PSP and included a mode where you could play as Vile. They have the "Day of Sigma" anime short included as an extra, which just makes the lack of the game itself stand out more.

For now, I'm going to refund this game and either buy the Switch version instead, or just wait a while for the PC version to get the bugs ironed out and/or have a price drop.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me preface this review by saying that, as of the date of this review's publication, this collection is unfortunately plagued by several technical issues ranging from random crashes at the bootup screen to low FPS or input delay on rigs that far outranks the recommended specs. Personally, I've only experienced random crashes at startup on my custom-built gaming PC (i7 quad-core CPU, dual GTX 860M SLI GPU), but as of right now, buy the Steam version at your own risk. Now, on to the review of the individual titles you get in this package:

Mega Man X easily makes a strong case for being one of those games that reaches sheer perfection, the fast and dynamic, ever-evolving gameplay that greatly rewards exploration and experimentation in ways the classic series had only flirted with, the rocking soundtrack without a single forgettable track in its entire library, the perfect difficulty that stays engaging without disuading players from trying new tactics... It's something truly special.

The sequel knew what worked and stayed its course while adding an exciting twist of unpredictability to every playthrough through the X-hunter system, ensuring that no two playthroughs played out quite the same, resulting in no less than four optional boss fights, all leading up to the series' first two separate endings.

X3, while introducing promising concepts, such as the ability to play as everyone's favorite SA-rank hunter Zero and the expanded ride armor system, unfortunately fell short of expectations for many thanks to its rather simplistic level design, overly punishing enemy design, the underutilization of the previously-mentioned Zero and ride armor mechanics along with a repetitive soundtrack. One thing the game does succeed at is providing a slightly tougher level of difficulty than your average X game, which makes it fill quite a nice niche for those who've played through the original two SNES classics over and over and are looking for something a bit more challenging in the same style. Though this slower, more methodical take on the X series is certainly an aquired taste compared to its speedier counterparts. It is also worth noting that the version of X3 featured in this collection is the SNES original rather than the PS1, Saturn and PC port that featured CD-quality music and animated cutscenes.

X4, at first glance primarily goes for a safer approach than any other X game by simply sticking to the standard Mega Man game progression structure without any real experimentation in terms of mechanics thrown into the mix. This is only from X's perspective though, as X4 finally introduces Zero as a full-time playable character with an entirely unique close-range and mobility-oriented fighting style that is completely unlike anything else seen from the franchise up to this point, and it works out wonderfully despite the radical shift in gameplay structure. The crisp 2D visuals that haven't aged a day are just icing on the cake.

Of course, the core games aren't all the gameplay you're getting here. As trailers made sure to highlight, there is also the X Challenge mode: A one-on-two boss rush bonanza wherein you suit up in a visually overhauled but functionally identical version of the nerfed fourth armor from X5 to take down a wide variety of X's 2D adversaries. You start off with two lives and get to choose between a handful of special weapons and then gets sent on your way to take on three matches back to back with no break or healing inbetween. The first two matches will be identical in both versions of the Legacy Collection but the third will be unique.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me briefly say that I own the US version of this Steam product on a Windows 10 Operating system. I preordered both collections for myself on July 3rd with my own money. I was not given this for free to review.

Ok. On with the review.

First of all, I own all 8 X games in their original versions. I also own all of the original consoles they were released on. I also own Mega Man X Collection for both PS2 and GameCube. I've been playing these games for decades. I know what these games are supposed to play like on original hardware. I'm also a big emulator user as well. I've been using emulators since they first came out. I've also invested over $1,000 in a variety of hardware options to upscale and/or convert these retro games to HD on HDMI TVs using their original consoles.

With that being said. I own a Windows 10 operating system on this PC. I've done a few tweaks to settings but nothing that should improve or hinder performance. All I did was download and installed a DirectX runtime from Microsoft, released in 2010. Then I went into Steam's Big Picture mode and changed some controller settings that are unique to that mode so my controller would be more compatible with this game. Also, FYI, apparently the controller settings that are exclusive to Steam's Big Picture mode also carry over when you play that game outside of Big Picture mode. Those settings solve a lot of controller problems that people have been experiencing with the Steam version.

Once I installed the DirectX runtime, I was able to get X Legacy Collection 1 to play audio. After I changed the settings in Steam's Big Picture mode, my controller was now compatible with the game and was giving me no more problems.

I've also noticed a bug with both collections where if you've already set either collection's screen mode to fullscreen, and you try to launch that collection, that collection's frame rate gets cut in half. To work around this... When you launch either collection and the frame rate is slow, go to the main screen and then select Options>Screen>Screen Mode and then change the mode to something other than fullscreen. Once you've change it, you can change it back and the frame rate will be normal again. However, that fix is only temporary. Because, if you quit either collection, and you had the screen mode set to fullscreen when you did so, you'll just run into the bug all over again when you re-launch that collection. At which point you just do the trick again to fix it. Or you can just have the screen mode permanently set to "Windowed" or "Windowed Full", since those modes don't seem to have this issue.

Outside of that. I tried to reproduce the problems that everyone has been complaining about. And I can tell you right now, I have 0% Input lag, virtually 0% slowdowns, and this is for all setting combinations. Yes. That's right. For Full Screen, Windowed, Windowed Full. And for all 3 different filter types with each of those modes. Even X1, X2, & X3's original slowdowns from the original SNES was improved. Not eliminated, but improved to the point where the original slowdowns are less noticeable.

Now before you think I'm some kind of lucky person, Think again. I own a $2,000+ desktop PC that I built myself. I've also got one of the more recent graphics cards installed from Nvidia. It's not the greatest one out there. But it's still pretty up there in performance.

I will be including links to articles (at the bottom of this review) that will help you if you are having the same problems that I had.

Now on to what I think of this collection.

I love the X series. This series is one of my all time favorite game series from any developer. In this collection, I love the fact that there is an option for different screen filter types, different screen aspect ratios, and different screen resolutions available in the settings. It really does give you a variety of ways to enjoy these classic games.

The extras are a nice touch as well. Lots of pictures, concept art, and a small collection of video trailers for the games from their original homeland of Japan. There are 1 or 2 English trailers featured as well.

As for the gameplay, I don't know what to say here since it plays exactly like the original games (depending on your controller). Everything is perfectly preserved gameplay wise.

There are 2 exceptions to this however.

In Megaman/Rockman X4's Intro, They removed a section of the video where General was reaching his hand out to salute his troops. I guess this was considered to offensive since you could look at it as a Hitler reference from Nazi Germany. I do not think this change was necessary since this edit was not made to any other release of this game in the past. Plus, by making this change, it slows down the pace of the entire video. And yes. The edit was made to both the Japanese and English version of this intro.

All Japanese songs have been replaced with the English music for all Rockman versions of each game. This includes all games. I'm guessing there was a licensing issue here, but only CAPCOM could answer this.

Outside of that, I love this collection. I love this collection a lot! If you are a fan of the X series or a fan of Mega Man as a whole, get this collection. It's fantastic. Just make sure you have a PC that can run the games at full speed.

I give this collection a 9 out of 10.

I'll be slightly altering this review for X Legacy Collection 2 since I have practically the same opinion for both collections. However, Steam won't let me post 1 review for both collections. I have to post the review 2 times. Once for this collection and another time for the 2nd collection.

Here are the links that I promised...

No Sound in X Legacy Collection 1: https://steamcommunity.com/app/743890/discussions/2/1760230157503351374/

How to access controller settings in Steam's Big Picture mode: https://www.howtogeek.com/234427/how-to-remap-buttons-on-your-steam-controller/

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tHey everyone! I have yet to play Legacy Collection 2, but I can say now, those negative reviews, people must be using something slow to have input lag. I had ZERO with an Xbox one Controller....Just throwing that out there. This is a heart felt review and strictly honest. I'm running a 1080GTX with a i7 -7700K@ 4.20GHZ, 16GB Ram, Very little slow down.....Sure, you may have a bit from the snes era, even in the two boss new challenge mode, theirs little slow down.

So, here we go...


Controls for me are responsive, you can change your controls at the title menu of the invidual game. I use an Xbox One controller and can re-program the buttons, honeslty, the best scheme is the LB dash, the rest is the same. I dont know why anyone would use a keyboard, but if you prefer keyboard, then I guess you can try it out. The game in my honest opinion is easier with a controller. No Input lag for me, Not sure what some of the others are complaining about, could be their controller preference, or computer.

Graphics-- Are the same as they use to be, you have filters you can place into the game, HD version, CRT HD version or Original Pixel Art. The Screen size ratio you can use original ratio, stretched out ratio or fit the screen ratio, honestly I go with the Original ratio with the borders, hardly notice. All sizes are respected also.

Soundtrack -- I do have one gripe, I believe in my heart that capcom could of done extra with this and made a remix soundtrack for each of the games, I think that would of been more enjoyable...However, its the original with better quality. I do notice that once a certain threshold of effects are in place, that certain sound effects are drowned out or overwritten, for example Megaman X, his charge ability sometimes loses its "effect" of sound, during moments where enemies may be firing, or the soundtrack picks up, but nothing ground breaking or omg i cannot play this.

Stability-- I have no problem while playing the game, The only issue I had were the game controller freezing out? from when I played it on Steam Link and left it on over night, coming back to it, the controller was not working anymore, but the PC controlls were, again I think thats a Steam Link issue not the Game itself. Other than that I had no crashes, or any performance issue, steady 60FPS 95% of the time, sometimes drops to 55...Again, not ground breaking and not I CANNOT PLAY issue...:mmx_sigma: 9/10


Overall Opinion

If you are a Megaman X fan , and you enjoy the games from the snes-Ps2, then yes, get this game, it also has a X challenge Mode, which is very interesting and challenging, not something you can just pick up and think yeah I got this, Although I recommend trying it with X Buster only, sometimes picking the weapons that special aid may provide an easy experience? they also have Hard mode and easy mode, and story mode,,, its very unique. I personall recommend this game to new and veterans alike.

IF YOU WANT YOUR MEGAMAN X9, GIVE HONEST TO GOODNESS REVIEWS, NOT SOME CHEAP HEARTED CAUSE YOUR MAD REVIEW. :mmx_sigma:
