At the new price point, the game can definitely be recommended.

Its biggest downside is without a doubt that there is very little longevity or replayability. Every single game runs according to the same scheme, almost round after round. After having finished the original campaign and tried all heroes, I really don't feel the need to play the new campaign or an endless mode.

Talking about heroes, they are really a missed opportunity. They all play almost identical. An HP sponge at your command that dishes out loads of damage. It would have been really great if the choice of your hero also influence your other units, building selection, etc.

Still, even with the downsides, I got about 20 hours of enjoyable survival/tower defense mix.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAn otherwise easy-to-pick-up but hard to master RTS and Tower
Defense hybrid suffers from an undersexualized aesthetic, objectifying Conan and the other selectable protagonists by hiding their physique behind layers of anonymizing fabrics.

Though some have called this a "dumbed-down version" of the more "realistic" RTS, that characterization is a bit of an overkill. In fact, it's a little surprising that so many people still get to enjoy these games — the "realistic" aspects may come across as cliched in some people's view. But they don't go unnoticed by anyone who's played the genre over the years — especially if you're familiar with the genre's history itself, which can certainly be a source of pride for the developers working on the title.

Though most games in this genre feature one of Conan's many sidekicks (usually a barbarian, but sometimes an elf or a dwarf as well, and in those rare cases, a human and an elf), each has their own unique abilities, strengths and weaknesses in combat. Most often these include more complex attacks or abilities, but some are more simple in nature – for example, a wizard with a more simple spellcasting, but more powerful attack.

The game's system is also unique in that it is designed to include players and AI-controlled units interacting with each other, rather than in some single, static environment – in other words, the player can be in control of the units rather than being the only player using their abilities. The game also includes a wide variety of "tiles" – different types of terrain – a set of tiles that is used together to create a "map" for an area, or "tiles" that represent an area of land or a single region. Most tiles can be interacted with and used, but a few such as the wilderness are simply inaccessible.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tInteresting strategy with defense elements. The gameplay is quite similar to They Are Billions. You can pause game at any time and issue orders while paused and there is also a top-down view.

I was really keen on playing co-op, but it turned out to be really bad:
  • Resources production is shared, but consumption is not and with 10 different resources, balancing it is really difficult. This mode requires both players to build exactly the same buildings and units.
  • You can pay with your resources to upgrade ally's buildings and can garrison ally's watch towers, but cannot repair ally's buildings.
  • Alerts about buildings being attacked are shown to one player only - you won't be alerted about your ally's building being destroyed.

There is a few minor bugs/issues:
  • Stone walls cannot be built as far from a battle standard as wooden walls (but you can build wooden walls and upgrade them).
  • Market's auto-sell doesn't work when picking up chests with resources.
  • Gate can be built only on top of existing walls, unless there is something close to the walls - this includes terrain and is especially annoying with ramps.
  • Loading screens take ages.

I have been playing on the infinite waves mode and conquered the entire map. The game was becoming slower and slower, but only when paused, when unpaused it run without any problems. At some point the game crashed and it now crashes consistently when trying to load this save...\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI regret buying this.
- Waves come too often and feel insignificant: there is not much progression of your base and technologies between the waves since they are timed closely after each other. In TAB, where there is more time between waves, you will and have to get new techs and a stronger base. Way more satisfying.
- Combat overall feels sluggish and slow. Like a game from 15 years ago. Easy to get a big yawn in between every archer shot.
- Towers require wood / metal ukeep. Those materials are limited on each map. Meaning there is a hard cap on how many towers you can build. And while i never reached that limit, it doesn't sit right with me to cheapely limit the amount of defenses. Should be only worker / gold dependent.
- Game gets very laggy after a while. The lag is worse when trying to build in pause mode. When the game does not have to do any real time operations, since it is paused. Doesn't make sense. Yeah.

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tIt's fun, but quickly becomes repetitive. I'm glad I bought it for 13 euros. It's worth that price.

The campaign mode is a missed oportunity. There's no story. You build very similar camps each time. And that's all there is to it.

No banter or story, corny or otherwise. No real options as to what you will be building or which troops you will use. It's engaging and difficult enough that you will want to beat a few levels and then you'll have done all there is to do.

The different waves become very samey rather quick too.

Things they could add:
1. A story
2 'equipment' for troops, more variety in soldiers. A lot more. The tech tree looks impressive, but really isn't.
3. A 'bad' guy campaign. With bad guy monsterkies.
4 Vehicles, boats.
5 a campaign with lasting elements. Like, fi, building a city and defending it trhough more than one mission.
6 Idem, with a more intricate leveling up system for heroes.
7 Naming your troops. If 'Kevin' gets eaten, it's always more hilarious than soldier nr 2.
8 Easier ways to select troops.
9. Attacking a city, instead of just defending one.
1O Inovative ideas!! :D\t\t\t\t\t
