\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI used to play this a lot more than my indicated time says. I had previously bought it on DotEmu, and before that I think my public library had a copy I used, too. It's definitely in my top 20.

A wonderful slow-economy real-time-strategy game. Its economies are much slower and much more detailed than the likes of Age of Empires or Rise of Nations. You play a tribe of Vikings and build up farms, industries, and weaponsmiths so you can trade and build and army to conquer your foes. It takes time, but the time it takes is enjoyable, and not as much of a scramble as faster RTS games.

The campaign leaves something to be desired. But the free-standing scenarios are pretty good, much fun to explore, and challenging.

Plus, it comes with an editor and you can play multiplayer games. So you theoretically have a limitless amount of things you can do with this game.

If you liked Knights and Merchants or the original Cultures, or The Settlers series, you will probably like Northlands, too. But I highly suggest trying the original Cultures, KaM, and Settlers II and III first.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI used to play this game when I was a kid, on an old computer. Loved it, but had no idea what I was doing.
Gladly Lord Gaben came to my aid and sold it to me for one euro, so I could finally finish it.

The game is good.

It offers an 8-mission campaign and various pre-made maps. Each mission (both campaign and non-campaign) features various objectives that can be carried out as you prefer. For example, you can either gain the favor of a king through tributes or destroy his kingdom to reach the catacombs under his city. The campaign takes about 8-9 hours.

The RTS gameplay is solid, but this game is more focused on diplomacy and resources, so the combat system is not that great. Luckily the game is fairly easy even on "the highest" difficulty (i.e. normal).
You might find it difficult to understand what goes on and how to get the people to do what you want at first unless you play the tutorial, which is fairly long but it's well done.

The game costs €5 but it often gets discounted down to €1. Go get it.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tCultures - Northland is one of those games I used to play quite a bit when I was far younger - back then, I got the game as a free extra to a gaming magazine. Now, I recently bought the game for the sake of nostalgia to be able to try it out again. While I kind of enjoyed the experience, mostly due to the nostalgia factor... if I am to be objective, I cannot really recommend the game due to its dated mechanics and numerous flaws.

Northland is a city builder game with some RTS elements to it, but at its heart it's a city builder game, so I'll focus on that aspect. You control a group of vikings (often the hero Bjarni, a swordfighter and the main character of the campaign, although there are free-play scenarios you can play as well) who have to set up shop in the unspoiled nature and fulfill various objectives. Depending on the mission, this can be "collect X resources" or "defeat an enemy faction" or "find an NPC that you have to talk to". You have to chop wood, mine clay and stone, and move up the tech tree to build up an increasingly sophisticated civilization. At first, it's actually quite a bit of joy to watch the adorable little vikings move around, do their work, talk to each other, and so on. The graphics are definitely quite good for their age, allowing for a nice atmospheric, if campy experience.

So far, so good. But one doesn't have to play much to see the numerous niggling problems of the game. The vikings are notoriously dumb and you need to erect a tight network of signposts to let them even find their way around their own village, but even then, often they will fail to find a critical resource they need. Sometimes an iron miner might be unable to find the iron vein right in front of their noses (NPCs seem to have problems with rough terrain), at other times the flour might be too far away from the bakery (despite not seeming to be too far at all). Many features are extremely finicky. You cannot transport resources en masse from the mill to the bakery, for example, at least not straight away. You have to either let the baker do it himself, carrying flour bags one by one, or assign a carrier to do so, who will still only carry them one by one. Then, after a carrier gains enough experience to become a merchant, you can have the merchant use a hand cart or ox cart (which are both quite far up another branch of the tech tree) to transport goods, but the mechanics to do so are still non-intuitive. Vikings who extract raw resources will put them on the ground, those who work in a workshop will amass their finished goods there. One would think that you could, for example, decide to carry all the bricks, stone slabs and marble in the town to a centrally located warehouse, but in practice, this is also extremely cumbersome. It seems impossible to "lock" goods out of a warehouse, so a carrier assigned to the warehouse will ferry random goods into there, while a carrier assigned to the stonemasonry will not know to carry the finished goods away. You need to manually task merchants to transport those exact goods - again, a very frustrating process that cannot really be automated. The lack of automation and other user-friendliness features will often have you scratching your head or yelling at the screen, depending on your temperament, thinking "What are these idiots doing? Is there any way to make them act sensibly?" The answer is usually no, or at least not very obvious, as the tutorial only teaches the very basics and leaves out quite a few important tricks that you have to learn as you go. And worse yet, while a lot of things cannot be automated, often even manual intervention is not enough to fix idiocy. I've encountered some of my iron miners half-starved to death because they couldn't notice the iron vein in front of them, but also didn't want to eat for some reason despite having a berry bush next to them and lots of fresh bread farther away in the town bakery. Ordering him to eat didn't help either, however, and it took quite a bit of trial and error to figure out how to make this unviable specimen survive.

Of the RTS elements, I'll speak less. You can train soldiers and equip them with weapons, armor, shoes to help them move faster, and potions to restore their health, stamina and nourishment. This is a ton of micromanagement, and unfortunately it's necessary since not stocking them up with nourishment and stamina potions just leads to even more micromanagement and frustration as soldiers fall asleep in the middle of battles, and not stocking them up with health potions means they die like flies (replenishing your townsfolk is done by ordering your married women to have children of a sex of your choosing, but this takes some time). The combat AI is not too good, but fortunately, it isn't too good with your enemies either. Or, rather, unfortunately, since this means it's not much of a challenge. During the campaign and the combat-oriented scenarios, enemies usually just stand still and wait for you to engage them. If you kill them, they either won't replenish at all, or only to a very modest degree.

This also means that the campaign isn't that much fun as you basically have to build the same town over and over again in every scenario (the same resources are always available, only with a slightly different layout every time, and you pretty much always go through the same motions when you build a new town). Either you have to gather an arbitrary amount of resources, or you have to defeat an enemy force that, as I mentioned before, does not replenish much or at all, and it's only a matter of time. The enemy is never a threat to your town unless you accidentally lure the whole stack back to your town prematurely. Later, there are fixed-force missions which tend to have even more boredom and even less challenge.

Verdict: avoid. If you want cheap older city-building games with a historical theme, then Tropico 4, or even titles like Poseidon, Caesar, Pharaoh should be more to your fancy. Northland is just an exercise in frustration to get into and does not offer much in the way of replayability. If you've built up your town once, you've seen it all.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI don't mind complexity in my games but it has to serve a purpose. In a first person shooter you wouldn't want to load a magazine bullet by bullet and release the slide every time you reload your pistol, but it works in Receiver because the whole game is built around the detailed functions of your firearm. Plenty of city-builders have complex systems like detailed citizen needs or production chains, but most pull back from having too many at once. Cultures does no such thing, piling on every possible system and consideration until you are running every facet of every resident's life. And if that somehow sounds like fun to you, well, read on.

Cultures - Northland puts you in charge of a band of plucky settlers in a cartoonish approximation of Middle Ages Europe. Each scenario in the lengthy campaign is heavily story-driven but at the heart of each you'll need to establish a thriving village and a standing army. You do this by gathering natural resources like wood and stone, using them to build workshops to refine them into more complex materials, and work your way up the enormous chain of upgrades and structures. Your settlements will sprawl like few others, with dozens of workshops ringed by a sea of dwellings and fortifications. It's quite a pleasure to watch your little folks putter along in their daily lives once you're established, moving goods and chatting with each other.

Getting established is the hard part, and it's mainly because of the depth afforded to your citizens. Every single inhabitant of your settlement is like a tiny RPG character, with meters for needs like hunger, sleep, and even socializing. They'll duck away from their duties to resolve these needs, so you'll need to keep their homes and food sources nearby. They also gain experience in the dozen plus different professions as they work, which is key to progressing because you can't staff the higher-level buildings until your workers in that profession level up as well. And if that wasn't enough, they also have equipment and inventories, along with special settings specific to their job like what kind of goods to make or which trading posts to visit.

Pressing spacebar with a citizen selected opens a menu with over a dozen different commands to issue them. Some of these aren't even necessary as the game uses context-sensitive right clicks (and in fact only issues orders with right-click, which threw me off when trying to build), while others are essential. You move your people around as you would in a proper real-time strategy game, except the pathfinding is atrocious, sending them on longcuts through dense forests instead of along the straight roads. This might be purposeful because your scouts can build signposts to mark paths, but this option is hidden among a dozen others in that tiny, cluttered spacebar menu.

The simple fact is that it will take you an inordinate amount of time and effort to do anything in Cultures, and you'll be required to do all that and more in each scenario. I haven't even touched on combat, or trading, or vehicles, or the religion system, after all. A game this deep and complex needs solid tutorials, but I could only get about half of them to even work. I did manage to extract the basics of orders, building, and combat so armed with that knowledge I started the campaign. The very first scenario has you defending a village under attack, using hero units that will earn you a game over if they die.

I don't think Cultures - Northland is a bad game necessarily, but it's not one I could ever recommend to anyone. The bewildering level of complexity formed from the crossing of so many disparate systems makes the audience for this game too tiny to even identify. It feels like a game from a completely different time, the kind you had to be there for at release and willing to devote weeks and months to playing. Well, times have changed and when I sit down to build a city or raise an army, I don't want to have to equip my people or put them down for naps. I mean, I already have two kids of my own.
