\r\n\t\t\t\t\tAnno 1404 is going to drain your time like no other - you constantly have more to do than you can, but its mostly tedious work after you have learnt the ins and outs of production.

Main gameplay consists of building, expanding and maintaining supply chains under time pressure. The game throws you curve balls while you are busy with existing growth challenges.

Main issues come down to missing information (again... like in all of these games)
- What size and how many fields does a building need? (Counteracted by full refund when destructing buildings -only on easy- BUT time is your most valuable resource!)
- How much wood does a woodchuck chuck if a woodchuck would chuck wood? (even tutorial tip mentions some buildings requiring multiple sources, but... how many and which? You have to try it out)
- How many and where are XXX production buildings on the island/whole map? (all buildings look alike after a while)
- killing AI is like playing whack-a-mole as they generate resources out of thin air. When conquering their resource depots they will just build another one just outside your armies range. Only way to force them out of an island is to maintain area presence where the AI can not build.

A lot of these challenges could be better if the game had better logistics simulation. In the grand year of 1400 island wide quantum teleportation storage has been invented and all resources within an island exist in ALL of the storages at the same time and can not be destroyed unless the last building holding them is erased... Road connection is required in some cases, but for citizens it matters not how long said road connection is (there just needs to be a path). Thus building most efficiently includes having a storage depot in the middle and huddle all production buildings as close as possible to it (only logistics simulation is from storage to pick up resources from the production sites and back in a cart).

I miss connection between population and production... manpower wise. As it stands, population exists only to consume resources and generate gold. They do not participate in work in any way. Production is always fully employed...

The game is good. You will be working while playing. As the game mainly comes down to microfest I am not convinced this is an interesting multiplayer game for me.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tPros: This is a fascinating game of city-building, managing multiple medieval cities and colonies on various islands connected by ships. The economics are elaborate. The music is beautiful. The graphics are good without being beyond the abilities of a mid-range video card several years old.

Cons: What you get here from Steam is "Anno 1404 Venice", which is enhanced from the original but with the original tutorial campaign suppressed. You can get access to it only by starting the game up in a special way. Be prepared for a LOT of micro-management, especially the need to start and stop farms and factories when an island's storage is empty or full. It's a pity that the designers provided no way to automate this adjustment. I recently received an "achievement" for having halted my production units five hundred times in a single game! Trade routes are a crucial part of tying your economy together but are not sophisticated enough. (I'm told that this shortcoming was rectified somewhat in Anno 1800). All in all, optimal play requires slowing the game down to the slowest possible speed, which in my experience cannot be done with the '-' key that is supposed to control it. Instead you must press F11 and click on the left arrow on the bar at the bottom of the screen. With this done, the game is much less nerve-wracking, but a conscientious play-through of any scenario will require many hours.
