\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is really more of a thumbs-medium.

Deathtrap is a feature-length adaptation of the tower defense minigame from Van Helsing. Van Helsing is quite good, and very worth checking out if you like casual arpgs, but it's not built to play a tower defense in, and right from the start you can feel the limitations of the engine in Deathtrap.

Controls are a bit cumbersome. Items spawn on the map. Side objectives are limited by what is already possible in Van Helsing.

None of these things make the game objectively bad, but they wouldn't be there if Deathtrap had been built ground up as a tower defense.

On the plus side, saving a bunch of time and effort by recycling the engine should mean Deathtrap is crammed with content, but unfortunately it's not.

Design-wise, there are three classes with complex skill sets that feel good to play. Enemies are recycled from Van Helsing, and have mostly the same qualities, but there's an okay variety of them. There's no "puzzle" enemies in the campaign that need to be solved with a particular damage type, but different enemy categories have different vulnerabilities and each mission has multiple levels of difficulty that it can be replayed at once you clear the campaign.

However, the campaign is only thirteen missions. It fits on a single small map. My playtime is reflective of leaving the program running, not a standard playthrough of the game. The game itself is shorter.

If this isn't a barrier for you, if you just want a timekill that scales up in difficulty, Deathtrap is probably going to be a fine game. It's decently fun, the little bit of writing that there is is solid, and I didn't run into any game-breaking bugs or massive UI frustrations.

However, if you want a TD with some meat on its campaign, this isn't that. Defender's Quest or Gemcraft might be your bag instead.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tLet me start off by saying that I really love this game. If you think you might enjoy a tower-defence game loosely crossed with an ARPG, then you'll likely get your money's worth out of it - especially if you bring a friend along.
That said, I play solo these days and still enjoy it without feeling restricted.
At the current discounted price, it's hard to go wrong.

The core gameplay is easy to get a grip on, but does have some depth and strategy to it. A feeling of progression is present; you level up your character, invest in skills, invest in traps, and find, buy or craft better gear as you get further in the game.
Some of these reviews complain about repetitiveness, which is definitely there - but I find that other Tower Defence games are fairly similar in that regard.
The game is no longer being worked on, but I haven't come across any bugs (I read about one where map portals won't let you teleport in high-res, but it is not present for me on Very High graphics settings and 2560x1440 res). This game feels more polished than a lot of AAA games out today.

The balance is good; it's quite achievable to 3-star each map on hard from a fresh character, including the bonus objectives if you have good map awareness and invest in the right traps in the right areas. The motivation to do so is there, too - you receive more XP and additional trap skill points for doing so.

The three classes; Mercenary, Sorceress and Marksman, each have their own individual strengths that you can focus on - or completely ignore if you find it more fun. You will be relying on your character quite a bit to make up for any shortcomings in your defences - this generally keeps you engaged as opposed to simply having to wait for your traps to clean up, though you will find yourself running back and fourth a bit.
For example, the Mercenary has skills which benefit physical/mechanical traps, so you're generally better off investing in those traps than others.
You can have multiple characters, and each holds its own progression through the different maps - you can't take a different character into a map or difficulty tier you haven't unlocked yet, or transfer gear between characters.

Enemy variation is interesting - not only is there a wide variety, but they also gain different abilities and effects at different points in the game. Bosses have unique and interesting mechanics, too.

Finishing all the maps grants you access to the next tier of difficulty to progress through, which although may be a bit repetitive and grindy at points, still provides progression and challenge. I found the endless mode to be harder than other games, however I don't find this to be a bad thing; you don't have to wait so long for things to become challenging, and the XP reward is relatively significant.
Workshop maps can provide a change, however they don't provide the normal rewards so people can't abuse it.

All in all, if you like this style of game then you'll likely get your money's worth.\t\t\t\t\t
