\r\n\t\t\t\t\tThis is a pretty safe game when it comes to the library of the choice/host games. I mean that in the sense that it isn't an outstanding work but it also isn't a dumpster fire.

The hero customization stuff is awesome, really lets you cook up an idea of your character to be pretty close to what your ideal super powered persona would be. However, like what a lot of other reviews say, a lot of those choices are mostly just for flavor, as they don't really change the outcome of a lot of choices when it comes to combat.

The story is interesting enough to keep you captivated, but from my first play though, there isn't really anything that pushes the boundaries what what being a superhero/vigilante would deal with. Just mainly your standard "here is your new arch-nemesis" and the constant juggle between your personal life, work life, and hero life.

Still, not a bad game, but not a great one either.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tSo be forewarned there will be many somewhat non-specific spoilers in the review below, though if you play the demo you'll find out who the mech i'm often referring to is pretty fast.
So I found this story to be exceedingly annoying to play, it has the typical problem these games have where you will make decisions you think are consistent but leave your personality stats all over the place because the author has their own idea of what you should be choosing rather than being consistent. Other times your stats don't matter, like relationship stats, I spent time invading an antagonists dreams and improving our relationship, but it changed nothing at all about our interactions even after we met outside of our alter ego personas, based on the stats she should be in love with me but reacts to me exactly the same way as a previous play through where she hated me for foiling her plots. So how relevant your stats are and "when" they are relevant are invisible and unknowable to the player at all times.

The paragraph above is common for these type of games, but the big problems for me where the frustratingly blatant ex machina moments where a main antagonist gets away every time even though you can teleport or move at eight times normal speed, and they are in a large mech suit, and you have no option to chase after the antagonist in these encounters. Another moment where I can move at twice human speed, have bullet proof skin, heal from any injury in half an hour and somehow she, in a dress not a mech suit, manages to react to my movement enough to scratch me with a vaguely defined really "sharp nail", leaving me temporarily incapacitated from the poison enough for her to smugly walk away, which given my abilities should be implausible at best, also she can make a camera drone that can follow a someone who can teleport home, somehow she managed is the best explanation we get. So game play is frequently sacrificed and often irrelevant in favour of the authors favourite characters rather than branching stories.

Other major problems that have no doubt been touched on by others are:
- Your Mother's existence is to solely to extract cash from you, and even if you decide you've always hated her guts she reacts to you the same way with "love you lots" despite the player having chosen that you have a terrible relationship. I also live in Canada so the treatment of your character's Mother and her having cancer, well I'll explain. Apparently she needs to fork over 2 grand every couple weeks or her doctors tell her shes gonna die without her meds, which is both horrifying to me and strikes me as a criminal racket akin to the periodic mugging of a sick person.
- There are very few characters I liked enough to consider romancing, and most of them are either shallow or don't get enough screen time to matter. The ones I tended to be interested in exploring also never crossed my path or gave me an opportunity to explore the option.
- Being a villain isn't a full time job for some reason, I can rip an armored mech apart with my bare hands but still have to work a normal job for poorly defined reasons.
- Only applicable jobs you can choose from rarely have overlap with your super abilities, unless you pick tech and play as Iron Man, forcing you to have to branch out your stats too much or be poor.
- Not all power sets you can choose from are equal, so you won't be able to go full Tatsumaki with telekinesis and the mech person I keep talking about has an antenna that makes her immune to psi, while she can easily make up 75% of the fights in a play through.
- Your character runs away from the prospect of having to fight large groups of people even though they are incapable of hurting you or overpowering you, unless they are classified as "minions" of a super villain
- All attacks boil down to an "x" damage so hitting a random guy with plasma doesn't instantly kill him
- This one is a more serious spoiler but very relevant and since the sequel is out I guess its not too huge of a spoiler, nothing you do in this first game really matters because the second one takes place on another planet, because aliens. Seriously that is how the story ends, an alien comes in the middle of the night and pokeballs you. You are now a battle thrall to fight for their amusement gladiator style.

TL;DR Not recommended, I was morbidly curious and while I may still fiddle with it I would definitely rate it below a 5/10 which is an automatic not recommended in my book. There are too many times where the "choice" the player made in this choose your own adventure style game ends up being ignored in favour of where the author wants to go. This author has you on railroad tracks and will god mod their way through any choice you want to make, so like I would with similar D&D Dungeon Masters I recommend not playing with them.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tChoice of Games/Hosted Games is no stranger to superhero stories. I'm happy to report that this game constitutes one of the strong entries of that type of stories it has to offer.

On a world much like our own, meteorites rained down one day. Any humans that touched one of them gained powers. The bigger the meteorite, the greater the power. This event is very recent, and the world is just adjusting to these individuals, and the first few famous ones are just making a name for themselves. On such a world, you control one of these individuals that got powers not so long ago. The type of powers, and the number and strenght of them, are completely up to you to choose. This is done by first selecting the difficulty of the game: the harder, the less Hero Points you are granted at the beggning, the easier, the more. You then convert these points to abilities, gadgets, or money. I recommend choosing Very Easy, you get more freedom to pick what you want, and you can still lose fights if you are careless. You have to pick a certain concept all your powers are based around: Elements (Earth, Air, Water, Fire, Shadow, Light), Animal traits, Time powers, Mind powers, or a high intelligence that allows you to build handy gadgets.

The story has you trying to juggle your hero life and your civilian life, and deciding what kind of hero do you want to be. There are "main events" that happen at certain intervals, with free afternoons in between where you can pick which activity you want to perfom. Do you want to work on a report for work, do you want to patrol the streets, do you want to treat your wounds at a hospital (if you are hurt), do you want to work with the police, do you want to chase down a lead independently? This all forges your path.

I'm very happy with the path I ended up taking, and I'm actually very excited for the sequel. Yeah, this is just the first part of the story, but it has a nice climax, at least I got a nice one. Well worth a purchase, in my honest opinion.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tA quite interesting hero/villian romp. It had varied powers, good story, my created villain playing hero was so great that I grew attached to him - always a good sign. Characters and setting felt alive, I'd really like to play one more time and try to take a completely different route to see what happens.

You basically try to balance your heroic/villanous life with your work. Contrary to some other titles I've played, it feels natural and believable. There are even "sidequests" that seem to have no consequence save for purely emotional ones (no spoilers, but one was really touching and nicely intervowen). Its small touches like these that make feel the setting to be bigger than it really is, and gives depth to characters. Also, you have a lot of varied adventures and can do surprisingly lot of stuff. More than I'd expect from something clearly labeled as "Genesis", which is to say: I am pleasantly surprised.

That said... I didn't like the epilogue. I feel it's unnecessary and a step in the wrong direction - it is a similar thing to something I reviewed before in the same genre. Without spoilers, yes, it flows logically from the events preceeding it, but I would rather do without it and not go this route. Frankly, I just disregarded it and ended the story of my character just a moment before.

Overall, one of the better titles, if you overlook the epilogue (I'm rather biased against this sort of thing which happens there, I find it a cheap storytelling gimmick). I've had my fun, and I'll definately play once more given time. Also, it allowed me to create one of the more interesting superhero characters I roleplayed (build, powers, characteristics, history). Good job.\t\t\t\t\t

\r\n\t\t\t\t\tI'm going to come straight up and say it: this game is not worth its price.
I'm not saying it's not worth your time or to play it, I am merely stating that, in the current market, there are far better investments of both resources.
It is still quite cheap, and if you have a few hours to kill you can give it a try, just don't expect too much. The main part about enjoying this game is that you probably have to already like this flavor of stories - superheroes and whatnot - or else, due to it not being exceptional or... all that good to begin with, you're not going to have an easy time enjoying it.

I like it, and I'm giving it a recommended, but I can also easily, objectively see that most won't and chances are that's you. If I had to come up with a target audience for this game, I would say children who enjoy the superhero genre; I can't see many adults being content with their purchase.

I'd say... 4.8 out of 10 everything considered. That is regularly not a passing grade, but I did draw some enjoyment out of fooling around with the options and cannot be honest with myself if I were to give it a not-recommended. You don't have to acknowledge a game is good in order to like it.

I liked exploring and exhausting the options... and was done surprisingly soon. The game also uses A LOT of its core regardless of what you do, so choices don't matter a lot. This is the first game of its genre that I play and so far I gotta say that I'm not impressed.\t\t\t\t\t
